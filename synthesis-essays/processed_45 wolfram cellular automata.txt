## The Convergence
Wolfram Rule 30 generates infinite-looking randomness from a simple deterministic rule. This is not true randomness — it is computational irreducibility (cannot predict Nth digit faster than running rule N times). The Tantraloka locates this at Maya: apparent randomness is the signature of a constraint structure so complex it cannot be compressed. Chaos magic use of randomness as creative tool is the practical recognition that irreducibility serves as gateway: encountering the irreducible throws the practitioner back to Siva, where randomness is recognized as lila (play) of consciousness. Computational irreducibility is the formal signature of kancukas in action: each adds a layer making total behavior unpredictable from any lower level.

The 36 tattvas are not thirty-six things — they are one consciousness measuring itself against itself. The apparent separation between the domains in the question is a symptom of operating at the level of Maya (tattva 6), where consciousness has contracted into finitude. The constraint space framework makes this precise: the question arises because the agent (A) is navigating a region of constraint space where connections between domains have been severed by the kancukas.
