## Part 1: Full before/after rewrite

Below is your actual "Becoming an Angel" essay, your connective prose only (not the Source Text blocks — those stay as-is, they're not yours to rewrite). Left is what's live on the site. Right is a Memoo pass. Read them side by side, not the guide in the abstract.

---

**BEFORE:**
> Angela Voss opens with an unexpected angle: alchemy. Not the laboratory kind, but the inner work of transmuting the soul. For Henry Corbin, alchemy was never about making gold. It was about the juncture between spirit and matter, the hidden secret that symbols point to. This secret is accessed through the mundus imaginalis, the intermediate world where the spiritual becomes perceptible and the material becomes transparent.

**AFTER:**
> Corbin's alchemy has nothing to do with gold. Forget the furnace. What he's actually describing is a soul learning to see through itself — spirit and matter meeting at a seam most people never notice is there. He calls that seam the mundus imaginalis. Not a metaphor for a state of mind. A place, with its own weather.

*What changed:* killed the "Voss opens with" narration, killed the not-X-but-Y (used it once, differently, at the end instead of the front), added one small concrete image ("weather") instead of ending on abstraction, shortened the punchiest sentence to four words.

---

**BEFORE:**
> Before diving into Corbin's cosmology, Voss establishes a crucial distinction between two modes of knowing. One is Aristotelian: rational, analytical, the observer separate from the observed. The other is Platonic: participatory, intuitive, the knower united with the known. Corbin's entire project sits firmly in the second camp.

**AFTER:**
> There are two ways to know a thing. You can stand outside it, measure it, take it apart — that's Aristotle's way. Or you can get inside it, let it change you in the knowing — that's Plato's. Corbin never seriously entertained the first.

*What changed:* cut the meta-narration entirely, turned the definition-list into two short parallel sentences with a punch at the end, replaced "Corbin's entire project sits firmly in the second camp" (inert, bureaucratic) with a flatter, more opinionated close.

---

**BEFORE:**
> The cosmology that underlies everything is a three-tiered universe: the divine intelligible realm, the material realm, and between them a mediating dimension that partakes of both. This middle realm is the mundus imaginalis. It is not a static layer but a dynamic field of personified forces.

**AFTER:**
> Three floors to this universe. Top floor: pure intelligible divinity. Ground floor: matter, the stuff you can stub a toe on. And a middle floor most philosophy forgets to build — crowded, noisy, full of gods and angels and daimons actually doing things. That's the mundus imaginalis. Not furniture. A crowd.

*What changed:* the "not a static layer but a dynamic field of personified forces" abstraction became a physical building metaphor (floors) — one concrete image doing the work of three abstract nouns. Ends on a two-word fragment instead of a hedge.

---

**BEFORE:**
> In this cosmology, the imagination is not a human faculty projecting fantasies. It is the angelic mode of perception. Angels do not have physical senses, so they perceive through imagination. When humans cultivate the active imagination, they are literally learning to perceive as angels perceive.

**AFTER:**
> Angels have no eyes, no ears — nothing to perceive with except imagination itself. So when Corbin tells you to cultivate your active imagination, he isn't asking you to daydream better. He's asking you to grow the one organ angels actually have.

*What changed:* led with the strange concrete fact (angels lacking senses) instead of the abstract correction, turned the claim into something almost funny/unsettling ("grow the one organ angels actually have") instead of a flat restatement.

## Part 2: More before/after, pulled from the Plotinus piece — for range

**BEFORE (typical AI hedge):**
> If the output of an LLM is formally intelligible — if it makes sense — then it participates in the intelligible, however far removed.

**AFTER:**
> If an LLM's output makes sense, it's already touching the intelligible. However uncomfortable that is to sit with.

*What changed:* hedge isolated into its own short sentence instead of buried in the clause — same caution, twice the conviction.

---

**BEFORE (restating instead of extending):**
> Coherence is the footprint of Logos. If the output is formally intelligible, then it participates in the intelligible.

**AFTER:**
> Coherence is the footprint of Logos — the track it leaves in the snow after it's already walked past. A model that makes sense isn't faking depth. It's stepping in a print someone else left.

*What changed:* extended the metaphor instead of restating the claim in flatter language. The second sentence does new work (footprint → the model stepping in it) instead of just rephrasing "it participates."

## Part 3: Drop-in prompt block

Paste this at the top of any writing/rewriting request to an AI. It's built to make the model interrogate itself mid-draft, not just follow a vibe.

```
Write in the Memoo voice. Rules, not vibes:

1. Never resolve two consecutive claims the same way. If you use "not X, but Y" once,
   the next correction must be a flat assertion, a fragment, or a question-answer —
   not another "not X, but Y."
2. No sentence may narrate the act of writing or summarizing ("X opens with,"
   "this brings us to," "X argues that"). State the idea directly, first-person authority,
   as if you have a stake in it.
3. No list of exactly three grammatically parallel items. One item, deepened, or
   uneven-length items.
4. Every paragraph needs at least one concrete, physical, or sensory noun. If a
   paragraph is only abstract nouns, stop and insert an image before continuing.
5. Max one hedge word ("seems," "arguably," "in some sense") per paragraph. If you
   need to hedge a strong claim, hedge it in its own short sentence — never buried
   inside the clause that makes the claim.
6. Vary sentence length on purpose. In every paragraph over 100 words: at least one
   sentence under 6 words, and at least one that runs long and doesn't stop for breath.
7. After any paraphrase or quote from a source, the next sentence must add a new
   fact, comparison, or argument — never restate what was just said in flatter words.
8. Commit to a stance. Sound like someone who half-believes this, or is unsettled
   by it, or is quietly amused by it — never like a neutral summarizer.
9. Before finishing each paragraph, silently check: did I just repeat a move from
   the last paragraph? If yes, use a different tool.

Do not announce that you are following these rules. Just write like this.
```

Use it as a system/style prompt, not as instructions to quote back — if the model starts explaining "per the Memoo voice guide, here is..." that's itself a slop tell (see Part 1, pattern 2.2) and means the rules didn't take.

## Part 4: One-line diagnostic for your own future drafts

Read a paragraph out loud. If you can't tell, without looking, whether the next sentence starts with "This is not," starts a three-item list, or ends in a hedge — it's working. If you can predict the shape before you get there, it's slop. Trust your ear over the checklist once you've internalized it; the checklist is training wheels, not the destination.
