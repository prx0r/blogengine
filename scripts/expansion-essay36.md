# you are already an angel

What if the goal of spiritual practice is to become what you already are? The Sufi tradition calls it the Perfect Nature — an eternal companion that has never fallen into limitation.

In the Hermetic text known as the *Ghayat al-Hakim* (The Goal of the Wise), a story is told. Hermes Trismegistus, the founder of all wisdom traditions, is alone in a subterranean vault. The winds howl. The darkness is absolute. And then a person appears — not in the physical space but in the space of vision — a being of such beauty that Hermes knows immediately that this is no ordinary visitor.

> When I wished to bring to light the science of the mystery and modality of Creation, I came upon a subterranean vault filled with darkness and winds... Lo and behold, a person then appeared to me in my sleep in a form of the greatest beauty. He said to me: 'Take a lamp and place it under a glass to shield it from the winds; then it will give thee light in spite of them...' At that I said, 'Who then art thou?' He answered: 'I am thy Perfect Nature. If thou wishest to see me, call me by my name.' (Ghayat al-Hakim, RO Corbin p_004)

Perfect Nature. The phrase is deceptively simple. It means the nature that is already complete, already whole, already what it is supposed to be. Hermes does not need to acquire wisdom. He needs to recognize the one who has always been with him.

> It is the heavenly entity, the philosopher's Angel, conjoined with his star, which rules him and opens the doors of wisdom for him, teaches him what is difficult, reveals to him what is right, in sleeping as in waking. (Ghayat al-Hakim, RO Corbin p_003)

The Perfect Nature is the philosopher's Angel — the entity that is "conjoined with his star." In astrological terms, this means the spirit of the nativity, the daimon assigned before birth. But Suhrawardi, the 12th-century founder of the Illuminationist (Ishrāq) school, developed this into a complete spiritual anthropology. Every soul has a celestial counterpart, a person of light, who exists in the imaginal world (ʿālam al-mithāl) and who knows everything the soul is meant to become.

> Thou, my lord and prince, my most holy angel, my precious spiritual being, Thou art the Spirit who gave birth to me, and Thou art the Child to whom my spirit gives birth... Thou who art clothed in the most brilliant of divine Lights... may Thou manifest Thyself to me in the most beautiful of epiphanies, show me the light of Thy dazzling face, be for me the mediator... lift the veils of darkness from my heart... (Suhrawardi, RO p_005)

Suhrawardi's psalm to his Perfect Nature is one of the most intimate and radical prayers in the world's literature. Notice the paradox: the Perfect Nature is the Spirit who gave birth to Suhrawardi, AND it is the Child to whom Suhrawardi's spirit gives birth. It is both parent and offspring. The relationship is not one of hierarchy but of reciprocity — a mutual creation that occurs outside of time.

Henry Corbin, the great French scholar of Iranian Sufism, called this the unus-ambo formula: 1 × 1.

> Having cut in two the thread spun by a spider, the prince puts it together again, saying: 1 x 1. This is also the formula that we suggested above, because he who deciphers it holds the key to the secret that preserves him both from pseudomystical monism (whose formula is 1 = 1) and from abstract monotheism which is content to superimpose an Ens supremum on the multitude of beings (n + 1). (Corbin, RO p_005)

One times one equals one — but it is a one that contains relationship. It is a bi-unity: a oneness that preserves the distinction between the one who sees and the one who is seen, while knowing that they are the same being in two modes. This avoids the collapse into monism — where the individual dissolves — and the gap of dualism — where God and creature remain forever separate.

> My image looks at me with my own look; I look at it with its own look. Their relation is this unus-ambo in which each of the two simultaneously assumes the position of the I and the self — image and mirror. (Corbin, RO p_006)

When you see your Perfect Nature, you are not seeing a separate being. You are seeing yourself as you are in the imaginal world — yourself as light, as you were before birth and will be after death, as you are in the dimension where time does not apply.

The encounter happens in the imaginal world, which Suhrawardi located at the cosmic north, in the eighth clime beyond the seven geographical climes of the physical earth.

> Between the world of pure spiritual Lights (Luces victoriales, the world of the 'Mothers' in the terminology of Ishraq) and the sensory universe, at the boundary of the ninth Sphere (the Sphere of Spheres) there opens a mundus imaginalis which is a concrete spiritual world of archetype-Figures, apparitional Forms, Angels of species and of individuals. (Corbin, RO p_001)

This is imagination in the ancient sense — the mundus imaginalis, as real as the physical world but composed of a different substance. It is the world where what is bodily becomes spirit and what is spiritual acquires a body; the Perfect Nature lives there, and the soul can visit during sleep, during visionary experience, and at death.

The practice that opens the door to this world is dhikr — the Sufi practice of remembrance.

> Of all spiritual practices... the dhikr is the practice most apt to free spiritual energy, that is, to allow the particle of divine light which is in the mystic to rejoin its like... The fire of the dhikr is visualized as a pure and ardent blaze, animated by a rapid upward movement... It sets fire to all that is there to be consumed, and sheds light on any darkness it may encounter. If light is there already, the two lights associate with each other and there is light upon light. (Corbin, RO p_008)

The dhikr burns through the dark envelope — the false self, the nafs ammāra, the accumulation of conditioned responses that the Sufis call the "commanding soul." When the dark envelope is consumed, the Guide of light becomes visible. The Perfect Nature appears not as a concept but as a presence.

> When the circle of the face has become pure, it effuses lights as a spring pours forth its water... Before you, before your face, there is another Face also of light, irradiating lights... In reality this Face is your own face and this sun is the sun of the Spirit (shams al-ruh) that goes to and fro in your body. Next, the whole of your person is immersed in purity, and suddenly you are gazing at a person of light (shakhs nurani) who is also irradiating lights... This person of light before you is called in Sufi terminology the suprasensory Guide (moqaddam al-ghayb). (Najm Kobra, apud Corbin, RO p_007)

Your own face, but made of light. Your own eyes, but seeing without organs. The Guide is not other than you. It is you in your eternal mode, liberated from the constraints of time and space and the conditioning that made you forget who you are.

The path is about burning away everything that is not already you; the Perfect Nature is in the present, waiting to be recognized — not in the future, waiting to be achieved. Every spiritual practice — every prayer, every meditation, every act of kindness — is a step toward the one who has been standing beside you since before you were born, waiting for you to turn your head and see.
