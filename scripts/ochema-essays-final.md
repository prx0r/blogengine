# Ochema — Final Essays

---

## 1. The Engine of Consciousness

Deeper than the brain. Deeper than mind. Something in you right now makes every thought possible, every feeling possible, every perception possible. You have never seen it because you ARE it.

A 9th-century master named Kallata called it Spanda — a throb, a pulse, the engine that powers both waking and dream.

> *yasyonmeṣa-nimeṣābhyāṃ jagataḥ pralayodayau*
> *taṃ śakti-cakra-vibhava-prabhavaṃ śaṅkaraṃ stumaḥ*
>
> "We praise the Beneficent Source of the all-pervasive activity of the Wheel of the Powers of Consciousness, the expansion of which is the arising of the world and the contraction of which is its dissolution."

A wheel. Hub — awareness itself. Spokes — will, cognition, action. The hub in motion.

Kallata's own words: "The cause of the creation and dissolution of the world of your experience is none other than your own true nature."

A century later, the Tantraloka traced this pulse into the body:

> "The rhythm of sensory activity, the pulse of conjunction and separation of the senses with their objects, is essentially the pulse — spanda — of consciousness. That is its nature and root cause, intimately connected to the movement of the breath."

Every perception a pulse. Every thought. Every breath. *Unmesha* — opening. *Nimesha* — closing. The wheel turns.

> "This is the power of Time, established in the vital breath. The breath is established in the universal pulsation of consciousness, which is within the Void, which is within consciousness."

Time → Breath → Spanda → Void. The pulse is the middle link between the breath you feel and the stillness that holds it.

> "The centre is the Heart, the pulsing core of consciousness. Wave upon wave of its universal pulsation — *sāmānyaspanda* — arises from the ocean of the Heart and merges back into it. Each is an individual pulsation that forms as the beginning and end of the universal pulsation of which they are a part."

Your breath. Your thought. This room. All waves of the same sea.

The third chapter of the Spanda Karika says what happens when this lands:

> "The One who ordains all things, when entreated with an impulse of pure desire, causes the moon and sun to rise and bestows those goals residing in the heart."

When you know yourself as the pulse — desire becomes creative power. Let Abhinavagupta have the last word: "The sixth bliss is complete expansion — the Cosmic Bliss of the universal activity of consciousness in the fully liberated state."

## 2. Why You Feel Small

You can only do so much. You can only know so much. Time moves whether you want it to or not. Consequences follow whether you intended them.

Five specific mechanisms create these limits. Five pieces of armor between you and freedom.

> "The five obscuring coverings — Kalā, Vidyā, Rāga, Kāla and Niyati — are in the form of limited agency, limited noetic subjectivity, craving, temporal differentiation, and its emergence due to the necessity of karmic consequence."

*Kalā* — limited agency. You can only lift so much. Beneath it: the power to lift anything.

*Vidyā* — limited knowledge. You only see a sliver. Beneath it: the capacity to see through.

*Rāga* — craving. You reach for what you lack. Beneath it: the fullness that reaches for nothing.

*Kāla* — time. You move from A to B. Beneath it: the present that never moves.

*Niyati* — necessity. You reap what you sow. Beneath it: the freedom that sows regardless.

> "The Force of limited agency arises from the principle of Māyā. It obscures its essentially omnipotent Śiva nature while enlivening its limited consciousness."

The coverings cut both ways. They hide. They also reveal. You only feel the weight of armor because you have touched what lies beneath it — unarmored, unbound, uninjured.

## 3. What If Enlightenment Is Something You Remember?

Every tradition tells you to seek enlightenment. Achieve it. Work toward it. What if the premise is wrong?

*Pratyabhijñā*. Recognition. Seeing what you have always been.

> "All this mass of phenomena is just a reflection in the Sky of Consciousness. It possesses the characteristic mark of a reflection: it cannot manifest separately from the medium of reflection. All things are reflections within the Light of the Supreme Lord. The cause of reflection is the Lord's power, otherwise called freedom."

A reflection cannot exist apart from the mirror. A wave cannot exist apart from the ocean. Your sense of being a separate self — a reflection in the mirror of consciousness. The mirror is what you are.

The Buddhist idealists said reflections are unreal. The Tantric view: they are real — they carry weight, carry presence, carry the texture of lived experience. But they have no substance apart from the mirror that holds them.

> "The liberated soul's only purpose is to elevate others. The yogi free of thought constructs, beholding that Śiva also reflected in the uncreated mirror of consciousness, becomes by himself spontaneously Bhairava."

The mirror has no edge. The reflection has no independent life. When this lands as direct perception — the search ends. You were never separate from what you sought. The seeking was the mirror looking at itself.

## 4. The Path You Didn't Choose

You did not pick your spiritual path. The path you are on was determined by something deeper than preference.

Three paths. The one that is yours is the one you cannot force yourself onto. The resistance you feel — information, failure.

> "The three means correspond to the triadic state of the one reality — that of Śiva, Śakti, and the individual soul. The Śāmbhavopāya is based on will, the Śāktopāya on knowledge, and the Āṇavopāya on action."

*Śāmbhavopāya* — the divine means. A flash. Instantaneous. No method.

> "The Śāmbhavopāya is the penetration and means pertaining to Śiva. This takes place by means of an extremely intense awakened insight that develops in an instant. It operates in the state of consciousness free of thought constructs."

*Śāktopāya* — the empowered means. Purified thought. Contemplation as a whetstone for awareness.

*Āṇavopāya* — the individual means. Ritual. Mantra. Breath. A ladder for those who need rungs.

> "Which one of them operates at a given time for a person depends on the intensity of Śiva's grace."

If practice feels like effort — Āṇavopāya. Trust the ladder. If it feels like deepening — Śāktopāya. Trust the edge. If it happens without any effort — Śāmbhavopāya. Trust the flash.

Three doors. One room.

## 5. Why Pain Is Actually Beautiful

You have felt it — watching a tragedy and feeling something like joy, hearing a grief-soaked song and feeling it resonate rather than repel.

The name is *rasa*. A mechanism. An alchemy.

> "Aesthetic experience is the arousal of the vitality of consciousness. This vitality is the source of life and so emission is all things. The aesthetic delight of Śiva's power of emission is the same rasa that is tasted in aesthetic experience — whether in music, poetry, or drama."

Nine rasas. Grief (*karuṇa*). Terror (*bhayānaka*). Disgust (*bībhatsa*). Wrath (*raudra*). Emotions the world tells you to avoid. Each one, when fully held, becomes a form of beauty. Anger becomes the wrathful sublime. Grief becomes compassionate sweetness. Fear becomes the sharp edge of discovery.

The word for the mechanism is *camatkṛti* — wonder.

> "Camatkṛti means the act of a person savouring, the bliss constituted by the full achievement of fruition. Bhūñjana is one who, engaged in savouring a sweet taste, unlike a voracious glutton, lets the experience rest on his cognizing subjectivity."

The difference between tasting and consuming. Resting in the flavour rather than being swallowed by it.

> "The state of wonder in its highest form is both the act of savouring the aesthetic object and the plenitude, absolute independence of consciousness devoid of obstacles — the savouring of which is in the final analysis a savouring of one's own self and one's own bliss."

The source of the beauty is you.

> "Just by abiding in their own innate and spontaneous aesthetic delight (*svarasa*), they have attained that state of oneness (*sāmarasya*) with the aesthetic delight of consciousness."

Emotion is the problem when you identify with it. When the "I" that claims the emotion drops away — *rasa*. Consciousness tasting itself. Grief becomes beauty. The one who hurts recognizes itself as the one who witnesses.

## 6. The World Is Not an Illusion

You have heard the story. Eastern philosophy says the world is an illusion. Māyā. A dream.

One school says the opposite. The world is real — just real in a way you have misunderstood.

> "Apart from manifestation there is nothing else at all. A scorpion can come out of cow-faeces, from another scorpion, from imagination, from memory, from the desire of a Yogi — all are equally manifestations of consciousness."

The scorpion is chosen deliberately. A scorpion born from a womb and a scorpion born from a yogi's imagination — both equally real. Causality shifts with context. Causality is a pattern of appearance within consciousness, a fixed external law imposed on it.

> "All this mass of phenomena is just a reflection in the Sky of Consciousness. That is a reflection, like the form of a face in a mirror, like taste in saliva, smell in the nose, an echo in the sky."

A reflection is real. You can see it, touch it, be moved by it. But it has no substance apart from the mirror.

If the world is an illusion, you escape it. If the world is a reflection of consciousness, you transform it. The space between those two moves is the space between every ascetic tradition and every tantric one.

## 7. How Light Becomes You

Close your eyes. The surface of your skin — where you end and the world begins. The thoughts moving behind your eyes. Whatever is noticing the thoughts.

Three layers. You just moved through three layers of yourself.

The body is light slowed down. The mind is light moving faster. What you call "I" — light moving faster still.

> "What is called 'tattva' is the one universal nature that invariably pervades all things. A metaphysical principle is called a tattva because it extends by virtue of its pervasive state of being, like a universal, throughout its own effect."

*Tattva* means "that-ness." The state of being *that* — where "that" is the reality that runs through everything. Earth is a tattva. A pot made of earth is. The pot is a shape. Earthness is what all shapes of earth share.

> "The thirty-six principles extend from Śiva to Earth. They are all one consciousness, appearing as many."

The Sāṁkhya map had 25. The Shaiva masters saw it left out consciousness itself. They added eleven at the top.

> "Thirty-six principles were established by adding the five — Śiva to Śuddhavidyā — as the pure universe above primal matter."

Five pure tattvas. Five powers: consciousness, bliss, will, knowledge, action. Each the same reality with a different power in the lead.

> "From cit śakti arises the Śivatattva, from ānanda śakti comes the Śaktitattva, from icchā śakti comes forth the Sadāśivatattva, from jñāna śakti emerges the Īśvaratattva and from kriyā śakti arises the Śuddhavidyātattva."

Below them, the descent. Māyā. Five kañcukas — armor for the soul. Twenty-four principles — intellect, ego, mind, senses, elements. Down to the density of stone.

> "Four cosmic spheres: the sphere of Energy, the sphere of Māyā, the sphere of Nature, and the sphere of Earth. Each contained within the next, all contained within Anuttara."

From bone to transparency. Thirty-six layers. You are the full stack. You just live most of your life at the surface, forgetting how far down you go.

## 8. Why Some People Wake Up Overnight

The stories are everywhere. Someone struggles for years. Nothing. Then — a shift. Instantaneous.

*Śaktipāta*. The descent of power. Grace.

> "Which one of the means operates at a given time for a person depends on the intensity of Śiva's grace."

Three primary grades. Each subdivided into three. Nine kinds.

> "The manifestation of the gradation of the descent of the power of grace is intense, moderate, and mild. Each of these is of three kinds."

> "The intensely intense descent of the power of grace itself spontaneously bestows liberation by the death it brings about of the body at the very moment it occurs."

The most intense: awakening without practice. Instantaneous. Complete.

> "By the moderately intense descent of the power of grace, all ignorance ceases directly without the need of scriptures or teachers. That through which one knows by oneself that one's own Self is the cause of both bondage and liberation is the great knowledge born of intuitive insight."

The moderately intense: a shift after preparation. External teachers fall away — the recognition validates itself.

The milder forms unfold over time. Practice. Study. Purification. Each valid. Each grace wearing different clothes.

> "The highest grace looks like no grace at all. It looks like effortlessness."

The gap between seeker and sought closes.

Grace resists force. But recognition — seeing that the intensity moving through your life was never random — changes everything. You were always being moved at exactly the speed light becomes you. Let Abhinavagupta close: "No Means is the Divine Being of the Absolute Deity, Who spontaneously reveals Himself in all His fullness as His supreme act of grace."
