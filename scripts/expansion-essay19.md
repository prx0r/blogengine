# the battle you are fighting

The classic Indian text opens on a battlefield. Arjuna looks at the army facing him and sees his own teachers, his own family, his own ancestors. He drops his bow. He says "I will not fight." And then a god explains why he must. What follows is a complete metaphysics of action, delivered between two armies waiting for a signal to kill each other.

---

Never the spirit was born; the spirit shall cease to be never;
Never was time it was not; End and Beginning are dreams!
Birthless and deathless and changeless remaineth the spirit for ever;
Death hath not touched it at all, dead though the house of it seems!
...
Nay, but as when one layeth
His worn-out robes away,
And taking new ones, sayeth,
"These will I wear to-day!"
So putteth by the spirit
Lightly its garb of flesh,
And passeth to inherit
A residence afresh.

The hiss of breath leaving a body in a cold room — that is all death amounts to. The one who breathes was never born. Krishna begins with ontology: you cannot kill what does not die, and you cannot be killed if you were never born. He dismantles the entire battlefield by changing what the word "you" means.

---

Let right deeds be thy motive, not the fruit which comes from them. And live in action! Labour! Make thine acts thy piety, casting all self aside, contemning gain and merit; equable in good or evil: equability is Yog, is piety! ... When one, O Pritha's Son! abandoning desires which shake the mind — finds in his soul full comfort for his soul, he hath attained the Yog — that man is such! In sorrows not dejected, and in joys not overjoyed; dwelling outside the stress of passion, fear, and anger; fixed in calms of lofty contemplation; — such an one is Muni, is the Sage, the true Recluse!

A potter's hands move clay on the wheel, wholly absorbed yet utterly unattached to the bowl forming under his palms. That is the shape of right action: complete engagement, zero clinging. The archer who releases the arrow and forgets it instantly. The surgeon whose hand does not tremble whether the patient lives or dies. Action without attachment to outcome is the highest form of presence — complete engagement, zero clinging. You give everything to the act because the act is its own reward.

---

Sattwan, Rajas, and Tamas, so are named the qualities of Nature, "Soothfastness," "Passion," and "Ignorance." These three bind down the changeless Spirit in the changeful flesh. Whereof sweet "Soothfastness," by purity living unsullied and enlightened, binds the sinless Soul to happiness and truth; and Passion, being kin to appetite, and breeding impulse and propensity, binds the embodied Soul, O Kunti's Son! by tie of works. But Ignorance, begot of Darkness, blinding mortal men, binds down their souls to stupor, sloth, and drowsiness.

Three strands of rope twisting into a single cord — one the color of sunlight, one the color of blood, one the color of mud. Every thought, every action, every breath is a mixture of these three. The spirit is what the rope holds, not the rope itself. You escape the gunas by seeing them as what they are — costumes the self wears, not the self itself — not by choosing one over the others.

---

Yea! Son of Kunti! for this flesh ye see is Kshetra, is the field where Life disports; and that which views and knows it is the Soul, Kshetrajna. In all "fields," thou Indian prince! I am Kshetrajna. I am what surveys! ... The elements, the conscious life, the mind, the unseen vital force, the nine strange gates of the body, and the five domains of sense; desire, dislike, pleasure and pain, and thought deep-woven, and persistency of being; these all are wrought on Matter by the Soul!

The field beneath your feet after rain — black soil, worms turning, seed splitting open underground, the whole slow churn of growth and rot. And separate from it, standing at the edge, the farmer who knows what the field is because he is not the field. Pleasure and pain are crops. They grow, they are harvested, they rot. The knower watches all of it from a stillness that does not move.

---

Earth, water, flame, air, ether, life, and mind, and individuality — those eight make up the showing of Me, Manifest. These be my lower Nature; learn the higher, whereby, thou Valiant One! this Universe is, by its principle of life, produced; whereby the worlds of visible things are born as from a Yoni. Know! I am that womb: I make and I unmake this Universe: than me there is no other Master, Prince! No other Maker! All these hang on me as hangs a row of pearls upon its string.

A string of pearls in lamplight — each pearl a galaxy, the thread invisible, the hand that holds it nowhere to be seen. The pearls do not see the thread. They see only each other, bumping in the dark, believing they hold themselves together. The higher nature is the thread. The lower nature is the pearls. Both are Krishna. Both are you.

---

Hari! I see Thy limitless, unnumbered forms; on every side, in every shape: no end of Thee, no beginning, nor midst, O Lord of All! All-Pervading! ... I see Thee, infinite in might; without beginning, middle, end; of arms immeasurable; the Sun and Moon Thine eyes; the burning fire Thy mouth; pouring forth splendour, filling with radiance all the spaces betwixt heaven and earth. ... Thou art the Imperishable: the supreme object of all knowledge: Thou art the ultimate resting-place of this universe: Thou art the immortal guardian of the eternal law: Thou art the Ancient Being, I take Thee to be Purushottama!

Arjuna's hands tremble. He sees what no eye can see — a mouth eating every star, teeth of light, the taste of ash and honey at the same instant. He asked for a vision; he got the end of the world. The bow slips from his fingers again, but this time not from grief. This time from the sheer weight of seeing everything at once. When the universe shows you its face, you do not stand. You kneel.

---

Whoever serve Me — as I show Myself — constantly true, in full devotion fixed, those hold I very holy. ... Yet, if thy thought droops from such height; if thou be'st weak to set body and soul upon Me constantly, despair not! give Me lower service! seek to reach Me, worshipping with steadfast will; and, if thou canst not worship steadfastly, work for Me, toil in works pleasing to Me! For he that laboureth right for love of Me shall finally attain! ... Who hateth nought of all which lives, living himself benign, compassionate, from arrogance exempt, exempt from love of self, unchangeable by good or ill; patient, contented, firm in faith, mastering himself, true to his word, seeking Me, heart and soul; vowed unto Me, — that man I Love!

A woman kneeling in a dark temple, oil lamp flickering, her forehead pressed to cold stone. She knows whose photograph hangs above the altar, and that is enough. Krishna makes devotion the easiest path — not because it is simple, but because it requires only one thing: the willingness to love something more than yourself. The intellect stumbles. The will falters. But the heart that has found its home rests.

---

Let the Yogi constantly exercise himself in concentration, remaining in a secret place, alone, with mind and body controlled, free from desires, without possessions. Let him set in a clean place his firm seat, neither too high nor too low, covered with cloth, with a deer-skin, and with kusa grass. Then, sitting there, making his mind one-pointed, with the action of the mind and senses controlled, let him practise Yoga for self-purification. Holding the body, head, and neck erect, immovably fixed, looking not around, let him gaze on the tip of his nose. Serene, fearless, firm in the vow of Brahmacharya, with mind controlled, with thought fixed on Me, let him sit, having Me as his supreme goal.

Dust motes drift in a shaft of afternoon light. The spine is a mountain. The breath is a thread from which the whole world hangs. The one who sits does nothing; the one who sits does everything. The seat is a clean cloth spread on earth: deer-skin, kusa grass, the body erect as a pillar, the gaze soft on the tip of the nose. From this simple geometry, liberation grows.

---

The battlefield is still there. The armies have not moved. But Arjuna is no longer the same man who dropped his bow. Krishna's teaching has not changed the circumstance — it has changed the one who stands inside it. This is the whole secret: you cannot choose your war, but you can become the one who fights it without becoming the fight. The body that picks up the bow is the body that will fall. But the one who watches the body pick up the bow has never been born and will never die. In the space between two armies, suspended in the terror and the glory of a single moment, a man who thought he was a soldier discovers he is the field, the knower, the string, the pearl, and the hand that holds them all. The chariot moves. The conch sounds. And the war that cannot touch the self goes on.
