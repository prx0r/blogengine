# the path chose you

Your spiritual path picked you before you ever chose it. Something deeper than preference.

Three paths. The one that is yours is the one you meet with resistance. The resistance itself: information, failure.

> "The three means correspond to the triadic state of the one reality — that of Śiva, Śakti, and the individual soul. The Śāmbhavopāya is based on will, the Śāktopāya on knowledge, and the Āṇavopāya on action."

The word 'śāmbhava' is derived from the name 'Śambhu' for Śiva. Śāmbhavopāya thus literally means 'the means pertaining to Śambhu (i.e. Śiva)'. The corresponding state to which this means corresponds is Śāmbhava, that is, Śiva's own state. Similarly, 'Śāktopāya' literally means the 'means pertaining to Śakti', and 'Āṇavopāya' is the 'means pertaining to the aṇu', that is, the individual soul. Thus, the three means correspond to the triadic state of the one reality, namely, that of Śiva, Śakti and Nara. Each of these is the full and complete triadic reality experienced from one or other of these perspectives, and so each means is based on, and leads to, the same ultimate reality — Anuttara. Also understood to be Bhairava, Abhinava equates it with No Means (Anupāya), which is the fundamental state of the one reality, which is the Light of Śiva consciousness.

Three windows into the same room. The room stays as it was. The entry — will, understanding, or action — hinges on the doorway awareness has carved in you.

*Śāmbhavopāya* — the divine means. A flash. Instantaneous. No method.

> "The Śāmbhavopāya is the penetration and means pertaining to Śiva. This takes place by means of an extremely intense awakened insight that develops in an instant. It operates in the state of consciousness free of thought constructs."

The expression 'one who does not think of anything' implies that in this means thought constructs are of no use. Thus, as they are of no use and do not arise, the attainment of the object of realization is said to be instantaneous. It operates in the state of consciousness which is free of thought constructs, pertaining to pure subjectivity free of the contamination of objectivity. The means pertaining to Śiva is said to be that of unity (abheda). The Empowered means is said to be unity-in-diversity, and the means based on difference is the Individual one.

No steps. No ladder. The ground simply appears beneath your feet as you step — because there was never any gap between you and where you needed to be.

*Śāktopāya* — the empowered means. Purified thought. Contemplation as a whetstone for awareness.

*Āṇavopāya* — the individual means. Ritual. Mantra. Breath. A ladder for those who need rungs.

The Empowered Means is jñānopāya — the means based on conceptual knowledge. Practice here centres on perception (jñāna), that involves working in various ways and degrees with the flux of vikalpa. The Individual Means (Āṇavopāya) operates through the power of action externally as well as internally. The sphere of practice of the Individual Means includes ritual, Yoga, the recitation of Mantra, which are explicitly excluded in the domain of the practice of Empowered Means. The difference between the Empowered and Individual Means is that the former operates through the cognitive power of consciousness and so is internal, while the latter operates through the power of action. The entire extent of ritual action will be included in a clearly evident form in the last form of knowledge listed here, along with its means, that is, the Individual Means.

Working with thought: sharpening a blade on a stone. The motion is mental, the friction invisible — a whet for awareness. Working with action: digging a well, each stroke physical, visible, measurable.

That which manifests clearly in the first moment of perception within one's own self-awareness, the sole abode of pure thought-free consciousness, is said to be the direct means to realisation called the will (icchā). The first moment of perception is free of thought constructs. This is the phase in which reality 'as it is' (yathābhūta) is perceived directly. In the second phase, the content of the first phase is analysed and thus rendered amenable to the daily commerce of life, in which individual objects and perceivers are identified and known through the process of conceptualization. The first moment of self-reflective awareness is the subtlest state of knowledge. It is the will, that is, the pure intention to perceive which is the initial moment of perception.

The first glance at a face before you name it — that gap, that silence between seeing and saying — that is the door.

> "Which one of them operates at a given time for a person depends on the intensity of Śiva's grace."

Abhinava reverses the order in which the three means and their corresponding states of penetration are defined, because he would like those who practice the teachings to attain the goal as soon as possible. The higher means are more immediate than the lower ones and therefore more effective and efficient. However, which one of them or combination of them operates at a given time for a particular person depends on the intensity of Śiva's grace. It is not a matter of personal choice. All means ultimately culminate in the flash of insight that consciousness cannot be conditioned in any way, because there is nothing outside it. The basis of all the means to realisation, by virtue of which they are effective, is the Divine Means. This is because it is essentially pure, perpetually expanding consciousness, apart from which nothing could exist.

The path chooses you — the way a river is carved by the weight of water already moving through it.

The Pervasive Lord reveals His own complete form in all His fullness to one perceiver, while He does so to another part by part, successively. The Lord reveals His full form through the rapidly heightening gradation of intense obscuration decrease, and to another part by part in accord with the slow gradation of decreasing covering. The full experience of one's own nature as the one Being of all beings is the supreme knowledge individual souls possess. The direct means is the Divine Means (Śāmbhavopāya). That alone is the unimpeded instrumental means to supreme knowledge. When it reaches its supreme summit, it is called No Means (Anupāya). The means to the Divine Means is the Empowered one, and the means to that is the Individual one. Thus the one means is divided, but this division is figurative, not ultimate.

To one, the room floods with light all at once. To another, a single lamp is lit in a corner, then another, then another. Both see the same room. One sees it in a flash, the other piece by piece.

No Means (Anupāya): the Divine Being of the Absolute Deity, Who spontaneously reveals Himself in all His fullness as His supreme act of grace. Śiva's supreme act of grace liberates the individual soul — once and for all, without effort from the one it liberates. Anupāya means anuttaropāya — the Means to attaining Anuttara, the Absolute. The one who attains realisation through this path simply finds it happening — by itself, as a matter of course, at some point, possibly quite early in life, the result of an intense descent of the power of grace.

The absence of a path is itself the path. Like waking from a dream — you simply find yourself awake, and the dream dissolves because the one who dreamed has recognised the light.

In order to discern who is qualified to receive initiation and practice, Abhinava discusses the sequence of the various types of descent of the power of grace (śaktipāta). The attainment of the plane of Anuttara alone is the fruit of all the means to realization, including the Individual Means and the rest. The descent of grace is graded from the most intense, which brings immediate liberation without any need for practice, through moderate degrees, to the mildest, which requires extensive practice over many lives before liberation is attained.

Grace falls like rain — some are soaked through instantly, others stand in it for years before the water penetrates. The rain falls everywhere. The soil receives according to its thirst.

The greater the power of intuition (pratibhā), the greater the capacity for realization. Pratibhā is the supreme means to realization, for it is by virtue of this creative intuitive genius that consciousness recognizes itself. Abhinava compares pratibhā to the cow who grants all wishes, as it renders that reality which is beyond even the most wishful imagination vividly evident. Poets and sages shine with pratibhā. Even animals possess it to some limited degree — birds know when it is time to migrate, lions have a sense of their strength.

Intuition: the baseline of all cognition. The flash of knowing that precedes every thought. The path you are on is simply intuition taking the shape suited to your ground.

If practice feels like effort — Āṇavopāya. Trust the ladder. If it feels like deepening — Śāktopāya. Trust the edge. If it happens without any effort — Śāmbhavopāya. Trust the flash.

Three doors. One room.
