# the stars are in your blood

---

A Renaissance priest wrote a medical textbook that reads like occult science. Your health depends on the stars — the actual planetary forces entering your body through the food you eat, the air you breathe, and the images you hold in your mind, not your horoscope.

He was a priest, a physician, a Platonist, and an astrologer — all simultaneously. He called his book *Three Books on Life* (*De vita libri tres*), and it is the strangest and most sincere work of Renaissance medicine. It is a book about how the cosmos literally enters your body through the medium of *spiritus* — rather than a book about symbolism — a subtle, vaporous substance produced from blood, refined by the heart, rising to the brain, and used by the soul for both inner and outer sense. The stars are in your blood — influencing you from within, rather than from a distance.

---

The soul and body, far different in nature, are joined together by the spirit as a medium, which is a certain very thin and clear vapour, produced by the heat of the heart from the thinnest part of the blood.

This is the single most important passage in the entire *De vita*. The *spiritus* is the link between body and soul — the interface, neither purely material nor purely spiritual. Ficino's entire medical and magical system depends on this concept. The *spiritus* receives impressions from music, air, odour, light, mood, rhythm, and image. It is tempered and nourished by smells, sounds, and song — and also by syrups, pills, and electuaries.

Painters care for their brushes; soldiers care for their weapons; musicians care for their instruments. Ficino compares scholars to craftsmen — yet scholars neglect the instrument by which they measure and grasp the whole world: the spirit. The chain is precise: the stomach and liver produce blood; the heart refines blood into spirit; the spirit rises to the brain; the brain serves the senses; the senses serve reason. Damage any link in this chain and thought itself becomes cloudy.

---

"Gold is incorruptible, solar, bright, noble, stable, anti-putrefactive."

Ficino's Book II treats aging as an ecology of intake, decay, and preservation. The enemy is putrefaction — the breakdown of ordered life against corruption — rather than time. The foods that resist putrefaction are those that are already close to the celestial: wine, white sugar, gold, precious stones. These substances concentrate the world-spirit, the *spiritus mundi*, and when ingested, they transfer their celestial properties to the human spirit.

Diligent natural philosophers, when they separate this sort of spirit from gold by sublimation over fire, will employ it on any of the metals and will make it gold.

Ficino's reference to practical alchemy is rare but explicit. The spirit of gold, separated from its gross matter through sublimation, could be taken as medicine. The same gold that preserves the body in Book II becomes a vehicle for celestial reception in Book III. The continuity between material medicine and celestial medicine is visible in the simplest substance.

---

Spirit, which is the airy vapour of our blood and the link between body and soul, is tempered and nourished by airy smells, by sounds, and by song... To the Egyptian priests medicine, music and the mysteries were one and the same study.

Book III of the *De vita* — the longest and most controversial — is about how to open the human spirit to celestial influx. The *spiritus* is the medium through which stellar influence reaches the soul. Music is the most direct method because it moves the spirit without passing through discursive reason. Ficino wrote hymns to the planets and sang them to his lyre, not as aesthetic performance but as spiritual medicine.

Our spiritus is in conformity with the rays of the heavenly spiritus, which penetrates everything either secretly or obviously. It shows a far greater kinship when we have a strong desire for that life and are seeking a benefit that is consistent with it, and thus transfer our own spiritus into its rays by means of love, particularly if we make use of song and light and the perfume appropriate to the deity, like the hymns that Orpheus consecrated to the cosmic deities.

The human spirit and the cosmic spirit are conformable. Through desire, love, song, light, and perfume — through the full engagement of the imaginative and affective faculties — the human spirit transfers itself into the cosmic rays. The talisman, the hymn, the planetary image: these are technologies of transfer. They configure the human spirit to receive celestial power — rather than creating it.

For sound and song arise from consideration in the mind, the impulse of fantasy and the desire of the heart, and in disturbing the air and lending measure to it they vibrate the airy spirit of the listener, which is the link between body and soul. Thus sound and song easily arouse the fantasy, affect the heart and reach the inmost recesses of the mind; they still, and also set in motion, the humours and the limbs of the body.

The mechanism is physical. Sound disturbs the air. The disturbed air vibrates the *spiritus* of the listener. The *spiritus* transmits the vibration to the soul. Music is medicine because it operates at the level of the medium itself.

---

Spiritus, which is the airy vapour of our blood and the link between body and soul, is tempered and nourished by airy smells, by sounds, and by song... To the Egyptian priests medicine, music and the mysteries were one and the same study.

This passage unites the three books. Spirit (Book I) is tempered by smells, sounds, and song (Book III). Medicine, music, and the mysteries were one. Ficino's project is to reunite them. The remedies in Book I — syrups, pills, bloodletting, electuaries — are the same work as the spiritual, operating at the material level — rather than a separate category.

---

Ficino advises his reader to paint a 'figure of the universe' on one's bedroom ceiling in blue, green and gold (the colours of Jupiter, Venus/Moon and the Sun) so that the image of its harmonious proportion may be held, like a mandala or symbol of unity, in the mind and may tune and temper the soul into conformity with its order through the meditative process of active visualisation.

The talismanic image is a configured receptor — rather than a symbol to be interpreted. Its form, colour, and placement actively shape the soul that contemplates it. The soul, by contemplating the ordered form, becomes ordered itself. After contemplating such an image, the individual will not note with so much attention the spectacle of individual things as the figure of the universe and its colours, and will be led to fashion a better image of the harmonious order of the heavens in his own imagination.

Images act on spirit through the subtle medium linking perception, body, and celestial influence. Spirit acts on images in return. The relationship is reciprocal. The operator's emotional state matters: affect, intention, confidence, fear, joy, devotion, or disturbance alter the work.

Ficino's magic is a discipline of hospitality — rather than a technique of compulsion. The practitioner builds a field — physical, imaginative, emotional — in which celestial intelligence can be received. The room, the hour, the scent, the posture, the inward orientation: all must be prepared. The stars are always pouring their influence into the world. The question is whether you have made yourself capable of receiving it.

---

The first music takes place in reason, the second in fantasy and the third in words; thence follows song and after that the movement of the fingers in sound. Lastly the movement of the whole body in gymnastics or dancing. Thus we may see that the music of the soul is led by steps to all the limbs of the body.

Ficino's hierarchy of expression moves from the intelligible through the imaginative to the physical. The first music is the soul's own interior harmony. The descent through fantasy, words, song, gesture, and dance is the soul making its inner order perceptible. This is the map of how the celestial becomes the physical — rather than aesthetic theory.

The same principle governs the planetary images. A Jupiter image teaches expansion. A Solar image teaches clarity. A Venusian image draws the soul toward proportion. The image is the world deliberately re-rendered through a higher pattern until perception itself can receive what the pattern carries.

---

Ficino's medicine is about receptivity — not belief. The stars are in your blood because the *spiritus* that carries your life is continuous with the *spiritus* that flows through the cosmos. The food you eat, the air you breathe, the images you hold in your mind — these tune you toward the celestial or drag you toward the putrefactive. The choice is between conscious and unconscious reception, not between medicine and magic.

Ficino himself sang to the lyre every day, to banish vexations of both soul and body, and to raise the mind to the highest considerations and to God as much as he could. He was a practitioner — rather than a theorist. The *Three Books on Life* is a manual to be used — rather than a treatise to be studied. The stars are in your blood. The question is whether you will learn to sing with them or merely be moved by them without knowing why.
