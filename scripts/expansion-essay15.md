# the light that illuminates itself

Light is visible by itself — it needs nothing else to see it. What if consciousness works the same way? What if awareness is, from the beginning, its own self-showing, rather than something that happens *to* a subject?

---

Between the world of pure spiritual Lights (*Luces victoriales*, the world of the 'Mothers' in the terminology of *Ishraq*) and the sensory universe, at the boundary of the ninth Sphere (the Sphere of Spheres) there opens a *mundus imaginalis* which is a concrete spiritual world of archetype-Figures, apparitional Forms, Angels of species and of individuals.

Before you can understand consciousness, you must understand that there is a world between worlds — neither fully material nor purely abstract. This is the realm where light takes shape, where seeing and being-seen are the same act. You visit this world every night in dreams, though you mistake it for fantasy. The imagination is the faculty of perceiving what is real but not physical — the faculty of making things up is a shadow of this deeper capacity.

---

The 'eighth' *keshvar*, the mystical Earth of *Hurqalya* with emerald cities; it is situated on the summit of the cosmic mountain, which the traditions handed down in Islam call the mountain of *Qaf*. This is the paradisal Earth of Light, the world of *Hurqalya*, an Orient intermediate between the 'lesser Orient,' which is the soul's rising to the highest point of its desire and consciousness, and the 'greater Orient,' which is the further spiritual Orient, the pleroma of pure Intelligences.

There is a geography of the soul that maps onto no physical atlas. The journey moves from the periphery of awareness to its center — rather than from one place to another. The cosmic north is the point within you where light originates — rather than a direction in space. Suhrawardi calls this place Na-koja-abad — the land that is nowhere. And it is nowhere precisely because it is everywhere: the center of the circle of consciousness, which is equally present at every point on the circumference.

---

The central intuition is made explicit in the form of a number of Figures, amongst which the role assumed by the Hermetic figure of the Perfect Nature (*al-tiba' al-tamm*) is especially noteworthy. An essential detail: the Arabic tradition of Hermetism is the only one that allows us to give this Figure its context. From it we learn that Perfect Nature is the heavenly *paredros*, the Sage's Guide of light.

A being of light accompanies you from before your birth — your own higher nature, your perfect nature — so you are never alone in your quest for knowledge. It is *your own* celestial counterpart — rather than an angel sent by God — the you that you are before you entered time. This is the daimon of Socrates, the genius of the Hermeticists, the Holy Guardian Angel of the Western tradition. But Suhrawardi gives it a twist: this being is your own completion — rather than merely a guide — the half of you that exists in the world of light while you wander in the world of shadows.

---

Thou, my lord and prince, my most holy angel, my precious spiritual being, Thou art the Spirit who gave birth to me, and Thou art the Child to whom my spirit gives birth... Thou who art clothed in the most brilliant of divine Lights... may Thou manifest Thyself to me in the most beautiful of epiphanies, show me the light of Thy dazzling face, be for me the mediator... lift the veils of darkness from my heart...

This is a conversation with your own deepest self — the self that is both parent and child to you, both origin and goal — rather than a prayer to a distant deity. The Guide of Light is you — in your eternal aspect — but you cannot reach it as long as you insist on being only the small self you know. The relationship is reciprocal: you give birth to it by seeking it, and it gives birth to you by revealing itself. Both births are the same event, seen from opposite sides.

---

Hermes kept vigil all night long, meditating in the 'temple of light' (*haykal al-nur*, his own microcosm), but a sun shone in this night. When the 'pillar of dawn' burst forth... Hermes saw an Earth being swallowed up and with it the 'cities of the oppressors' drowning in the divine wrath. This downfall of the sensory, material world... is heralded by the outburst of light of the 'midnight sun' (as in Apuleius: *media nocte vidi solem coruscantem*).

At the darkest point of the night, the sun shines. This is the paradox of illumination: it comes when you have endured the darkness completely — rather than when you have accumulated enough light. The midnight sun is the light that consciousness is to itself when all external lights have gone out. The dawn seeks Hermes — rather than Hermes seeking the dawn. His vigil is a willingness to be present when illumination arrives — rather than an effort to achieve it.

---

The *Fravartis* are, in Mazdean cosmogony, feminine entities, heavenly archetypes of all the beings composing the Creation of light. Each being has his *fravarti* in the heavenly world which assumes the role of his guardian angel. What is more, all the Celestial beings, gods, angels and archangels, even Ohrmazd himself, have their respective *fravarti*. Syzygies of light, 'light upon light.'

The structure of reality is paired: every being here below has a counterpart above — the same being in its celestial aspect. Light reflects light, and the universe is built of these mirrorings. There is nothing alone in this cosmos. Your loneliness is the forgetting of your twin; the truth of your condition is that you have never been separate. When you remember the fravarti, you remember that union.

---

The Latin equivalent would be the expression *cognitio matutina*, used in Renaissance Hermetism... Whereas the evening knowledge, 'occidental,' *cognitio vespertina*, is the outer man's knowledge — knowledge of the outside of things — the morning knowledge, 'oriental,' *cognitio matutina*, is the knowledge of the man of light, having attained the 'abode which secretes its own light,' that is to say the Emerald Rock, this being the knowledge which is self-consciousness.

There are two kinds of knowing. One looks at things from the outside, measures them, categorizes them — this is evening knowledge. The other knows from within, by identity, by becoming what it knows — this is morning knowledge. Consciousness knowing itself is the dawn. The entire project of Suhrawardi's illuminationist philosophy is the conversion of evening knowledge into morning knowledge — the transformation of the knower from an observer into the very thing observed. When you know light as light knows itself, you have become light.

---

The exile, the stranger, faces up to the powers of oppression which try to force him to forget and to conform to the demands of their collective mastery... He has to reach the summit, the Emerald Rock that rises up before him like the translucent wall of a mystical Sinai; there, on the threshold of the pleroma of Light, the pilgrim meets his Perfect Nature, his Holy Ghost, in an ecstasy of anticipation corresponding, in the Mazdean dramaturgy, to the meeting in the dawn with the celestial Person, at the entrance to the Chinvat Bridge.

You are in exile. You have forgotten where you came from, and the world around you conspires to keep you forgetful. But the memory stirs. And at the end of the journey, at the threshold between this world and the next, your own light — the Guide, the Perfect Nature, the daimon — waits to welcome you home. The exile ends when you recognize the one who has been walking beside you the entire time — rather than when you reach a new place. The Chinvat Bridge is the moment, available now, when you decide which self you will be — the exile or the one who was never lost — rather than a place you cross after death.

---

What the illuminationist tradition asks you to consider is that you are already light — that what you call consciousness is the same stuff as what the cosmos is made of — not that there is light somewhere you need to reach. The difference between you and the Light of Lights is a difference in intensity, not in kind. You are a candle that has forgotten it is fire.

The Emerald Rock is the ground beneath your feet when you stop running — rather than the top of a mountain you must climb. The Guide of Light is the one who is looking through your eyes right now, waiting for you to notice — rather than a being you will meet after death.

Suhrawardi's entire system — the hierarchy of lights, the cosmic north, the mundus imaginalis, the midnight sun — is a map of return — rather than a cosmology in the modern sense. It describes how the journey home proceeds — rather than how the world is. And the home is the recognition that the light you have been seeking has always been the light you are — rather than a place. The seeker and the sought are the same luminosity. The only thing that separates them is time — and time is the last veil to fall.

When the veil falls, there is no longer a question of where the light comes from. You are the light. You always were. The rest was just the dream of separation, the long forgetting that makes the remembering possible. And in the remembering, the cosmos completes its circuit — light knowing light, through light, as light.
