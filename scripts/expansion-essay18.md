# the body you cannot see

You have a body you have never touched. It runs alongside your physical body like a second current — invisible, but measurable by its effects. You have felt it in moments of sudden knowing, in the rush of energy that precedes inspiration, in the inexplicable sense that someone is watching you when no one is there.

---

Of the 10 principal nāḍīs, three are the most important: iḍā, piṅgalā and suṣumṇā. Of the three again, one is the fundamental — suṣumṇā. Nāḍīs are of two kinds: gross (nerves, vessels) and subtle (yoga-nāḍīs). The subtle nāḍīs are non-material prāṇic force-motion lines. In the posterior aspect of mūlādhāra, the three nāḍīs are united; iḍā (white force-motion line) and piṅgalā (red force-motion line) are united with suṣumṇā (fire-like central force-motion line). From Tantrāloka: iḍā resonates with the ciñcinī sound, piṅgalā with the cricket (cīra) sound, and suṣumṇā reverberates like a bell; when the bell-like resonance dies down the Yogin attains the silent, liberated state of Śiva.

The subtle body has its own anatomy. Three primary channels run along the spine — two that carry the dualities of experience and one that carries something beyond both. Each has its own sound, its own signature, its own mode of vibration. When the central channel awakens, the other two fall silent, and the yogin enters the state that has no location. These channels are as real as arteries, but they carry force, not blood. The left channel (iḍā) is lunar, cool, associated with the parasympathetic nervous system. The right channel (piṅgalā) is solar, hot, sympathetic. The central channel (suṣumṇā) is neither — it is the fire of pure awareness that burns away all duality.

---

The seven cakras of the Tantrika system: (1) mūlādhāra — perineal region, four red petals, earth region, seat of kuṇḍalinī; (2) svādhiṣṭhāna — genital region, six red petals, water region; (3) maṇipūra — navel region, ten dark petals, fire region; (4) anāhata — heart region, twelve red petals, air region, seat of non-sensory sound (anāhata nāda); (5) viśuddha — neck region, sixteen smoke-coloured petals, void (ākāśa) region; (6) ājñā — eyebrow region, two petals, seat of bindu; (7) sahasrāra — supracerebral point, thousand white petals, seat of Supreme Bindu where concentration develops into samādhi.

These seven centers are thresholds in the subtle body where consciousness condenses into different densities — from the density of survival at the base to the density of transcendent awareness at the crown. Each is a world, with its own elements, colors, and deities.

---

The nāḍīs are the subtle prāṇic force-motion lines created by the operation of the prāṇa-vāyus. The ordinary operation of the prāṇa-vāyus produces two effects: maintaining life in the body by complex activities and supporting mentation. The extraordinary function centralizes force-motions as the suṣumṇā-line. The term 'nāḍī' is derived from 'nāda' = bhraṃśa, meaning radiating motion. Kalicharana, commentator of Ṣaṭcakranirūpaṇa, defines the subtle yoga-nāḍīs as distinct from the gross nervous system — they constitute the 'wireless' system operating without being vehicled by the nerves.

The subtle body is a parallel infrastructure, as real as the nerves but operating at a different frequency. The nerves are the physical extension of this deeper, non-material system — like a radio that manifests the signal it receives. When Goswami says the nāḍīs are "wireless," he means it literally: they are not carried by any physical substrate. They are patterns in the prāṇic field itself, and the nervous system is their gross projection into the material world.

---

Goswami systematically argues that Kuṇḍalinī is the power of consciousness itself, operating on its own plane — categorically different from chemical energy (derived from food and tissue, a gross transformation) and from any biological process. Every attempt to reduce it to a physical mechanism fails. 'Is it this material Kuṇḍalinī that the yogis strive to realize in deep concentration? Is the yogi's Kuṇḍalinī so fragmented and limited? Is Kuṇḍalinī a chemical phenomenon?'

---

Kuṇḍalinī-'light' has three aspects: sunlike bright light by which subtle phenomena are illuminated and experienced in concentration; moonlike 'cool' light which deepens concentration; and firelike burning light which exhibits great absorption-power and develops absorptive concentration. Kuṇḍalī-power is in the nature of illumination — it first illuminates appropriate objects and then they are absorbed into it. When everything is absorbed, Kuṇḍalinī as Supreme and splendorous power remains, and this power is all consciousness, mind-transcendent.

When the inner power awakens, it manifests as light — but not one light. Three qualities of light correspond to three phases of the process: illumination of what was hidden, deepening of the state, and finally the burning absorption of all objects into the light itself.

---

Goswami develops a polarity model: energy polarizes itself as static and dynamic. The dynamic form is prāṇic force; its static support is Kuṇḍalinī. The sum total of activities in which body, life-force, and mind are involved is the dynamic aspect of existence — prāṇic dynamism. But prāṇa requires a static background — a still pole to existence — though normal functioning shows no indication of it; the polarization of energy demands that the dynamic form must have a static form, and that static form is Kuṇḍalinī.

The energy that moves through your body and mind is not the whole story. There is a static pole to existence — the stillness that makes motion possible. Kuṇḍalinī is that stillness, coiled at the root of awareness, waiting to be recognized.

---

After the union with Supreme Śiva, Kuṇḍalinī goes back to the mūlādhāra, and in its descent the cakras, their powers and divinities — all are restored. This recoiling process is essentially the spiritualization of consciousness which functions in everyday life: a transference of spiritual reality, developed in concentrated consciousness, to sense-consciousness to be able to realize spiritual truth in daily life. In the uncoiling and recoiling of Kuṇḍalinī, the practitioner experiences the Divine in its formless aspect in supreme concentration as well as in form in post-concentration waking consciousness.

The power rises, illuminates, and then returns — but it returns transformed, and it transforms the ordinary consciousness it descends into. The spiritual is the ordinary seen from the other side. This recoiling process is often overlooked in popular accounts of kuṇḍalinī, which focus only on the dramatic ascent. But the descent is equally important. Without it, the awakening remains a peak experience, untethered from daily life. With it, the entire field of ordinary awareness becomes spiritualized. You do not escape the world; you bring the light back into it.

---

Tantrāloka describes the three channels through their sonic signatures. Iḍā, on the left, resonates with the ciñcinī sound, which is insentient (prākṛta) and coarse (sthūla). Piṅgalā, to the right of suṣumṇā, resonates with the sound of the cricket (cīra); it is conscious (pauruṣa) and subtle. Suṣumṇā, the central channel, reverberates like a bell; its resonance is supreme. When the bell-like resonance dies down, the Yogin attains the unsupported, unlocated state — liberation.

The body has its own music. The left channel buzzes like an insect, the right chirps like a cricket, and the center rings like a temple bell. When the bell fades into silence, the yogin does not hear silence — he becomes it. These sounds are the actual auditory correlates of the prāṇic flow in each channel, audible to the yogin whose concentration has been refined through practice. The Tantrāloka describes them as precise indicators of the state of the channels — diagnostic signs on the inner anatomy.

---

The body you cannot see is a more fundamental version of the body you know. The physical body is its outer expression, its densest form — like ice floating on water, visible but dependent on the invisible medium beneath. The tradition calls this subtle body the *sūkṣma śarīra*, and it is the actual field of spiritual transformation. The cakras are real centers of force that can be felt, awakened, and eventually transcended. The nāḍīs are the pathways along which consciousness moves when it travels inward. And Kuṇḍalinī is the actual power that, when awakened, reorganizes consciousness from the ground up.

You cannot touch this body, but it touches everything you experience. It is the body of energy that dreams the physical body into existence. And when you learn to feel it, to move it, to still it, you discover that you are the light the flesh was made to contain.
