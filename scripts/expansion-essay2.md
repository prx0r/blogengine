# you're wearing armor

You can only do so much. You can only know so much. Time moves whether you want it to or not. Consequences follow whether you intended them.

Five specific mechanisms create these limits. Five pieces of armor between you and freedom.

The five kañcukas are the principles that contract Śiva's infinite capacities into the finite experience of the bounded soul: kalā (limited agency), vidyā (limited knowledge), rāga (attachment), kāla (time), and niyati (necessity). These emerge from Māyā and together constitute the apparatus of finitude. Māyā itself is the 'knot' (māyāgranthi) — the root principle of differentiation that, when pierced, releases the soul from the impure universe into the pure path. The aggregate of obscuring coverings is made of Māyā, and so too the collection of the instruments of perception and action that constitute the psychophysical organism.

A knot pulls tighter the more you wrestle it. The same is true here: the struggle against limitation tightens the noose, while relaxed attention finds the thread and quietly undoes it, one loop at a time.

> "The five obscuring coverings — Kalā, Vidyā, Rāga, Kāla and Niyati — are in the form of limited agency, limited noetic subjectivity, craving, temporal differentiation, and its emergence due to the necessity of karmic consequence."

*Kalā* — limited agency. You can only lift so much. Beneath it: the power to lift anything.

*Vidyā* — limited knowledge. You only see a sliver. Beneath it: the capacity to see through.

*Rāga* — craving. You reach for what you lack. Beneath it: the fullness that reaches for nothing.

*Kāla* — time. You move from A to B. Beneath it: the present that never moves.

*Niyati* — necessity. You reap what you sow. Beneath it: the freedom that sows regardless.

As Torella observes, the kañcukas form a foreign body with respect to the Sāṅkhya basis of the tattvas, giving the impression of doubles with an uncertain status. The Śaiva justification for having vidyā alongside buddhi is that the intellect cannot examine itself, and therefore the soul must have an additional instrument of cognition. As Sadyojyotis argues: 'Without an instrument, an agent is not known to act with respect to an object. Therefore, there must exist an instrument, and that is vidyā, which discriminates among the awarenesses of the intellect.'

An eye cannot see itself without a mirror. Vidyā is that mirror — the capacity to watch your own watching, to know that you know.

The five obscuring coverings are factors that render the soul subject to the limitations of space, time, and form. Nature, the individual soul, and the five obscuring coverings — all of these are included within the realm of Māyā. Necessity and the rest are indicated by the phrase 'I know just now, that I do just now' — suggesting the temporal and conditional nature of limited knowledge and action. As the kañcukas arise, along with the subtle body (puryaṣṭaka) and the gross elements, they constitute the complete apparatus of the bound soul's embodiment and experience.

The words "just now" are a cage. Each thought arrives stamped with a timestamp, each action tethered to a moment that is already gone. The cage is made of time.

The Force of limited agency arises from the principle of Māyā, and its characteristic is limited agency (kiñcitkartṛtva). This, the first of the obscuring coverings, obscures its essentially omnipotent Śiva nature while enlivening its limited consciousness. The coverings persist through cycles of withdrawal and return — woven into the lattice of manifestation itself.

The fist that closes also brings blood to the palm. Contraction itself generates the heat of life. You feel the squeeze because life still pulses inside it. Even limitation is a form of energy.

> "The Force of limited agency arises from the principle of Māyā. It obscures its essentially omnipotent Śiva nature while enlivening its limited consciousness."

The craving for worldly experience has been aroused by the Lord's will. The disturbance brought about by this Impurity called 'craving' is its clearly evident propensity to act as a concomitant cause of Karmic impurity. That too is due to the Lord's will. The craving for worldly experience is the fundamental Impurity of the individual soul (āṇavamala), the nature of which is craving (abhilāṣa). Those that have this kind of Impurity are sentient beings, that is, those whose Self is contracted.

Hunger is how the stomach knows it exists. Rāga is how the contracted self feels its own shape — by grasping, by wanting, by the ache of not-yet.

The seven perceivers (saptapramātṛ) are arranged along the gamut of the principles, corresponding to the states of consciousness: waking, dreaming, deep sleep, the Fourth, and beyond the Fourth. The states of waking, dreaming and deep sleep correspond to the impure domain of the thirty-one tattvas from Earth to Māyā. The Fourth state corresponds to the pure tattvas from Śuddhavidyā up to Śiva. Beyond even the Fourth is the state of the supreme perceiver — Bhairava — who encompasses and transcends all.

The kañcukas map directly onto the states you pass through every night. Waking, dreaming, sleep — each is a different configuration of the same five restraints.

Māyā is the night (rātrī) of the Lord's consciousness — the principle of obscuration that veils the true nature of reality from the individual soul. It is the 'knot' (māyāgranthi) that binds the soul to the cycle of transmigration. Māyā itself is insentient (jaḍa) and must be stimulated by the Lord to assume its creative state. When the practitioner pierces through the knot of Māyā through knowledge and grace, he passes from the impure universe into the pure path of the five pure tattvas.

Night: the sun's own turning away from itself. The same turning that makes stars visible.

The Supreme Lord renders the individual souls subject to the chain of cause and effect of Fundamental Nature. This is heralded by the progressive operation (kalanā) of the obscuring coverings. They consist of the lack of reflective awareness of the essential nature of the soul which does and knows everything, is free of craving, is undifferentiated by time and is all things. The group of principles ranging from the Force of limited agency up to Earth differs for each individual soul. This is clear from the fact that the joy and suffering each one experiences are different. If the universe were to be a single aggregate of means to worldly experience, the experience of pleasure and the like would be the same for all souls. How then could one account for the differences between each individual's experiences?

Your suffering is yours alone — the configuration of covering that produces your experience is unique to you. No two souls wear the same armor. Your limits are your signature.

The Power that Sustains Everything (sarvādhāraśakti) is the ground of all manifestation. Everything appears within Śiva like a reflection or image in a dream or notion of the mind; it is inscrutable, formless consciousness. The relationship between the sustainer and the sustained is that of consciousness to its own self-manifestation. All things, including time and space, are manifestations within Śiva. Moments (kṣaṇa) do not exist as independent realities; they are divisions superimposed by consciousness upon its own eternal, timeless nature.

Time: a gesture consciousness makes — a slicing of the eternal into manageable pieces. The clock ticks because awareness permits it.

The Impurity called Māyā is generated from Karma. As the kañcukas arise, along with the subtle body and the gross elements, they constitute the complete apparatus of the bound soul's embodiment and experience. The subtle body (puryaṣṭaka) is the vehicle that carries the impressions of karma across lifetimes, and it is through this vehicle that the five coverings continue their work beyond a single life. The aggregate of the instruments of perception and action is woven together with the coverings into a single garment.

The body you inhabit: a garment woven from the same thread as the armor — karma spun into form, limitation worn as skin.

The coverings cut both ways. They hide the infinite. They also reveal the finite as a viable way to be — beautiful, limited, temporary. You only feel the weight of armor because you have touched what lies beneath. Unarmored. Unbound. Uninjured. Always have been.
