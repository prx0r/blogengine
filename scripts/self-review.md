# Comparison: Death Everyone Forgets vs. Ibn Arabi Gold Standard

## Structure

| Element | Ibn Arabi | Death Essay |
|---------|----------|-------------|
| Hook mechanism | Riddle about a "secret" | Bold claim ("No one remembers dying") |
| Tradition/figure named | Line 28 (Ibn Arabi) | Never explicitly named until late |
| Sustained metaphor | Ocean/wave (25 lines), mirror/face, sun/light | River (mentioned but not sustained) |
| "You" presence | 18x in 1,600 words | Present but fades in body |
| Paradox | "He is so present you can miss Him" | No single paradox at center |
| Failed answer pattern | Sufi asks "How can I find God?" → answer | Absent |
| Emotional arc | Wonder → paradox → depth → implication → peace | Starts bold, becomes informative |
| Ending | Ibn Arabi's own words (full quote) | Tibetan Book of the Dead quote |

## What the Ibn Arabi does better

**One metaphor that carries weight.** Ocean/wave IS the entire teaching. Every time it returns, it deepens. My Death essay mentions the river but doesn't sustain it — it jumps between Tibetan, Egyptian, Steiner terms without letting any single image land.

**The failed answer move.** The Sufi's question "How can I find God?" and the wise man's reply "You cannot find what has never been lost" creates a miniature drama. My essay has nothing comparable — it just moves from phase to phase.

**A central paradox.** "He is so present you can miss Him entirely." That one line holds the whole teaching in tension. My essay has no equivalent — no line that the reader will carry with them.

**The "you" thread doesn't break.** Even in the middle of explaining, Ibn Arabi asks "Do you understand what that means?" The viewer is constantly re-hooked. My essay asks questions but they cluster in specific sections.

**Ending lands on the source's voice.** Ibn Arabi's last word is Ibn Arabi. My last word is a Tibetan quote — better than summary, but less integrated.

## What to fix in the Death essay

1. **Pick one metaphor and sustain it.** The river works. Every phase is a bend in the river. Return to it.
2. **Add a failed answer moment.** Before the traditions are introduced, let a natural doubt arise. "You probably think death is the end. Most people do."
3. **Find the paradox and place it at the center.** "What dies is not what lives. What lives was never born."
4. **Add "you" questions throughout.** Not just "imagine" but "what would you see? what would you feel?"
5. **The hook landing.** The original landing ("Does what survives carry your name? Who were you before that?") is better than my revised version. Revert to that.
