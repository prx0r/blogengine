# the 8 steps you're already taking

You have heard of yoga as postures, but the Yoga Sutras — the root text — mentions postures in exactly three sentences; the other 194 sutras map the mind and how to free it. The system is called ashtanga — eight limbs — and it begins with how you treat other people: before you ever sit on a mat, you must learn not to lie, not to steal, not to harm. The postures come third.

---

OM: Here follows Instruction in Union. Union, spiritual consciousness, is gained through control of the versatile psychic nature. Then the Seer comes to consciousness in his proper nature. Heretofore the Seer has been enmeshed in the activities of the psychic nature.

A man has been looking out a window his whole life, mistaking the glass for the world. The first sutra hands him a cloth and says: wipe it. Yoga is about stopping the wrong kind of activity — the churning of the mind that keeps you from seeing what you already are. The Seer is revealed when practice clears the obstructions; the Seer is not produced by practice.

---

The psychic activities are five; they are either subject or not subject to the five hindrances. These activities are: Sound intellection (pramana), unsound intellection (viparyaya), predication (vikalpa), sleep (nidra), memory (smriti).

Five streams flowing into a single pool — clear water, muddy water, water that does not know if it is wet, water that is dreaming, water that remembers rain that fell last year. The mind is the pool, not any one stream. Even correct knowledge is a vritti. Even sleep is a mental activity. Even memory, which feels like a faithful record, is a construction, a re-weaving of the past every time you recall it. The goal is to stand as the witness of all five — not to have the right thoughts.

---

These are the hindrances: the darkness of unwisdom (avidya), self-assertion (asmita), lust (raga), hate (dvesha), attachment (abhinivesha). The darkness of unwisdom is the field of the others. These hindrances may be dormant, or worn thin, or suspended, or expanded. The darkness of ignorance is: holding that which is unenduring, impure, full of pain, not the Soul, to be eternal, pure, full of joy, the Soul. Self-assertion comes from thinking of the Seer and the instrument of vision as forming one self. Lust is the resting in the sense of enjoyment. Hate is the resting in the sense of pain. Attachment is the desire toward life, even in the wise, carried forward by its own energy.

A man mistakes his reflection in a shop window for another person. He waves. It waves back. He is offended. He makes peace. He falls in love. All of it with himself. The hindrances are this: the Seer mistaking the seen for who he is. Avidya is the original error — not ignorance in the sense of not knowing facts, but the deep, structural mistake of identifying with what you are not. From that single error, the other four grow like branches from a single root.

---

The practices which make for union with the Soul are: fervent aspiration (tapas), spiritual reading (svadhyaya), and complete obedience to the Master (Ishvara pranidhana). Their aim is, to bring soul-vision, and to wear away hindrances.

A blacksmith heats iron until it glows, reads the grain of the metal, and surrenders to the hammer's rhythm. Three acts. One fire. Tapas is the heat of sustained practice — not self-torture, but the friction of showing up every day. Svadhyaya is the study that turns into self-knowledge: you read the text, and the text reads you. Ishvara pranidhana is the release — letting the bowstring go, offering the fruit before the fruit has grown. Together they wear down the kleshas the way water wears down stone: imperceptibly, irresistibly.

---

The eight means of Yoga are: the Commandments, the Rules, right Poise, right Control of the life-force, Withdrawal, Attention, Meditation, Contemplation. ... The Commandments are these: non-injury, truthfulness, abstaining from stealing, from impurity, from covetousness. ... The Rules are these: purity, serenity, fervent aspiration, spiritual reading, and perfect obedience to the Master.

Eight rungs on a ladder that does not lean against anything. The first rung is how you treat the person next to you on the bus. The last rung is what happens when there is no longer anyone to treat. The first two limbs — yama and niyama — are ethical, and they are the foundation — not preliminary. Meditation cannot untangle a life built on lies and theft; the mind will not settle if the life is crooked. The postures come third, as preparation for what follows: pranayama steadies the breath, pratyahara turns the senses inward, and then the inner limbs — dharana, dhyana, samadhi — begin the real work of unbinding the Seer from the seen.

---

The binding of the perceiving consciousness to a certain region is attention (dharana). A prolonged holding of the perceiving consciousness in that region is meditation (dhyana). When the perceiving consciousness in this meditation is wholly given to illuminating the essential meaning of the object contemplated, and is freed from the sense of separateness and personality, this is contemplation (samadhi). When these three, Attention, Meditation, Contemplation, are exercised at once, this is perfectly concentrated Meditation (sanyama).

A magnifying glass held over a dry leaf. First you steady your hand — this is dharana, the effort of focusing. Then you hold it still — this is dhyana, the sustained flow of attention. Then the leaf smokes, catches, becomes light — this is samadhi, where the boundary between observer and observed dissolves, where you are no longer looking at the leaf but are the leaf looking at you. When these three happen together without break, that is sanyama. The practitioner has become concentration itself.

---

The cause of what is to be warded off, the root of misery, is the absorption of the Seer in things seen. Things seen have as their property manifestation, action, inertia. ... The Seer is pure vision. Though pure, he looks out through the vesture of the mind. The bringing of this association to an end, by bringing the darkness of unwisdom to an end, is the great liberation; this is the Seer's attainment of his own pure being.

A projector in a dark room throws light onto a screen. Everyone watches the screen. No one turns around to look at the projector. The Seer is the projector. The seen is the screen. Liberation is a slow turning — not of the screen, but of the head. When you finally look at the source of the light, the images on the screen lose their power. They become what they always were: patterns of light.

---

The perfected spiritual man stands in his own nature, as purity and light. ... The spiritual man, thus extricated and standing on his feet, viewing life with open eyes, attains to illuminated perception. ... His illumination is sevenfold, rising in successive stages. Freed from these potencies, the spiritual man stands forth in his own nature as purity and light.

A man walks out of a cinema into broad daylight. The film is still running inside, but he is no longer in the dark. The cars on the street are real. The sky is too bright. He blinks and keeps walking. The illumination is sevenfold — seven stages of insight that rise one after another, each one stripping away another layer of misidentification. First he knows what must be known. Then he releases what must be released. Then the obstacles fall away. Then the path becomes clear. Then the mind turns inward. Then the veils dissolve. Then he stands in his own nature. Kaivalya is the most ordinary thing in the world: standing in your own nature, seeing things as they are for the first time. The supernatural was the trance; the ordinary has been here all along, waiting for you to stop looking at the screen long enough to notice the light behind your own eyes.

---

The eight limbs are simultaneous, recursive — a spiral that tightens as it rises — not a checklist where you finish yama and move on to niyama like courses in a meal. The person who stops harming others discovers they have stopped harming themselves; the person who can sit still discovers they were never sitting still at all. And somewhere in the middle of this spiral, the Seer wakes up, rubs his eyes, and realizes he has been watching a movie about himself that he mistook for a life. The theatre is empty now. The screen is white. He is the light that made the pictures move. The light was there before the first frame flickered to life, and it will be there after the last reel runs out. You have been this light all along. You just forgot to look away from the screen.
