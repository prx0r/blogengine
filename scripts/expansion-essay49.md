# the gods need you

---

A 4th-century philosopher argued that the gods need human rituals as much as humans need divine grace. Without theurgy — the work of the gods — the cosmos is incomplete. Prayer is participating.

Iamblichus wrote *On the Mysteries* as a response to his own teacher. Porphyry, the great disciple of Plotinus, had written a letter to an Egyptian priest named Anebo, asking a series of devastating questions. If the gods are perfect, why do they need our prayers? If matter is base, how can physical rituals touch the divine? If the gods are impassive, how can offerings affect them? Iamblichus answered from inside the temple, writing as Abammon, an Egyptian prophet — while Porphyry wrote as a philosopher standing outside the tradition, examining religious phenomena with cool reason. The entire work is a defence of the sacred against the philosopher, a defence of practice.

---

Hermes, the God who presides over language, was formerly very properly considered as common to all priests; and the power who presides over the true science concerning the Gods is one and the same in the whole of things.

The book begins with an invocation. Hermes, the god of language, is the one who presides over all priestly speech. The entire work depends on the claim that sacred language is different from ordinary language — that the names of the gods, spoken in the right context with the right intention, participate in it. Porphyry had asked why theurgists use barbarous names, untranslatable sounds, foreign words. Iamblichus answers through the figure of Hermes: because language at this level is operation.

Admit it to be me who discourses with you in writing, or some other prophet of the Egyptians, for this is of no consequence.

The level of speech — rather than the identity of the speaker — is what matters. The response must come from within the sacred tradition, not from a philosopher observing it from outside. This is the epistemological stance of the entire work: theurgy can only be understood by those who practice it.

---

Such questions, indeed, as are theological, we shall answer theologically; such as are theurgic, theurgically; but such as are philosophical, we shall, in conjunction with you, philosophically explore.

This is the method rule that governs the entire work. Iamblichus refuses to let any single method police the entire sacred field. Theology answers theological questions; theurgy answers theurgic questions; philosophy answers philosophical questions — each has its proper domain, unlike readings that collapse everything into one lens such as psychology reducing rites to inner states, anthropology reducing them to cultural performance, or occultism treating them as power techniques.

An innate knowledge of the Gods is co-existent with our very essence... prior to reason and demonstration.

This is the foundation on which everything rests. The gods are a presence discovered when we stop thinking in the mode of detachment. The soul is already in relation to divinity before argument. Theurgy works because the soul is already oriented toward the gods, already participating in their order, already held within the knowledge that precedes reason.

---

These middle genera... give completion to the common bond of the Gods and souls, and cause the connexion of them to be indissoluble.

Why do the gods need human rituals? Because the cosmos is structured as a hierarchy of intermediate beings — gods, daemons, heroes, archangels, angels, souls — and each level depends on the one below it for the completion of the circuit. The middle genera — the daemons — are the very structure that makes participation possible. Without them, the gap between god and embodied soul would be impassable. Ritual activates these intermediate beings. They carry the offering upward and the blessing downward.

It renders that which is ineffable in the good of the Gods effable, illuminates that which is formless in forms, and produces into visible reasons that which in divine good is above all reason.

Daemons are metaphysical translators. They make the ineffable effable. They transmit divine good downward without reducing it to the lower level. The daemon is a function: the translation of the infinite into terms the soul can receive.

The soul having a twofold life, one being in conjunction with body, but the other being separate from all body...

The soul is amphibious. It belongs to two worlds at once. In sleep, the soul is loosened from bodily life and can receive from higher levels. In ritual, the soul is deliberately loosened — through prayer, sacrifice, sacred sound — so that the daemonic and divine transmission can occur. Sleep is because the bodily mode can be loosened and the soul can receive from wholes.

---

No longer as man, nor as using a human soul.

This is the key passage for understanding how theurgy works. The priest acts through divine order, signs, and union. As human: inferior. As theurgist: participating in divine symbols. As united to gods: acting from a higher order. The theurgist's agency is transformed. This is the difference between theurgy and goetia. Goetia tries to compel the divine from below. Theurgy participates in the divine from within.

No one, however, threatens the Gods.

If threat-language appears in ritual texts — and Porphyry had found it there — it applies to lower, divided, irrational powers, not to gods or the highest orders. The hierarchy is preserved. The theurgist becomes solar.

---

What utility or power they possess in the universe, and with the Gods.

Porphyry's simplest and most devastating question: if gods are immaterial, impassive, and superior, how can material offerings matter at all? Iamblichus answers by rejecting every crude theory of sacrifice. The gods are beyond being nourished by vapours, honoured by human gifts, or paid for services rendered.

Sacrifice works because the cosmos is held together by friendship. Iamblichus calls it *philia* — a bond that runs through all levels of reality.

Friendship and familiarity... binds fabricators to the things fabricated, and generators to the things generated.

Generated things preserve relation to their generating causes. Sacrifice takes something generated — a material offering — and turns it back toward its generator. Procession: divine cause → generated thing. Sacrifice: generated thing → divine cause. The rite is a return along the same path by which the offering came into being.

All the causes are moved in conjunction by a perfect sacrifice.

Perfect sacrifice moves physical sympathies, daemonic causes, divine causes, the soul of the sacrificer — the whole order of return. This is why the gods need human rituals. The need arises from the circuit of procession and return that is the structure of the cosmos itself — rather than from hunger, loneliness, or vanity, and the human being is the point where return becomes conscious.

Prayer and sacrifice mutually corroborate.

Sacrifice returns matter. Prayer turns soul and intellect upward. Together they form one sacerdotal operation. The ritual requires both for completion. The material offering carries the physical world back toward its source. The prayer carries the soul. Both are necessary because the human being is both matter and soul.

---

They... exhibit certain images through symbols of mystic, occult, and invisible intellections.

Egyptian symbols are visible carriers of invisible divine intelligence. The lotus, the mire, the god sitting above the lotus — these are concentrations of meaning that act on the soul directly, bypassing discursive reason.

By the God 'sitting above the lotus,' a transcendency and strength which by no means come into contact with the mire, are obscurely signified.

The god above the lotus signifies transcendence without abandonment. The god is above the mire — above generation, above flux, above material contact — yet symbolically related to the generative field below. The image holds both transcendence and relation together.

It is too excellent to be divided into knowledge.

Some sacred names are powerful partly because they exceed discursive division. Their unknownness protects their superiority. Meaning and power diverge: translation may preserve concept but lose force. The name participates.

Though it should be possible to translate them, yet they no longer preserve the same power when translated.

This is the climax of the sacred-name argument. The power of the name is in the sound, the rhythm, the tradition, the context. Strip these away and you have a definition. You do not have an operation.

---

Matter is good — the radical claim that separates Iamblichus from the Gnostics and from certain tendencies in Platonism. The world is a statue of the intelligible — not a prison. Matter can carry the divine because matter is already within the divine procession. The same fire that burns the offering elevates it. The same matter that constitutes the body can become the vehicle of the soul's return.

The gods need human rituals because the cosmos is a circuit, and the circuit must be complete. The human being is the point where the material world becomes conscious of its source and turns back toward it. Without theurgy, the circuit is broken. The procession downward has no corresponding return. The gods give, but nothing gives back.

Iamblichus believed that needing was the structure of love. The gods need human rituals the way a fire needs fuel — because burning is its nature. The theurgist feeds what is already burning — does not create the fire. And in feeding it, the theurgist burns too.
