# the self that's looking for itself

There is a teaching in the ancient texts that says: if you try to grab hold of the self, you will find nothing. If you try to let go of the self, you will find that nothing was ever there to let go of.

---

> All this, whatsoever exists in the universe, should be covered by the Lord. Having renounced (the unreal), enjoy (the Real). Do not covet the wealth of any man. ... He who sees all beings in the Self and the Self in all beings, he never turns away from It (the Self). He who perceives all beings as the Self for him how can there be delusion or grief, when he sees this oneness (everywhere)?

A man stands at the edge of the ocean and watches a wave rise. When it collapses, does the ocean grieve? The wave is the ocean. The other is the self. The seeing of this is the end of grief.

---

> Yama said: The good is one thing and the pleasant another. These two, having different ends, bind a man. It is well with him who chooses the good. He who chooses the pleasant misses the true end. The good and the pleasant approach man; the wise examines both and discriminates between them; the wise prefers the good to the pleasant, but the foolish man chooses the pleasant through love of bodily pleasure.

Two doors stand before every person, every moment. One door opens onto a garden that smells of honey. The other opens onto a long dark corridor. The wise choose the dark corridor because they have walked the garden path a thousand times and found it always ends at a wall.

---

> Know the Atman (Self) as the lord of the chariot, and the body as the chariot. Know also the intellect to be the driver and mind the reins. The senses are called the horses; the sense objects are the roads; when the Atman is united with body, senses and mind, then the wise call Him the enjoyer.

A chariot races down a dusty road. The horses are wild. The driver holds the reins but his hands are shaking. The passenger in the back sits perfectly still. He is the one who never moves.

---

> Yama replied: That goal which all the Vedas glorify, which all austerities proclaim, desiring which (people) practice Brahmacharya (a life of continence and service), that goal I tell thee briefly — it is Aum. This Word is indeed Brahman. This Word is indeed the Supreme. He who knows this Word obtains whatever he desires. This is the best Support, This is the highest Support; he who knows this Support is glorified in the world of Brahman.

Aum is the sound the universe makes when it opens its mouth. When you chant Aum, you are remembering the sound you have been making since before you were born. It was already vibrating in the space between your thoughts before you began.

---

> This Self is never born, nor does It die. It did not spring from anything, nor did anything spring from It. This Ancient One is unborn, eternal, everlasting. It is not slain even though the body is slain. If the slayer thinks that he slays, or if the slain thinks that he is slain, both of these know not. For It neither slays nor is It slain. The Self is subtler than the subtle, greater than the great; It dwells in the heart of each living being.

A sword falls on a shadow. The shadow was never a body — only the absence of light taking a shape. The self is like that. The sword cannot touch light.

---

> By whom commanded and directed does the mind go towards its objects? Commanded by whom does the life-force, the first (cause), move? At whose will do men utter speech? What power directs the eye and the ear? ... It is the ear of the ear, the mind of the mind, the speech of the speech, the life of the life, the eye of the eye. The wise, freed (from the senses and from mortal desires), after leaving this world, become immortal.

The eye sees a tree. But what sees the seeing? The ear of the ear — the hearing that makes hearing possible. You cannot find it because it is what is doing the finding.

---

> There the eye does not go, nor speech, nor mind. We do not know That; we do not understand how It can be taught. It is distinct from the known and also It is beyond the unknown. ... That which speech does not illumine, but which illumines speech: know that alone to be the Brahman (the Supreme Being), not this which people worship here. That which cannot be thought by mind, but by which, they say, mind is able to think: know that alone to be the Brahman, not this which people worship here.

The hand reaches for an object and closes around nothing. The self you cannot find is the one thing that cannot be lost.

---

> Beyond the senses are the objects, beyond the objects is the mind, beyond the mind is the intellect, beyond the intellect is the great Atman. Beyond the great Atman is the Unmanifested; beyond the Unmanifested is the Purusha (the Cosmic Soul); beyond the Purusha there is nothing. That is the end, that is the final goal. ... When the five organs of perception become still, together with the mind, and the intellect ceases to be active: that is called the highest state. This firm holding back of the senses is what is known as Yoga.

A ladder with rungs that disappear as you climb them. The ladder was leading everywhere, going nowhere in particular. The climbing was the arrival.

The Upanishads are instructions for an experiment you can perform on yourself. Turn your attention back on itself. Look for the one who is looking. You will not find anything solid. You will find that the seeker and the sought are the same thing, and that thing is not a thing at all.

What remains? The teachers say: not nothing. But they refuse to say what it is, because saying what it is would turn it into another object. It is the not-thing that makes all things possible. It is the space in which every experience arises and into which every experience dissolves. It is you — but not the you that has a name and a history. The you that was never born and cannot die.

This is called the fourth state — turiya. Not waking, not dreaming, not deep sleep. The silent witness that notices when you are awake, notices when you dream, notices when you are in dreamless sleep, and notices even when there is nothing to notice.

This is a practical teaching. When the wise man sees this oneness, he is freed from the mouth of death. Freed from the terror of death — not from the event of dying — freed from the sense that death is an ending rather than a return. The self that was never born cannot die.

"That thou art." Tat tvam asi. You are that. The ground of the universe and the ground of your own awareness are one ground. The eye cannot see itself, but it can know that it sees. The self cannot find itself, but it can know that it is. And that knowing is the silence in which all thoughts appear. It is the silence you are reading these words in right now.

---

*Source: Isa Upanishad, Katha Upanishad, Kena Upanishad, Mundaka Upanishad*
