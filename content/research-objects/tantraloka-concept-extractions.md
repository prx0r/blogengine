# Systematic Passage Extraction from Tantrāloka Volumes

## Concept 1: Spanda (Pulsation of Consciousness)

### Passage 1 — Mantra as the Pulsing Nature of Consciousness
**Source:** Tantrāloka Āhnika 7, verse 2cd-3ab (Dyczkowski Vol.5, pp. 3-5)

**Full passage:**
All Mantras consisting of seed syllables (bīja) and syllabic groups (piṇḍa) are the dynamic pulsing nature (spandanātmatā) of consciousness. Thus, a Mantra is said to serve as a means to attain Supreme Consciousness because such is its nature. Mantra is the pulsation of consciousness within the flow of the breath. Mantra practice initially starts in the domain of the Individual Means (Āṇavopāya). Purifying thought, it induces penetration and absorption into the energy of Śiva consciousness (śāktāveśa). It is then the adept's higher mind that is absorbed into the Śāmbhava state of supreme consciousness. Purified and freed of thought constructs, penetrating into Śiva and penetrated by Him, it becomes the Great Mantra (mahāmantra), which is the reflective awareness of authentic, uncreated 'I' consciousness. The pure spontaneity of the freedom of this pure reflective awareness, free of objectivity because it generates it, effortlessly develops by the power of the Lord's most intense form of grace into the Inexplicable, where there is nothing to do, nor has anything ever taken place.

**Original terms:** spandanātmatā, bīja, piṇḍa, mahāmantra, śāktāveśa, āṇavopāya

**Analysis:** This foundational passage establishes spanda as the dynamic, pulsing nature of consciousness itself. Mantras are not arbitrary sounds but are the very pulsation of consciousness within the breath. The practice moves from the individual means through purification into the supreme state of uncreated self-awareness.

### Passage 2 — Spanda as the Pulse of Sensory Activity
**Source:** Tantrāloka Āhnika 7, verses 28-32ab (Dyczkowski Vol.5, pp. 19-22)

**Full passage:**
The rhythm of sensory activity, the pulse of conjunction and separation of the senses with their objects, is essentially the pulse — spanda — of consciousness. That is its nature and root cause, intimately connected to the movement of the breath. The pulsation (spanda) of consciousness is the nature (prakṛti), that is, the root cause of the activity of the senses. Until another pulse (spanda) of cognitive consciousness arises, a single representational ideation (vikalpa) abides as a unified whole, conceiving the diversity of manifest reality. The pulse (spanda) of cognitive consciousness is essentially its movement from one object to another, which is thus conjoined and disjoined in the sphere of sensorial cognitive consciousness. The absence of one entails the absence of the other. As long as a subsequent pulse, which has as its field of operation some other object of representational ideation, does not take place, the thought construct is one only, whether long or short, not many.

**Original terms:** spanda, vikalpa, prakṛti, prāṇa, indriya

**Analysis:** This passage connects spanda directly to the mechanism of perception itself. The pulse of consciousness is what drives the conjunction and disjunction of senses with their objects, making spanda the ground of all sensory and cognitive activity. Each discrete perception is a pulse within the unified field of consciousness.

### Passage 3 — The Power of Time, Breath, Pulse, and the Void
**Source:** Tantrāloka Āhnika 7, verses 62cd-65ab (Dyczkowski Vol.5, pp. 42-44)

**Full passage:**
This is the power (vibhava) of Time, that is established in this form within the vital breath (prāṇa). This is within the pulsation (spanda) of consciousness, which is within the Void (kha) that is within consciousness. Thus, all things are established within consciousness. The exhaled vital breath (prāṇa) is the main one, and so it stands for the inhaled and all the other forms of the vital breath also. The breath is established in the universal pulsation (of consciousness), that is, within the modality of consciousness that impels and imparts life, which is its initial expansion. The Void is the perceiver of the emptiness of deep sleep. Thus, the emanation and withdrawal of all things is established in consciousness, and so the innumerable pulsations (spanda) of consciousness along the cosmic Path up to Śakti are real.

**Original terms:** spanda, prāṇa, kha, kālavibhava, śakti

**Analysis:** This is one of Abhinavagupta's most important structural passages, showing the hierarchy: Time → Breath → Spanda → Void → Pure Consciousness. The pulsations of consciousness are real and extend along the entire cosmic path, establishing the ontological basis for all spiritual practice.

### Passage 4 — Universal Pulsation (sāmānyaspanda) from the Heart
**Source:** Tantrāloka Āhnika 5 (Dyczkowski Vol.4, pp. 50-51)

**Full passage:**
The centre is the Heart, the pulsing core of consciousness. Wave upon wave of its universal pulsation (sāmānyaspanda) arises from the ocean of the Heart and merges back into it. Each is an individual pulsation that forms as the beginning and end of the universal pulsation of which they are a part. The sixth is complete expansion (pūrṇavikāsa), which is experienced as the Cosmic Bliss (jagadānanda) of the universal activity (sāmānyaspanda) of consciousness in the fully liberated state. This is the dynamic aspect of Anuttara, the pure reflective awareness of its own unconditioned nature as the Pure Light (prakāśamātra), which is Anupāya consciousness. The practitioner experiences the state of unfathomable emptiness, remaining lucid and alert, and the breath becomes extremely subtle.

**Original terms:** sāmānyaspanda, jagadānanda, pūrṇavikāsa, anuttara, anupāya

**Analysis:** The universal pulsation (sāmānyaspanda) is the cosmic rhythm of consciousness manifesting as the Heart's expansion and contraction. Each individual pulsation is a wave within this universal ocean, and the fully liberated state experiences the cosmic bliss of this universal activity directly.

### Passage 5 — Spanda established in Consciousness along the Cosmic Path
**Source:** Tantrāloka Āhnika 7, verses 63cd-64ab (Dyczkowski Vol.5, pp. 43-44)

**Full passage:**
Thus, the emanation and withdrawal of all things is established in consciousness, and so the innumerable pulsations (spanda) of consciousness along the cosmic Path up to Śakti are real. Thus, because time is serially established in consciousness itself, and so the emanation and withdrawal of all things is brought about by consciousness, those aforementioned pulsations of consciousness consisting of emanation and withdrawal along the Path up to Śakti encompass many secondary pulsations, in accord with the teaching imparted previously. The six-fold Path is grounded in consciousness in the same way as that of Time, mediated through the same series. Thus, he opens the door for the practice of awareness in the course of engaging in outer practices.

**Original terms:** spanda, saṃvit, śakti, ṣaḍadhvan, udaya, laya

**Analysis:** The innumerable pulsations of consciousness along the cosmic path up to Śakti are affirmed as real (vāstavī), not metaphorical. This grounds all the paths and worlds of the Śaiva cosmos in the actual pulse of consciousness, making outer practice a valid means to insight and liberation.

## Concept 2: 36 Tattvas (Ontological Principles)

### Passage 1 — Definition of a Tattva as Generic Pervading Nature
**Source:** Tantrāloka Āhnika 9, verses 2-6ab (Dyczkowski Vol.6, pp. 3-8)

**Full passage:**
According to the Supreme Lord's philosophy, what is called 'tattva' is the one universal nature that invariably pervades the world-orders and the rest in every circumstance. Moreover, the one universal nature is of many kinds, as Earth and the rest of the tattvas. Thus, the fundamental state of being of Earth and the rest of the reality levels is said to be a tattva, for the sake of instruction in this way. Again, that is the supreme reality, which is the one dense uninterrupted Light of consciousness that shines universally, pervading the world-orders of many kinds described previously. This must be so, because their manifest state could not be explained otherwise. A metaphysical principle is called a tattva because it extends by virtue of its pervasive state of being, like a universal, throughout its own effect, the multitude of properties, or else also throughout the group of perceivers similar to oneself. These are, in due order, those that range from Earth to Nature, the individual soul and Śiva etc.

**Original terms:** tattva, anugāmin, sāmānya, vyāpti, pṛthvī, śiva

**Analysis:** A tattva is defined functionally as that which pervades all members of its class — a generic form that extends through effects, properties, and perceivers. The thirty-six tattvas from Earth to Śiva represent the complete hierarchy of ontological categories from gross to supreme consciousness.

### Passage 2 — The 36 Tattvas and the Pure/Impure Path
**Source:** Tantrāloka Āhnika 9, introductory commentary (Dyczkowski Vol.6, pp. 2-3)

**Full passage:**
The Śaiva system extends the Sāṁkhya 25 tattvas by adding another eleven reality levels, ending with Śivatattva, above these twenty-five. Thirty-six principles were established by adding the five — Śiva to Śuddha Vidyā — as the pure universe (Śuddhādhvan) above primal matter; so too Māyā, and then the following, with which the Impure universe of thirty-one tattvas begins. These are the reality principles that serve to account for the capacity of the individual soul to know and act, and the attendant circumstances. These are the power the soul possesses for limited action (kalā) and knowledge (vidyā), the attachment (rāga) that instigates them, the time (kāla) in which it takes place, and the necessity (niyati) of Karmic consequences that model the content of the soul's experience. Moreover, apart from the nondualist, phenomenological exegesis, drawn from the Pratyabhijñā, of the higher so-called Pure Principles, Abhinava adds two more principles above these thirty-six, which are distinctive of his Trika system; namely, Bhairava or Supreme Śiva, and the feminine Inexplicable (Anākhyā) Kālī, beyond.

**Original terms:** śuddhādhvan, aśuddhādhvan, kalā, vidyā, rāga, kāla, niyati, māyā, anākhyā kālī

**Analysis:** This establishes the complete 36-tattva system with its division into pure (5 tattvas from Śiva to Śuddhavidyā) and impure paths (31 tattvas from Māyā to Earth). Abhinavagupta distinctively adds a 37th (Bhairava/Paramaśiva) and 38th (Anākhyā Kālī) beyond the standard enumeration.

### Passage 3 — The Five Pure Tattvas and the Five Powers
**Source:** Kaul, "Ontological Hierarchy in the Tantrāloka" (Dyczkowski / Āhnika 9, verses 49-61)

**Full passage:**
Parama Śiva, being of the nature of autonomous consciousness overflowing with the five powers, is categorised into five tattvas through the division manifested by his own autonomy. From cit śakti arises the Śivatattva, from ānanda śakti comes the Śaktitattva, from icchā śakti comes forth the Sadāśivatattva, from jñāna śakti emerges the Īśvaratattva and from kriyā śakti arises the Śuddhavidyātattva. This distinction is based on predominance. For instance, one cannot think of cit without the other four. Where cit is the predominant element in Parama Śiva's autonomous nature (svātantrya) that is regarded as citśakti; when ānanda is predominant that is ānanda śakti. So Śivatattva is where cit is predominant. From the Saiddhāntika point of view, which is mostly adopted in such exegesis in terms of hierarchy, there is contraction as we go from cit to kriyā. Moreover, in each of these tattvas, though each one is full, each one is in fact replete with all these Śaktis and each of these distinctions are taught to be the various divisions on the basis of predominance of one over the other.

**Original terms:** cit śakti, ānanda śakti, icchā śakti, jñāna śakti, kriyā śakti, śivatattva, śaktitattva, sadāśivatattva, īśvaratattva, śuddhavidyātattva, svātantrya

**Analysis:** The five pure tattvas emerge from the five powers of Parama Śiva, each representing a predominance of one power while containing all five. The hierarchy from Śiva to Śuddhavidyā reflects a progressive contraction from pure consciousness to the threshold of duality, with each tattva full yet distinguished by which power predominates.

### Passage 4 — The Four Cosmic Spheres (aṇḍacatuṣṭaya)
**Source:** Kaul, "Ontological Hierarchy in the Tantrāloka" (TĀ 9, referencing Āhnika 8-9)

**Full passage:**
In the Śaiva cosmology the entire creation exists within the four cosmic spheres (aṇḍacatuṣṭaya): the sphere of Energy (śaktyaṇḍa), the sphere of māyā (māyāṇḍa), the sphere of Nature (prakṛtyaṇḍa) and the sphere of Earth (pṛthvyaṇḍa). These spheres contain within themselves an infinite number of bhuvanas. The śaktyaṇḍa has five tattvas: śakti, sadāśiva, īśvara, śuddhavidyā and mahāmāyā. Within the māyāṇḍa there is māyā, kalā, vidyā, rāga, kāla, niyati and puruṣa. In the prakṛtyaṇḍa there are tattvas from prakṛti to jala, and in the pṛthvyaṇḍa there is only the pṛthvī tattva. The pṛthvyaṇḍa is contained within the prakṛtyaṇḍa and the prakṛtyaṇḍa is contained within the māyāṇḍa. In the same way the māyāṇḍa is contained in the śaktyaṇḍa and the latter is contained in the Anuttara. Anuttara is beyond all the spheres, tattvas, bhuvanas, etc., and it contains all of them within itself at the same time.

**Original terms:** aṇḍacatuṣṭaya, śaktyaṇḍa, māyāṇḍa, prakṛtyaṇḍa, pṛthvyaṇḍa, bhuvana, anuttara

**Analysis:** The four cosmic spheres form a nested hierarchy, each contained within the next, with Anuttara as the transcendent ground containing all. This provides the macrocosmic framework for the 36 tattvas distributed across the spheres, with the purest sphere (śaktyaṇḍa) containing the five pure tattvas.

### Passage 5 — Sadāśiva and Īśvara: The aham-idam Polarity
**Source:** Kaul, "Ontological Hierarchy in the Tantrāloka" (TĀ 9)

**Full passage:**
With that projection of Śiva there arise two possibilities which further manifest into two tattvas: Sadāśiva and Īśvara. In both cases the state represented is: aham-idam. In both cases the supreme Lord is manifested in this aspect (objective aspect) represented by idam along with the I aspect (subjective aspect) represented by aham. However, at the Sadāśiva level I is principle and this has a subordinate position while at the Īśvara level, this has a principle position and I is at a subordinate place. The ground of the aham aspect is nothing but pure consciousness. In Sadāśiva tattva, urge (icchā) is predominant because that is underdeveloped while in Īśvara tattva knowledge (jñāna) is predominant. At the Sadāśiva level, Śiva is supposed to take as his object the mass of phenomena which are like a picture of which the mere outline has been drawn and that takes the form aham-idam (the aham element is predominant). But when the mass of phenomena has become fully vivid, and he submerges the I element within the This element which has that mass of phenomena as its basis, then there arises Īśvara tattva.

**Original terms:** sadāśiva, īśvara, aham-idam, icchā, jñāna

**Analysis:** This passage illuminates the subtle dynamic between subjectivity and objectivity within the pure tattvas. Sadāśiva and Īśvara represent two phases of the same aham-idam structure, distinguished by whether the subjective 'I' or objective 'this' predominates. This is key to understanding how consciousness gradually articulates the subject-object distinction.

## Concept 3: Three Upāyas (Means to Realization)

### Passage 1 — Definition of the Three Means
**Source:** Tantrāloka Āhnika 1 (Dyczkowski Vol.1, pp. 309-310)

**Full passage:**
The word 'śāmbhava' is derived from the name 'Śambhu' for Śiva. Śāmbhavopāya thus literally means 'the means pertaining to Śambhu (i.e. Śiva)'. The corresponding state to which this means corresponds is Śāmbhava, that is, Śiva's own state. Similarly, 'Śāktopāya' literally means the 'means pertaining to Śakti', and 'Āṇavopāya' is the 'means pertaining to the aṇu', that is, the individual soul. Thus, the three means correspond to the triadic — Trika — state of the one reality, namely, that of Śiva, Śakti and Nara (the individual soul). Thus, the three means are based on, and lead to, their corresponding states. Each of these is the full and complete triadic reality experienced from one or other of these perspectives, and so each means is based on, and leads to, the same ultimate reality — Anuttara. Also understood to be Bhairava, Abhinava equates it with No Means (Anupāya), which is the fundamental state of the one reality, which is the Light of Śiva consciousness. In this case each means is direct, although they may require more or less time and operate in different domains.

**Original terms:** śāmbhavopāya, śāktopāya, āṇavopāya, anupāya, trika, anuttara, bhairava

**Analysis:** The three upāyas are grounded in the triadic structure of reality itself — Śiva, Śakti, and the individual soul (nara). Each means corresponds to a perspective on the same ultimate reality, with Anupāya as the fundamental state beyond all means. The hierarchy is not of different goals but of differing immediacy.

### Passage 2 — The Śāmbhavopāya: Instantaneous Realization
**Source:** Tantrāloka Āhnika 1, verses 171-172 (Dyczkowski Vol.1, pp. 312-322)

**Full passage:**
The Śāmbhavopāya is the penetration and means pertaining to Śiva. The expression 'one who does not think of anything' implies that in this means thought constructs are of no use. Thus, as they are of no use and do not arise, the attainment of the object of realization is said to be instantaneous. This takes place by means of an extremely intense awakened insight directed at the object of realisation that develops in an instant. It operates in the state of consciousness which is free of thought constructs, pertaining to pure subjectivity free of the contamination of objectivity. The means pertaining to Śiva (śāmbhava) is said to be that of unity (abheda). The Empowered means is said to be unity-in-diversity, and the means based on difference is the Individual one.

**Original terms:** śāmbhavopāya, abheda, nirvikalpa, samāveśa

**Analysis:** The Divine Means operates through a flash of awakened insight that is instantaneous and free of all thought constructs. It is the most direct path, based on the recognition of unity (abheda) without any mediation through knowledge or action.

### Passage 3 — The Śāktopāya and Āṇavopāya
**Source:** Tantrāloka Āhnika 1, verses 214-225ab (Dyczkowski Vol.1, pp. 357-366)

**Full passage:**
The Empowered Means is jñānopāya — the means based on conceptual knowledge (vaikalpikajñāna). Practice here centres on perception (jñāna), that involves working in various ways and degrees with the flux of vikalpa. The Individual Means (Āṇavopāya) operates through the power of action externally as well as internally. The sphere of practice of the Individual Means includes ritual, Yoga, the recitation of Mantra, which are explicitly excluded in the domain of the practice of Empowered Means. The difference between the Empowered and Individual Means is that the former operates through the cognitive power of consciousness and so is internal, while the latter operates through the power of action. The entire extent of ritual action will be included in a clearly evident form in the last form of knowledge listed here, along with its means, that is, the Individual Means.

**Original terms:** śāktopāya, āṇavopāya, jñānopāya, vaikalpikajñāna, vikalpa, kriyā

**Analysis:** The Śāktopāya works through the cognitive power — the purified play of thought constructs — while the Āṇavopāya works through external and internal action including ritual, yoga, and mantra recitation. The two are distinguished by whether the means operates through knowledge (internal) or action (external), though both ultimately lead to the same goal.

### Passage 4 — The Hierarchy of Means and Grace
**Source:** Tantrāloka Āhnika 1, verses 228cd-232ab (Dyczkowski Vol.1, pp. 368-373)

**Full passage:**
Abhinava reverses the order in which the three means and their corresponding states of penetration are defined, because he would like those who practice the teachings to attain the goal as soon as possible. The higher means are more immediate than the lower ones and therefore more effective and efficient. However, which one of them or combination of them operates at a given time for a particular person depends on the intensity of Śiva's grace. It is not a matter of personal choice. All means ultimately culminate in the flash of insight that consciousness cannot be conditioned in any way, because there is nothing outside it. The basis of all the means to realisation, by virtue of which they are effective, is the Divine Means (śāmbhavopāya). This is because it is essentially pure, perpetually expanding consciousness, apart from which nothing could exist.

**Original terms:** śaktipāta, śāmbhavopāya, anupāya, tīvraśaktipāta

**Analysis:** The effectiveness of any means depends not on personal preference but on the intensity of divine grace (śaktipāta). Abhinava presents the higher means first to encourage aspirants toward the most direct path. All means are grounded in the Śāmbhavopāya, which is the very nature of consciousness itself.

### Passage 5 — No Means (Anupāya) as Supreme Grace
**Source:** Tantrāloka Āhnika 1, verses 144-145, 242-247ab (Dyczkowski Vol.1, pp. 285-289, 377-382)

**Full passage:**
In reality, Śiva alone, Who is the supreme light of consciousness, is the goal, and He shines everywhere because He does not cease to exist anywhere. Thus, no purpose whatsoever is served by any means to realization, because by definition they make known something that was previously unknown. No Means (Anupāya) is not understood to be just that there is no means to attain the one reality; it is the Divine Being of the Absolute Deity, Who spontaneously reveals Himself in all His fullness as His supreme act of grace. It is Śiva's supreme act of grace that liberates the individual soul without any effort on its part once and for all. Anupāya does not just mean 'No Means', it also means anuttaropāya, that is the Means to attaining Anuttara, the Absolute. Anupāya is the subject of chapter two.

**Original terms:** anupāya, anuttaropāya, śaktipāta, anuttara, svātantrya

**Analysis:** Anupāya is the paradoxical "means without means" — the supreme act of grace wherein Śiva reveals Himself spontaneously without any effort. It is simultaneously "no means" (because there is nothing to be attained) and the "means to Anuttara" (because it is the most direct path), representing the highest form of grace.

## Concept 4: Rasa / Aesthetics (Camatkāra and Aesthetic Delight)

### Passage 1 — Aesthetic Rapture as the Wonder of Freedom
**Source:** Tantrāloka Āhnika 3 (Dyczkowski Vol.2, p. 138)

**Full passage:**
The goddess who is the Absolute is the aesthetic rapture (camatkāra) of the sovereignty of the Lord's unsurpassed Freedom. This is the meaning. Thus, She Who is the Absolute's creative intuition (pratibhā) is endowed with the manifestation of the aggregate of the endless powers of absolute consciousness, beginning with Anuttara (the Absolute, symbolized by the letter A) onwards. Everything manifests here in two ways, as words and the things that are their objects of denotation. The Light itself shines primarily as the universe of objects of denotation. This is said in order to bring to stress that everything is a reflection within consciousness. Reflective awareness also emerges as the progressive development of each form of reflective awareness, starting with Anuttara (the Absolute) (A) and Ānanda (Bliss) (Ā) onwards.

**Original terms:** camatkāra, pratibhā, anuttara, svātantrya, vimarśa

**Analysis:** Camatkāra is identified with the supreme creative intuition (pratibhā) of absolute consciousness. Aesthetic rapture is not a mere human emotion but the very nature of Śiva's freedom realizing itself as the universe of words and meanings.

### Passage 2 — Camatkāra as the Act of Savouring
**Source:** Tantrāloka Āhnika 2 (Dyczkowski Vol.2, pp. 41-43), citing Utpaladeva and Torella

**Full passage:**
Objects that appear within consciousness, unlike a crystal, evoke emotive, aesthetic responses within them. They are 'savoured by it'. Camatkṛti means the act of a person savouring, that is, the bliss constituted by the full achievement of fruition. Bhuñjana is he who, engaged in savouring a sweet taste or the like, unlike a voracious glutton, lets the experience rest on his cognizing subjectivity, which thus predominates over the objective part. The state of 'wonder' (camatkṛti, camatkāra) in its highest form is thus both the act of 'savouring' the aesthetic object and the plenitude, absolute independence of consciousness devoid of obstacles (avighnā saṃvit), the savouring of which is in the final analysis a savouring of one's own self and one's own bliss, which eludes the mediation of the world of objects, and even goes beyond that relative and momentary transcendence that one has in the aesthetic experience of poetry and theatre.

**Original terms:** camatkṛti, camatkāra, avighnā saṃvit, bhuñjana, svarasa

**Analysis:** Camatkāra is defined as the act of aesthetic savouring where consciousness rests in its own subjective nature rather than being lost in the object. The highest form of wonder is the absolute independence of consciousness savouring itself — a self-reflexive bliss that transcends even the aesthetic experience of poetry and drama.

### Passage 3 — Aesthetic Experience as the Vitality of Consciousness
**Source:** Tantrāloka Āhnika 3, verses 229-247ab (Dyczkowski Vol.2, pp. 351-371)

**Full passage:**
Aesthetic experience is the arousal of the vitality of consciousness. This vitality is the source of life and so emission is all things. The aesthetic delight of Śiva's power of emission is the same rasa that is tasted in aesthetic experience — whether in music, poetry, or drama. Abhinava understands aesthetic rapture (rasa) as a form of spiritual cognition, a tasting of the bliss of one's own consciousness. The supreme triad and the groups of six, the all-embracing wheel of the twelve Kālikās — all are forms of the one aesthetic-spiritual consciousness that, when tasted as rasa, reveals the self-luminous nature of the perceiver. The yogi who rests in the power of bliss is one with the aesthetic delight of consciousness (samarasa). By abiding in their own innate and spontaneous aesthetic delight (svarasa), they have attained that state of oneness (sāmarasya) with the aesthetic delight of consciousness.

**Original terms:** rasa, camatkāra, svarasa, sāmarasya, samarasa, ānanda

**Analysis:** Aesthetic experience is the very vitality of consciousness made manifest. Rasa, the aesthetic rapture, is not separate from spiritual realization — it is the tasting of one's own consciousness in its self-luminous nature. The yogi who abides in this delight attains oneness with consciousness itself.

### Passage 4 — The Aesthetic Delight of the Tattvas
**Source:** Tantrāloka Āhnika 9, verse 2cd (Dyczkowski Vol.6, pp. 3-4)

**Full passage:**
Again, when diversity manifests out of Śiva's abode by the aesthetic delight (rasa) of His inherent freedom, the other immanent nature that inheres in and pervades in many ways particulars, of which it is their common nature, is this principle of existence (tattva) mentioned in the all-pervasive Lord's teaching. Jayaratha understands that by Śiva here is meant the thirty-sixth principle. As the Light of consciousness, Śiva is the necessary condition for the manifestation of all things, and yet is none of them. Śiva is the underlying nature of all the principles, and though by making them manifest in various ways as Earth and the rest of the principles, with all the worlds they contain, he never wavers from His innate, transcendent, unmanifest nature.

**Original terms:** rasa, svātantrya, tattva, śiva, prakāśa

**Analysis:** The very emanation of the tattvas from Śiva is described as an act of "aesthetic delight" (rasa) arising from His freedom. Cosmogony is thus aesthetic in nature — the universe manifests because of the rasa of Śiva's autonomous delight, making ontology inseparable from aesthetics.

### Passage 5 — Rasa as Reflected in Experience
**Source:** Tantrāloka Āhnika 3 (Dyczkowski Vol.2, p. 106)

**Full passage:**
That is a reflection, like the form of a face in a mirror, like taste (rasa) in saliva, smell in the nose, an erotic touch in the organ of touch, and an echo in the sky. All things are reflections within the Light of the Supreme Lord. The cause of reflection is the Supreme Lord's power, otherwise called freedom. Taste (rasa) is not the primary original object, as one does not perceive the effect of the medicinal rasa, that is, the quelling of disease and the like. Nor are smell and touch the primary objects. The aesthetic savour of the unique juice of aesthetic rapture has explained this, the savour of the complete emission, which is the aesthetic delight of the wheel of the rays of the senses.

**Original terms:** rasa, pratibimba, svātantrya, camatkāra

**Analysis:** Rasa is used analogically across multiple domains — from taste to aesthetic experience to spiritual cognition. The reflection doctrine extends to rasa, showing that all aesthetic savouring is a reflection of the one supreme rasa of consciousness within the mirror of the Lord's freedom.

## Concept 5: Pratyabhijñā / Reflection (Pratibimbavāda)

### Passage 1 — The Doctrine of Reflection Defined
**Source:** Tantrāloka Āhnika 3 (Dyczkowski Vol.2, pp. 63-64)

**Full passage:**
Abhinava begins his presentation of the Supreme Means, that is, Śāmbhavopāya, with an exposition of the Doctrine of Reflection (pratibimbavāda). This maintains that all that is objectively perceived, whether outer objects, sensory perceptions or inner cogitations, shines within consciousness at one with it as do reflections with the mirror in which they are reflected. But while the reflections (pratibimba) in a mirror are those of an original object (bimba) outside the mirror, these reflections are produced by the inherent power of the mirror of Bhairava's consciousness. The idea that all that exists, with or without outer physically objective content, appears within consciousness, through which it is made manifest, is not new. The Buddhist idealists (Yogācāra) had made it a cornerstone of their idealism. But for them, it serves to illustrate that the world is just an unreal appearance. However, Abhinava, following the lead of Utpaladeva, takes care to stress that the reflections are real.

**Original terms:** pratibimbavāda, pratibimba, bimba, bhairava, svātantrya

**Analysis:** The Pratibimbavāda is the cornerstone of Abhinavagupta's ontology. Unlike Buddhist idealism where reflections are unreal, here reflections are real manifestations of consciousness's own power. The mirror of Bhairava's consciousness does not reflect external originals but generates reflections from its own inherent freedom.

### Passage 2 — The Characteristics of a Reflection
**Source:** Tantrāloka Āhnika 3, verses 24-34ab (Dyczkowski Vol.2, pp. 99-108)

**Full passage:**
All this mass of phenomena is just a reflection in the Sky of Consciousness, because it possesses the characteristic mark of a reflection. This is the characteristic mark of a reflection, namely, that it cannot manifest separately from the medium of reflection, and that it manifests mingled with other reflections. That is a reflection, like the form of a face in a mirror, like taste in saliva, smell in the nose, an erotic touch in the organ of touch, and an echo in the sky. All things are reflections within the Light of the Supreme Lord. The cause of reflection is the Supreme Lord's power, otherwise called freedom. The pure Light of consciousness expounded in the previous chapter is Bhairava's supreme glory. Apart from that Light, anything more one may conceive to discern there for the purpose of instruction and practice is only His freedom.

**Original terms:** pratibimba, cittākāśa, svātantrya, prakāśa, vimarśa

**Analysis:** The defining characteristic of a reflection is its inseparability from the medium in which it appears. Phenomena cannot appear apart from consciousness just as reflections cannot appear apart from their mirror. The cause of all reflection is the Lord's freedom (svātantrya) — the self-reflexive power of consciousness.

### Passage 3 — Pratyabhijñā as Recognition
**Source:** Tantrāloka Āhnika 2 (Dyczkowski Vol.2, pp. 47-48)

**Full passage:**
Having somehow attained the state of a servant of the Great Lord (maheśvara), which is the cause of attaining all that is of value, and wishing to render service to humanity, I am establishing by reasoned argument the Recognition of that reality. A state devoid of craving within oneself — craving, which is desire, consists of the denial of a particular object and the acceptance of what remains. As there is no other condition of desire, the other person is not an area of its concern. Nor does it make sense that helping others is its concern, because the object of desire is that of those whose nature is unfulfilled. However, one who is completely fulfilled by his condition as a servant of the lord, which is the cause of attaining all that is of value, there is nothing at all within himself that needs to be done that is desirable. His will is unrestricted because its nature is consciousness.

**Original terms:** pratyabhijñā, maheśvara, ātman, icchā, svātantrya

**Analysis:** Pratyabhijñā is the "Recognition" of one's own true nature as Śiva. The passage shows how this recognition transforms desire from personal craving to the unobstructed flow of will that serves others spontaneously, since the one who recognizes his nature as Śiva is already complete.

### Passage 4 — Recognition and Jīvanmukti (Liberation in Life)
**Source:** Tantrāloka Āhnika 3, verses 271cd-274ab (Dyczkowski Vol.2, pp. 395-396)

**Full passage:**
He who enters repeatedly into this state of penetration (samāveśa) into pure Bhairava consciousness free of thought constructs, attains Bhairava's state, otherwise called liberation in this life (jīvanmukti). The liberated soul's only purpose is to elevate others. The yogi free of thought constructs, beholding that Śiva also reflected in the noble and uncreated (akalpita) mirror of consciousness, becomes by himself spontaneously Bhairava. If the Goddess of Consciousness, who is the Centre, expands as the inner nature of all things by the process of the practice taught, or if by the process that will be taught next, Brahmā's Channel, which is the Centre, expands, then by virtue of that expansion, one attains the bliss of consciousness. And so he attains the liberation in this life taught previously.

**Original terms:** jīvanmukti, samāveśa, bhairava, akalpita, nirvikalpa

**Analysis:** Liberation in life (jīvanmukti) is attained through repeated penetration (samāveśa) into Bhairava consciousness free of thought constructs. This is accomplished by seeing all things as reflections in the uncreated mirror of one's own consciousness, spontaneously becoming Bhairava without effort.

### Passage 5 — Reflective Awareness and the Fifty Phonemes
**Source:** Tantrāloka Āhnika 3, verses 65cd-197ab (Dyczkowski Vol.2, pp. 136-314)

**Full passage:**
The letter A is a measure of the power of consciousness, which does not belong to the domain of Māyā; it transcends hearing, spontaneous, it is the wonder of one's own nature, which is the great sea of consciousness that, without waves, rests in the Great Light. It is present equally in both two phases, initial and final of perception, as the completely full and all-embracing 'I-ness' as the initial outpouring of the power of consciousness and the final reflective awareness of all things. From the perspective of the Trika of the PT, the letter A stands for Anuttara and is Akula. Its power is the unfolding perception of the totality of all things in their fundamental nature as the flow of the energies of pure self-awareness i.e. AHAṀ ('I') of Bhairava/Śiva, the supreme perceiver. It encompasses the energies of the letters from A to H, that are as many forms of reflective awareness (parāmarśa), that extend through, even as they produce, the reality levels from Earth to Śiva in a perpetual cycle.

**Original terms:** parāmarśa, aham, anuttara, akula, AHAṀ, kalā

**Analysis:** The letter A, as the first articulated form of reflective awareness, is identified with Anuttara itself. The entire phonemic universe, from A to H, forms the cycle AHAṀ — the 'I' of Bhairava. Each letter is a form of reflective awareness (parāmarśa), and together they produce and pervade the thirty-six tattvas in a perpetual cycle of manifestation.

## Concept 6: Five Kañcukas (Obscuring Coverings)

### Passage 1 — The Five Kañcukas Described
**Source:** Tantrāloka Āhnika 9, verses 166cd-170 (Dyczkowski Vol.6, pp. 144-147)

**Full passage:**
Thus the Supreme Lord renders the individual souls subject to the chain of cause and effect of Fundamental Nature. This is heralded by the progressive operation (kalanā) of the obscuring coverings (kañcuka) Kalā, Vidyā, Rāga, Kāla and Niyati. They are in the form of limited agency, limited noetic subjectivity (jñatva), craving, temporal differentiation, and its emergence due to the necessity of karmic consequence respectively. They consist of the lack of reflective awareness of the essential nature of the soul which does and knows everything, is free of craving, is undifferentiated by time and is all things. The group of principles ranging from the Force of limited agency up to Earth, that is within Māyā and will be described further ahead, differs for each individual soul. This is clear from the fact that, for example, the joy and suffering each one experiences are different. If the universe were to be a single aggregate of means to worldly experience, the experience of pleasure and the like would be the same for all souls. How then could one account for the differences between each individual's experiences?

**Original terms:** kañcuka, kalā, vidyā, rāga, kāla, niyati, māyā, kalanā

**Analysis:** The five kañcukas are the principles of contraction that reduce Śiva's infinite nature to the finite experience of the individual soul. They produce limited agency (kalā), limited knowledge (vidyā), attachment (rāga), time (kāla), and karmic necessity (niyati). Together they account for the diversity of individual experience within the sphere of Māyā.

### Passage 2 — Kalā as the First Covering
**Source:** Tantrāloka Āhnika 9, verse 174 (Dyczkowski Vol.6, pp. 150-151)

**Full passage:**
The Force of limited agency arises from the principle of Māyā, and its characteristic is limited agency (kiñcitkartṛtva). This, the first of the obscuring coverings, not only obscures its essentially omnipotent Śiva nature, it enlivens its limited consciousness. The relation between cause and effect is variously conceived in other scriptures, but we limit ourselves to explain it from the point of view of the Mālinīśāstra. It is not eternal, never destroyed, as a principle amongst the other principles, because it is said that then comes Māyā after the preceding principles. Moreover, that is so also because it will be established that its withdrawal occurs periodically.

**Original terms:** kalā, kiñcitkartṛtva, māyā, kañcuka, śiva

**Analysis:** Kalā (limited agency) is the first kañcuka to arise from Māyā. It simultaneously obscures Śiva's omnipotence and activates the limited consciousness of the individual soul. It is not an eternal principle but subject to periodic withdrawal, emphasizing the impermanent nature of the covering.

### Passage 3 — The Kañcukas As a Foreign Body in Sāṁkhya
**Source:** Tantrāloka Āhnika 9 (Dyczkowski Vol.6, p. 167), citing Torella

**Full passage:**
As Torella observes: 'the kañcukas (kalā, vidyā, rāga, kāla and niyati) form a foreign body with respect to the Sāṅkhya basis of the tattvas', and further, they give 'the impression of doubles, having an uncertain status, so much so that not only all the Śaiva philosophers but also some of the Śaiva scriptures themselves feel bound to answer the objections they already foresaw in the opponents' minds, and to highlight the specificity, for example, of rāga 'Attachment' and vidyā 'Science', with respect to their seeming doubles, avairāgya and buddhi.' The Śaiva justification for having vidyā as well is that buddhi cannot examine itself, and therefore the soul must have an additional instrument of cognition. As Sadyojyotis argues: 'Without an instrument, an agent is not known to act with respect to an object. Therefore, there must exist an instrument, and that is vidyā, which discriminates among the awarenesses of the intellect.'

**Original terms:** kañcuka, vidyā, buddhi, rāga, kalā, kāla, niyati

**Analysis:** The kañcukas are the distinctively Śaiva addition to the Sāṁkhya tattva system. They solve a real philosophical problem: how the soul can be aware of its own cognitions. Vidyā, for instance, is justified as the meta-cognitive principle that discriminates among the contents of the intellect (buddhi), which cannot know itself.

### Passage 4 — The Kañcukas as the Apparatus of Finitude
**Source:** Tantrāloka Āhnika 9 (Dyczkowski Vol.6, pp. 135-144), Cosmology RO

**Full passage:**
The five kañcukas (coverings) are the principles that contract Śiva's infinite capacities into the finite experience of the bounded soul: kalā (limited agency), vidyā (limited knowledge), rāga (attachment), kāla (time), and niyati (necessity). These emerge from Māyā and together constitute the apparatus of finitude. Māyā itself is the 'knot' (māyāgranthi) — the root principle of differentiation that, when pierced, releases the soul from the impure universe into the pure path. The relation between Māyā and the individual soul is carefully distinguished: Māyā is the principle of obscuration, while the soul is the conscious subject whose innate freedom is veiled by it. The aggregate of obscuring coverings (kañcuka) is made of Māyā, and so too the collection of the instruments of perception and action that constitute the psychophysical organism.

**Original terms:** kañcuka, māyāgranthi, kalā, vidyā, rāga, kāla, niyati, māyā

**Analysis:** The kañcukas are the complete apparatus of finitude, emerging from Māyā and constituting the bridge between pure consciousness and conditioned existence. Māyā is the root knot (granthi) that binds these coverings together, and piercing this knot releases the soul into the pure path beyond all limitation.

### Passage 5 — The Impurity of Māyā Generating the Kañcukas
**Source:** Tantrāloka Āhnika 11 (Dyczkowski Vol.7, pp. 1584-1596)

**Full passage:**
The same applies to the five obscuring coverings (kañcuka), as these are factors that render the soul subject to the limitations of space, time, and form. Nature, the individual soul, and the five obscuring coverings — all of these are included within the realm of Māyā. Necessity and the rest are said to be — 'I know just now, that I do just now' — indicating the temporal and conditional nature of limited knowledge and action. The Impurity called Māyā is generated from Karma. As the kañcukas arise, along with the subtle body (puryaṣṭaka) and the gross elements, they constitute the complete apparatus of the bound soul's embodiment and experience.

**Original terms:** kañcuka, māyā, puryaṣṭaka, karma, niyati

**Analysis:** The kañcukas are described as factors that render the soul subject to spatial, temporal, and formal limitations. They arise from Māyā and are intimately connected with karma, the subtle body, and the gross elements, forming the complete apparatus of embodiment that keeps the soul in bondage until pierced by grace and recognition.

## Concept 7: Ābhāsa (Appearance Doctrine)

### Passage 1 — All Phenomena as Manifestations of Consciousness
**Source:** Tantrāloka Āhnika 6 (Dyczkowski Vol.6)

**Full passage:**
As will be explained, 'bare manifestation' (ābhāsamātra) is a universal. The cause is the appearance, associated with the senses, of its being the cause of its effect. Bare manifestation, not some gross entity existing as the effect of clay. According to whether consciousness is, for example, pure or impure, it is 'of such a kind'. Apart from manifestation there is nothing else at all. The single manifestation (ābhāsa) is that of the relation of cause and effect. There is no contradiction in maintaining that that is a single manifestation, which is only a manifestation of consciousness. Thus, a scorpion can come out of cow-faeces, from another scorpion, from imagination, from memory, from the desire of a Yogi, from such factors as the power of certain substances and mantras — all are equally manifestations of consciousness.

**Original terms:** ābhāsa, ābhāsamātra, saṃvit, kāryakāraṇa

**Analysis:** The Ābhāsavāda (Doctrine of Appearance) holds that all phenomena are bare manifestations of consciousness. There is no underlying "thing" apart from its appearance in consciousness. The famous scorpion example illustrates that diverse causal sequences are all equally valid as manifestations — causality itself is a form of appearance.

### Passage 2 — The Reflection of All Things in Consciousness
**Source:** Tantrāloka Āhnika 3 (Dyczkowski Vol.2, pp. 99-108)

**Full passage:**
All this mass of phenomena is just a reflection in the Sky of Consciousness, because it possesses the characteristic mark of a reflection, namely, that it cannot manifest separately from the medium of reflection, and that it manifests mingled with other reflections. That is a reflection, like the form of a face in a mirror, like taste in saliva, smell in the nose, an erotic touch in the organ of touch, and an echo in the sky. All things are reflections within the Light of the Supreme Lord. The cause of reflection is the Supreme Lord's power, otherwise called freedom. Free and completely full is this Lord, the omnipresent Bhairava. There is nothing that exists that He does not make manifest in the pure mirror of His own nature.

**Original terms:** ābhāsa, pratibimba, svātantrya, bhairava, darpaṇa

**Analysis:** The Ābhāsavāda is presented through the analogy of reflections. Phenomena have the defining characteristic of reflections: they cannot appear independently of the medium (consciousness) and they appear intermingled with other reflections. The cause of all appearance is the Lord's freedom (svātantrya), not any external object.

### Passage 3 — Appearance as the Only Reality
**Source:** Tantrāloka Āhnika 6 (Dyczkowski Vol.6, pp. 19418-19425)

**Full passage:**
As all things are appearances (ābhāsavāda), apart from manifestation there is nothing else at all. The epistemological unity of cognisors in respect of the object of cognition is made one in respect of both an appearance of smoke and an appearance of fire. Each single generic manifestation (ābhāsa) is said to be the fifteenth. Other manifestations, such as unconsciousness and the like, are also forms of the one appearance. The fact that the same object is perceived by multiple perceivers does not require a mind-independent external object; the shared appearance is itself the reality.

**Original terms:** ābhāsa, ābhāsavāda, jñāna, viṣaya

**Analysis:** This passage makes the radical claim that apart from manifestation there is nothing else at all. The Ābhāsavāda eliminates the need for mind-independent external objects — shared appearances are themselves real, and the unity of cognition across perceivers is explained by the unity of consciousness, not by a shared external object.

### Passage 4 — Pratibimbavāda and the Matter of Wonder
**Source:** Biernacki, The Matter of Wonder (OUP, 2023, referenced in RO)

**Full passage:**
Abhinavagupta's doctrine of reflection (pratibimbavāda) holds that the world is a reflection in the mirror of Śiva's consciousness. This is not idealism — matter is not illusory but is a real manifestation of consciousness, sharing in its reality. The reflection model preserves both the unity of the source (Śiva) and the reality of the reflected world. Unlike the Advaita Vedānta model where the world is a false appearance (vivartavāda), Abhinavagupta's reflection doctrine grants the world ontological weight while maintaining its dependence on consciousness. This makes it a promising resource for contemporary philosophy of mind and ecology. Camatkāra — wonder or astonishment — is the central aesthetic category in Abhinavagupta's theory of rasa. In Abhinavagupta's panentheism, the world is not dead matter but a wondrous expression of the divine. Camatkāra is not merely a human response to beauty — it is the ontological signature of reality itself.

**Original terms:** pratibimbavāda, vivartavāda, camatkāra, śiva, ābhāsa

**Analysis:** Biernacki clarifies that Abhinavagupta's reflection doctrine is distinct from both Buddhist idealism and Advaita Vedānta. The world is not illusory (vivarta) but a real manifestation of consciousness. The reflection model allows for both the unity of the source and the ontological reality of the reflected world.

### Passage 5 — Manifestation and the Void
**Source:** Tantrāloka Āhnika 7, verses 62cd-65ab (Dyczkowski Vol.5, pp. 42-44)

**Full passage:**
This is the power of Time, that is established in this form within the vital breath. This is within the pulsation of consciousness, which is within the Void (kha) that is within consciousness. Thus, all things are established within consciousness. The breath is established in the universal pulsation of consciousness, that is, within the modality of consciousness that impels and imparts life, which is its initial expansion. The Void is the perceiver of the emptiness of deep sleep. All the six-fold Path is established in pure consciousness (cinmātra), for what does not rest there is as unreal as a sky-flower. Through and mediated by consciousness, the Path is established in that which it has emitted, namely, in the void, in the intellect, in the vital breaths, in the wheels of the channels of the vital breath, in the secondary wheels of the senses, and in the external body.

**Original terms:** ābhāsa, kha, cinmātra, prāṇa, spanda, ṣaḍadhvan

**Analysis:** This passage traces the hierarchy of manifestation from the power of Time through breath, pulsation, and Void into pure consciousness. Everything that exists is an appearance within consciousness — the sixfold path, the body, the senses, the breath. Whatever does not rest in consciousness is as unreal as a sky-flower.

## Concept 8: Mantra / Sound / Speech

### Passage 1 — The Vitality of Mantra (Mantravīrya)
**Source:** Tantrāloka Āhnika 4, verses 181cd-193 (Dyczkowski Vol.3, pp. 279-297)

**Full passage:**
As consciousness is reflective awareness, its spontaneous and perpetually emergent resonance (dhvani) of pure self-awareness, which is the supreme level of Speech, is called the supreme, Great Heart (SAUḤ). The resonance that arises spontaneously is Supreme Speech, which is the Sound (nāda) that is the reflective awareness of supreme consciousness. The god who resides in the chest of living breathing beings spontaneously utters the Mantra not uttered by anybody and that none obstructs. The radiant pulsation (sphuraṇa) of the Heart which is this manifest universe manifests as the Being (S) of the Abode of Emission (H) to the degree in which it penetrates here into the energy of Trika (AU). The wise explain that this Heart (SAUḤ) is the supreme nectar of Bhairava consciousness. Present in the prongs of the Trika Trident, consisting of the powers of will, knowledge and action (AU), it is emitted into Supreme Abode (S).

**Original terms:** mantravīrya, SAUḤ, dhvani, nāda, sphuraṇa, hṛdaya, trika

**Analysis:** The Heart of consciousness is SAUḤ, the supreme seed-mantra of the Trika. It is the spontaneous resonance (dhvani) of pure self-awareness that perpetually arises as Supreme Speech. The letters S-AU-Ḥ encode the entire structure of manifestation: Being, the Trika powers, and emission.

### Passage 2 — SAUḤ and KHPHREṀ as Emanation and Withdrawal
**Source:** Tantrāloka Āhnika 4, verses 186cd-191 (Dyczkowski Vol.3, pp. 286-295)

**Full passage:**
The energies of SAUḤ in the process of emanation, in the forward order, and that of withdrawal, in the reverse order, which in the TĀ is represented by the energies of KHPHREṀ. The expansion of the former and the contraction of the latter together constitute the resonance (dhvani) of the throb (sphuraṇa) of the Heart. This is the ceaseless repetition of Mantra which is the reflective awareness of consciousness. SAUḤ, the seed-syllable of Parā, the supreme goddess of the Trika, is that of emanation (sṛṣṭibīja), whereas Piṇḍanātha (KHPHREṀ), the seed-syllable of Kālī Kālasaṁkarṣiṇī, is that of withdrawal. Thus, in the sphere of the Empowered Means, the utterance of SAUḤ is experienced as the very pulse of the reflective awareness of consciousness, generating the universe in its expansion and consuming it in its withdrawal.

**Original terms:** SAUḤ, KHPHREṀ, sṛṣṭibīja, dhvani, sphuraṇa, kālasaṁkarṣiṇī

**Analysis:** SAUḤ and KHPHREṀ are the two fundamental seed-mantras of emanation and withdrawal. Their alternating resonance constitutes the ceaseless heartbeat of consciousness — the expansion of manifestation and its contraction back into the source. Mantra practice thus participates in the cosmic rhythm of creation and dissolution.

### Passage 3 — The Four Levels of Speech
**Source:** Tantrāloka Āhnika 3, verses 235-247ab (Dyczkowski Vol.2, pp. 361-371), Appendix H

**Full passage:**
The four-fold manifestation of Speech ranges from the Supreme to the Corporeal (vaikharī). Parā (Supreme Speech) is the pure, undifferentiated reflective awareness identical with Śiva. Paśyantī (Seeing Speech) is the first differentiation, where the universe is seen as a single undivided whole in consciousness. Madhyamā (Intermediate Speech) is the level of inner mental speech — the form of thought before it is uttered. Vaikharī (Articulate Speech) is the audible, gross vocalization that reaches the ear of the listener. The goddesses of the Śāmbhava Siddhas are intent on devouring the duality of this four-fold manifestation of Speech. The seal (mudrā) that moves in the Expanse of No Touch, free of all obscuring coverings, is said to be Khecarī — the lightning flash that travels through the Sky of Consciousness, her sole nature the expansion of her own innate light.

**Original terms:** parā vāc, paśyantī, madhyamā, vaikharī, śāmbhava, khecarī

**Analysis:** The four levels of speech are successive stages of the cosmogonic emanation of all reality from pure undifferentiated consciousness (Parā) to fully articulated audible sound (Vaikharī). These are not merely linguistic levels but ontological stages of manifestation. The highest Siddhas transcend even the fourfold speech, abiding in the pure sky of consciousness.

### Passage 4 — The Twelve Dvādaśānta Stages
**Source:** Tantrāloka Volume 1, Appendix A (Dyczkowski Vol.1, pp. 485-488)

**Full passage:**
The End of the Twelve (dvādaśānta) is the summit of sonic ascent, described as the uppermost station of the subtle body. The core teaching is a sequence of twelve stages ranging from A to the Transmental. These mark the stations of ascent from the Heart up to the End of the Twelve, at the summit of the subtle body. They retract and reverse the downward flow of the cosmogonic energy of Speech from its supreme form (parā vāc) at one with Śiva as His reflective awareness and freedom down to audible corporeal Speech (vaikharī vāc). The sequence is: A, U, M, Bindu (the Point), Ardhacandra (Half Moon), Nirodhīnī (the Obstructress), Nāda (Sound), Nādānta (End of Sound), Śakti (Power), Vyāpinī (the Pervasive One), Samanā (the Equal One), and Unmanā (the Transmental). Beyond these lies the Untainted (nirāmaya) absolute.

**Original terms:** dvādaśānta, bindu, ardhacandra, nirodhinī, nāda, nādānta, śakti, vyāpinī, samanā, unmanā, nirāmaya

**Analysis:** The twelve-stage dvādaśānta is the complete map of sonic ascent from the most differentiated level (A) to the Transmental (Unmanā) beyond all sound. It retraces the descent of cosmogonic Speech from Parā to Vaikharī, providing the yogi with a path to ascend through the levels of sound back to the undifferentiated source.

### Passage 5 — Mantras as the Pulsing Nature of Consciousness (Spanda)
**Source:** Tantrāloka Āhnika 7, verse 2cd-3ab (Dyczkowski Vol.5, pp. 3-5)

**Full passage:**
All Mantras consisting of seed syllables (bīja) and syllabic groups (piṇḍa) are the dynamic pulsing nature (spandanātmatā) of consciousness. Thus, a Mantra is said to serve as a means to attain Supreme Consciousness because such is its nature. Mantra is the pulsation of consciousness within the flow of the breath. These Mantras that arise within the vital breath are the dynamic pulsing nature of consciousness and serve as a means to supreme consciousness. They initially induce the penetration of individual consciousness into Śakti's essential nature, and then successively into Śiva. Thus, the description of the practice of projecting cycles of Mantras into the flow of the breath, as part of the nature of the letters, which serve to practice in the domain of the Individual Means, is not useless.

**Original terms:** spandanātmatā, bīja, piṇḍa, prāṇa, āṇavopāya, śāktāveśa

**Analysis:** Mantras are identified as the pulsing nature (spandanātmatā) of consciousness itself, not as external sounds. They arise within the vital breath and serve as a bridge from individual consciousness to Śiva, first through penetration into Śakti's nature and then into Śiva Himself. Mantra practice is thus a direct participation in the pulse of consciousness.

### Passage 6 — The Cakrodaya: The Dawning of the Wheels of Mantra
**Source:** Tantrāloka Āhnika 7, verses 1-5ab (Dyczkowski Vol.5, pp. 2-10)

**Full passage:**
The word 'cakrodaya' literally means 'emergence, arising or dawning of the wheels'. Mantras recited in consonance with the breathing cycle (prāṇacakra), depending on their length, form daily cycles of varying sizes. As the cycle of the breath does its round, the mantra recited in consonance with it arises with each turn from the heart. The dawn of the Sun of exhalation is that of mantra also. The dawning of the cycle of the repetition of mantra (mantracakrodaya) coincides with the dawning of the cycle of the vital breath (prāṇacakrodaya). A waterwheel moves a series of machines for raising water located in front of it, and possesses the varied emergent activity of the machines it sets into operation by its single, unified exertion. Similarly, when mantras arise by the strength of a single one-pointed and continuous application of attention and intention (anusaṁdhāna), the deity of those mantras, because it is at one with them, becomes propitious by virtue of that exertion.

**Original terms:** cakrodaya, prāṇacakra, mantracakrodaya, anusaṁdhāna, prāṇacakrodaya

**Analysis:** Cakrodaya is the practice of synchronizing mantra recitation with the natural cycles of the breath. The waterwheel simile illustrates how a single continuous application of attention (anusaṁdhāna) activates the entire machinery of mantra, causing the deity to become propitious through the same exertion that moves the mantras.

### Passage 7 — The Alphabet of Reflective Awareness
**Source:** Tantrāloka Āhnika 3, verses 65cd-197ab (Dyczkowski Vol.2, pp. 136-314)

**Full passage:**
In Āhnika 3, Abhinavagupta presents the 50 phonemes of the Sanskrit alphabet not as mere linguistic units but as the fifty forms of Śiva's reflective awareness (vimarśa). The sequence begins with A as Anuttara, the absolute, and unfolds through the vowels (each embodying a specific power — will, knowledge, action), the diphthongs, the Point (bindu), Visarga, the consonants, semivowels, and sibilants, culminating in the fiftieth letter, KṢ, which contains all the others within itself. Each letter is a form of Śakti, and the entire alphabet together constitutes the supreme phonemic energy (śabdarāśi) from which all mantras and all language are derived. The entire phonemic universe is encapsulated in the cycle of the flow of AHAṀ — 'I am'. The letter A represents Anuttara (the Absolute), H represents the power of emission (visarga), and the Anusvāra (Ṁ) represents the Point (bindu) of repose. The Lord united with emission is all things as the fifty energies of the letters.

**Original terms:** vimarśa, śabdarāśi, bindu, visarga, AHAṀ, anuttara, kṣa

**Analysis:** The fifty phonemes are forms of Śiva's reflective awareness (vimarśa), not arbitrary signs. The entire alphabet culminates in the cycle AHAṀ — 'I am' — where A is the Absolute, H is emission, and Ṁ is the point of repose. The phonemes are the structural DNA of reality, from which all mantras and all language derive their power.
