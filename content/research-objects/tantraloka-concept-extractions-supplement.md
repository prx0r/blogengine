## Volume 1 (Āhnika 1)

### Passage 45 — The Four Levels of Speech and the Descent of Scripture

**Source:** Tantrāloka Āhnika 1, commentary (Dyczkowski Vol.1, pp. 85-88)

**Full passage:**
Supreme Speech is the reflective awareness Śiva, who is absolute consciousness, has that 'I am' (aham), which embraces within itself all the energies of this self-awareness. Devoid of objectivity in any form, it is the perpetual affirmation of Being, which communicates and affirms its own perfect infinite existence to itself. As such it is understood to be an inner discourse (saṅjalpa) which, nonconceptual and not formed into words, is not based on convention (saṁketa). It is the inherent freedom, which is the reflective awareness of absolute consciousness, by virtue of which it is the one absolute reality. All the scriptures abide at this level, merged at one with the supreme power of Speech. At the next level, that is, the Speech of Vision (paśyantī), consciousness develops into that of a perceiving subjectivity. As such, it is related to its object as yet undivided from it. Held within the perceiving subjectivity, it is the reflective awareness of itself as the perceiver in relation to all things shining within it, as one with it in a potential form of all that will be emitted on the following levels. This level of Speech 'sees' all things that are to be emitted, within itself, at one with itself, 'like a flame in a fire'. The next level, Middle Speech (madhyamā), manifests on the plane of ideation and is the Speech of thought. At this stage, words and their meanings are experienced as distinct realities, although still only mental ones. Finally, Speech issues out externally as Corporeal Speech (vaikharī). At this level language denotes external, objectively existing things and the relationships between them, their qualities and activity etc.

**Original terms:** parā vāc, paśyantī, madhyamā, vaikharī, aham, saṅjalpa, saṁketa

### Passage 46 — Liberation is Not Separate from the Self; the Nature of Freedom (svātantrya)

**Source:** Tantrāloka Āhnika 1, verses 31-33ab (Dyczkowski Vol.1, pp. 130-131)

**Full passage:**
Liberation is not some separate reality, whether insignificant or significant, that differs from the Self that is free; indeed, one does not even use a different name for it. If liberation is 'insignificant', then in accord with the teaching stated previously, it would just be bondage that does affect the true nature of the Self; and if it is 'significant', then it is ultimately real, and so it does not differ from the Self that is free. Liberation is nothing but the full and complete experience of one's own nature. And the nature of the Self is pure consciousness. Swami Lakshmanjoo explains: "Mokṣa [liberation] occurs when your being becomes absolutely independent from all sides. Without that svātantrya [freedom] whatever exists in this world would be tuccha (i.e. worthless) and is not mokṣa." Liberation and the Self are literally the same reality — not like two things that are so similar that one can say they are 'the same'. The former is not an 'insignificant illusion'. Nor can it be said to be a substantial, independent reality, because the only one that can be such must be truly, ultimately existent.

**Original terms:** mokṣa, svātantrya, tuccha, atuccha, ātman, pūrṇaprathā

### Passage 47 — The Nourishing Power of Creative Intuition (pratibhā)

**Source:** Tantrāloka Āhnika 1, verses 115cd-116ab, commentary (Dyczkowski Vol.1, pp. 280-282)

**Full passage:**
The body of the one Lord of Consciousness is His inner creative imagination (pratibhā). It is worshipped externally as the cycle of the waves of the powers of consciousness in a conditioned, limited form as the deity made manifest thereby, whether peaceful or otherwise. 'The inner creative imagination' (āntarī pratibhā) is the power of freedom (svātantryaśakti). 'Wheel of the Tide of Energies' — ūrnicakra — is pratibhā, which means conscious creative energy. The term 'Wave' to denote the activity and nature of consciousness is drawn from Krama sources. This Wave of creative imagination that arises inwardly within consciousness generates configurations — 'wheels' — of divine forms along with their associated Mantras and the consonant outer ritual action. As the fluctuations of the mind, such as anger and the like, are one with the aesthetic relish of the wonder of consciousness, and also because they could not otherwise assume their own specific nature, they are the divine instruments (karaṇadevatā) of the Supreme Lord which, as the rays of the Sun of Śiva, each in their own way unfold his divine play.

**Original terms:** pratibhā, āntarī pratibhā, svātantryaśakti, ūrnicakra, karaṇadevatā

### Passage 48 — The Three Upāyas and the Hierarchy of Grace

**Source:** Tantrāloka Āhnika 1, verses 140-148 commentary (Dyczkowski Vol.1, pp. 307-316)

**Full passage:**
The Pervasive Lord reveals His own complete form in all His fullness to a certain perceiver, while He does so to some other part by part, successively. The Lord reveals His own complete form to a certain perceiver by the rapidly heightening gradation of the intense modality of the decrease of obscuration, and to another perceiver part by part, successively, in accord with the gradation, ranging from slow onwards, in which the obscuring covering decreases. The full and complete experience of one's own nature as the one Being of all beings is the supreme knowledge individual souls possess. Inferior and multiple is that knowledge which is other than that. The means that is direct is the Divine Means (Śāmbhavopāya). That alone is the unimpeded instrumental means to the attainment of supreme knowledge. When that has reached its supreme summit, it is called No Means (Anupāya). The means to that Divine Means is the Empowered (Śākta) one, and the means to that is the Individual (Āṇava) one. Thus, the one means to realisation is divided, but this division is just figurative, not ultimate.

**Original terms:** Śāmbhavopāya, Śāktopāya, Āṇavopāya, Anupāya, pūrṇaprathā, tīvranirhrāsa

### Passage 49 — The Purification of Thought (vikalpasaṁskāra) in the First Moment of Perception

**Source:** Tantrāloka Āhnika 1, verses 146-148 commentary (Dyczkowski Vol.1, pp. 314-316)

**Full passage:**
That which manifests clearly in the first moment of perception within one's own self-awareness, the sole abode of pure thought-free consciousness, is said to be the direct means to realisation called the will (icchā). The first moment of perception is free of thought constructs. This is the phase in which reality 'as it is' (yathābhūta) is perceived directly. In the second phase, the content of the first phase is analysed and thus rendered amenable to the daily commerce of life, in which individual objects and perceivers are identified and known through the process of conceptualization. The first moment of self-reflective awareness is the subtlest state of knowledge. It is the will, that is, the pure intention to perceive which is the initial moment of perception. The first phase is the domain of the Divine Means of Śiva consciousness itself, free of thought constructs. The other means of realisation operate in the second moment, in which conceptual representation, and with it duality, arise. If the yogi practices attention to himself as the perceiver, that experience of self-awareness develops, and as it does so, he experiences in the very core of subjectivity an intense desire. This is not a desire for anything objective. It is the immensely powerful intention to exist and be conscious, to experience Śiva consciousness directly.

**Original terms:** icchā, vikalpa, nirvikalpa, savikalpa, prathamikālocanajñāna, sphuraṇa

### Passage 50 — Prakṛti and Puruṣa: The Sāṁkhya View Critiqued

**Source:** Tantrāloka Āhnika 1, verses commentary on 12/49-52 (Dyczkowski Vol.1, pp. 143-144)

**Full passage:**
As insentient Prakṛti cannot assume the agency of the individual soul, and that soul does not accept its association with Prakṛti, then even when they are in contact, the universe would not arise. However, as long as the Lord does not assume His role as the agent of their release from bondage, although liberated by the Sāṁkhya teaching, the soul returns back again because a full knowledge of reality has not arisen. The ultimate cause of liberation is Śiva's grace. Everything else that may serve as a means to it is an instrumental, attendant cause — even full and perfect knowledge. The word 'nimitta' does include the sense of 'means' to something, but it is so insofar as it is an instrumental cause to attain it. The term 'upāya' literally means a 'means'. In this context, it is a means to realisation. Any means to realisation is such by the power of Śiva's grace, and so one could say that it is an 'instrumental cause' — nimitta — of realisation.

**Original terms:** prakṛti, puruṣa, nimitta, upāya, śaktipāta, kāraṇa

## Volume 2 (Āhnika 2-3)

### Passage 51 — Liberation in Life (jīvanmukti) and the Śāmbhava Penetration

**Source:** Tantrāloka Āhnika 3, verses 271cd-277ab (Dyczkowski Vol.2, pp. 395-398)

**Full passage:**
He who enters repeatedly into this state of penetration (samāveśa) into pure Bhairava consciousness free of thought constructs, attains Bhairava's state (bhairavībhāva), otherwise called liberation in this life. The Awakened One should apply the fifty aforementioned kinds of reflective awareness free of thought constructs, here called the Divine Means (śāmbhavopāya). Beholding the Earth principle reflected within one's own nature by means of this reflective awareness free of thought constructs, it assumes Bhairava's nature. This same procedure is applied to all the other principles also, from that of Water onwards right up to the last, where the yogi reaches the supreme principle beyond all obscuring coverings, which pervades all things, is free, omniscient, and conceived to be Śiva. The yogi free of thought constructs, beholding that Śiva also reflected in the noble and uncreated (akalpita) mirror of consciousness, becomes by himself spontaneously Bhairava. From this point onwards we will discuss the nature of this liberation in life, even though this notion that there is a means and a goal persists there in a subtle form.

**Original terms:** jīvanmukti, samāveśa, bhairavībhāva, nirvikalpa, śāmbhavopāya, akalpita

### Passage 52 — Camatkāra: The Aesthetic Rapture of Consciousness

**Source:** Tantrāloka Āhnika 2, commentary (Dyczkowski Vol.2, pp. 41-43)

**Full passage:**
Objects that appear within consciousness, unlike a crystal, evoke emotive, aesthetic responses within them. They are 'savoured by it'. Camatkṛti means the act of a person savouring, that is, the bliss constituted by the full achievement of fruition. Bhuñjana is he who, engaged in savouring a sweet taste or the like, unlike a voracious glutton, lets the experience rest on his cognizing subjectivity, which thus predominates over the objective part. The state of 'wonder' (camatkṛti, camatkāra) in its highest form is thus both the act of 'savouring' the aesthetic object and the plenitude, absolute independence of consciousness devoid of obstacles (avighnā saṃvit), the savouring of which is in the final analysis a savouring of one's own self and one's own bliss, which eludes the mediation of the world of objects, and even goes beyond that relative and momentary transcendence that one has in the aesthetic experience of poetry and theatre.

**Original terms:** camatkṛti, camatkāra, avighnā saṃvit, bhuñjana, svarasa

### Passage 53 — The Doctrine of Anupāya: No Means as the Supreme Path

**Source:** Tantrāloka Āhnika 2, verses 2-7, commentary (Dyczkowski Vol.2, pp. 2-15)

**Full passage:**
The penetration into ultimate reality devoid of means (anupāya) is not understood to be just that there is no means to attain the one reality; it is the Divine Being of the Absolute Deity, Who spontaneously reveals Himself in all His fullness as His supreme act of grace. It is Śiva's supreme act of grace that liberates the individual soul without any effort on its part once and for all. Anupāya does not just mean 'No Means', it also means anuttaropāya, that is the Means to attaining Anuttara, the Absolute. A fundamental difference between Anupāya and any other means is that Anupāya is not a consciously applied means, because the one who attains realisation by this path does not do anything to attain it; it happens by itself, as a matter of course, at some point, possibly quite early in life, as the result of an intense descent of the power of grace (tīvraśaktipāta). In the domain of Anupāya there is no question of 'division' into the agent, instrument, material cause and the like. Thus, he says: 'who is liberated, how and where?'

**Original terms:** anupāya, anuttaropāya, tīvraśaktipāta, anuttara, svātantrya

### Passage 54 — The Doctrine of Reflection (Pratibimbavāda) — Introduction

**Source:** Tantrāloka Āhnika 3, introduction (Dyczkowski Vol.2, pp. 62-70)

**Full passage:**
Abhinava begins his presentation of the Supreme Means, that is, Śāmbhavopāya, with an exposition of the Doctrine of Reflection (pratibimbavāda). This maintains that all that is objectively perceived, whether outer objects, sensory perceptions or inner cogitations, shines within consciousness at one with it as do reflections with the mirror in which they are reflected. But while the reflections (pratibimba) in a mirror are those of an original object (bimba) outside the mirror, these reflections are produced by the inherent power of the mirror of Bhairava's consciousness. The idea that all that exists, with or without outer physically objective content, appears within consciousness, through which it is made manifest, is not new. But for the Buddhist idealists, it serves to illustrate that the world is just an unreal appearance. However, Abhinava, following the lead of Utpaladeva, takes care to stress that the reflections are real. All this mass of phenomena is just a reflection in the Sky of Consciousness, because it possesses the characteristic mark of a reflection, namely, that it cannot manifest separately from the medium of reflection, and that it manifests mingled with other reflections.

**Original terms:** pratibimbavāda, pratibimba, bimba, bhairava, cittākāśa

### Passage 55 — The Fifty Phonemes as Reflective Awareness (Vimarśa)

**Source:** Tantrāloka Āhnika 3, verses 65cd-197ab, summary (Dyczkowski Vol.2, pp. 136-314)

**Full passage:**
The letter A is a measure of the power of consciousness, which does not belong to the domain of Māyā; it transcends hearing, spontaneous, it is the wonder of one's own nature, which is the great sea of consciousness that, without waves, rests in the Great Light. It is present equally in both two phases, initial and final of perception, as the completely full and all-embracing 'I-ness' as the initial outpouring of the power of consciousness and the final reflective awareness of all things. From the perspective of the Trika, the letter A stands for Anuttara and is Akula. Its power is the unfolding perception of the totality of all things in their fundamental nature as the flow of the energies of pure self-awareness i.e. AHAṀ ('I') of Bhairava/Śiva, the supreme perceiver. It encompasses the energies of the letters from A to H, that are as many forms of reflective awareness (parāmarśa), that extend through, even as they produce, the reality levels from Earth to Śiva in a perpetual cycle. The entire phonemic universe is encapsulated in the cycle of the flow of AHAṀ — 'I am'. The Lord united with emission is all things as the fifty energies of the letters.

**Original terms:** parāmarśa, AHAṀ, anuttara, akula, vimarśa, śabdarāśi

### Passage 56 — Bindu and Visarga: The Point and the Emission

**Source:** Tantrāloka Āhnika 3, verses 110cd-146ab (Dyczkowski Vol.2, pp. 215-255)

**Full passage:**
The Point of Śiva (śivabindu) is the Light of the Three Abodes. Visarga is the supreme emission of the Lord, the power of creative effusion. Through the bliss of orgasm (visarga) the male and female principles unite and emit their creative energy. Visarga is the ultimate outpouring of the Absolute — the Heart (hṛdaya) itself. The Heart, which is supreme visarga, is said to be omnipotent. The energies that constitute the fourfold Drop (bindu) are the potential form of phonematic emanation. From the phenomenological perspective of Anuttara Trika, the aspects or energies (kalā) of bindu are the subjective and objective poles of consciousness. When these two points (bindu of subjectivity and objectivity) come together, the supreme emission (visarga) arises. The vitality (vīrya) of Śiva's seed (bindu) is activated by the one-pointed and sustained application of attention (anusaṁdhāna). The threefold emission is understood as Śakti, Prāṇa and Parā Kuṇḍalinī, representing the three fundamental powers of the Absolute's self-projection.

**Original terms:** śivabindu, visarga, hṛdaya, anusaṁdhāna, kalā, śakti, prāṇa, parā kuṇḍalinī

## Volume 3 (Āhnika 4)

### Passage 57 — The Twelve Kālīs and the Consumption of Objectivity

**Source:** Tantrāloka Āhnika 4, verses 146cd-173ab (Dyczkowski Vol.3, pp. 195-252)

**Full passage:**
The Twelve Kālīs are arranged in three groups of four, each group consuming one of the three spheres of empirical reality. The first four Kālīs consume the sphere of objectivity: Sṛṣṭikālī (the Kālī of Creation) emits objectivity, Raktakālī (the Red Kālī) sustains it, Sthitināśakālī (the Kālī of the Destruction of Persistence) withdraws it, and Yamakālī (the Kālī of Restriction) brings it to its inexplicable state. The next four Kālīs consume the sphere of the means of knowledge: Saṁhārakālī withdraws the means, Mṛtyukālī sustains death, Rudrakālī withdraws the means of knowledge through fierceness, and Mārtaṇḍakālī brings them to their inexplicable state as the Kālī of the Sun. The final four consume the sphere of subjectivity itself: Paramārkakālī emits the subject, Kālāgnirudrakālī sustains the subject through the fire of time, Mahākālakālī withdraws the subject through great time, and Mahābhairavaghoracaṇḍakālī is the Horrific and Fierce Kālī of the Great Bhairava who brings subjectivity to its inexplicable consummation. These twelve together constitute the complete cycle of the Krama system's mapping of the dynamics of consciousness.

**Original terms:** kālī, sṛṣṭi, sthiti, saṁhāra, anākhya, krama, tridoṣa

### Passage 58 — The Four Types of Guru: From Spontaneously Enlightened to Created

**Source:** Tantrāloka Āhnika 4, verses 40cd-78ab (Dyczkowski Vol.3, pp. 49-93)

**Full passage:**
The true teacher (gurusatattvaka) is of four types according to the degree to which his enlightenment is spontaneous or dependent on external means. The Spontaneously Enlightened (sāṁsiddhika) or Uncreated (akalpita) teacher is one who has realized his identity with Śiva without relying on any external means, purely by the power of his own innate intuitive insight (pratibhā). The Uncreated-cum-created (akalpitakalpaka) teacher attains realization through a combination of innate insight and some external aid, such as the study of scripture or the guidance of a previous teacher. The Created-cum-uncreated (kalpitākalpita) teacher attains realization primarily through external means but with some degree of spontaneous insight. The Created (kalpita) teacher attains realization purely through external means, such as ritual practice, mantra repetition and formal initiation. Even this lowest type of teacher, however, is empowered by the grace of the Goddess to initiate others, for the Goddess Herself can grant initiation directly. Learning Mantras from books is also accepted as valid if no living teacher is available.

**Original terms:** gurusatattvaka, sāṁsiddhika, akalpita, kalpitākalpita, kalpita, pratibhā

### Passage 59 — The Purification and Empowerment of Thought (Vikalpasaṁskāra)

**Source:** Tantrāloka Āhnika 4, verses 1-7, commentary (Dyczkowski Vol.3, pp. 1-10)

**Full passage:**
The Empowered Means (Śāktopāya) is the purification and empowerment of thought (vikalpasaṁskāra) without outer support. The term 'vikalpa', here translated simply as 'thought', has a complex series of meanings. One interpretation is that all things are known in two ways. One is free of vikalpa (nirvikalpa). It is a direct and correct perception. The other type of perception is 'with vikalpa' (savikalpa). It is a 'differentiated perception' of reality. In daily life, it is functional; indeed, daily life would not be possible without vikalpa. Complete and integral knowledge is free of vikalpa and is liberating. The purpose of the practice of the means to realization in the domain of the Empowered Means is to make vikalpa progressively approach the condition free of vikalpa. A notion (vikalpa) of the sort 'I am Śiva and this universe is my expansion', when purified through sustained reflection, generates another notion of the same nature, which in turn generates another, and so on, progressively deepening until the notional quality falls away and direct realization dawns.

**Original terms:** vikalpa, vikalpasaṁskāra, śāktopāya, nirvikalpa, savikalpa

### Passage 60 — The Vitality of Mantra (Mantravīrya) and the Heart (SAUḤ)

**Source:** Tantrāloka Āhnika 4, verses 181cd-193 (Dyczkowski Vol.3, pp. 279-297)

**Full passage:**
As consciousness is reflective awareness, its spontaneous and perpetually emergent resonance (dhvani) of pure self-awareness, which is the supreme level of Speech, is called the supreme, Great Heart (SAUḤ). The resonance that arises spontaneously is Supreme Speech, which is the Sound (nāda) that is the reflective awareness of supreme consciousness. The god who resides in the chest of living breathing beings spontaneously utters the Mantra not uttered by anybody and that none obstructs. The radiant pulsation (sphuraṇa) of the Heart which is this manifest universe manifests as the Being (S) of the Abode of Emission (H) to the degree in which it penetrates here into the energy of Trika (AU). The wise explain that this Heart (SAUḤ) is the supreme nectar of Bhairava consciousness. Present in the prongs of the Trika Trident, consisting of the powers of will, knowledge and action (AU), it is emitted into Supreme Abode (S). The energies of SAUḤ in the process of emanation, in the forward order, and that of withdrawal, in the reverse order, which in the TĀ is represented by the energies of KHPHREṀ. This is the ceaseless repetition of Mantra which is the reflective awareness of consciousness.

**Original terms:** mantravīrya, SAUḤ, KHPHREṀ, dhvani, nāda, sphuraṇa, hṛdaya, sṛṣṭibīja, saṁhārabīja

## Volume 4 (Āhnika 5-6)

### Passage 61 — The Six Blisses: Progressive Expansion through the Vital Breaths

**Source:** Tantrāloka Āhnika 5, verses 43-52ab, commentary (Dyczkowski Vol.4, pp. 39-53)

**Full passage:**
The six blisses (ṣaḍānanda) are experienced by the progressive expansion of consciousness in consonance with the emergence of the forms of the vital breath. The yogi is initially established in the Heart, the pure subjective aspect alone, which is Innate Bliss (Nijānanda) of repose in the pure emptiness of deep sleep. When the exhaled breath (prāṇa) emerges, he should contemplate the Bliss of Stillness (Nirānanda). When the object of knowledge arises in the form of the inhaled breath (apāna), he should contemplate the Bliss of the Other (Parānanda). Content and alone, having attained the plane of the Equal Breath (samāna), he becomes the Bliss of Brahman (Brahmānanda). Then, intent on devouring the differentiated manifestation of the flux of the means and object of knowledge, resting in the fire of the Rising Breath (udāna), he should contemplate the Great Bliss (Mahānanda). If he attains a state of rest beautifully full of consciousness devoid of limitations, then the Bliss of Consciousness (Cidānanda) dawns. Where there is no limitation, that which shines radiantly everywhere, fed by the Supreme Nectar of freedom, is the abode of Cosmic Bliss (Jagadānanda), wherein meditation or the like finds no direct application.

**Original terms:** nijānanda, nirānanda, parānanda, brahmānanda, mahānanda, cidānanda, jagadānanda, prāṇa, apāna, samāna, udāna

### Passage 62 — The Individual Means (Āṇavopāya) — Outer Support for Purification

**Source:** Tantrāloka Āhnika 5, introduction (Dyczkowski Vol.4, pp. 2-4)

**Full passage:**
Āṇavopāya — the Individual Means — is the category of practice that comprises the widest range of spiritual techniques, including those that involve the body and the external world as supports for purification and realization. All forms of practice in this category of means are based in various ways on the psychophysical organism, that is, the body, the vital breaths and the intellect. These are the three dimensions of individual existence, characterized by the fact that the individual soul identifies with them. The practices associated with these three dimensions are respectively the postures (karaṇa) and offerings of the physical rites, the utterance (uccāra) linked to the breath, and meditation (dhyāna) of the intellect. The first is the most external, the next is subtler, and the last is the subtlest and therefore the most internal of these three forms of practice. Although the Āṇavopāya involves the most effort and is the most indirect of the means, all practices taught in this chapter lead ultimately to Anupāya — No Means — which Abhinava calls here Anuttaropāya.

**Original terms:** āṇavopāya, uccāra, karaṇa, dhyāna, anuttaropāya, prāṇa, buddhi

### Passage 63 — The Heart (Hṛdaya) and the Universal Pulsation (Sāmānyaspanda)

**Source:** Tantrāloka Āhnika 5, commentary (Dyczkowski Vol.4, pp. 50-51)

**Full passage:**
The centre is the Heart, the pulsing core of consciousness. Wave upon wave of its universal pulsation (sāmānyaspanda) arises from the ocean of the Heart and merges back into it. Each is an individual pulsation that forms as the beginning and end of the universal pulsation of which they are a part. The sixth is complete expansion (pūrṇavikāsa), which is experienced as the Cosmic Bliss (jagadānanda) of the universal activity (sāmānyaspanda) of consciousness in the fully liberated state. This is the dynamic aspect of Anuttara, the pure reflective awareness of its own unconditioned nature as the Pure Light (prakāśamātra), which is Anupāya consciousness. The practitioner experiences the state of unfathomable emptiness, remaining lucid and alert, and the breath becomes extremely subtle. All the practices Abhinava teaches in this chapter, although it deals with the Individual Means, lead to Anupāya — No Means — which he calls here Anuttaropāya — the means to attaining Anuttara, the Absolute. The experience of the deployment of the energies takes place in the Śāmbhava state free of thought constructs.

**Original terms:** sāmānyaspanda, jagadānanda, pūrṇavikāsa, anuttara, anupāya, prakāśamātra

### Passage 64 — Kuṇḍalinī and the Vital Breath

**Source:** Tantrāloka Āhnika 5, commentary (Dyczkowski Vol.4, pp. 69-78)

**Full passage:**
The first primary form of the breath is called 'vitality' (prāṇanā) and is the supreme utterance (paroccāra). It is the initial throb of life (jīvana), the bare vital force alone (prāṇanāmātra). This is the first stirring of consciousness as it expands to create, which is the universal exertion of consciousness, variously called vibration (spanda), effulgence (sphuraṇa) and the like. The activity of the vital breath (prāṇa) as the vitality (prāṇanā) and the other macrocosmic counterparts of the breaths of the microcosmic body can be applied as a means to the unfolding realization of the Absolute. The form of Kuṇḍalinī — Śaktikuṇḍalinī — which is the coiled energy of consciousness, is the power of emission as the Kuṇḍalinī of the cosmic breath (prāṇakuṇḍalinī). The upward movement of Kuṇḍalinī, who is situated in the Mūlādhāra, is associated with the purification of the subtle channels. The experiencing subject in a state of pure wonder (camatkāra) experiences the Kuṇḍalinī of consciousness rising through the susumṇā.

**Original terms:** kuṇḍalinī, prāṇanā, paroccāra, spanda, sphuraṇa, śaktikuṇḍalinī, prāṇakuṇḍalinī, camatkāra

### Passage 65 — Cakrodaya: The Dawning of the Wheels of Mantra in the Breath

**Source:** Tantrāloka Āhnika 7, verses 1-5ab (Dyczkowski Vol.5, pp. 2-10)

**Full passage:**
The word 'cakrodaya' literally means 'emergence, arising or dawning of the wheels'. Mantras recited in consonance with the breathing cycle (prāṇacakra), depending on their length, form daily cycles of varying sizes. As the cycle of the breath does its round, the mantra recited in consonance with it arises with each turn from the heart. The dawn of the Sun of exhalation is that of mantra also. The dawning of the cycle of the repetition of mantra (mantracakrodaya) coincides with the dawning of the cycle of the vital breath (prāṇacakrodaya). A waterwheel moves a series of machines for raising water located in front of it, and possesses the varied emergent activity of the machines it sets into operation by its single, unified exertion. Similarly, when mantras arise by the strength of a single one-pointed and continuous application of attention and intention (anusaṁdhāna), the deity of those mantras, because it is at one with them, becomes propitious by virtue of that exertion. The sole purpose of the waterwheel is to make the jars move. Likewise, here (in the case of the arising of mantras), a person who exerts a single, unified effort accomplishes the arising of mantras.

**Full passage:**

### Passage 85 — Mantra System: Japa, Homa, Arcana, and the Six Limbs

**Source:** Tantrāloka Āhnika 30-31, commentary (Dyczkowski Vol.11, pp. 42-45, 165-166)

**Full passage:**
The mantra system of the Trika includes the six limbs (ṣaḍaṅga) of mantra practice: the Heart (hṛdaya), Head (śiras), Topknot (śikhā), Armour (kavaca), Eye (netra), and Weapon (astra). These six limbs represent the qualities of the deity. For example, they are Śiva's omniscience, contentment, beginningless knowledge, freedom, unobstructed power, and infinite power. The classes of mantras are used for specific purposes: NAMAḤ for repetition of mantra (japa), SVĀHĀ for offering of oblation (homa), VAUṢAT for nourishment, HUṀ for expulsion, VAṢAT for pacification (śāntika), and PHAT for black magic (abhicāra). The offering of oblation (homa) into the fire is the external counterpart of the inner offering of one's limited selfhood. The worship (arcana) of the deity is performed through the appropriate mantras combined with the six limbs. Deliberation on the vitality (vīrya) of Mudrā is also described, along with the various occasions when the seals are applied (kālabheda).

### Passage 86 — The Trika Maṇḍala and the Unifying Contemplation

**Source:** Tantrāloka Āhnika 31-33, commentary (Dyczkowski Vol.11, pp. 169-177)

**Full passage:**
The Trika maṇḍala is the sacred diagram that represents the entire cosmic and spiritual hierarchy of the Trika system. The basic form is the Trident (triśūla) combined with the lotus, representing the triadic nature of reality — Śiva, Śakti, and Nara — and their ultimate unity. The gathering together of the deities (ekīkāraḥ) according to the Mālinīvijayottara involves the Six-spoked wheel of six Yoginīs, the Twenty-four spoked wheel made of two groups of twelve Yoginīs, the Sixteen-spoked wheel of sixteen Yoginīs, and the various powerholders of these wheels. The Bhairavas of the wheel with thirty-four spokes and the mantras of the various wheels are all described in detail. The assembly of thirteen Kālīs according to the Krama system is also incorporated. The unifying contemplation of the constituents of the maṇḍala (maṇḍalātmānusaṁdhāna) is the practice whereby the practitioner visualizes the entire maṇḍala within himself, recognizing all the deities as aspects of his own consciousness. The drawing and worship of the Triple Trident maṇḍala is the concluding practice, integrating all the diverse elements of the Trika pantheon into a single unified field of awareness.

**Original terms:** trika maṇḍala, triśūla, ekīkāraḥ, maṇḍalātmānusaṁdhāna, yoginī, bhairava, kālī, krama

### Passage 87 — Mantra KHPHREṀ (Pañcapiṇḍanātha) as the Supreme Withdrawal

**Source:** Tantrāloka Āhnika 30, verses 47-62ab (Dyczkowski Vol.11, pp. 42-44)

**Full passage:**
The Mantra KHPHREṀ (Pañcapiṇḍanātha) is presented as the main form of Mātr̥ṣadbhāva, the supreme seed-mantra of withdrawal (saṁhārabīja). It is the most basic and extensively used monosyllabic Vidyā of Kālasaṁkarṣiṇī. As many as are the accomplishments (siddhi) one aspires to — he who aspires to the most excellent accomplishment of liberation should repeatedly recite this mantra. The penetration of Rudra's power (rudraśaktisamāveśa) is always established in it, for it is said to be the Supreme Power (Parā) in another form. The practitioner should raise this mantra from the heart to the supreme wheel in the End of the Twelve, bending each finger as he passes from wheel to wheel. Thus this mantra has been taught with its secret which, supreme, bestows liberation immediately. The Point (bindu) is at the end, the Moon is S, Fire is Ṛ, the Peak is KṢ, Fire is H, and so on. This mantra, along with SAUḤ (the heart-mantra of emanation) and KṢMRYŪM (another seed-mantra), constitutes the three fundamental seed-syllables of the Trika system.

**Original terms:** KHPHREṀ, pañcapiṇḍanātha, mātr̥ṣadbhāva, saṁhārabīja, kālasaṁkarṣiṇī, rudraśaktisamāveśa, SAUḤ, KṢMRYŪM

### Passage 88 — Siddhi and Accomplishment Through the Mantras

**Source:** Tantrāloka Āhnika 30, commentary (Dyczkowski Vol.11, pp. 44-45, 64)

**Full passage:**
The accomplishment of mantras (mantrasiddhi) is the result of sustained practice combined with the grace of the teacher and the deity. The mantras received through the lineage are effective because they carry the power of transmission. Abhinava received these mantras through a direct oral transmission through the Śrīṣaṇṭati lineage. The mantras of the Trika system, especially the seed-mantras SAUḤ, KHPHREṀ, and KṢMRYŪM, are not merely sounds but are the very forms of the deities they represent. When recited with the proper intention (bhāvanā), pronunciation, and visualization, they generate the presence of the deity and bestow both worldly accomplishments and liberation (nirvāṇa). The sixty-two-verse section (30/47-62ab) is a direct quote from the Mālinīvijayottara, confirming the authority of the scriptural tradition. Deliberation on the vitality (vīrya) of Mudrā and the various occasions when the seals are applied are also described, along with the mantra usages for the sacraments (saṁskāra) and initiation.

**Original terms:** siddhi, mantrasiddhi, bhāvanā, nirvāṇa, śrīṣaṇṭati, saṁskāra, vīrya
