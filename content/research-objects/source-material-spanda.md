# Source Material — Spanda (The Divine Pulsation)

**Primary sources:** Tantrāloka Āhnika 5-6 (Dyczkowski Vol.4), Āhnika 7 (Dyczkowski Vol.5), Āhnika 4 (Dyczkowski Vol.3), Spanda Kārikā (Kallata)

---

## Passage S.1 — Spanda as the inner exertion of consciousness

**Source:** Tantrāloka Āhnika 5, Vol.4 pp. 5-6 (on TĀ 5/18cd)

**Context:** Abhinavagupta is defining the vital breath (prāṇa) and its relationship to consciousness. He distinguishes between the physical breath and the inner pulsation that animates it.

**Full passage:**

"It is called 'vitality'. It is the inner exertion (udyoga) which impels and fuels the activity of mind, senses and body. Also called 'life' (jīvana), it is just the bare vital force alone.

'This vitalizing power of the breath, which is the will of the inner exertion of consciousness, is variously called vibration (spanda), effulgence (sphurattā), repose, the living being (jīva), the Heart and intuition. This, the activity of the vital breath, lays hold of the body in five ways, as the exhaled breath (prāṇa) and the rest. It is because of this that the body appears to be full of consciousness.'

The particular modalities of the vital force, exhalation (prāṇa) and the rest, are meant here, and so it is rightly said that 'the utterance of the vital breath consists of the five modalities'. Its nature will be described further ahead."

**Original terms:**
- *spanda* — vibration, pulsation
- *sphurattā* — effulgence, radiant pulse
- *udyoga* — inner exertion
- *prāṇanā* — vitality

**Analysis:** This passage establishes spanda as the *inner exertion* of consciousness that precedes and animates the physical breath. It is not merely a phenomenon that occurs *in* consciousness — it is the very activity of consciousness itself. Abhinavagupta gives it multiple names (vibration, effulgence, repose, the living being, the Heart, intuition), indicating that spanda is a fundamental category that can be approached from many angles.

## Passage S.2 — The universal pulsation (sāmānyaspanda) as Cosmic Bliss

**Source:** Tantrāloka Āhnika 5, Vol.4 pp. 39-40 (on the six blisses)

**Context:** Abhinavagupta describes the expansion of the breath from individual to universal, culminating in the realization of the universal pulsation.

**Full passage:**

"The expansion of the breath from individual to universal corresponds to that aspect of consciousness. Each one is experienced as a form of bliss. The sixth is complete expansion (pūrṇavikāsa), which is experienced as the Cosmic Bliss (jagadānanda) of the universal activity (sāmānyaspanda) of consciousness in the fully liberated state. This is the dynamic aspect of Anuttara, the pure reflective awareness of its own unconditioned nature as the Pure Light (prakāśamātra), which is Anupāya consciousness."

**Original terms:**
- *sāmānyaspanda* — universal pulsation, universal activity
- *jagadānanda* — cosmic bliss
- *pūrṇavikāsa* — complete expansion
- *prakāśamātra* — pure light

**Analysis:** The universal pulsation (sāmānyaspanda) is not merely a philosophical concept but an experience — specifically, the experience of cosmic bliss. It is the dynamic aspect of the Absolute (Anuttara) and is identical with the state of Anupāya, the "no-means" realization where no practice is needed because the goal is already fully present.

## Passage S.3 — The Heart as the pulsing core of consciousness

**Source:** Tantrāloka Āhnika 5, Vol.4 p. 84 (on centring practice)

**Context:** Abhinavagupta describes the practice of centring between two states as a means to access the universal pulsation.

**Full passage:**

"This involves paying attention to the centre in between two thoughts, for example, one step and another when walking, one perception and the next, the space between one object and another. In short, the centre between any two things, especially, for this practice, between two breaths. The centre is the Heart, the pulsing core of consciousness. Wave upon wave of its universal pulsation (sāmānyaspanda) arises from the ocean of the Heart and merges back into it. Each is an individual pulsation that forms as the beginning and end of the universal pulsation of which they are a part. If the aspirant has practiced attention to the centre when awake, when he goes to sleep, he will do so lucidly, that is, maintaining awareness that he is making the transition from waking to sleep. He will be aware that the junction is, as Swami Lakshmanjoo puts it, 'only a gate, the entrance to turya'. This is Nijānanda (Swami Lakshmanjoo: 'the bliss of your own Self')."

**Original terms:**
- *sāmānyaspanda* — universal pulsation
- *turya* — the fourth state (beyond waking, dream, sleep)
- *nijānanda* — the bliss of one's own self

**Analysis:** This is one of the most practical passages in the Tantraloka on spanda. The universal pulsation is not a theory — it is accessible through the simple practice of attending to the space between two things. The Heart (hṛdaya) is not the physical organ but the pulsing core of consciousness itself. Each individual pulsation is a wave on the ocean of the universal pulsation.

## Passage S.4 — The Heart as the one all-embracing reality

**Source:** Tantrāloka Āhnika 5, Vol.4 p. 93 (commentary on MVV 2/41cd)

**Context:** Abhinavagupta clarifies that the Heart is not a subtle-body centre but the ultimate reality itself.

**Full passage:**

"The Heart meant here is not one of the centres in the subtle body. It is the one all-embracing reality which pulses like a heart with the vitality of consciousness, generating and withdrawing all things through and as its universal vibration (sāmānyaspanda)."

**Original terms:**
- *hṛdaya* — heart (as ultimate reality)
- *sāmānyaspanda* — universal vibration

**Analysis:** A concise and powerful definition. The Heart is not located anywhere in the body — it IS the body of reality, pulsing with consciousness. The universal vibration is not an effect produced by the Heart; it IS the Heart's own activity of generating and withdrawing all things.

## Passage S.5 — The plane of the Fish: universal pulsation

**Source:** Tantrāloka Āhnika 5, Vol.4 pp. 64-65 (on TĀ 5/57cd-58ab)

**Context:** Abhinavagupta describes the "Belly of the Fish" as a metaphor for the state of universal pulsation.

**Full passage:**

"'There on the plane of the Upper Kuṇḍalinī is emission, beautiful with the noble pulsation of consciousness. The yogi should rest there, on the plane of the Belly of the Fish.'

This is so because 'there' on the plane of the Transmental, 'the noble pulsation of consciousness', that is, the essence of the outpouring of one's own nature (svātmocchalattā), which is its radiant pulse (sphuraṇa) as none other than itself, and so is the 'beautiful' and desirable 'emission'.

'The plane of the Belly of the Fish is described as that of the universal pulsation of consciousness (sāmānyaspanda). It is likened to the throb of the belly of a fish when it is pulled out of the water. Similarly, individual consciousness at this level, pulled out of the waters of transmigratory existence, pulses in unison with the rhythm of universal consciousness.

The pulsation of the genitals of a female donkey when she is having an orgasm suggests that this pulsation is also experienced during sexual climax, although it is fleeting for those who are not accomplished Yogis.

Abhinava identifies the plane of the Belly of the Fish with the activity of transcendental pure consciousness, which is the Absolute (Anuttara). Thus he writes: 'The essence of consciousness is the Lordship of its unlimited and completely full freedom.'"

**Original terms:**
- *sāmānyaspanda* — universal pulsation
- *svātmocchalattā* — outpouring of one's own nature
- *sphuraṇa* — radiant pulse
- *anuttara* — the Absolute, the Unsurpassable

**Analysis:** Two striking metaphors for the same reality. The fish pulled from water — individual consciousness recognizing its identity with universal consciousness, pulsing in unison. The orgasmic pulsation — the same spanda experienced in sexual climax, normally fleeting but capable of becoming permanent in the realised yogi. This is the Tantric refusal to separate spiritual experience from bodily experience.

## Passage S.6 — Spanda as mantra-vīrya (the vitality of mantra)

**Source:** Tantrāloka Āhnika 4 (Dyczkowski Vol.3), from ro-tantraloka-twelve-kalis p_007

**Context:** Abhinavagupta explains why mantras are effective — they access the universal pulse.

**Full passage:**

"The universal pulse of the Heart of consciousness is the vitality of Mantra (mantravīrya). The self-awareness from which all things have flowed forth and dissolved away within the Heart of consciousness, present in the beginning and end of perception, is called according to the Spanda teachings the universal vibration of consciousness (sāmānyaspanda), which is the outpouring of consciousness within one's own nature. This vibration is that subtle motion which is the non-different radiance, the wave of the ocean of consciousness. Mantra is effective because it is one with this pulsation of consciousness."

**Original terms:**
- *mantravīrya* — the vitality/power of mantra
- *sāmānyaspanda* — universal vibration
- *spandanātmatā* — the dynamic pulsing nature

**Analysis:** This passage directly connects spanda to mantra practice. A mantra is not powerful because of correct pronunciation or ritual purity — it is powerful because it is identical with the universal pulsation of consciousness. When a mantra "awakens," it is because it has attuned itself to spanda itself.

## Passage S.7 — Spanda Karika 1.1: The Wheel of Powers

**Source:** Spanda Kārikā 1.1 (Kallata, 9th cent.), tr. Christopher Wallis

**Full passage:**

> *yasyonmeṣa-nimeṣābhyāṃ jagataḥ pralayodayau*
> *taṃ śakti-cakra-vibhava-prabhavaṃ śaṅkaraṃ stumaḥ*
>
> "We praise the Beneficent Source of the all-pervasive activity of the Wheel of the Powers of Consciousness, the expansion of which is the arising of the world of one's experience and the contraction of which is its dissolution."

Kallata's auto-commentary: "This reverent invocation teaches that the cause of the creation and dissolution of the world of your experience is none other than your own true nature, which is Divine; and that this true nature, whose body is Consciousness, gives rise to the state of sovereignty over the Wheel of Powers."

## Passage S.8 — Spanda Karika 2.26: How mantras work

**Source:** Spanda Kārikā 2.26 (Kallata), tr. Christopher Wallis

**Full passage:**

> *ṭadākramya balaṃ mantrāḥ sarvajñabalaśālinaḥ*
> *pravartante 'dhikārāya karaṇānīva dehinām*
>
> "Mantras, attaining that innate power, become suffused with Divine force, enabling them to perform their respective functions, like the senses of embodied beings."

Kallata: "Mantras, having inhabited and attained that innate power — which is unveiled Awareness — are endowed with that divine power which is omniscience and omnipotence, and thus perform their functions of bestowing grace and so on. The senses of an embodied being function likewise, through accessing the innate power of awareness."

## Passage S.9 — Spanda Karika 3.33-34: The awakened being

**Source:** Spanda Kārikā 3.33-34 (Kallata), tr. Christopher Wallis

**Full passage:**

> *yathecchābhyarthito dhātā jāgrato 'rthān hṛdi sthitān*
> *somasūryodayaṃ kṛtvā sampādayati dehinaḥ*
>
> "The One who ordains and orders all things, when entreated with an impulse of pure desire, causes the 'moon' and 'sun' to rise and bestows those goals residing in the heart of an embodied being who is awake."

> *tathā svapne 'py abhīṣṭārthān praṇayasyānatikramāt*
> *nityaṃ sphuṭataraṃ madhye sthito 'vaśyaṃ prakāśayet*
>
> "In the same way, even while dreaming, that One residing in the Center always clearly reveals all desired things and goals, because that One never disregards any loving entreaty."

## Passage S.10 — The rhythm of sensory perception is spanda

**Source:** Tantrāloka Āhnika 7 (Dyczkowski Vol.5), from ro-tantraloka-mantra-breath p_005

**Full passage:**

"The rhythm of sensory activity, the pulse of conjunction and separation of the senses with their objects, is essentially the pulse — spanda — of consciousness. That is its nature and root cause, intimately connected to the movement of the breath. Until another pulse of cognitive consciousness arises, a single representational ideation abides as a unified whole, conceiving the diversity of manifest reality. The pulse (spanda) of cognitive consciousness is essentially its movement from one object to another, which is thus conjoined and disjoined in the sphere of sensorial cognitive consciousness. The absence of one entails the absence of the other."
