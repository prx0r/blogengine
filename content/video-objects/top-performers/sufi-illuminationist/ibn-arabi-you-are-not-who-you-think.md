## Transcript

There is a secret.
It's so close that if you reached for it, you'd miss it. It's so present that if you searched the entire universe, you would not find anything closer.
It is not hidden in the mountains of Tibet, nor locked in the catacombs of Cairo.
It is not in the stars, though the stars speak of it.
It is not in the oceans, though the oceans reflect it.
This secret is you and everything that is not you and everything in between.

Over eight centuries ago, a man walked the streets of Murcia, Andalusia.
His name was Muhyiddin Ibn Arabi. Some called him Sheikh al-Akbar, the greatest master. Others called him dangerous, even heretical — because he spoke of something so absolute, so overwhelming that it shook the pillars of conventional thought.
He called it *wahdat al-wujud* — the unity of being — and his words still echo like a secret whispered from eternity into the ear of the present moment.

Ibn Arabi said, "There is nothing in existence but God. Yet you do not see Him because He is veiled by His manifestation in created things."

The idea was not his invention. It is written in the Quran: "He is the First and the Last, the Manifest and the Hidden" (57:3).
But Ibn Arabi dared to follow that verse to its final consequence. If God is the First, nothing before Him. If God is the Last, nothing after Him. If God is the Manifest, nothing outside Him. If God is the Hidden, nothing beyond Him.

Then what is left for you? For me? For the mountains, the oceans, the dust in the air?

He would answer: "We are appearances — shadows upon the face of the One Light."

Imagine the ocean. It is vast, deep, immeasurable.
Now imagine waves on that ocean. Each wave has a form, a height, a motion. You can name it — "this wave," "that wave." But in truth, each wave is only the ocean appearing in a particular shape. If the ocean withdrew, the wave would vanish instantly.

Ibn Arabi said: "Being is one. The many are the loci of manifestation for the One Being. Their existence is His existence. Without it, they would not exist for a single instant."

In another place, he paints a different picture. The cosmos is the mirror of God, and man is the polished face of that mirror — in which God contemplates His Names.

Do you understand what that means?
It means you are not just seeing reality. You are part of the instrument through which the Divine sees Himself.
Every creature, every grain of sand, every star — a facet in the infinite mirror of being.
You are not separate from the mirror.
You are a reflection inside the reflection, thinking yourself independent.

If this is true, then the separation you feel — between you and others, between you and the world, between you and God — is the greatest illusion ever staged.

Ibn Arabi would tell you: "The seeker's journey is not about finding God out there. It is about recognizing that there is nowhere you can stand where God is absent."

But here is the paradox. He is so present you can miss Him entirely.

The doctrine came to be known as *wahdat al-wujud* — the Unity of Being. The phrase was not his own but distilled from his writings. And here is what it means:

God alone truly exists (*al-wujud al-haqq*). Everything else exists only by borrowing existence from Him. Creation is the infinite self-disclosure (*tajalli*) of God. Multiplicity is real in form but unreal in essence.

Think of the sun. It shines and its light fills the world. The sunlight on your face is not the sun. Yet without the sun, it would not exist for a single instant.
Similarly, the existence you feel right now is not yours. It is God's existence flowing through you.

So why don't we see this clearly? Why do we feel separate, isolated, cut off from the One Being?

Ibn Arabi said: "He is manifest in every graspable sense and He is hidden. The veil is nothing other than His manifestation" (*Fusus al-Hikam*).

That's the cruel joke. The very thing that hides God from you is God Himself. The forms of the world are veils, yet they are also revelations. The Beloved hides behind His own face.

A Sufi once asked a wise man: "How can I find God?"
The wise man said: "You cannot find what has never been lost."
The seeker frowned: "But I feel far from Him."
"That," said the wise man, "is because you see yourself."

Here is where Ibn Arabi's genius shines. He never collapses Creator and creation into one flat sameness. He says: "The servant is the Lord and the Lord is the servant, if only you knew. Yet the servant remains a servant and the Lord remains the Lord" (*Fusus al-Hikam*).

Unity does not erase distinction.
A wave is still a wave, even as it is nothing but the ocean.

If you understood this — truly understood it — you could never hate another. For in hating them, you would hate the face of the One who animates them.
You could never despair completely. For even in despair, it is God who holds you.
You could never be arrogant, for the breath you call yours belongs entirely to Him.

Not everyone welcomed Ibn Arabi's words.
Some accused him of *hulul* (incarnationism) or *ittihad* (union in the literal sense). They feared he blurred the line between Creator and creation.
Later Sufis like Ahmad Sirhindi offered a gentler framing — *wahdat al-shuhud* (Unity of Witnessing) — emphasizing that the unity is perceived rather than ontologically absolute.

But Ibn Arabi did not flinch. He would say: "When you know yourself, you will know your Lord — not through yourself, but through Him."

In *Al-Futuhat*, Ibn Arabi even dismantles our concept of time. If God is the First and the Last, then all moments are present to Him at once. The past is not gone, the future is not waiting. Everything unfolds in a single eternal Now — God's Now. And in that Now, the entire cosmos is eternally dependent on His Being.

Words can only take you so far.
*Wujud* is not a theory to memorize. It is a state to taste.

Close your eyes.
Feel your breath.
Who is breathing you?
Let the thought dissolve.
Listen for the hum that holds the world together.
Ibn Arabi would say that hum is the Name of God spoken in every atom.

The secret you were seeking was never far.
It was never even near.
It was only ever the One (*al-Ahad*) wearing the mask of you, playing the role of me, staging the grand drama of the world.

Now you know: the waves are the ocean. The mirror is the Face. The seeker is the Sought. And the secret is closer than your breath.

Let Ibn Arabi have the last say:

"So the Reality is manifest in every created thing, and He is hidden in it by the fact that the thing exists in itself. He is the Outward and the Inward. He is the First and the Last. And there is nothing in existence but Him."
