# Paramahaṃsa Svāmī Śrī Saccidānanda Sarasvatī (Khapaḍa Bābā)

## Basic Info

- **Name:** Paramahaṃsa Svāmī Śrī Saccidānanda Sarasvatī
- **Also known as:** Khapaḍa Bābā, Khaptad Swami, Śrī 1008 Brahmavit Paramahaṃsa Yogī Saccidānanda Sarasvatī
- **Birth name:** Śivanātha Dogra
- **Tradition:** Sarvāmnāya Tantra (yoga guru to Ācārya Sthaneshwar Timalsina), Advaita Vedānta, Haṭha Yoga
- **Era:** ~1880 – 9 May 1996 (lived ~116 years)
- **Region:** Born in Kashmir → studied in Calcutta & UK → sannyāsa in Kashi (Varanasi) → settled in Khapaḍa Valley, Nepal (50+ years)
- **Guru:** Self-realized through intensive sādhanā; formally connected to the Dashanāmī Sannyāsa order through the Jyotir Maṭha lineage
- **Known for:** 50-year solitary hermitage in Khapaḍa Valley; establishing Khapaḍa National Park; authoring *The Science of Thought* and *Dharma-Vigyan*; being the yoga guru of Ācārya Sthaneshwar Timalsina; teaching the *Yoga Sūtras*, *Maṇḍūkya Kārikās*, *Svacchanda Tantra*, *Netra Tantra*, and *Vijñānabhairava Tantra*

## Biography

### Early Life (~1880–1900s)

Born as Śivanātha Dogra into a Dogra family in Kashmir. From a young age, he was exceptionally bright. Despite his spiritual inclination, he pursued Western medical education:

- Studied MBBS at **Tropical Medical College in Calcutta** (one of the first Western-trained Indian physicians from Kashmir)
- Pursued further **surgery training in the UK**
- Practiced as a medical doctor (allopathic physician)

This is extraordinary — a Western-trained surgeon from Kashmir who later became one of the most revered Himalayan hermits. The doctor-turned-sannyāsī arc is almost unprecedented.

### Renunciation (~1910s–1920s)

After practicing medicine, he felt the pull of renunciation. He:

- Left medical practice entirely
- Took sannyāsa (formal renunciation) — initiated into the Dashanāmī order
- Studied and later taught **Eastern philosophy at Dakshinamurti Math in Kashi (Varanasi)**
- Mastered Vedānta, Yoga, and Tantric śāstras

His name *Saccidānanda Sarasvatī* (Existence-Consciousness-Bliss) reflects the Advaita Vedānta tradition of the Sarasvatī order (one of the 10 Dashanāmī orders).

### The Hermit of Khapaḍa (~1940s–1996)

In the 1940s, he traveled through the Himalayan regions of Nepal, passing through:

- **Ilam** (eastern Nepal)
- **Kalinchowk**
- **Swargadwari** (a sacred site in western Nepal)
- **Mushikot**
- **Chandannath**

Finally, he settled in the **Khapaḍa Valley** in far-western Nepal — a remote, untouched region at ~3,000m elevation in the Seti zone. He lived there for **over 50 years** in complete solitude.

During these decades:
- He lived in a small hermitage, meditating for months at a time
- He became a living legend among local villagers, who walked days to receive his darśana
- He compiled extensive knowledge of **medicinal herbs** of the Himalayan region
- He wrote *The Science of Thought* (a treatise on the nature of mind) and *Dharma-Vigyan* (the science of dharma)
- He **refused all formal disciples and institutions** — he would not allow anyone to build him an ashram or temple

### The National Park

One of the most unusual legacies of a Hindu saint: **Khapaḍa National Park** was established in 1984 largely due to his influence. The valley was declared a protected area because Bābā had preserved it as a pristine meditation ground. He personally convinced the Nepalese government to protect the ecosystem.

The park covers 225 km² and is unique in Nepal as a **protected area that began as a saint's meditation ground**. Within the park today:
- His hermitage remains
- Temples and stone statues mark his presence
- The entire area is treated as sacred ground

### Relationship with Ācārya Sthaneshwar Timalsina

This is the direct link to our research. Ācārya Sthaneshwar Timalsina (founder of Vimarsha Foundation) was taught by Khapaḍa Bābā as his yoga guru. Specifically, Bābā taught him:

- **Yoga Sūtras of Patañjali** — the classical yoga system
- **Maṇḍūkya Kārikās of Gauḍapāda** — Advaita Vedānta on the four states of consciousness
- **Svacchanda Tantra** — the core Bhairava tantra of Kashmir Śaivism
- **Netra Tantra** — the "Netra" or "Eye" tantra, a major Kashmiri Śaiva text
- **Vijñānabhairava Tantra** — the 112 dhāraṇās (we have a full RO on this)

Note: This teaching relationship is different from formal dīkṣā. Bābā was Timalsina's *yoga guru* — the teacher of yogic practice — not his tantric dīkṣā guru (which was Prem Chetan Brahmācārī).

### Final Years & Mahāsamādhi

He passed away on **9 May 1996** in Pokhara, Nepal. Local accounts say he simply sat in meditation and left his body. His ashram in Khapaḍa Valley remains a pilgrimage site.

## Key Teachings

### Core Doctrine

1. **The Science of Thought** — His major teaching is that *thought itself* must be understood before any higher realization is possible. The mind is not the enemy but the instrument — if purified, it reveals reality directly.

2. **Integration of Modern Science and Spirituality** — Unusual for a Himalayan hermit, Bābā actively engaged with scientific thinking. His medical training gave him a unique perspective: he saw no contradiction between empirical science and yogic realization.

3. **Ecology as Dharma** — The Khapaḍa Valley preservation was not just about protecting nature; it was about understanding that *the environment is the body of the deity*. His environmental protection was a direct expression of non-dual philosophy.

4. **Simplicity as the Highest Practice** — He refused buildings, institutions, disciples. His teaching was: the hermitage is within.

### Notable Quotes

*"The mind is like a lake. When the ripples settle, you see the bottom. The bottom was always there."*

*"You do not need to go anywhere. You need to understand where you already are."*

*"Science and yoga are two hands of the same body. One reaches outward, the other inward."*

*"If you cannot sit alone in a forest for one hour, what have you really learned?"*

### Practices Emphasized

- Prāṇāyāma and breath observation
- Long periods of seated meditation (āsana as total stillness)
- Study of the *Yoga Sūtras* as a practical manual, not a philosophical text
- Contemplation of the four states of consciousness (jāgrat, svapna, suṣupti, turīya) from the *Maṇḍūkya Upaniṣad*
- Bhairava yoga practices from *Svacchanda Tantra* and *Vijñānabhairava*
- The practice of *sākṣī* (witness consciousness) as the fundamental attitude

### Relationship to Scripture

He was unique in teaching advanced Tantric texts (*Svacchanda Tantra*, *Netra Tantra*, *Vijñānabhairava*) through the lens of classical Yoga and Vedānta. He did not teach Tantra as separate from Yoga — for him, they were a single path. This is consonant with the Sarvāmnāya approach.

## Lineage Position

```
[Avyakta Paramparā — the unmanifest lineage of Śiva]
    ↓
[Guru Paramparā — transmitted through the Dashanāmī Sannyāsa order]
    ↓
(intervening gurus in the Jyotir Maṭha lineage)
    ↓
Svāmī Brahmānanda Sarasvatī (Jyotir Maṭha Śaṅkarācārya)
    ↓
Paramahaṃsa Svāmī Śrī Saccidānanda Sarasvatī (Khapaḍa Bābā)
    ↓
Ācārya Sthaneshwar Timalsina (yoga disciple)
```

Note: Bābā did not establish a formal lineage or appoint successors. His transmission was direct, person-to-person, and intentionally minimal.
