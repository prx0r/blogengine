## SEG 2 — The Problem of One and Many (1:00-2:30)

**Visual:** K3 (Linga in still water — reflection, ripples, settling back to glass)
  → S3 (Seeker looks up)

**Narration:**
"Every spiritual tradition faces the same problem. If ultimate reality is one, how do we explain the many? If God is transcendent, how is God also immanent? The Trika — which literally means 'the three' — answers with a trinity of goddesses. Not three gods. Not three separate beings. Three aspects of one consciousness. The Trika teaches that reality is structured as a triad: the subject who knows, the object that is known, and the act of knowing that connects them."

**Audio:** Single bell. Quiet.

*LTX clips used: K3, S3*

## SEG 3 — Parā — The Supreme Goddess (2:30-4:00)

**Visual:** AC1 (Emanation — gold light flowing downward from a single point in three streams)
  → S5 (Seeker kneels before light)
  → AT2 (Incense smoke)

**Narration:**
"Parā means 'supreme.' She is the transcendent aspect of consciousness — pure awareness before it takes any form. She is the light of consciousness itself, what the tradition calls prakāśa. She is not a person. She is the ground of all experience. In the Tantrāloka, Abhinavagupta invokes her as 'the dynamic creativity of consciousness, the yogic consort of Bhairava.' She is the silence before the first thought, the presence that remains when everything else falls away. To rest in Parā is to rest as awareness itself."

**Audio:** Quiet. No effects.

*LTX clips used: AC1, S5, AT2*

## SEG 4 — Aparā — The Immanent Power (4:00-5:30)

**Visual:** GC20 (Apara as lightning in monsoon clouds — jagged white-violet bolt, dancing goddess within)
  → AC2 (Shiva-Shakti — crimson thread spiraling around gold point)

**Narration:**
"Aparā means 'not supreme' — but that does not mean lesser. She is the immanent power of consciousness, the world itself experienced as real. She is the dynamic, creative aspect — the lightning that flashes in the dark sky, the energy that builds worlds, the intelligence that moves your breath. If Parā is the ocean, Aparā is the wave. If Parā is the screen, Aparā is the movie. She is the power of consciousness to appear AS the world, to take form, to become everything you see, touch, and know."

**Audio:** Subtle thunder rumble at lightning visual (LTX T2A). Then silence.

*LTX clips used: GC20, AC2*

## SEG 5 — Parāparā — The Both/Neither (5:30-7:00)

**Visual:** AC2 (Shiva-Shakti — continue from previous, the crimson thread and gold point in dynamic balance)
  → S3 (Seeker looks up)

**Narration:**
"Parāparā is the middle goddess. Her name means 'supreme-not-supreme' — she is both and neither. She is the power that mediates between transcendence and immanence. Without Parāparā, there would be a gap between the absolute and the relative — they would be two separate things. But Parāparā is the bridge, the tensile arc of awareness itself, the recognition that the supreme and the worldly are not two different realities. She is the heartbeat of consciousness, the throb that connects silence to sound, awareness to experience. She is spanda — the divine vibration."

**Audio:** Heartbeat pulse (subtle, LTX T2A). Silence.

*LTX clips used: AC2, S3*

## SEG 6 — The Trident Throne — Knower, Knowing, Known (7:00-8:30)

**Visual:** GC8 (Trident throne — lotus, trident, three prongs, goddess Parā above)
  → K4 (Shiva-Parvati carving)

**Narration:**
"These three goddesses sit on a trident throne — the triśūlābja-maṇḍala. The trident has three prongs: the knower, the act of knowing, and the known. Each prong corresponds to one of the three goddesses. Parā is the knower — pure subjectivity. Aparā is the known — all that appears. Parāparā is the act of knowing itself — the dynamic connection between them. The throne rises from an eight-petaled lotus, which represents the manifest world. And above the trident sits Parā Devī herself — not as a separate goddess, but as the unity that contains all three."

**Audio:** Bell tone at trident description.

*LTX clips used: GC8, K4*

## SEG 7 — The Lotus-and-Trident Mandala (8:30-10:00)

**Visual:** GC13 (Trident mandala icon — geometric, luminous, rotating slowly)
  → GC5 (Twelve Kalis — wheel of goddess-figures, rings rotating in opposite directions)

**Narration:**
"The lotus-and-trident is a mandala — a map of reality for meditation. The lotus petals are the powers of consciousness: will, knowledge, action. The trident is the threefold structure of every experience. To meditate on this mandala is to recognize that every experience you have — seeing a tree, hearing a sound, feeling a thought — is structured by the Trika. Every moment of awareness is a Trident Throne. The knower, the known, and the knowing — always present, always three, never separate."

**Audio:** Ambient drone.

*LTX clips used: GC13, GC5*

## SEG 8 — The Three Upāyas and the Three Goddesses (10:00-11:30)

**Visual:** AC3 (36 tattvas descending — nodes lighting in sequence, amber pulses)
  → S2 (Seeker sits in meditation)
  → S5 (Seeker kneels before light)

**Narration:**
"The three goddesses also correspond to the three upāyas — the means to liberation. The individual means, āṇavopāya, works with Aparā — with form, with practice, with the world. The empowered means, śāktopāya, works with Parāparā — with energy, with thought, with the middle. And the divine means, śāmbhavopāya, IS Parā — it is recognition itself, sudden and complete. The three upāyas are the three goddesses manifested as paths. You are always on one of them. The question is: which one are you practicing right now?"

**Audio:** Soft breath at the question.

*LTX clips used: AC3, S2, S5*

## SEG 9 — The Goddess as Dynamic Power of Consciousness (11:30-13:00)

**Visual:** AC6 (Spanda pulses — blue-white point pulsing, concentric rings emanating)
  → AT4 (Stars pulse in dark sky)

**Narration:**
"In the Trika, the goddess is not a passive object of worship. She is the dynamic power of consciousness itself — the śakti that makes awareness aware of itself. Without her, Śiva would be inactive, luminous but static. With her, reality pulses, creates, recognizes itself. The goddess is not other than consciousness. She is consciousness in motion. This is why the Trika is not a philosophy but a living practice — because consciousness is not a thing to be understood. It is a power to be recognized."

**Audio:** Quiet. Stars pulse.

*LTX clips used: AC6, AT4*

## SEG 10 — Parā as the Supreme Goddess of the Trika (13:00-14:30)

**Visual:** GC8 (Return to the trident throne — Parā luminous above, mandala rotating)
  → AC5 (Liberation expands — rings of gold light emanating, filling the frame)
  → S3 (Seeker looks up)

**Narration:**
"Parā is the supreme goddess of the Trika. But this does not mean she is above the other two in a hierarchy. She contains them. Parāparā and Aparā are not different from Parā — they are Parā appearing in two modes. The Trika is not a tritheism. It is an analysis of one reality. The word Trika means 'the three,' but what it really means is 'the three-in-one.' To know Parā is to know that the three are never separate. To know Parā is to know yourself."

**Audio:** Long pause. Silence.

*LTX clips used: GC8, AC5, S3*

## SEG 11 — How to Practice with the Three Goddesses (14:30-15:30)

**Visual:** S2 (Seeker sits in meditation)
  → AT2 (Incense smoke spirals)
  → BR3 (Shadowed guru Kashmir)

**Narration:**
"How do you practice the Trika? In any moment, notice the three. Notice what you are aware of — that is Aparā. Notice that you are aware — that is Parā. Notice the awareness itself that connects them — that is Parāparā. The three are always present. The practice is not to create them. The practice is to recognize them. When you recognize the three as one, you have recognized the Trika. And in that recognition, you are Parā."

**Audio:** Bell. Silence.

*LTX clips used: S2, AT2, BR3*
