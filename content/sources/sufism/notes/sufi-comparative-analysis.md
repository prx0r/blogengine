# Sufi Comparative Analysis: Corbin, Suhrawardi, Ibn Arabi, Avicenna

## Part 1: Corbin's Mundus Imaginalis

### The 'Alam al-Mithal (Imaginal World)

Corbin's central contribution is the recovery of the *mundus imaginalis* ('alam al-mithal) as a distinct ontological region — neither matter nor spirit, but a concrete spiritual universe of autonomous Forms and Figures. As he states in *Man of Light* (p. 42-43):

> "Between the world of pure spiritual Lights (*Luces victoriales*, the world of the 'Mothers' in the terminology of Ishraq) and the sensory universe, at the boundary of the ninth Sphere (the Sphere of Spheres) there opens a *mundus imaginalis* which is a concrete spiritual world of archetype-Figures, apparitional Forms, Angels of species and of individuals."

This is the world Sohravardi calls the "eighth clime" (*keshvar*), the mystical Earth of Hurqalya with its emerald cities, situated at the summit of the cosmic mountain Qaf (p. 43). Crucially, this is "where what is bodily becomes spirit and what is spiritual acquires a body" (p. 44).

Corbin insists this is neither abstraction nor fantasy: "The inability to conceive of a concrete suprasensory reality results from giving too much importance to sensory reality... the universe which in Sohravardi's neo-Zoroastrian Platonism is called the *mundus imaginalis* is a concrete spiritual universe. It is most certainly not a world of concepts, paradigms, and universals" (p. 5).

This maps directly to:
- **Steiner's astral world**: The region of Imagination, where spiritual beings appear in qualitative, living pictures rather than abstract concepts. Steiner's "Imagination" is precisely the organ for perceiving this intermediate realm.
- **Tantraloka's madhyamā level of speech**: Abhinavagupta's *madhyamā* is the middle stage of speech where thought and sound are still one, prior to differentiation into gross utterance — the imaginal space between pure consciousness and manifestation.
- **Daimon path's imaginal pneuma**: Synesius' imaginative pneuma (*phantasia*) as the vehicle of daimonic contact — the medium through which the daimon impresses images onto the soul.

### The Guide of Light (Shakhs Nurani) and Perfect Nature (Tabiat-e Tammah)

Perfect Nature is the Hermetic figure of the heavenly *paredros* — the Sage's Guide of light, the philosopher's Angel, initiator and tutor. Corbin traces it through Sohravardi, the *Ghayat al-Hakim*, and the Kubrawi Sufi tradition.

The classic formulation (p. 18): When Hermes asks "Who then art thou?" the luminous being answers: **"I am thy Perfect Nature. If thou wishest to see me, call me by my name."**

Abu'l-Barakat Baghdadi defines it (p. 20): "For each individual soul... there is a being in the spiritual world which throughout their existence watches over this soul... leads them to knowledge, protects, guides, defends, comforts them... and this being is what they called Perfect Nature. This friend, defender and protector is what in religious terminology is called the Angel."

### The Unus-Ambo Formula (1×1)

The relationship between the man of light and his Guide is not 1 = 1 (identity) but **1 × 1** — an essence raised to its total power by being multiplied by itself (p. 25). The prince cuts a spider's thread in two, then puts it together again, saying: 1 × 1.

Corbin explains (p. 9): "My image looks at me with my own look; I look at it with its own look... the identity of an essence raised to its total power by being multiplied by itself and thus put in a condition to constitute a bi-unity, a dialogic whole whose members share alternately the roles of first and of second person."

This is the same structure as:
- **Steiner's unus-ambo** of the I and the higher I: the Spirit Self (*Geistselbst*) as the transformed astral body, the double that is both self and other.
- **Tantraloka's sākṣin**: The witness consciousness that is both the seer and the seen — "Two friends with beautiful wings, closely entwined... one eats its sweet fruits; the other does not eat, but looks on" (Corbin p. 35, quoting the Upanishads).
- **Daimon path's HGA**: Crowley's "I am He! the Bornless Spirit!" — the identity-with-difference that constitutes Knowledge and Conversation.

### The Celestial Witness (Shahid)

In Najm Kobra, the Guide is called the *shahid* (witness), the "Sun of the heart," the "Sun of the Spirit" (p. 85). The vision:

> "When the circle of the face has become pure... before you, before your face, there is another Face also of light, irradiating lights... In reality this Face is your own face and this sun is the sun of the Spirit... suddenly you are gazing at a person of light (*shakhs nurani*) who is also irradiating lights... This person of light before you is called in Sufi terminology the suprasensory Guide (*moqaddam al-ghayb*)."

The *shahid* is also the "Scales of the suprasensory" (*mizan al-ghayb*) — by him the states of the soul are weighed (p. 89). If the mystic sees darkness, it means he has no witness: his own shadow is projecting the veil.

### The Dark Envelope vs. the Guide

Corbin emphatically distinguishes the heavenly Witness from the *Doppelgänger*: "The Double belongs to the personal unconscious, 'the dark envelope, the shadow, exactly what the *dhikr* has to destroy by fire so that the Guide of light may become visible'" (p. 95). The Guide is "shadowless" (p. 50).

### The Daena at the Chinvat Bridge

The Zoroastrian prototype: "Then who art thou, whose beauty outshines all other beauty...?" "I am thine own Daena. I was loved, thou hast made me more loved still. I was beautiful, thou hast made me still more beautiful" (p. 30). This is the post-mortem encounter where the soul meets its own celestial self, transfigured by its earthly choices.

### Sohravardi's Psalm to Perfect Nature (p. 21)

> "Thou, my lord and prince, my most holy angel, my precious spiritual being, Thou art the Spirit who gave birth to me, and Thou art the Child to whom my spirit gives birth... Thou who art clothed in the most brilliant of divine Lights... may Thou manifest Thyself to me in the most beautiful of epiphanies, show me the light of Thy dazzling face, be for me the mediator... lift the veils of darkness from my heart..."

### Najm Kobra's Vision (p. 86-87)

> "I fell passionately in love with a young girl... the flame of love within me became extraordinarily intense. My breath exhaled flames of fire. And each time I breathed out fire, lo and behold, from the height of heaven someone was also breathing out fire which came to meet my own breath... at last I understood that it was my witness in Heaven."

## Part 2: Suhrawardi's Illuminationist (Ishraq) Philosophy

### The Hierarchy of Lights

Suhrawardi's *Hikmat al-Ishraq* (Philosophy of Illumination) posits a universe of graded lights. The Light of Lights (*Nur al-Anwar*) is the supreme Principle, the pure, self-sufficient light from which all other lights derive. From it emanate the archangelic lights (the "Longitudinal Lights" or *Luces victoriales*), then the angelic souls of the spheres, and finally human souls — each a "particle of light" trapped in the darkness of matter.

The hierarchy (summarized from Corbin pp. 42-48, Suhrawardi's own text):
1. **Nur al-Anwar** (Light of Lights) — the Godhead, pure light invisible in itself
2. **The Archangelic Lights** (*al-anwar al-qahira*) — the "Mothers," the Cherubinic Intelligences
3. **The Angel-Souls** (*al-anwar al-mudabbira*) — the regent lights governing the celestial spheres
4. **Human souls** — particles of light imprisoned in the body
5. **The Barzakh** (screen/intermediary) — matter as the darkening veil

### The Cosmic North (Na-koja-abad)

The "land of nowhere" — the Orient-origin that is not on any geographical map. Corbin (p. 39-46): "the eighth clime," the heavenly Earth of Hurqalya, situated at the cosmic north, the pole, the Emerald Rock at the summit of Mount Qaf. This is where the Guide of light appears, where Hermes meets his Perfect Nature, where the pilgrim finds the "climate of the Soul."

This is the source of the Guide: the "midnight sun" that bursts forth when Hermes reaches the battlements of the Throne (p. 45-46). The *Ishraq* (illumination) is direct spiritual perception — the *cognitio matutina* (morning knowledge) as opposed to the *cognitio vespertina* (evening knowledge) of discursive reason.

### Ishraq vs. Steiner's Imagination, Tantraloka's Pratibhā

| Suhrawardi's Ishraq | Steiner's Imagination | Tantraloka's Pratibhā |
|---|---|---|
| Direct illumination from the Light of Lights | Supersensible perception through purified thinking | Flash of intuitive recognition (*pratibhā*) |
| Requires purification (*dhikr*) to receive | Requires meditation and moral development | Requires *śaktipāta* and *mantra* practice |
| The vision of the Guide as "midnight sun" | The vision of the higher Self as spiritual sun | The recognition of the *sākṣin* as the witness |
| *Cognitio matutina* — knowledge of the man of light | *Imagination* — living pictorial knowing | *Pratibhā* — the sudden dawn of non-dual awareness |

### Angelology: Suhrawardi vs. Steiner vs. Tantraloka

| Suhrawardi | Steiner | Tantraloka |
|---|---|---|
| *Nur al-Anwar* (Light of Lights) | The Trinity / Divine Being | Paramāśiva |
| Archangelic Lights (*al-anwar al-qahira*) | Archai, Exusiai, Dynamis | Anuttara, the supreme level |
| Angel-Souls of spheres | Angeloi, Archangeloi | Vidyeśvaras (lords of knowledge) |
| Perfect Nature (individual Angel) | Spirit Self (individual higher being) | Iṣṭadevatā (chosen deity) |
| Human soul as particle of light | Human soul as individualized spirit | Individual soul (*aṇu*) in Śiva's power |

The key structural parallel: each system places an intermediate order between the absolute and the individual — Suhrawardi's *mundus imaginalis* corresponds to Steiner's astral world and Tantraloka's *madhyamā* level.

## Part 3: Ibn Arabi's Unity of Being (Wahdat al-Wujud)

### Theophany (Tajalli) as Self-Disclosure

For Ibn Arabi, *tajalli* is the self-disclosure of the Real (*al-Haqq*) — the divine Being manifesting through the forms of creation. The famous *hadith qudsi*: "I was a hidden treasure, and I loved to be known, so I created the creation that I might be known."

This is not pantheism but *transcendental unity of being* (*wahdat al-wujud*): the act of being remains unique while multiplying itself in the actualities of beings (Corbin p. 115). As Corbin notes, Lahliji's commentary on the *Rose Garden of Mystery* clarifies: the expression does not signify "existential monism" but "the transcendental unity of being."

### The Perfect Man (Al-Insan al-Kamil)

The Perfect Man is the complete daimonic being — the one in whom the divine Names are fully reflected, the microcosm that contains all the realities of the macrocosm. He is the "polished mirror" in which God contemplates Himself.

Ibn Arabi's *Fusus al-Hikam* (The Bezels of Wisdom) presents 27 prophets as archetypal manifestations of the divine Names — each is a "bezel" through which a particular aspect of the Real is disclosed. The Perfect Man synthesizes all these aspects.

This maps to:
- **Corbin's Guide of light**: The person of light that is simultaneously the Self and the Other
- **The daimon**: The "complete being" that knows your destiny and guides you to it
- **Steiner's Spirit Self**: The transformed astral body that becomes the vessel of the higher I
- **Layayoga's Iṣṭadevatā**: The chosen deity that embodies the totality of one's spiritual potential

### A'yan Thabita (Immutable Entities)

These are the archetypes of individual souls in divine Knowledge — the *loci* of possibility that subsist eternally in the divine Self-knowledge. Each *ayn thabit* (immutable entity) is the "face" of a being turned toward God, its permanent possibility in the divine mind.

Compare to:
- **Suhrawardi's archetypal Lights**: The longitudinal and latitudinal lights that are the "Angels of species"
- **Corbin's Perfect Nature**: The pre-eternal partner of each soul in the heavenly Earth
- **Plato's Forms**: But actualized as individual angelic beings, not abstract universals

### The Imagination (Khayal) as Intermediate World

For Ibn Arabi, *khayal* is the intermediate world — exactly homologous to Corbin's *mundus imaginalis*. In the *Futuhat al-Makkiyya*, Ibn Arabi describes the "world of the similitude" (*'alam al-mithal*) where spiritual realities take on sensory form and sensory realities are spiritualized.

>"Imagination is the isthmus (*barzakh*) between the world of spirits and the world of bodies" — the same function as Corbin's imaginal world and the daimon path's *imaginal pneuma*.

### The Two Emanations: "Most Holy Effusion" and "Holy Effusion"

1. **The Most Holy Effusion** (*al-fayd al-aqdas*): The self-disclosure of the Real to itself — the eternal manifestation of the *a'yan thabita* in divine Self-knowledge. This is the ontological ground of all possibility.

2. **The Holy Effusion** (*al-fayd al-muqaddas*): The externalization of the *a'yan* into concrete existence in the sensory world. This is the actual creation.

The first corresponds to the "hidden Treasure" aspect, the second to the "creation for the sake of being known." Between them lies the entire intermediate world of the imagination.

## Part 4: Avicenna's Visionary Recital (Corbin)

### The Oriental Philosophy (Hikmat al-Mashriqiyya)

Avicenna's "Oriental Philosophy" is distinugished from his Peripatetic works — it represents an esoteric, visionary knowledge accessible only through initiatic ascent. Corbin interprets it as a direct precursor to Suhrawardi's *Ishraq*.

The key distinction: *cognitio matutina* (morning/oriental knowledge) vs. *cognitio vespertina* (evening/occidental knowledge). The first is the direct presence of the known to the knower; the second is discursive, representational knowledge.

### Hayy ibn Yaqzan: The Guide Narrative

The recital of *Hayy ibn Yaqzan* (the Living Son of the Awake) is Avicenna's initiatic narrative. Hayy ibn Yaqzan is the personification of the Active Intelligence — the Angel of Knowledge who guides the pilgrim from the Occident of matter to the Orient of light.

Corbin (from the Avicenna text, Part I): The recital contains the encounter with the Angel, the journey through the 10 celestial spheres, the crossing of the "Darkness at the approaches to the Pole," and the arrival at the Water of Life. The structure is the same as Suhrawardi's *Recital of the Occidental Exile* — the soul's descent into exile and its return to the true Orient.

### The 10 Intelligences vs. the 10 Sefirot vs. the 10 Bhuvanas

| Avicenna's 10 Intelligences | Kabbalistic 10 Sefirot | Vedic 10 Bhuvanas (worlds) |
|---|---|---|
| First Intelligence (Angel-Logos) | Keter (Crown) | Satya-loka (truth world) |
| Second Intelligence | Hokhmah (Wisdom) | Tapa-loka (austerity world) |
| Third Intelligence | Binah (Understanding) | Jana-loka (wisdom world) |
| Fourth-Sphere Intelligence | Hesed (Mercy) | Mahar-loka (greatness world) |
| Fifth (Mars) | Gevurah (Severity) | Svar-loka (heaven) |
| Sixth (Jupiter) | Tiferet (Beauty) | Bhuvar-loka (atmosphere) |
| Seventh (Saturn) | Netsach (Victory) | Bhu-loka (earth) |
| Eighth (Fixed Stars) | Hod (Splendor) | Atala (lower worlds...) |
| Ninth (Sphere of Spheres) | Yesod (Foundation) | Vitala |
| Tenth (Active Intelligence) | Malkhut (Kingdom) | Patala |

The structural parallel: both Avicenna's emanative cosmology and the Kabbalistic *Sefirot* describe a graded descent from the One through 10 principles, each governing a level of reality. The Vedic *bhuvanas* map the same vertical cosmology. In all three, the lower levels are "thicker" or more material, and the spiritual ascent reverses the descent.

## Part 6: Key Extracts from Man of Light

### 1. Perfect Nature — Definition (p. 17)
> "It is the heavenly entity, the philosopher's Angel, conjoined with his star, which rules him and opens the doors of wisdom for him, teaches him what is difficult, reveals to him what is right, in sleeping as in waking."

### 2. The Guide of Light — Hermes' Vision (p. 18)
> "I came upon a subterranean vault filled with darkness and winds... Lo and behold, a person appeared to me in my sleep in a form of the greatest beauty. He said to me: 'Take a lamp and place it under a glass...' I said, 'Who then art thou?' He answered: 'I am thy Perfect Nature. If thou wishest to see me, call me by my name.'"

### 3. The Unus-Ambo — 1×1 Formula (p. 25)
> "Having cut in two the thread spun by a spider, the prince puts it together again, saying: 1 × 1... the formula that we suggested above, because he who deciphers it holds the key to the secret that preserves him both from pseudomystical monism (whose formula is 1 = 1) and from abstract monotheism (n + 1)."

### 4. The Celestial Witness — Shahid Vision (p. 85)
> "When the circle of the face has become pure... before you, before your face, there is another Face also of light, irradiating lights... In reality this Face is your own face and this sun is the sun of the Spirit... suddenly you are gazing at a person of light (*shakhs nurani*) who is also irradiating lights... This person of light before you is called in Sufi terminology the suprasensory Guide (*moqaddam al-ghayb*)."

### 5. The Dark Envelope vs. the Guide (p. 95)
> "What we are speaking of is a counterpart, a celestial, transcendent counterpart, rather than a 'double'; the idea of this heavenly partner is antithetic to the Double whose role is suggested in a number of fantastic tales... psychological analysis shows this Double to be the manifestation of the personal unconscious, hence belonging to the functions of the lower psyche, that is, the *nafs ammara*, the dark envelope, the shadow, exactly what the *dhikr* has to destroy by fire so that the Guide of light may become visible."

### 6. Daena at the Chinvat Bridge (p. 30)
> "'Then who art thou, whose beauty outshines all other beauty ever contemplated in the terrestrial world?' 'I am thine own Daena. I was loved, thou hast made me more loved still. I was beautiful, thou hast made me still more beautiful,' and embracing her devotee, she leads him into the Abode-of-Hymns."

### 7. Sohravardi's Psalm to Perfect Nature (p. 21)
> "Thou, my lord and prince, my most holy angel, my precious spiritual being, Thou art the Spirit who gave birth to me, and Thou art the Child to whom my spirit gives birth... Thou who art clothed in the most brilliant of divine Lights... may Thou manifest Thyself to me in the most beautiful of epiphanies, show me the light of Thy dazzling face, be for me the mediator... lift the veils of darkness from my heart..."

### 8. Najm Kobra's Vision of the Heavenly Witness (p. 86-87)
> "I fell passionately in love with a young girl... the flame of love within me became extraordinarily intense. My breath exhaled flames of fire. And each time I breathed out fire, lo and behold, from the height of heaven someone was also breathing out fire which came to meet my own breath... at last I understood that it was my witness in Heaven."

### 9. The Scales of the Suprasensory (p. 89)
> "The suprasensory Witness, the suprasensory Guide, the suprasensory Scales: this is what you are shown when you close your eyelids. According to whether what appears to you is light or darkness, your witness is light or darkness... If it so happens that at the midpoint of the mystical journey, the two circles of light of the eyes appear, it is the sign of an excellent spiritual state. If they remain hidden, this concealment indicates a lack, a preponderance of the dark nature."

### 10. The Mundus Imaginalis Defined (p. 42-43)
> "Between the world of pure spiritual Lights and the sensory universe, at the boundary of the ninth Sphere there opens a *mundus imaginalis* which is a concrete spiritual world of archetype-Figures, apparitional Forms, Angels of species and of individuals; by philosophical dialectics its necessity is deduced and its plane situated; vision of it in actuality is vouchsafed to the visionary apperception of the active Imagination."
