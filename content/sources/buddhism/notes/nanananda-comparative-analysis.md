# Nanananda's 33 Sermons on Nibbana: Comparative Analysis

> Bhikkhu K. Ñāṇananda's *Nibbāna – The Mind Stilled* (1988-1991) compared with Steiner's Occult Science, our daimon path, Tantraloka, Layayoga, and the death systems. Written 2026-07-14.

---

## Overview: What Nanananda Adds

Nanananda's 33 sermons are a **deconstruction of the very process by which we construct reality**. Where every other system in our corpus provides a map of what IS — the subtle body, the cakras, the after-death journey, the hierarchies — Nanananda asks: **"How do you know that the map is real?"**

His answer: the map (nāma, "name") and the territory (rūpa, "form") arise together. They are not separate. The world of experience is **nāma-rūpa** — name-and-form — an interdependent pair.

This is not idealism (reality is mental) or materialism (reality is physical). It is a **third position**: what you call "reality" is the relationship between name (the act of naming/perceiving/recognizing) and form (the raw qualities that are named). They cannot exist without each other.

---

## The Core Doctrines

### 1. Nāma-Rūpa (Name-and-Form)

Nanananda restores the literal meaning of nāma-rūpa from commentarial abstraction:

> *"Feeling, perception, intention, contact, attention — this, friend, is called 'name'. The four great primaries and form dependent on the four great primaries — this, friend, is called 'form'."* (Sermon 1, citing Sammādiṭṭhisutta)

**Nāma is not "mind"** (the commentaries say it means "bending" toward objects). It is **name** in the most primitive sense — the act of recognizing raw sense qualities before conventional naming.

**Rūpa is not "matter"**. It is **form** as perceived through the four elements: hardness/softness (earth), cohesion/fluidity (water), hot/cold (fire), motion/pressure (air).

The child with a rubber ball illustrates this: the child knows the ball not by its conventional name but through feeling, perception, intention, contact, attention. The prototype of all naming.

### 2. The Fire Simile

> *"When a fire burns, it does so with the help of firewood. If someone were to ask us: 'What is burning?' — what shall we say? Is the wood burning or the fire burning? The truth: the wood burns because of the fire and the fire burns because of the wood."*

**Upādāna** means both **fuel** and **grasping**. Existence (bhava) is a fire kept burning by the fuel of grasping. When the fuel of grasping is removed, the fire goes out — **not because it was annihilated, but because it never had independent existence**.

This dissolves the being/nonbeing dilemma entirely. There is no "self" that exists or does not exist. There is only the burning dependent on fuel.

### 3. Paṭicca Samuppāda (Dependent Arising)

Nanananda rejects the three-life commentarial interpretation of the twelve links. Instead:

> *"Dependent arising is a principle, not a list. The twelve links are an illustration of the principle of idappaccayatā — 'this being, that arises.'"*

The banana trunk simile: cut a banana trunk in the middle and you see the whole structure in cross-section. Likewise, present experience contains the entire chain of dependent arising. It is **not a timeline but a structure visible here and now**.

### 4. Nibbāna

Nibbāna is **extinction** — the going out of the fires of lust, hate, and delusion. The commentaries were "too shy of the implications of the word extinction" and invented the etymology "exit from weaving."

> *"The destruction of craving itself is called the highest bliss."* (Udāna, cited by Nanananda)

Nibbāna is not a place. It is not annihilation (the fire never had independent existence). It is **unestablished consciousness** — consciousness that no longer leans on name-and-form.

### 5. Rebirth

Rebirth is **not primarily life-to-life transmigration**:

> *"The mind's journey is quite different from the way the body travels. The body leaves one place to go to another. The mind's journey is a whirling, as in a whirlpool."*

Rebirth is the **endless becoming of self-identity in different states, moment to moment**. Each moment of craving creates a self that experiences the consequence of that craving. The cycle of becoming (bhava) operates continuously, not just between lives.

### 6. Karma

Karma is the **conditioning of habitual pathways** — neural grooves that become default responses:

> *"Now we have created that as a pathway as a working solution to deal with the craving. Next time we get it, the mind will follow this route again. This is karma."*

These pathways operate **outside conscious awareness**. The practice is to stay with the raw sensation **before** it turns into urge, not feeding the cyclical mechanism.

## Comparative Mapping

### 1. The Three Realms — Nanananda, Steiner, and the Daimon Path

| Steiner's Three Worlds | Nanananda's Two Realms | Daimon Path |
|---|---|---|
| **Physical world** (objects) | **Rūpa** — form as raw sense qualities (hard/soft, hot/cold, etc.) | The body and its sensations |
| **Astral world** (images, prototypes) | **Nāma** — the pre-verbal act of naming/perceiving; the five factors (feeling, perception, intention, contact, attention) | The imaginal pneuma; the sphere of sensation |
| **Spirit world** (beings, thoughts) | **Nāma-rūpa as a whole** — the interdependent pair that constitutes all experience | The daimon as the "I" that sees through both name and form |

**The key insight**: Steiner's "astral body" is the vehicle of name (nāma). The "ether body" is the vehicle of form (rūpa). Their interweaving IS the world of experience. What Steiner calls "the astral prototypes that maintain the ether body" is what Nanananda calls "name leaning on form."

### 2. Papanca (Conceptual Proliferation) and the Guardian of the Threshold

Nanananda's papanca is the mind's tendency to **proliferate concepts out of raw sense data** — to spin a world of "I," "mine," "self," "other" out of simple contact.

This is exactly what Steiner describes as the **Doppelganger / Guardian of the Threshold**:

| Nanananda | Steiner |
|---|---|
| *Saññā* (perception) → *vitakka* (thought) → *papañca* (proliferation) | Sense perception → intellectual elaboration → illusion of separate self |
| "The self is a conditioned control-pattern generated by body, feeling, perception, memory, desire, attention and action" | "The Doppelganger contains all the unresolved karma, hidden inclinations, and unconfessed faults" |
| The tangle (*jaṭā*) that gets snapped at Nibbāna | The Guardian that must be passed through |
| "The thought 'I am' is the root of proliferation" | "The 'I' is the Ineffable Name of God — the point where the Divine dwells in the soul" |

**The convergence**: Both identify the conceptual construction of "I" as the core obstruction to direct reality. Both say the way through is not to destroy the "I" but to **see it as constructed** — as name leaning on form, as a fire dependent on fuel.

### 3. Unestablished Consciousness and the After-Death State

The Arahant passes away with **appatiṭṭhita viññāṇa** — "unestablished consciousness." It no longer leans on name-and-form. Māra searched for Godhika's consciousness in vain after his parinibbāna.

This maps precisely to Steiner's description of the **spirit self (manas)** after death:

| State | Worldling's Consciousness | Arahant's / Adept's Consciousness |
|---|---|---|
| **During life** | Established on nāma-rūpa; leans on body and concepts | Unestablished; sees nāma-rūpa as empty |
| **At death** | Elements dissolve → seeks new footing (new birth) | Elements dissolve → consciousness does not seek a new footing |
| **After death** | Enters Sidpa Bardo / kamaloca / seeks rebirth | Parinibbāna — complete extinction of the fires |
| **Steiner** | The ego seeks new incarnation to continue its development | The perfected spirit self unites with the spiritual world |

**However**: Steiner and Nanananda differ on the ultimate goal. Steiner says the spirit self continues to evolve through successive incarnations toward ever-higher development. Nanananda says the ultimate goal is the **complete stilling** — not further becoming, but cessation.

This is the deepest tension between the systems: **Is the goal endless evolution (Steiner, the daimon path, Tantraloka) or cessation of becoming (Nanananda)?**

### 4. Dependent Arising and the Daimon Path's "One Rule"

Nanananda's reading of dependent arising:

> *"When this is, that arises. With the cessation of this, that ceases."*

This is the same logic as the daimon path's "one rule" from makingcontact.md:

> *"Every paragraph in every Research Object is kind: 'source'. Hermes never writes original claims. It compiles, links, voices, deploys, and improves — but never creates."*

Both describe a universe where **nothing arises from nothing** — every phenomenon depends on conditions. The daimon path applies this to knowledge: every claim must trace back to a source. Nanananda applies it to existence: every state of being traces back to its conditions.

### 5. The Fire Simile and Layayoga's Absorption

The fire simile in Nanananda:
- Fire has no existence independent of fuel
- When fuel ceases, the fire "goes out" — not into non-existence, but beyond the framework of existence/non-existence

Layayoga's absorption (laya):
- Each principle (tattva) is absorbed into the next higher
- The self is not destroyed but **reabsorbed into its source**

**Both agree**: the self was never an independent entity. The difference:

| Nanananda | Layayoga |
|---|---|
| Fire and fuel are dependent co-arising | Fire and fuel are levels of the same emanation |
| When fuel ceases, the fire goes out — no direction, no location | When fuel is absorbed, the fire returns to its source — the path of recoil |
| The goal is cessation (*nirodha*) | The goal is union (*yoga*) |

### 6. The Child and the Initiate

Nanananda's child with the rubber ball — the pre-verbal, pre-conceptual encounter with reality — is the same state that:

- **Steiner** calls "imaginative cognition" — perception without the conceptual framework of the physical brain
- **The daimon path** calls "the void meditation" (Josephine McCarthy) — perception without the filter of the false self
- **Tantraloka** calls "śāmbhavopāya" — the Divine Means, realization through will without conceptual constructs
- **Synesius** calls "the imaginative pneuma" — the subtle body's capacity to receive images before the rational mind interprets them

Nanananda adds the crucial warning: **the return to childlike perception must be accompanied by knowledge, not ignorance**. The meditator does not become a child. The meditator recovers the child's directness while retaining adult discernment.

## Key Tensions

### 1. Rebirth: Moment-to-Moment vs. Life-to-Life

| System | View | Ground |
|---|---|---|
| **Nanananda** | Rebirth is moment-to-moment becoming. The "life-to-life" interpretation is a commentarial distortion that violates the Dhamma's timelessness. | Suttas, which he reads as describing present-moment experience |
| **Steiner** | Rebirth is life-to-life reincarnation across ~2,100 year cycles. The soul descends through spheres and chooses parents. | Clairvoyant research, which he claims observes the process objectively |
| **Tibetan** | Rebirth is both: the Sidpa Bardo shows the soul seeking a womb, but the daily becoming of the mental body is the same mechanism operating at a micro scale. | Both textual and experiential |

**Can both be true?** Nanananda describes the *mechanism* of becoming — how consciousness establishes itself on name-and-form moment by moment. Steiner describes the *scale* at which this mechanism operates across lives. They are not contradictory: the same dependent arising that produces the next thought also produces the next birth.

### 2. Nibbāna vs. Spirit Self

| Nanananda's Nibbāna | Steiner's Spirit Self (Manas) |
|---|---|
| Complete cessation of craving | Transformed astral body; the ego's highest achievement |
| No further becoming | Continues to evolve across lives |
| Unestablished consciousness | Established on the spiritual world |
| Not a state of being | A state of being — the highest |

**The unresolved question**: Is the spirit self what Nanananda would call "a subtle form of becoming"? Steiner would say the spirit self is not craving-based becoming but conscious, willed evolution. Nanananda would ask: "What fuel does it burn?"

### 3. The Daimon as Construct

The daimon path treats the daimon/HGA as a real being — a higher intelligence that guides the practitioner. Nanananda would ask:

> *"Is the daimon not itself nāma-rūpa? Is it not another name leaning on another form?"*

The daimon path's answer (from daimoncontact.md):

> *"The daimon is the operational interface. It is real because the relationship is real. You do not need to believe in it as a literal entity. You need to USE the symbols consistently."*

Nanananda would approve of this honesty but would add: **even the relationship is dependent arising**. Even the unus-ambo is name-and-form at the highest level. The ultimate stilling is not union but cessation.

## What Nanananda Gives That Nothing Else Does

1. **A deconstruction of the map itself** — Every other system gives a map of the spiritual world. Nanananda asks: "How do you know the map is not just more name-and-form?" This is the ultimate safeguard against spiritual materialism.

2. **The mechanism of papanca** — Why the mind spins concepts out of raw perception. No other system explains the *engine* of conceptual proliferation with this precision.

3. **Nāma-rūpa as the fundamental structure** — Not mind and matter, not subject and object, but name and form arising together. This resolves the idealist/materialist debate that haunts Western esotericism.

4. **Rebirth as a present-moment process** — The daimon path speaks of "the death of the false self" as a single initiatory event. Nanananda shows it must be a **moment-to-moment practice**: each moment of craving is a birth; each moment of release is a death.

5. **The compatibility of cessation and evolution** — Steiner's endless evolution and Nanananda's cessation of becoming are not opposed if we understand that **cessation of craving-based becoming IS the condition for conscious evolution**. The fires of grasping must go out before the spirit can move freely.
