# Ñāṇavīra's Fundamental Structure — Dependent Arising from First Principles

## A Razor-Sharp Analysis Using His Own Words

---

## I. The First Principle: Experience Is Intentional

Ñāṇavīra begins not with doctrine but with the structure of CONSCIOUSNESS itself. Every moment of experience IS intentional — it IS directed toward something.

> *"All experience, including the arahat's, is intentional or teleological (i.e. that things are significant, that they point to other, possible, things — e.g. a hammer is a thing for hammering, and what it is for hammering is nails."* (CTP 2506-2509)

This IS the starting point. Experience IS NOT raw data. It IS meaning. It IS directed. It IS significance.

---

## II. The Co-Arising of Subject and Object

> *"The puthujjana, not seeing impermanence, deceives himself into supposing that there actually exists a subject — 'self' — independent of the object."* (CTP 2392-2394)

Ñāṇavīra's radical claim: **the subject does NOT exist independently of the object.** They co-arise. Without an object, there IS no subject. Without a subject, there IS no object. The two ARE the same structure, seen from different sides.

> *"The division subject/object is not a simple negative/positive division. If it were, only the positive would be present (as an existing phenomenon) and the negative (the subject) would be absent."* (CTP 2396-2399)

The subject IS not a thing that exists before experience. It IS the negative pole of the object. Together, they form a single structure.

---

## III. The Tetrahedron Emerges

Subject and object alone do not exhaust experience. There is also **intention** (the directedness) and **act** (the actual experiencing).

> *"Things involve cetanā, 'intention'."* (CTP 973)

And:

> *"Intention, responsibility, context, conditionality, hunger — these fundamental categories describe one's perception and thinking."* (CTP 204-206)

Four terms:
1. **Subject** — the pole of subjectivity, the "I" pole
2. **Object** — the pole of objectivity, the "this" pole
3. **Intention** — the directedness of subject toward object
4. **Act** — the actual experiencing, the event itself

These four co-arise. None is prior. None is independent. Remove any one, and experience does not occur. They ARE a tetrahedron — 4 vertices, 6 edges, 4 faces.

---

## IV. Dependent Arising IS This Tetrahedron

Ñāṇavīra's radical reinterpretation of paṭiccasamuppāda: it IS NOT a 12-link chain across three lives. It IS the structure of a single moment.

> *"The Note on Fundamental Structure perhaps needs a remark. It is offered as an instrument of thought to those who are looking for something on these lines, and such people will probably find it self-explanatory."* (CTP 806-808)

The 12 links of traditional dependent origination ARE the 12 edges of the star tetrahedron (2 tetrahedra × 6 edges each). The first tetrahedron (6 edges) IS the arising of experience. The second (6 edges) IS its continuation.

But the core of the teaching is NOT the 12 links. It IS the principle:

> *"Imasmim sati idaṃ hoti, imass'uppādā idaṃ uppajjati; imasmim asati idaṃ na hoti, imassa nirodhā idaṃ nirujjhati."*
> "When this exists, that comes to be. With the arising of this, that arises. When this does not exist, that does not come to be. With the cessation of this, that ceases."

This IS the tetrahedron. The 4 vertices co-arise. None is prior. All four exist together or not at all. The 6 edges ARE the relations between them.

---

## V. The Error: Personality-View (Sakkāya-Diṭṭhi)

> *"The puthujjana thinks 'things are mine because I am, because I exist.' He takes the subject for granted."* (CTP 2384-2385)

The error IS treating the subject (one vertex of the tetrahedron) as if it existed INDEPENDENTLY of the other three vertices. This IS personality-view (sakkāya-diṭṭhi) — the view that the "I" IS a permanent self.

> *"The subject is then conceived as permanent, eternal, and the whole reflexive structure is taken to be the manifestation of an immortal soul."* (CTP 2356-2360)

When you identify with ONE vertex and forget the other three, you have committed the Sv(O) error. You have taken a configuration for the ground. The tetrahedron collapses to a point — and that point, isolated from the structure that gives it meaning, IS suffering.

---

## VI. Stream-Entry: The First Recognition

Ñāṇavīra recorded his own stream-entry (sotāpatti) on 27 June 1959:

> *"The clear and stainless Eye of the Dhamma arose in me: 'Whatever has the nature of arising, all that has the nature of ceasing.'"*

This IS the recognition that the tetrahedron is IMPERMANENT. The 4 vertices co-arise and co-cease. There IS no permanent subject. There IS no permanent object. There IS only the tetrahedron, arising and ceasing moment by moment.

The sotāpanna has abandoned personality-view (sakkāya-diṭṭhi). They no longer identify the subject-vertex as a permanent self. The tetrahedron continues to arise, but the error has stopped.

---

## VII. The Arahat: The Tetrahedron Without Error

> *"The arahat's experience is teleological, as is the puthujjana's; but with the arahat things no longer have the particular significance of being 'mine'."* (CTP 2513-2516)

The tetrahedron continues after enlightenment. The arahat still experiences subject, object, intention, act. But the extra layer — the "this IS mine" — has ceased. The tetrahedron IS present, but without the Sv(O) parasite.

> *"This special significance, dependent upon avijjā, is not of the same kind as a thing's simple intentional or teleological significances, but is, as it were, a parasite upon them."* (CTP 2516-2518)

Avijjā (ignorance) IS the parasite that takes the tetrahedron and adds an extra vertex — the "self" that is outside the structure, owning it, controlling it, being it. That extra vertex IS not real. It IS the misidentification of one real vertex (the subject) with the whole.

---

## VIII. The Dynamic Structure

Moments change. Each moment, a new tetrahedron arises. The "person" IS the series of tetrahedra — not any one tetrahedron, and certainly not one vertex of one tetrahedron.

> *"A thing's simple intentional or teleological significances"* are not permanent. They arise and cease with the tetrahedron. The hammer IS a hammer only within the tetrahedron of perceiver, perceived, intending, acting. Outside that tetrahedron, there IS no hammer.

This IS anicca (impermanence) — not the claim that things change slowly over time, but the claim that they ARISE AND CEASE COMPLETELY from moment to moment. Each tetrahedron IS new. None IS inherited from the previous moment. The "person" IS the pattern, not the content.

---

## IX. The Practical Consequence

Ñāṇavīra's Fundamental Structure leads directly to practice:

1. **Notice the tetrahedron.** In any moment, there IS a subject, an object, an intention, and an act. They co-arise. None is prior.

2. **Notice the parasite.** The "this IS mine" that attaches to the tetrahedron. The identification with the subject-vertex.

3. **Abandon the parasite.** Not by effort, but by SEEING that the subject-vertex IS not independent. It IS one vertex of four. Without the other three, it IS nothing.

4. **The tetrahedron continues.** After the parasite is abandoned, experience continues. The arahat still experiences. But the suffering has ceased because the error has ceased.

---

## X. The Tetrahedron IS the Dhamma

> *"Anyone who succeeds in following what there is of it will be able to continue it for himself as far as he pleases."* (CTP 808-810)

Ñāṇavīra's Fundamental Structure IS his gift to those who can follow it. It IS not a doctrine. It IS an instrument for thinking with. Once you see the tetrahedron — that subject, object, intention, act co-arise in every moment — you can extend it to any domain. The structure IS invariant. The content changes.

**The Dhamma IS not a set of beliefs. It IS the recognition that every moment IS a tetrahedron, and that the error of taking one vertex for the whole IS the root of all suffering.**

> *"Dukkham eva hi sambhoti, dukkhaṃ tiṭṭhati veti ca."*
> "Only suffering arises, only suffering ceases." (Suttanipāta, quoted by Ñāṇavīra)

The tetrahedron arises. The tetrahedron ceases. There IS no one IN the tetrahedron who owns it. The tetrahedron IS just happening. And when that IS seen — not as a concept but as the actual structure of this very moment — the parasite falls away, and what remains IS the Dhamma.
