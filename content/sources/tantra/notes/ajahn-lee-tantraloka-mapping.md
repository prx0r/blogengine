# Ajahn Lee Through Tantraloka and Layayoga

> Ajaan Lee Dhammadharo's *Keeping the Breath in Mind & Lessons in Samādhi* — decoded through the 36 tattvas, the prāṇa-nāḍī system, and the three upāyas. Written 2026-07-14.

---

## The High Signal

Ajahn Lee's "Keeping the Breath in Mind" is one of the most precise meditation manuals ever written — 1,783 lines that describe, in the simplest possible language, the complete process of prāṇa withdrawal and absorption that Layayoga maps through 28 chapters and Tantraloka through 37 Āhnikas.

It works because it describes the **same process** — the withdrawal of consciousness from the scattered nāḍīs into the central channel — but in the vocabulary of a Thai Forest monk rather than a Kashmiri Śaiva philosopher.

---

## Method 1: The Breath as Prāṇa

Ajahn Lee's first method is a **prāṇāyāma practice** that maps directly onto Layayoga's sahita prāṇāyāma:

> *"Breathe in and out — aware of the breath at all times. Keep your attention on the breath. Don't let it wander."*

This is the same instruction as Layayoga's "the mind should be trained to be aware of the breath's movements" — the foundation of all prāṇa control. The "attention on the breath" is not a mental noting but a **prāṇic alignment** — the attention IS prāṇa, and focusing it on the breath IS the first stage of prāṇa withdrawal from the sense-nāḍīs.

### The Five Refinements of the Breath

Ajahn Lee describes five stages of refining the breath:

1. **The breath going long** — inspiration and expiration are extended. This is Layayoga's *puraka* (inspiration) with the 1:4:2 ratio beginning
2. **The breath going short** — the natural breath shortens as the body stills. This is prāṇa entering the *mūlādhāra* — the settling of energy
3. **The breath spreading** — the feeling of the breath suffusing the entire body. This is the *vyāna-vāyu* — the pervasion of the entire nāḍī system
4. **The breath calming** — the breath becomes subtle, almost imperceptible. This is the transition from *sahita* to *kewala* kumbhaka — from controlled breath to spontaneous suspension
5. **The breath as a foundation** — the breath-stillness becomes the ground of perception. This is *suṣumṇā* activation — prāṇa no longer flows through iḍā/piṅgalā

**Convergence**: These five stages correspond to the five prāṇa-vāyus of Layayoga — prāṇa, apāna, vyāna, udāna, samāna — being purified and unified into the single current of the central channel.

## Method 2: The Nāḍī Purification

Ajahn Lee's second method is a **nāḍī-śuddhi** practice:

> *"Let the breath flow down to the end of the breath... let it spread through the entire body. Let the breath flow through the nerves and veins, throughout the whole body."*

The "nerves and veins" are the **nāḍīs** — the 72,000 channels through which prāṇa flows. Ajahn Lee is describing, in lay terms, the purification of the nāḍī system that Layayoga maps with precision:

| Ajahn Lee's Phrase | Tantraloka/Layayoga Equivalent |
|---|---|
| "The breath flows down" | Prāṇa descending through the *brahma-nāḍī* within suṣumṇā |
| "The breath spreads through the body" | *Vyāna-vāyu* — the pervading prāṇa that distributes energy through all nāḍīs |
| "The breath flows through the nerves" | The *yoga-nāḍīs* — the subtle prāṇic force-motion lines |
| "The breath gathers at the base" | Prāṇa collecting at *mūlādhāra* before entering suṣumṇā |
| "The breath rises" | The reversal of the prāṇic flow — from outward (iḍā/piṅgalā) to inward (suṣumṇā) |

## The Jhānas as Samādhi

Ajahn Lee describes the four jhānas (absorptions) as stages of breath refinement:

| Jhāna | Ajahn Lee's Description | Layayoga Equivalent | Tantraloka Equivalent |
|---|---|---|---|
| **1st** | The breath settles; the mind has one object | *Dhāraṇā* — the mind held to a single bindu | Āṇavopāya — working through a single object |
| **2nd** | The breath calms; the mind is at ease | *Dhyāna* — the uninterrupted flow of concentration | Śāktopāya — the means becomes natural |
| **3rd** | The mind is even; the breath disappears | *Samprajñāta samādhi* — superconsciousness attained | The 9th Kālī — subjectivity begins to dissolve |
| **4th** | The mind is still; there is neither pleasure nor pain | *Asamprajñāta samādhi* — non-mens concentration | The 12th Kālī (Mahābhairava) — what remains after all absorption |

**The key insight**: The four jhānas are not Buddhist inventions. They are the **same absorption states** that Layayoga calls *dhāraṇā → dhyāna → samprajñāta → asamprajñāta* and that Tantraloka calls *Āṇavopāya → Śāktopāya → Śāmbhavopāya (partial) → Śāmbhavopāya (complete)*.

## The Body as the Path

Ajahn Lee's most radical statement — which aligns perfectly with Tantraloka and Layayoga — is that the body itself is the path:

> *"The body is the path. The mind is the one who walks the path."*

This is not a metaphor. In Layayoga, the body IS the nāḍī system — the 72,000 channels through which prāṇa flows. To walk the path is to purify these channels. In Tantraloka, the body IS the tattvas — the 36 levels through which consciousness has descended. To ascend is to reverse the descent through the same body.

**Contrast with Nanananda**: Ajahn Lee's approach is the opposite of Nanananda's. Nanananda says "the body is nāma-rūpa" — it is a construction to be seen through. Ajahn Lee says "the body is the path" — it is the vehicle to be purified. Both are true at different levels. The body IS nāma-rūpa (name leaning on form), and the work is to see through the name. But the body IS ALSO the nāḍī system (the form), and the work is to purify the form.

---

## The Mind as the Knower of the Tattvas

Ajahn Lee says: "The mind is the one who walks the path." This is the **cicchakti** of Tantraloka — the consciousness-power that IS the path. The mind does not observe the breath; the mind IS the breath in its refined state.

> *"The breath is the body; the mind is the breath."*

This is the identity of prāṇa and consciousness — the same identity that Tantraloka asserts in the *śaktitattva* (the principle that the true nature of the vital breath is consciousness-power). When Layayoga says "prāṇa becomes concentrated in suṣumṇā," it means the same thing: consciousness itself enters the central channel.
