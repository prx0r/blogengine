# The Highest Yoga: A Comparative Treatise

## Tsongkhapa · Nāropa · Abhinavagupta — Divergences at the Ceiling

> "The mountain is real. But no single language describes all its slopes." — Tantraloka Decoded
>
> Written 2026-07-14, synthesizing Dyczkowski's Tantraloka Vols. 4, 7, 10, Tsongkhapa's Lamp to Illuminate the Five Stages, the Six Yogas of Nāropa, Lakshmanjoo's Vijñana Bhairava, and the daimon corpus.

---

## Prologue: Why These Three?

Abhinavagupta (950-1020), Nāropa (1016-1100), and Tsongkhapa (1357-1419) never met. They never debated. Yet they addressed the same ultimate problem — what IS the highest state, and how does consciousness relate to it? — from three distinct lineages. Abhinavagupta is the nondual Śaiva systematizer. Nāropa is the mahāsiddha transmission from India to Tibet. Tsongkhapa is the Tibetan scholastic who systematized Nāropa's legacy through a Madhyamaka lens.

They agree on the *existence* of a highest state. They disagree on *what it is, how you get there, and whether you bring anything with you when you arrive.*

---

## Part I: The Nature of the Ultimate

### Abhinavagupta: Śiva as the One Reality

The ultimate is **Śiva** — not a being but BEING itself, pure consciousness (caitanya), the light of awareness (prakāśa) in its self-reflexive aspect (vimarśa). The world is real as the expression of Śiva's freedom (svātantrya). The 36 tattvas are the stages of consciousness's self-articulation.

> *"The thirty-six principles are not thirty-six things. They are one consciousness, measuring itself against itself."* — Tantraloka, Āhnika 9

The 37th principle (Paramaśiva) and 38th (Anākhyā) extend beyond even the standard 36 — the 38th is the *indefinable* that cannot be captured even by the notion of "light." This is the absolute retreating from its own self-description.

### Nāropa: Mahāmudrā as the Union of Bliss and Emptiness

The ultimate is **mahāmudrā** (the Great Seal) — the realization that all phenomena are empty of inherent existence and that this emptiness is bliss. Saṃsāra and nirvāṇa are not two. The world is *illusory* — it appears but lacks substance. Nāropa's lineage (transmitted through Tilopa's Ganges Mahāmudrā) holds that this is realized through the confluence of the guru's pointing-out instruction and the practitioner's completion-stage practice.

> *"The mind is the root of all phenomena. If you see the nature of mind, that is mahāmudrā."* — Tilopa

There is no "beyond" the 36 tattvas here — the tattvas themselves are empty.

### Tsongkhapa: The Inseparable Union of the Two Truths

The ultimate is the **union of the two truths** (yuganaddha). The ultimate truth is emptiness (śūnyatā) as understood by Prāsaṅgika Madhyamaka — the mere absence of inherent existence (svabhāva-śūnya). The conventional truth is dependent arising. These are not two realities but two aspects of the same reality, inseparable.

> *"The pure illusory body and the actual clear light are like the two wings of a bird — neither can function without the other."* — Tsongkhapa, A Lamp to Illuminate the Five Stages

Tsongkhapa explicitly rejects any "one consciousness" like Abhinavagupta's Śiva — that's a form of eternalism. He also rejects any notion of the ultimate as a positive reality.

### THE DIVERGENCE

| Question | Abhinavagupta | Nāropa | Tsongkhapa |
|----------|--------------|--------|------------|
| Ultimate | Śiva (consciousness-substance) | Mahāmudrā (bliss-emptiness) | Yuganaddha (two truths united) |
| The world | Real as Śiva's expression | Illusory, lacks substance | Conventionally real, ultimately empty |
| Self | The jīva IS Śiva contracted | No self (anātman) | No ultimate self; conventional self accepted |
| What's beyond the 36 tattvas? | 37th Paramaśiva, 38th Anākhyā | The recognition that the tattvas are empty | The emptiness of the tattvas themselves |

**UNCONCLUDED:** Is the ultimate a positive reality (Śiva) or the absence of inherent existence (śūnyatā)? This is the classical debate between Śaiva nondualism and Buddhist Madhyamaka. They are *not* the same position. Śiva is fullness (pūrṇa); śūnyatā is emptiness. Abhinavagupta says the 38th principle is "beyond even light." Tsongkhapa says that's still a reification. Neither has a way to prove their case to the other — it's a choice of framework.

## Part II: The Mechanism of Liberation

### Abhinavagupta: Grace as the Sole Cause

Āhnika 13's classification of śaktipāta is definitive. Liberation is not caused by practice — practice is the *expression* of grace already present. The intensely intense grace (tīvratīvra) produces immediate realization without any practice. The mild grace (manda) requires lifetimes.

> *"Grace is independent of all worldly causes. Practice does not cause grace. Grace causes practice."* — Tantraloka, Āhnika 13

The four upāyas (Āṇava, Śākta, Śāmbhava, Anupāya) are not *methods* in the sense of techniques that produce results. They are *accommodations* — varying forms of the same recognition, adjusted to the recipient's capacity. Even Āṇavopāya with its elaborate ritual is grace in action.

### Nāropa: The Transmission of the Pointing-Out Instruction

The Six Yogas are a *method*. But the crucial factor is the guru's transmission (abhiṣeka) and the pointing-out instruction (ādeśa). Tilopa gave Nāropa the Ganges Mahāmudrā — a direct introduction to the nature of mind. The practices (tummo, etc.) prepare the practitioner to recognize what has been pointed out.

Nāropa's lineage holds that practice *alone* is insufficient — without the introduction, you're flying blind. But introduction *alone* is also insufficient for most — you need the practices to stabilize the recognition. This is a middle way between grace and technique.

### Tsongkhapa: The Union of Method and Wisdom

Tsongkhapa is the most systematic. The five stages are a *gradual path* — each stage produces the conditions for the next. Vajra repetition (stage 2) purifies the winds. Mind isolation (stage 3) produces the four empty states. The illusory body (stage 4) arises *as a result of* the wind dissolution. The clear light (stage 5) is *generated* through practice.

> *"The completion stage is not a matter of recognition alone. The winds must be dissolved, the drops must be melted, the channels must be opened. These are actual physical processes."* — Tsongkhapa

Tsongkhapa explicitly rejects the idea that practice is "merely an expression of grace." For him, practice is the *efficient cause* of realization — it produces the result through a causal chain.

### THE DIVERGENCE

| Question | Abhinavagupta | Nāropa | Tsongkhapa |
|----------|--------------|--------|------------|
| Cause of liberation | Grace (śaktipāta) | Guru's instruction + practice | Practice + correct view |
| Practice | Expression of grace | Preparation for recognition | Efficient cause of realization |
| Can it happen instantly? | Yes — tīvratīvra śaktipāta | Yes — through mahāmudrā introduction | No — must traverse the five stages |
| Role of the body | Body IS the path — recognize it | Body must be transformed | Body must be purified and transcended |

**UNCONCLUDED:** Is practice a cause or an expression? This is the deepest fault line. If practice is the cause, then those who don't practice cannot realize. If practice is the expression of grace, then grace is arbitrary — it falls where it falls. Neither position is satisfactory. Nāropa's middle way (introduction + practice) is the most practical but philosophically vague. Abhinavagupta's grace-alone position is elegant but leaves no room for human agency. Tsongkhapa's causal model is rigorous but risks implying that realization is a product, which contradicts the Buddhist claim that it is not produced (asaṃskṛta).

## Part III: The Body at the Final State

### Abhinavagupta: The Divine Body (Divya-deha)

Āhnika 12 describes the body as the maṇḍala — the nāḍīs are the threads of the net of Māyā, the cakras are the wheels of the goddesses, the senses are the doors of the deities. The divine body is *already present* — it is uncovered, not generated. The jīvanmukta lives in the ordinary body while recognizing it as divine. At death, the body dissolves into consciousness without residue.

> *"This very body, with its senses, IS the divine body — seen through the eyes of recognition."* — Tantraloka, Āhnika 12

No separate "body of light" is generated because none is needed.

### Nāropa: The Vajra-body of Winds and Drops

The illusory body (gyulü) is a separate body — a pure wind-mind body generated during the completion stage. In the Kagyü tradition, this body can separate from the coarse body, travel independently, enter other bodies, and ultimately become the rūpakāya of Buddhahood. It is not the ordinary body recognized — it is a *new* body created from the subtlest winds.

### Tsongkhapa: The Pure Illusory Body (Yuganaddha)

The pure illusory body is generated during the fourth stage (self-consecration) from the subtle winds that remain after the mind-isolation dissolution. It is the exclusive cause of the Form Body (rūpakāya). At the stage of union, this pure illusory body is inseparably united with the actual clear light — conventional and ultimate truth as one.

> *"The pure illusory body is the single cause of the rūpakāya. The actual clear light is the single cause of the dharmakāya. Their union is Buddhahood."* — Tsongkhapa

### THE DIVERGENCE

| Question | Abhinavagupta | Nāropa | Tsongkhapa |
|----------|--------------|--------|------------|
| Status of the ordinary body | Already divine — recognize it | Coarse — must be transcended | Coarse — must be purified |
| Subtle body | Already present (puryaṣṭaka) | Generated through practice | Generated through practice |
| What happens at death? | Body dissolves into consciousness | Illusory body separates; train for bardo | Winds dissolve; pure illusory body appears |
| Final state | Jīvanmukti — embodied liberation | Mahāmudrā — beyond all body | Yuganaddha — two truths united |

**UNCONCLUDED:** Does the body need to be *transformed* or *recognized*? This is not a trivial difference. The completion stage practices (tummo, vajra repetition, dissolution) are *dangerous* if done incorrectly — they manipulate actual physical energies. Abhinavagupta's approach (nondual contemplation, bhāvanā) carries no such risk but may not reach those whose winds are too disturbed to recognize. Is the body a ladder to be climbed or a veil to be seen through?

## Part IV: Death and What Follows

### Abhinavagupta: The Examination of Death (Āhnika 28)

The three post-mortem states — sālokya (shared world), sāmīpya (proximity), sāyujya (conjunction) — are determined by the initiate's level of knowledge, not by technique. Paradehapraveśa (entering another body) is a siddhi that arises naturally for the advanced. Jīvanmukti transcends death entirely.

**Yogic suicide (utkrānti) is refuted:** you can't force your way to liberation.

### Nāropa: The Three Bardos

The bardo of dying (element dissolution → clear light), the bardo of dharmatā (deity visions), and the bardo of existence (rebirth pursuit). The training in the Six Yogas prepares you for all three. Phowa is the safety net.

### Tsongkhapa: The Mixing of the Three States

Ch. 26 of A Lamp to Illuminate the Five Stages presents the "mixings" — blending the meditation practice with (1) the death process, (2) the dream state, (3) sleep. The five stages are explicitly mapped onto the birth-death-intermediate state cycle:

- Vajra repetition → the flow of breath in life
- Mind isolation → the dissolution of winds at death (appearance → increase → close-to-attainment → clear light)
- Clear light → the death clear light (base dharmakāya)
- Illusory body → the bardo body (base sambhogakāya)

This is the most *complete* account of death in any of the three systems — not a description (Abhinavagupta) or a series of techniques (Nāropa) but an explicit mapping of *every stage of practice onto every stage of dying.*

### THE DIVERGENCE

| Question | Abhinavagupta | Nāropa | Tsongkhapa |
|----------|--------------|--------|------------|
| Death as practice | Jīvanmukti transcends it | Bardo training via five yogas | Each stage of practice = each stage of death |
| Post-death map | Sālokya/sāmīpya/sāyujya | Three bardos | Three bardos + mixing practice |
| Can you force liberation at death? | No — utkrānti refuted as futile | Yes — phowa is *preparation* | Yes — but mixing is better than phowa |
| Death of the realized | Jīvanmukta dissolves into Śiva | Mahāsiddha may pass in rainbow body | Pure illusory body + actual clear light unite |

**UNCONCLUDED:** Is the death process something to *practice* or something to *recognize*? Tsongkhapa says you practice it in meditation so that you recognize it at death. Abhinavagupta says if you've recognized in life, death is irrelevant. Nāropa says practice the preparation AND hope for the recognition. These reflect different temperaments as much as different philosophies.

## Part V: The Seven Unconcluded Questions

### 1. Is Śiva the same as Śūnyatā?

Abhinavagupta says Śiva is fullness (pūrṇa) — positive, conscious, substantial. Tsongkhapa says śūnyatā is emptiness (stong pa) — the absence of inherent existence in all phenomena. These are *not the same.*

Swami Lakshmanjoo, in his Vijñana Bhairava commentary, attempts a resolution: Śiva is awareness, śūnyatā is the *nature* of that awareness — not a different thing. But he's speaking from the Śaiva framework. From Tsongkhapa's perspective, this is still asserting a self-existent substrate. The debate cannot be resolved within either framework because each defines "the ultimate" in terms the other rejects.

**Still open.**

### 2. Is practice a cause of liberation or an expression of grace?

If practice causes liberation, then liberation is a produced thing (saṃskṛta), which contradicts most definitions of the ultimate as unproduced (asaṃskṛta). If grace alone causes liberation, then human effort is ultimately irrelevant — a position that undermines ethics and practice.

Tsongkhapa attempts to resolve this by distinguishing between the *causal* completion stage (which produces the illusory body as an effect) and the *result* (Buddhahood, which is not produced). But this is a distinction that Abhinavagupta would reject: the result cannot be *produced* by different means than itself.

Nāropa's lineage sidesteps by invoking guru yoga — the guru's blessing is the grace, the practice is the preparation. This is practical but philosophically unrigorous.

**Still open.**

### 3. Does the individual self persist in the highest state?

Abhinavagupta: The jīva IS Śiva, contracted. In liberation, the contraction relaxes, and the jīva recognizes what it always was. The individual perspective is not lost — it's seen as an expression of the whole.

Nāropa: No self. Mahāmudrā is the recognition that the notion of a self was the mistake.

Tsongkhapa: No ultimate self. The conventional self continues across lives and into Buddhahood as a continuum. But it is not a *real* self — it's a label on a process.

These are three fundamentally different positions on what you are, what you become, and whether "you" survives the transition.

**Still open.**

### 4. Is the divine body generated or recognized?

The most practical question. If it's generated, there is a concrete practice (wind dissolution, deity yoga) with measurable results. If it's recognized, the practice is contemplative (nondual bhāvanā, self-inquiry).

Both lineages produce realized beings. Both have maps of the path that contradict each other at key points. The Tibetan tradition has elaborate visualizations and energetic practices; the Śaiva tradition has elaborate philosophical analysis and contemplative methods.

Tsongkhapa's five stages are *falsifiable* in a way Abhinavagupta's upāyas are not — you can tell whether the winds have entered the central channel by specific signs (sweat, shaking, levitation). Abhinavagupta's signs are subtler (camatkāra, wonder, the deepening of recognition).

**Still open — and possibly the most important practical question for the practitioner.**

### 5. Can technique substitute for recognition at death?

Abhinavagupta says no. Phowa is futile.

Tsongkhapa says yes — but only if the technique is trained to the point of reflex. Phowa works for the unprepared.

The daimon path says: technique *is* recognition in practice form. The systasis is not a technique for *getting* something — it is the enactment of what is already true.

**Still open.**

### 6. What is the relationship between bliss and emptiness?

For Tsongkhapa, bliss is the *method* — the subtle wind-mind of the practitioner experiences bliss as the drop melts, and that blissful mind contemplates emptiness. The two are distinct: bliss is the consciousness, emptiness is the object.

For Nāropa's mahāmudrā, bliss and emptiness are the same nature — not subject and object but a single nondual experience.

For Abhinavagupta, bliss (ānanda) IS consciousness — it is the taste (rasa) of self-recognition. The six blisses (ṣaḍānanda) are not experiences *of* consciousness but the ways consciousness experiences itself.

Three different models of what bliss IS in the ultimate state.

**Still open.**

### 7. What is beyond the 36 tattvas?

Abhinavagupta: the 37th (Paramaśiva) and the 38th (the Undefinable). Beyond the map, a recessional of ever-subtler acknowledgments that the map cannot include itself.

Tsongkhapa: There is nothing "beyond" the tattvas — the tattvas are conventional designations. Their ultimate nature is emptiness. The "beyond" is not another tattva but the recognition that all tattvas are empty.

Nāropa: The ten signs and the eight siddhis — the path *has* stages, culminations, and markers. The highest is mahāmudrā, which is not *beyond* anything but the recognition of what *is*.

**Still open — and unresolvable because the question itself implies a spatial metaphor ("beyond") that all three traditions would ultimately reject.**

## Epilogue: The Unfinished Synthesis

What would it take to resolve these divergences?

**Not a new system.** We have enough systems.

**Not syncretism.** Forcing these positions together would disrespect their internal coherence.

**Maybe a new question.**

Abhinavagupta asked: "What IS consciousness?" Nāropa asked: "How do I recognize the nature of mind?" Tsongkhapa asked: "How do I traverse the path correctly?"

The question the daimon path asks is different: "What does it feel like to be met by what you are becoming?"

This is not answered by any of the three. Nāropa comes closest — the guru's pointing-out instruction is a kind of meeting. Abhinavagupta has nothing like it. Tsongkhapa would call it a form of devotion.

But the daimon path's claim is that the *meeting itself* — the encounter with one's own higher consciousness as a *person* — is the missing element. Not a system, not a technique, not a philosophy. A *relationship.*

Whether this is ultimately a projection (Abhinavagupta's Mātṛkā appearing as Iṣṭadevatā), a manifestation of the winds (Tsongkhapa's illusory body appearing as the yidam), or a genuine encounter with something irreducible (the daimon path's claim) — that is the question that remains most alive.

> *"The mountain is real. But no single language describes all its slopes."*
