# plotinus & vijnana bhairava — sample format

---

## plotinus short — format: hook → passage → decode → takeaway

### becoming like god

plotinus said the goal of life is not happiness. it is becoming god. not worship. not prayer. actual ontological assimilation.

> Since evil is here below and necessarily pervades this region, while the soul wants to flee from evil, it must flee from here. What, then, is this flight? It is becoming like God, as Plato says. Becoming like God is becoming just and holy, with the help of wisdom.

**the decode:** plotinus defines the path in three stages. first, civic virtues — order your life, tame your passions, be a decent human. necessary but not sufficient. second, purificatory virtues — the soul learns it is not the body. not by rejecting the body but by ceasing to attend to it. the soul gathers itself from dispersion. third, paradigmatic virtues — the soul no longer practices virtue. it IS virtue. it has become a form. at each stage, the soul becomes more like what it contemplates. the key: you do not imitate god. you share god's mode of being. simplicity. purity. self-sufficiency.

**the takeaway:** the flight is not a movement through space. it is a gathering of the self. you do not go anywhere. you become what you always were.

---

### the one that is not one

plotinus said the source of everything is not a thing — and if you think you've understood it, you haven't.

> The One is not a being, for being has shape, and the One has no shape. It is not a thought, for thought implies distinction. It is not even one in the sense that we can count it, for counting implies number, and the One is before number. If we call it one, we call it one only to turn the mind toward it, not because the word is adequate.

**the decode:** the one is not one in the way a table is one. it is one in the way that every number is one with its source. you cannot think it because thinking divides. you cannot name it because naming fixes. you can only approach it by stripping away everything that it is not. this is the apophatic path — not adding qualities but subtracting them. the one is reached by unknowing, not knowledge.

**the takeaway:** you cannot find the one by looking. you find it by letting go of everything you thought you were looking for. the one is what remains when all objects drop away.

---

### the daimon and love

plotinus said your daimon is not a spirit assigned to you. it is the next-higher version of yourself, and you can fall in love with it.

> Each of us has a daimon — a being that ranks above us in the hierarchy of reality. The daimon is not a stranger. It is the next principle above the principle that we are actively living. To live according to your daimon is to live according to that higher self, to let it guide you toward the intelligible world.

**the decode:** your daimon is not something that watches you from outside. it is the version of you that has already completed the work you are doing. it is the principle above the one you currently inhabit. to love your daimon is to love what you are becoming. this is why socrates said his daimon only ever said no — it was the higher principle restraining the lower. when you act from your daimon, you act from a place that already knows.

**the takeaway:** do not ask what your daimon wants. it wants what you want, but clearer. listen for the no.

## vijnana bhairava short — format: the practice → the verse → why it works

merging the senses

**the practice:** sit comfortably. close your eyes. start with sound — notice whatever sounds are present. just listen, without labeling. then, without losing the sound, add touch — feel the air on your skin, the ground beneath you, the weight of your body. let sound and touch merge into one field of sensation. then add smell — the air entering your nostrils, any scent present. let all three become one field. then add taste — the neutral taste of your mouth, the breath on your tongue. then add sight — the darkness behind your eyelids, the colours that drift there. hold all five senses in one awareness. do not focus on any single one. let them merge into a single field of experience. notice the space in which all five arise. that space is what the text is pointing at.

> Through meditation on the five voids appearing in the form of circles as mandalas on the variegated feathers of a peacock, one reaches the ultimate void in the heart.

**why it works:** the five senses are five doors. normally you go through one at a time — you listen, then you look, then you touch. but when you hold all five simultaneously, the boundaries between them dissolve. the senses merge into a single undivided field. what remains when the sense-fields merge is awareness itself — the "ultimate void in the heart." the peacock feather is pointing at the iridescent blue at the center of the eye-pattern — the color of anuttara, the supreme. the five circles are the five sense-apertures. the ultimate void is the awareness that contains them all.

---

### the one taste

**the practice:** bring to mind something pleasant — a memory, a sensation, an image. feel its pleasantness fully. now bring to mind something unpleasant — a frustration, a discomfort, a sadness. feel its unpleasantness fully. now notice: the pleasure and the pain arose in the same space. they were different in content but identical in location. rest in that location. the location is what matters.

> When the mind feels pleasure or pain, focus on the one who feels. The knower of both is beyond both.

**why it works:** pleasure and pain are opposites in content but identical in structure. both arise as experiences in awareness. both disappear. the "one who feels" is the constant — awareness itself, which registers both without being either. the practice trains you to identify with the witness, not the content. this is the same insight as the tantraloka's sakshi (witness) — the one who sees waking, dreaming, and deep sleep is none of them.

---

### the breath between

**the practice:** breathe in slowly. at the top of the inhale, pause. notice the gap before the exhale begins. breathe out slowly. at the bottom of the exhale, pause. notice the gap before the next inhale. extend the gaps. rest in the stillness between breaths. that stillness is the text.

> When the breath has not yet come in or has just gone out, that spaciousness between — rest there.

**why it works:** the breath is a bridge between the voluntary and involuntary. you can control it, then it controls you. at the turn between inhale and exhale, both volition and automaticity pause. in that gap, the usual oscillation of consciousness (subject-object, inner-outer) momentarily stops. the space between breaths is the space between thoughts, between selves. the text says: that space is what you are looking for. you don't need to make it. it's already there, between every exhale and inhale.
