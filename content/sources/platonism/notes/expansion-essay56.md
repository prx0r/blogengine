# you chose this life

**Essay 56 — Proclus / Republic Commentary (Myth of Er)**

At the end of Plato's *Republic* is a story. A man named Er dies, travels to the afterlife, and watches souls choose their next lives. The choices are devastating — a soul that was good in its last life chooses absolute power in the next and ends up eating its own children. Proclus wrote a commentary on this myth that spans hundreds of pages. He said: you are choosing right now. Every moment is that choice.

The Myth of Er is the final passage of Plato's *Republic*, the capstone to a dialogue about justice. Socrates tells of a Pamphylian warrior named Er who was slain in battle, traveled to the afterlife, and returned to tell what he saw. The soul, after death, undergoes judgment, then journeys through the heavens and the underworld for a thousand years. After this cycle, the souls gather on a plain where the Spindle of Necessity rotates the cosmos. The Fates — Lachesis, Clotho, Atropos — preside. The souls are given the choice of their next lives.

Lots are cast. The order of choice is random. No soul is forced. Each soul selects from a display of lives — every possible human and animal existence. The choices are based on the experience of the previous life, not on divine decree. A soul that lived virtuously but without philosophy might choose poorly. A soul that suffered justly might choose wisely. After the choice, the souls drink from the River of Forgetfulness and are born into their new lives.

Proclus wrote a massive commentary on the *Republic* in which the Myth of Er receives the most extended treatment. For Proclus, the myth describes the structure of every moment — more than a story about a future event. The choice of lives is the structure of decision itself, not something that happens once between incarnations. Every choice you make is a choice of who you will become. Every moment is the plain of Necessity. Every decision is the casting of the lot.

The Spindle of Necessity is the axis of the cosmos, turning the eight celestial spheres. The Fates are not arbitrary powers but the logical structure of causality. Lachesis sings of the past — the accumulated karma of choices already made. Clotho sings of the present — the current moment of decision. Atropos sings of the future — the inevitable consequences of the choice. The three are not three gods. They are the three dimensions of every moral act: what you have done, what you are doing, what you will become.

Proclus maps the Myth of Er onto his entire metaphysical system. The plain where the souls gather is the *mundus imaginalis* — the intermediate world where the soul exists between embodiments, the same intermediate world that Corbin would later describe. The Spindle is the axis of the henads, the chain of divine causation that links the One to the material world. The Fates are the three moments of every level of reality: remaining, procession, and return — the same monē, proodos, and epistrophē that structure the *Elements of Theology*.

The choice of lives is the most radical moment. Plato describes a soul that had lived a virtuous life in its previous incarnation but chose badly because "virtue was a matter of habit, not philosophy." It chose the life of a tyrant, took power, and in its next life devoured its own children. The soul chose from ignorance — not ignorance of facts but ignorance of the meaning of power. It knew what power was but not what power does to the soul.

Proclus draws the lesson: virtue without wisdom is fragile. A soul can be good by habit and still make catastrophic choices. The only protection is philosophy — the direct contemplation of the Forms, the knowledge of the Good that makes right choice natural rather than accidental. The soul that has seen the truth sees through the glittering lie.

The myth is about the endless series of choices that constitute the soul's path through the cosmos — more than a single choice. Every life is a choice. Every moment within a life is a choice. The soul that chooses well in one life may choose poorly in the next if it becomes complacent. The soul that chooses poorly may learn from its suffering and choose better. The Fates spin continuously. The Spindle never stops turning. The choice is always now.

This is why the rivers of forgetfulness are essential. The souls drink from the River of Ameles (Carelessness) and forget their previous lives before being born. Memory would burden them with guilt or pride, turning the new life into a reaction against the old one rather than a genuine choice. Forgetfulness is the condition of freedom — you are free to choose again precisely because you do not remember.

But what you have chosen is inscribed in the structure of your being. The tyrant who devours his children chose that life from ignorance. But the choice was real. The consequences were real. The soul that chose tyranny is not the same soul that chose virtue in the previous life. The soul is the same in essence but transformed by its choices. This is Proclus' insight: the soul is not a static substance. It is a dynamic activity. The soul is what it does. The soul is what it chooses.

Proclus says you are choosing right now — not in some future afterlife, not at the moment of death, but in this very moment, choosing the life you will enter. Every choice is a selection of which life you will become. The soul that chooses anger becomes the life of anger; the soul that chooses compassion becomes the life of compassion; the soul that chooses wisdom becomes the life of wisdom.

The Spindle of Necessity rotates the heavens. The Fates spin the thread. But the choice is yours. The lot is cast in random order — you did not choose the circumstances of your birth, your talents, your time and place. But within those circumstances, you choose. And within that choice, you become.

This is the meaning of the myth for Proclus — a description of the metaphysical structure of freedom, more than a fairytale about the afterlife. The soul is the active chooser of its own destiny rather than a passive recipient of fate. The Fates register; the Spindle turns; the River of Forgetfulness clears the ground for a new decision.

Proclus' commentary on the Myth of Er is the most detailed ancient treatment of reincarnation, freedom, and moral responsibility. It brings together the entire Neoplatonic system — the hierarchy of being, the triadic structure of procession and return, the role of the henads, the function of theurgy — and shows that all of it converges on a single point: the soul's freedom to choose.

The soul that was good in its last life chooses absolute power and eats its own children. The soul that suffered justly chooses wisdom and becomes a philosopher. The soul that has seen the truth chooses the life of contemplation and ascends toward the One. Every possibility is present. The display of lives contains every option. The choice is yours.

The soul that chooses wisely does not choose a life. It chooses a capacity. It chooses a way of being that will make good choices possible in the future. The soul that chooses philosophy chooses the capacity to discriminate between the true good and the apparent good. The soul that chooses tyranny chooses the inability to see beyond its own power. The choice is not about what you will have. It is about what you will become.

The Myth of Er is a warning more than a consolation — every choice has consequences that extend across millennia. The soul that chooses poorly punishes itself by becoming the kind of being that will choose poorly again, rather than being punished by an external judge. The soul that chooses well rewards itself by becoming the kind of being that will choose well again. The judgment *is* the choice, not something that comes after it.

Proclus develops this through his theory of the soul's vehicle (τὸ ὄχημα). The soul does not enter embodiment naked. It carries with it the vehicle that it has built through its choices. The vehicle is the subtle body that mediates between the soul and its physical embodiment, and it is shaped by the moral and intellectual quality of the soul's choices. The soul that has chosen wisely has a luminous vehicle that makes further wisdom possible. The soul that has chosen poorly has a dark and heavy vehicle that makes further wisdom difficult.

The vehicle is built through time but exists outside time. It is the accumulated karma of the soul, not as punishment but as structure. You cannot escape what you have become, but you can choose what you will become next. Every moment is a new choice. Every choice reshapes the vehicle. Every vehicle determines the range of the next choice.

This is the inner meaning of the rivers of forgetfulness. The souls drink not to forget their past lives as a punishment but to make the next choice genuine. If you remembered the consequences of your past choices with perfect clarity, you would not choose freely — you would choose from fear or from calculation. Forgetfulness is the condition of freedom. It clears the ground for the next act of becoming.

The soul that has attained wisdom drinks from a different river — the river of memory that flows from the source of all being. The philosopher-king who has seen the Form of the Good can remember everything and still choose rightly. Forgetfulness is for those still in the making; the wise soul has graduated beyond it.

Proclus' commentary on the Myth of Er is the most detailed ancient treatment of reincarnation, freedom, and moral responsibility. It brings together the entire Neoplatonic system — the hierarchy of being, the triadic structure of procession and return, the role of the henads, the function of theurgy — and shows that all of it converges on a single point: the soul's freedom to choose. The Spindle is the axis of the cosmos. The Fates are the laws of causality. The River of Forgetfulness is the condition of temporal existence. And the choice is yours.

At the end of the *Republic*, Plato does not offer a doctrine. He offers a myth. And Proclus, centuries later, shows that the myth is not an allegory of something else. It is the literal truth of every moment. You are on the plain of Necessity right now. The Spindle is turning. The Fates are singing. The choice is before you. Choose wisely.
