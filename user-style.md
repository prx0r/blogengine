# user style

learned from 56 expansion essay titles + content edits.

## titles

lowercase. short. punchy. reads like something you'd say to a friend across a table.

things that work:
- "you died already" — direct provocative claim, no hedging
- "pain is juice" — absurd, memorable, makes you pause
- "the angel you've been ignoring" — "you" address, implies something obvious you missed
- "the stones are watching you" — animism as threat, makes the world alive
- "you are made of light" — declarative, no qualification
- "god is incomplete without you" — reverses the expected power dynamic
- "one thing looking at itself" — captures the whole philosophy in a phrase
- "the path chose you" — flips the expected direction of agency

things that don't:
- "the pulse you've never felt" — generic, sounds like every other spiritual title
- "why pain actually tastes good" — forced, loses the elegance of "why pain is actually beautiful"
- "the X that Y" formula repeated — "the light that became your body", "the body that travels between worlds", etc. they blur together
- anything that sounds like a lecture title ("the art and science of causing change")

rule: each title should feel like it could only belong to that essay. if you can swap titles between essays without noticing, they're not distinct enough.

## voice

the reader should feel like someone found something and is showing it to them — not like someone explaining something someone else found.

this is the difference between:
- "the pulse you've never felt" (lecture about a thing)
- "you died already" (someone telling you a secret)

## structure

the quotes carry the whole thing. commentary is just the space between them.

- pull quotes into blockquotes so they breathe
- cut any commentary that restates what the quote just said
- replace explanation with invitational framing (a question, a pause, "let that land")
- no throat-clearing: dates, publication history, biographical setup go in footnotes or get cut

pattern proven by the 200k ibn arabi video:
riddle → metaphor → primary source → paradox → return to riddle

our essays add more primary source density — that's the advantage.

## what to avoid

- "this is x, this is y" declarative explaining
- "not x but y" corrective tone
- short staccato sentences with hard full stops (bad for tts)
- academic framing ("in this essay we will explore")
- titles that follow the same grammatical template

## what to keep from our originals

- the strong ending that circles back transformed
- concrete texture language (thrum, whet, flint, salt, gravid)
- the hook → metaphor → source → paradox → return arc
- primary source density — no one else does this

## tts flow

write for the ear, not the eye. the voice resets on every period.

- em-dashes over periods for pivots
- comma chains over full stops for connected thoughts
- one short punchy sentence surrounded by longer ones reads as emphasis
- three short sentences in a row reads as choppy
- concrete nouns land better in speech than abstract ones
