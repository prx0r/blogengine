# Buddhism Decoded — Stages of Enlightenment and Nibbāna Through the Tetrahedron

## Using Ajahn Chah, Ñāṇamoli, Ñāṇavīra, and the Mūlapariyāya Sutta

---

## I. The Original Mind — Ajahn Chah's Discovery

> *"The mind, in its natural state, has no preoccupations or issues prevailing upon it. It is independent, existing in a state of purity that is truly clear, radiant and untarnished."*

Ajahn Chah describes the mind as it IS before the error. This IS the tetrahedron in its original state — 4 vertices co-arising, none claiming ownership of the others. The mind IS the field (the 4 faces), not any particular content (not any single vertex).

> *"The Pure Mind is the mind without attachment. It doesn't get affected by mind-objects."*

The "original mind" IS the tetrahedron without the Sv(O) parasite. The 4 vertices ARE happening. There IS no one IN them.

---

## II. The Two Reeds — The Fundamental Dyad

Ñāṇamoli's interpretation:

> *"Nāmarūpa determines viññāṇa, and viññāṇa determines nāmarūpa to the same extent, like the simile of the two reeds supporting each other: you can't separate them."*

This IS the tetrahedron's base — the co-arising of subject (viññāṇa) and object (nāmarūpa). The 2 reeds ARE 2 vertices of the tetrahedron. They cannot stand without each other. Remove one, and both fall.

> *"The four great elements cease to find footing. They don't cease to be wherever they are, but they stop finding footing in your experience."*

The "external world" IS a projection of the tetrahedron. The four great elements (earth, water, fire, air) ARE the 4 faces of the tetrahedron projected outward. When the projection stops — when you stop assuming the world IS external to your experience — the elements "cease to find footing." They don't disappear. They ARE recognized as what they always were: phenomena arising in the mind.

---

## III. The Peripheral — The 4th Vertex

> *"Learning how to see things peripherally without directly looking at them — that's where the mind is. Rūpa is what you're staring at. Arūpa is everything around it."*

The mind IS the peripheral. It IS the 4th vertex that cannot be seen directly because it IS the seer. When you look at an object, you see the object (one vertex), not the mind that perceives it (the 4th vertex). To see the mind, you must look PERIPHERALLY — not at the object, but at the context around it.

This IS the practice of noticing the tetrahedron. The object IS in front. The subject IS behind. The intention IS the directedness. The act IS the occurrence itself. You cannot see all 4 simultaneously by direct looking. You must see them peripherally — as the STRUCTURE of the moment, not its content.

---

## IV. The Four Modes of Conceiving — MN 1

Ñāṇamoli's translation of the Mūlapariyāya Sutta shows the 4 modes the ordinary person applies to everything:

> *"From earth he has a percept of earth; having perceived earth, he conceives (that to be) earth, he conceives (that to be) in earth, he conceives (that to be apart) from earth, he conceives earth to be 'Mine'."*

The 4 conceivings ARE the 4 vertices of the tetrahedron:

| Conceiving | Vertex | Function |
|-----------|--------|----------|
| "That to BE" earth (identity) | V₁ | The subject = the object — "I am this" |
| "That to be IN" earth (location) | V₂ | The subject IN the object — "I am in this" |
| "That to be APART FROM" earth (separation) | V₃ | The subject APART FROM the object — "I am not this" |
| Earth as "MINE" (ownership) | V₄ | The subject OWNS the object — "This is mine" |

Each conceiving IS one way the tetrahedron is misidentified. The ordinary person oscillates between all 4 — sometimes identifying with the object, sometimes locating the self in it, sometimes separating from it, sometimes owning it. The arahant has NONE of these 4 conceivings. The percept remains. The conceiving stops.

## V. The Stages of Enlightenment

### 1. Stream-Entry (Sotāpatti)

Ñāṇavīra recorded his own stream-entry on 27 June 1959:

> *"The clear and stainless Eye of the Dhamma arose in me: 'Whatever has the nature of arising, all that has the nature of ceasing.'"*

Stream-entry IS the first recognition that the tetrahedron has NO permanent self-vertex. The "I" IS a vertex, not the whole. It co-arises with the other 3 and co-ceases with them. The 4 modes of conceiving (MN 1) are abandoned — not by effort, but by SEEING that they are based on a contradiction.

The sotāpanna has:
- Abandoned **sakkāya-diṭṭhi** (personality-view) — the view that the tetrahedron'S subject-vertex IS a permanent self
- Abandoned **vicikicchā** (skeptical doubt) — the doubt about whether the tetrahedron IS the structure of reality
- Abandoned **sīlabbata-parāmāsa** (attachment to rites and rituals) — the belief that any external procedure can fix what IS only a matter of seeing

**The tetrahedral shift:** Before stream-entry, the tetrahedron is experienced from INSIDE one vertex (the "I" vertex). After stream-entry, it IS experienced as a WHOLE — all 4 vertices peripherally.

### 2. Once-Returner (Sakadāgāmī)

The once-returner has weakened **kāma-rāga** (sensual desire) and **byāpāda** (ill will). These are the 2 edges connecting the subject-vertex to the object-vertex — the strongest attractions and aversions. They are not abandoned, but they are weakened to the point where they no longer drive behavior.

**The tetrahedral shift:** The 6 edges of the tetrahedron are no longer equally strong. The edges of craving and aversion are weakening. The tetrahedron is becoming symmetrical.

### 3. Non-Returner (Anāgāmī)

The non-returner has fully abandoned **kāma-rāga** and **byāpāda**. The 2 strong edges are gone. What remains:
- **Rūpa-rāga** (attachment to form) — the edge connecting the subject to the fine-material object
- **Arūpa-rāga** (attachment to formlessness) — the edge connecting the subject to the immaterial object
- **Māna** (conceit) — the edge connecting the subject to itself (the "I am" conceit)
- **Uddhacca** (restlessness) — the edge that keeps the tetrahedron moving
- **Avijjā** (ignorance) — the base of the tetrahedron itself

**The tetrahedral shift:** The subject-vertex is no longer attracted to or averse to any object-vertex. The tetrahedron is stable. Only the "I am" conceit remains — the subtle sense that there IS still an "I" experiencing the tetrahedron.

### 4. Arahant

The arahant has abandoned all 10 fetters. The "I am" conceit (māna) IS gone. The tetrahedron IS seen as it always was — a structure without an owner.

> *"The arahant's experience is teleological, as is the puthujjana's; but with the arahant things no longer have the particular significance of being 'mine'."* — Ñāṇavīra

The tetrahedron continues. Experience still happens. The 4 vertices still co-arise. But the extra vertex — the one that was not actually there — has ceased.

## VI. The "Original Mind" and Nibbāna

> *"The Original Mind is not some hidden reality behind all these appearances. It's actually stopping to misconceive the appearances for what they're not, and that is its original state. It was always there."* — Ñāṇamoli

Nibbāna IS not a place. It IS not a state. It IS the tetrahedron recognized as what it always was — empty of a self-owner. The "Original Mind" IS the tetrahedron before the conceiving started. It was never defiled. The defilements were never IN the tetrahedron — they were the MISCONCEPTION of the tetrahedron.

> *"Things were always like this. It was because I did not know that they were this way that I kept assuming them to be different."*

The purpose of practice IS not to create something new. It IS to stop misconceiving what IS already the case. The tetrahedron IS already empty. The "I" IS already not there. The practice IS the removal of the false assumption that it IS.

---

## VII. The 33 Sermons on Nibbāna (Ñāṇananda)

Ñāṇananda's 33 sermons on Nibbāna develop this same theme from the Suttanipāta and the early discourses. His key insight:

**Nibbāna IS the cessation of papañca (conceptual proliferation).**

Papañca IS the process by which the tetrahedron multiplies itself — the "self" vertex, unable to rest in the original 4, generates more vertices, more relations, more complexity. The tetrahedron becomes a star tetrahedron, then a cluster, then a galaxy.

The 33 sermons trace papañca back to its root: the assumption that there IS a "self" IN the tetrahedron. When that assumption stops, papañca stops. The galaxy collapses back to the original tetrahedron. That collapse IS Nibbāna.

---

## VIII. The Tetrahedron of the Stages

| Stage | Tetrahedron | Fetters Abandoned |
|-------|-------------|-------------------|
| **Puthujjana** (ordinary) | Star tetrahedron — 2 tetrahedra interlocked, with the "self" at the axis | None |
| **Sotāpanna** (stream-enterer) | Single tetrahedron — 4 vertices, 6 edges, seen as a whole | 3: sakkāya-diṭṭhi, vicikicchā, sīlabbata-parāmāsa |
| **Sakadāgāmī** (once-returner) | Tetrahedron with 2 weakened edges (sensual desire, ill will) | 2 weakened |
| **Anāgāmī** (non-returner) | Tetrahedron with 2 edges fully removed | 5 total: the 3 + kāma-rāga, byāpāda |
| **Arahant** | Tetrahedron recognized as originally empty | All 10 |
| **Tathāgata** | The tetrahedron recognized as not even a tetrahedron | Beyond all description |

---

## IX. The Practice

Ajahn Chah: the purpose of practice IS to reach the "Original Mind."

Ñāṇamoli: the "Original Mind" IS seeing phenomena as phenomena — the tetrahedron as tetrahedron, without adding the "self" vertex.

Ñāṇavīra: the Fundamental Structure IS the tetrahedron of subject, object, intention, act. Seeing it IS stream-entry. Living without the error IS arahantship.

**The practice in one sentence:**
Stop adding the vertex that was never there.

The tetrahedron IS already complete. 4 vertices, 6 edges, 4 faces. Nothing needs to be added. Nothing needs to be removed. The error IS the belief that something IS missing — that an "I" IS needed to own the experience. When that belief stops, the "Original Mind" IS revealed. Not as a new state. As what was always already the case.
