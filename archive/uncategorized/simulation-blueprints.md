Companion Blueprint 13 — Plutarch’s On the Sign / Daimonion of Socrates
Source identification

Work: Plutarch, De genio Socratis / On the Sign of Socrates / On the Daimonion of Socrates
Author: Plutarch of Chaeronea
Stream: Middle Platonism, Socratic daimon, divine signs, liberation, ethical interpretation, Pythagorean-Platonic myth
Public access: LacusCurtius hosts the full English translation and Greek text; the site notes the Loeb translation by De Lacy and Einarson is public-domain due to non-renewal.

Why this matters for Imaginarium:
Apuleius gave the daimonic middle as doctrine. Plutarch gives the daimonic sign inside a dramatic field of political liberation, ethical timing, dreams, omens, interpretation, and soul-myth.

Core formula:

the city is enslaved → conspirators wait for the right sign → Socrates’ daimon becomes the model of divine guidance → signs require purified interpretation → liberation outside mirrors liberation inside → myth opens the soul’s cosmic destiny.

Structural map
Unit	Function	Imaginarium use
Frame: Theban liberation	Political-historical drama	External liberation as mirror of inner liberation
Grave/sign opening	Waiting for divine indication	Timing, omen, threshold
Debate on signs	Are omens real or superstition?	Discrimination of signal vs noise
Socrates’ daimonion	Central philosophical problem	Personal guidance as divine sign
Simmias / Pythagorean material	Philosophical authority	Link to Thebes, Pythagorean-Platonic lineage
Ethical preparation	Signs depend on interpreter quality	Purified soul receives cleaner signal
Mythic / eschatological material	Soul’s larger destiny	Daimon as cosmic not just psychological
Liberation action	Plot and philosophy converge	Right timing becomes embodied action
A modern SAPERE volume describes the dialogue as combining historical romance with serious philosophical and religious discussion, set around the liberation of Thebes from Spartan domination in winter 379/8 BCE.
Section functions
1. Thebes under occupation

The dialogue is not a floating essay about Socrates. It is set around the liberation of Thebes.

Function: daimonic guidance is tested inside danger, timing, courage, and civic action.

Imaginarium use: inner guidance is not escapist. It must eventually act in the world.

2. Waiting for a sign

The opening atmosphere includes waiting for divine indication.

Function: introduces a world where timing matters and reality communicates.

Imaginarium use: not all action is willpower; some action waits for the correct opening.

3. Signs and interpretation

The dialogue debates whether omens and signs are meaningful or irrational.

Function: establishes discernment. Signs exist, but not everyone reads them cleanly.

Imaginarium use: central to the project: signal is not the same as fantasy, projection, anxiety, or appetite.

A Duke-hosted study summarizes one key movement: signs and oracles are accepted as means by which the daimonion communicates, but interpretation depends on the interpreter’s nature and training.

4. Socrates’ daimonion

Socrates’ sign is the exemplary case. It is not a continuous voice giving doctrine. It appears as a divine check, warning, or prohibition.

Function: personal guidance as interruption and restraint.

Imaginarium use: the daimon often says “not this” before it says “become this.”

5. Ethical quality of the receiver

The dialogue repeatedly links divine communication to character. The sign is not mechanically available.

Function: daemonology becomes ethics.

Imaginarium use: signal quality depends on psychic hygiene, courage, restraint, and truthfulness.

6. Pythagorean / Simmias line

Simmias, associated with Socrates and Thebes, gives philosophical weight to the daimon discussion.

Function: connects Socratic daimonology to Pythagorean-Platonic soul doctrine.

Imaginarium use: personal guidance sits inside a larger metaphysics of soul.

7. Mythic soul horizon

The work opens beyond politics into soul-myth and cosmic destiny.

Function: daimonic guidance is not merely practical advice; it belongs to the soul’s larger itinerary.

Imaginarium use: the personal daimon is a local sign of a cosmic orientation.

8. Liberation as enacted philosophy

The philosophical discussion and political action are intertwined.

Function: right interpretation becomes right timing; right timing becomes liberation.

Imaginarium use: daimonic knowledge must become embodied action.

Coverage clusters
Cluster 1 — Political liberation frame

Owns: Theban setting
Use: external captivity / internal liberation.

Cluster 2 — Omen and sign

Owns: grave/sign opening and omen debate
Use: timing, threshold, reality as communicative.

Cluster 3 — Socratic daimonion

Owns: central discussion
Use: personal divine check, restraint, guidance.

Cluster 4 — Interpreter quality

Owns: ethical training passages
Use: signal-to-noise, purification, discrimination.

Cluster 5 — Pythagorean-Platonic lineage

Owns: Simmias and soul material
Use: daimon inside broader soul metaphysics.

Cluster 6 — Mythic afterlife / soul destiny

Owns: eschatological material
Use: cosmic scale of guidance.

Cluster 7 — Action and timing

Owns: liberation plot
Use: when signal becomes deed.

Refined non-overlapping essay sequence
1. The City Must Be Freed Before the Soul Can Speak

Plan:

Begin with Thebes under Spartan domination.
The dialogue fuses philosophy with political danger.
Liberation is not just metaphor, but it also becomes symbolic.
Imaginarium angle: daemonology is tested in action, not mood.
2. Waiting for the Sign

Plan:

Explore the opening atmosphere of divine indication.
Action is suspended until the right sign appears.
This creates a metaphysics of timing.
Imaginarium use: not every impulse is the moment; the daimonic often concerns timing.
3. Signal, Omen, or Projection?

Plan:

Debate over signs and superstition.
Signs require interpretation.
Bad interpreters corrupt true signals.
Imaginarium angle: build a serious discrimination model for dreams, omens, inner checks, synchronicities.
4. Socrates’ Sacred No

Plan:

Socrates’ daimonion often restrains rather than instructs.
It protects him from wrong action or wrong speech.
The daimon is not ego-flattery.
Bridge back to Phaedrus and Apuleius.
5. The Interpreter Must Become Worthy

Plan:

Ethical character determines signal quality.
Courage, moderation, philosophical training, and habit matter.
The same sign can become wisdom or delusion depending on the receiver.
Imaginarium angle: psychic hygiene is epistemology.
6. Simmias and the Pythagorean Thread

Plan:

Show why Thebes and Simmias matter.
The Socratic daimon belongs to a lineage of soul-doctrine.
Pythagorean elements bring number, purity, reincarnation, and cosmic order into the background.
Use carefully; do not turn this into generic Pythagoreanism.
7. The Daimon and the Soul’s Larger Journey

Plan:

Move into the mythic / eschatological material.
The daimon is not just practical intuition.
It points toward the soul’s wider postmortem and cosmic itinerary.
Bridge to Republic X, Greenbaum, and later astrological daimon.
8. When Philosophy Becomes the Right Deed

Plan:

The liberation action and philosophical discussion converge.
Right sign, right soul, right moment, right action.
The daimonic is complete only when embodied.
Imaginarium angle: the guide does not replace action; it makes action exact.
Cut / merge / split decisions

Keep separate:

Socrates’ daimonion and general sign interpretation. The first is the exemplar; the second is the broader epistemology.
Political liberation and soul-myth. One grounds the dialogue dramatically; the other expands the metaphysics.
Ethical receiver-quality and omen debate. One asks whether signs exist; the other asks who can read them.

Merge if shorter:

“Waiting for the Sign” and “Signal, Omen, or Projection?” can merge.
Simmias/Pythagorean thread and soul journey can merge.

Split if expanding:

Socrates’ daimonion can split into:
prohibition and restraint
divine communication
comparison with Apuleius, Maximus of Tyre, Plato
Overlap warnings
Plutarch vs Apuleius: Apuleius gives daimonic ontology; Plutarch gives dramatic, ethical, and interpretive daimonology.
Plutarch vs Greenbaum: Plutarch is not technical astrology. Use him for daimonic signs and soul-myth, then bridge later.
Omen-reading vs superstition: do not make the text credulous. Its whole usefulness lies in discrimination.
Political liberation vs inner liberation: keep both. Do not reduce Thebes to allegory.
Daimon as inner voice: too modern/flat. Plutarch preserves a religious-philosophical middle.
Missing-content review

Potential weak spots:

Skipping the Theban plot. The dialogue is not just a daemonology essay.
Reducing the daimonion to intuition. It is divine sign, ethical restraint, and cosmic communication.
Ignoring interpreter quality. This is the most useful practical insight.
Forgetting timing. Signs are about when, not only what.
Over-importing astrology. Keep astrology as later bridge, not as Plutarch’s direct method.

Companion Blueprint 14 — Macrobius’ Commentary on the Dream of Scipio
Source identification

Work: Macrobius, Commentary on Cicero’s Dream of Scipio
Author: Ambrosius Theodosius Macrobius, late antique Latin Platonist, c. 5th century CE
Stream: Latin Neoplatonism, dream-vision, cosmic ascent, soul descent, planetary spheres, number, music, immortality
Base text: Cicero’s Dream of Scipio, preserved through Macrobius’ commentary.
Access note: William Harris Stahl’s English translation is copyrighted, though available through libraries/Internet Archive lending. Public-domain summaries and older Latin/secondary materials are usable, but avoid heavy quotation from modern translations. Internet Archive lists the 1952 Columbia edition; summaries describe the work as a Neoplatonic commentary on Cicero’s dream, covering dreams, number, soul, astronomy, music, and cosmology.

Why this matters for Imaginarium

Macrobius is a perfect post-Proclus bridge because he turns a short Roman dream-vision into a full cosmic encyclopedia of soul, dream, number, planetary order, music of the spheres, and ascent.

Clean formula:

dream opens → ancestor-guide appears → soul sees cosmos from above → earthly fame shrinks → planetary spheres reveal order → number and music structure reality → soul descends through the heavens → philosophical virtue prepares return.

Structural map
Zone	Function	Imaginarium use
Cicero’s dream frame	Scipio receives revelation in sleep	Dream as imaginal access
Dream taxonomy	Sorts true dreams from false/ordinary dreams	Signal discrimination
Cosmic vision	Earth seen from above	De-centering ego/world-image
Soul immortality	Virtuous souls return to heaven	Ethical cosmology
Planetary spheres	Ordered heavens around Earth	Astrology/cosmology bridge
Music of the spheres	Cosmic motion as harmony	Ficino/music bridge
Number symbolism	Arithmetic as sacred structure	Sacred geometry / cosmic code
Soul descent	Soul enters generation through cosmic levels	Path of incarnation
Virtue and return	Civic/philosophical virtue leads upward	Practice as cosmic re-alignment
Section functions
1. Dream as threshold

Macrobius begins by treating Cicero’s dream seriously, not as random sleep-noise. The dream is an imaginal vehicle for philosophical revelation.

Function: establishes dream as a legitimate mode of knowing.
Imaginarium use: the dream is a rendering-space where higher order can instruct the soul.

2. Sorting dreams

Macrobius famously classifies dreams, distinguishing meaningful visionary dreams from confused or ordinary dream residues.

Function: prevents dream-work from becoming credulous fantasy.
Imaginarium use: essential for signal/noise discrimination in active imagination, scrying, dream temple practice, and imaginal work.

3. Ancestor-guide and revelation

Scipio is instructed by his ancestor, Africanus. The guide is familial, heroic, and transpersonal: not merely “inner monologue,” but a figure of lineage and cosmic memory.

Function: revelation arrives through a guide-image.
Imaginarium use: imaginal figures can function as mediators of orientation.

4. Earth from above

Scipio sees Earth from the heavens. Human politics, fame, and ambition become tiny.

Function: cosmic vision deconditions social obsession.
Imaginarium use: the ego-world shrinks when seen from the soul’s higher vantage.

5. Planetary spheres

The heavens are arranged in ordered spheres around Earth: Moon, Mercury, Venus, Sun, Mars, Jupiter, Saturn, and the stellar heaven. One teaching summary of the Dream describes this concentric order and the fiery/divine upper sphere.

Function: the cosmos becomes a layered ascent/descent map.
Imaginarium use: bridge to astrology dashboard, but at the level of cosmic order, not daily mood content.

6. Music of the spheres

Cosmic motion produces harmony. Human ears cannot easily hear it because they are habituated to it, but the cosmos is mathematically musical.

Function: order is not only spatial but harmonic.
Imaginarium use: bridges Plato, Ficino, planetary music, entrainment, subtle alignment.

7. Number as metaphysical structure

Macrobius uses number symbolism to explain order, proportion, and cosmic arrangement.

Function: number becomes more than quantity; it becomes intelligible architecture.
Imaginarium use: sacred geometry / mathematical rendering layer.

8. Soul descent

The soul descends from the heavens into embodiment and receives conditions through cosmic levels.

Function: incarnation becomes cosmological, not merely biological.
Imaginarium use: strong bridge to Chaldean soul-vehicle, Greenbaum, and planetary vocation.

9. Virtue and return

The dream is not abstract cosmology. Scipio is told that public virtue, justice, and philosophical orientation matter for the soul’s postmortem destiny.

Function: cosmic vision becomes ethical demand.
Imaginarium use: ascent requires lived order, not only symbolic knowledge.

Refined non-overlapping essay sequence
1. The Dream as Philosophical Portal

Plan:

Introduce Cicero’s dream and Macrobius’ commentary.
Dream is not treated as private fantasy.

Imaginarium angle: dream as legitimate imaginal interface.
2. Not Every Dream Is a Message

Plan:

Use this for imaginal hygiene.
Imaginarium angle: the system needs filters before interpretation.
3. The Ancestor as Daimonic Guide

Plan:

Ancestor-image combines memory, authority, fate, and cosmic orientation.
Compare carefully with Socrates’ daimon: Africanus instructs; Socrates’ daimon often restrains.
Imaginarium angle: guide-figures differ by function.
4. Seeing Earth from the Sphere of the Stars

Plan:

Cosmic vision shrinks earthly ambition.
Fame, empire, and reputation become local illusions.
This is the Roman version of cave-exit scale shock.
Imaginarium angle: higher perspective de-renders social hypnosis.
5. The Planetary Ladder

Plan:

Avoid deterministic astrology; keep it cosmic and pedagogical.
6. The Music No One Hears

Plan:

The cosmos moves by proportion.
Humans are deaf to constant harmony through habituation.
Bridge to Ficino’s musical magic later, without repeating Ficino blueprint.
7. Number as the Hidden Grammar of the Cosmos

Plan:

Number explains relation, scale, proportion, and intelligible order.
Macrobius becomes medieval transmitter of Platonic number-cosmology.
Imaginarium use: sacred geometry as structural, not decorative.
8. The Soul’s Descent Through the Heavens

Plan:

Imaginarium angle: birth is entry through layered rendering.
9. Virtue as the Return Path

Plan:

Action in the world affects the soul’s return.
The goal is not escapism but rightly ordered participation.
Imaginarium angle: the ascent must come back as ethical life.
10. Macrobius as the Medieval Cosmological Interface

Plan:

Dream, soul, number, music, planets, virtue become one portable cosmological system.

Imaginarium angle: Macrobius is a transmission-node between ancient Platonism and later symbolic cosmology.

Overlap warnings
Macrobius vs Plato’s Myth of Er: both end with cosmic soul-destiny, but Er emphasizes postmortem choice; Scipio emphasizes dream revelation, cosmic scale, and virtue.
Macrobius vs Greenbaum: Macrobius gives planetary cosmology and soul descent, not technical lots.
Macrobius vs Ficino: music and planets are a bridge, but Macrobius is cosmological-commentarial, not medical-magical.
Dream work risk: do not treat every dream as revelation. Macrobius is useful because he sorts dream-types.
Civic virtue: do not skip it. The work is Roman-Platonic: cosmic ascent is tied to duty and justice.

Ignoring number/music. These are key to its medieval influence.
Over-importing astrology. Use planetary spheres as cosmological structure, then bridge later.
Forgetting Cicero. Macrobius comments on a Roman political dream, not a free-floating occult manual.

Companion Blueprint 15 — Sallustius’ On the Gods and the World
Source identification

Work: On the Gods and the World / On the Gods and the Cosmos
Author: Sallustius / Salutius, 4th-century Neoplatonic pagan philosopher, associated with Emperor Julian’s circle
Stream: late pagan Platonism, mythic theology, divine hierarchy, providence, fate, ritual, world-affirming metaphysics
Public access: Thomas Taylor’s translation is public-domain and available on Wikisource; the 1793 edition is also catalogued through HathiTrust / Google Books / Archive scans.

Why this matters for Imaginarium

This is the clean late-antique “operating manual” for reading pagan myth and ritual philosophically. After Proclus’ heavy metaphysical architecture, Sallustius is compact, usable, and perfect for the site:

gods are eternal → myths conceal divine truths → the cosmos is ordered and imperishable → soul descends and returns → providence and fate operate at different levels → ritual maintains sympathy between worlds → the world is not evil but a divine image.

Structural map
Section	Function	Imaginarium use
1. What the hearer must be like	Prepares ethical/intellectual receiver	Signal quality and worthiness
2. What gods are	Defines gods as eternal, incorporeal, ungenerated	Anti-flat theology
3. Myths	Why divine truths are hidden in symbolic stories	Imaginal hermeneutics
4. Kinds of myths	Theological, physical, psychic, material, mixed	Myth-tagging taxonomy
5. First cause	God/source beyond ordinary grasp	Source beyond rendering
6. Intellect / soul / cosmos	Ordered hierarchy	Reality stack
7. Eternity of the world	World as imperishable divine order	Anti-world-hatred
8. Providence, fate, fortune	Different layers of causality	Dashboard/fate logic
9. Virtue and vice	Soul’s alignment or disorder	Practice layer
10. Worship and sacrifice	Ritual as participation in divine order	Theurgic bridge
11. Transmigration / afterlife	Soul consequences and return	Destiny arc
Coverage clusters
Cluster 1 — Worthiness of the receiver

The text opens with the kind of person who can understand divine matters.

Use: theology depends on the state of the soul. This keeps Imaginarium from becoming symbol-consumption.

Cluster 2 — Gods as eternal principles

Sallustius defines gods as ungenerated, incorporeal, not dependent on place, and not subject to change.

Use: gods are not local fantasy characters; they are metaphysical powers.

Cluster 3 — Myth as sacred concealment

Myths are not childish errors. They veil truths so the unready do not profane them and the ready are drawn inward.

Use: direct source for Imaginarium’s “image as compressed metaphysics” method.

Cluster 4 — Fivefold myth taxonomy

Theological, physical, psychic, material, mixed.

Use: extremely useful for tagging essays/art: is this myth about gods, cosmos, soul, matter, or several at once?

Cluster 5 — World-affirming pagan Platonism

The cosmos is not evil trash. It is ordered, divine, and worth reverent interpretation.

Use: guardrail against “escape the world” Gnosticism.

Cluster 6 — Providence, fate, fortune

Sallustius separates divine providence, fate, and chance/fortune-like event-field.

Use: bridge to Greenbaum: fate is layered, not crude determinism.

Cluster 7 — Ritual and sacrifice

Ritual does not “feed” gods literally. It aligns the human/cosmic order through sympathy and participation.

Use: theurgy-lite: ritual as symbolic participation.

Imaginarium angle: re-rendering means transfiguration, not contempt.

1. Source identity

Core sources: Iain McGilchrist, The Master and His Emissary; The Matter with Things.
Text type: psychiatry, neuroscience synthesis, philosophy of attention, cultural diagnosis, metaphysics.
Core function: McGilchrist gives Imaginarium a theory of attention as world-disclosure: different modes of attention do not merely inspect the world; they help constitute the kind of world that appears.

His official site summarizes the core point: each hemisphere pays a different kind of attention to the world, and the kind of attention we pay transforms the world we perceive.

2. Why this source matters for Imaginarium

Varela gives: train the receiver.
McGilchrist gives: the receiver has modes of disclosure.

For your thesis:

ego is not just a false belief. Ego is a mode of attention: narrow, grasping, detached, fragmenting, map-obsessed.

The “right hemisphere” becomes the richer receiver mode: embodied, relational, contextual, living, implicit, open to depth.

3. Status layers
A. Experimentally/scientifically grounded

Medium. McGilchrist draws heavily on lateralization research, psychiatry, neuropsychology, and split-brain / lesion studies.

B. Serious philosophical/speculative

High. His core philosophical claim is that attention discloses worlds, and modern culture over-privileges a left-hemisphere style of abstraction, control, and fragmentation.

C. Visionary/metaphorical

Medium-high. The “Master and Emissary” fable is extremely useful for Imaginarium, but must not become crude pop “left brain bad / right brain good.” Critics argue parts of the cultural-historical thesis overreach beyond the neuroscience.
