# Good Prose — The Art of Poetic Commentary

A study in what makes commentary writing alive, textured, and worthy of its subject. Not rules. A sensory vocabulary for recognising pulse.

---

## The Fundamental Distinction

**Good commentary is not summary.** It does not reduce the source to what it says. It extends the source into what it means, what it feels like to inhabit, what it asks of you.

The source text gives the raw material — the claim, the image, the argument. The commentary must answer: *so what?* Not by explaining, but by deepening. The difference:

> **Dead (paraphrase):** Ibn Arabi distinguishes between two modes of perception, one physical and one imaginal.
>
> **Alive (extension):** Two eyes. Physical sensation and imagination. How do you know which eye is active? Watch the object. If a chameleon shifts color as you watch it, and your watching does not arrest the shift, you are seeing with the eye of imagination.

The alive version adds: an image (the chameleon), a test (watch the object), a consequence (your watching does not arrest — meaning the imagination participates, it does not control). The commentary does not restate the source. It enters the source and finds something the source didn't say.

## The Texture Kit

### 1. The Unexpected Noun

Every alive AI block has at least one noun that does not belong in academic writing:

- "presences that **thrum** in the space between worlds"
- "a dissolution so thorough that everything familiar must **rot** before it can **fruit**"
- "a soul so **whetted** in intention"
- "a **lattice** of meaning **scored** into everything"
- "a **gravid** pull toward what the soul has forgotten"
- "the **seam** — part sensible, part immortal"
- "**scorches** the heavy rendering of the world"
- "a **brushwood** fire to the senses — a theophany that **scorches** through the ordinary"
- "the soul must learn to ascend by becoming like the **fire** that precedes it"

These words share a quality: they belong to poetry, craft, or the sensory world, not to academic discourse. They feel slightly out of register. That friction is what makes them stick.

### 2. The Fragment That Lands

A complete sentence with no verb, or a single phrase punctuated as a sentence. It breaks the rhythm so the reader wakes up:

- "A brushwood fire to the senses — a theophany to the imagination."
- "Same phenomenon, different organ of perception."
- "The imaginal became thinkable."
- "Integration costs something."
- "Broken. Imperative. Symbolic."
- "Three floors to this universe."
- "Not furniture. A crowd."
- "Learning to steer is the entire discipline."

The fragment works because it compresses a claim to its essence. Every word carries weight. No scaffolding.

### 3. The Concrete Image for the Abstract Idea

Watch how these replacements work:

| Abstract | Concrete |
|----------|----------|
| "the middle realm has ontological status" | "three floors to this universe. top floor: pure intelligible divinity. ground floor: matter, the stuff you can stub a toe on. and a middle floor most philosophy forgets to build — crowded, noisy, full of gods and angels" |
| "the imagination is a faculty of perception" | "the active Imagination is the preeminent mirror, the epiphanic place of the Images" |
| "space in heaven is not physical" | "space in heaven corresponds to states of interior. change of place is change of state." |
| "the soul needs purification" | "whetted in intention" (blade-sharp metaphor) |
| "the world is divinely ordered" | "a lattice of meaning scored into everything" |
| "poetry is inspired" | "the daimonic arrives as winged disturbance" |

The concrete image does not illustrate the abstraction. It replaces it. The reader does not need to translate "a lattice of meaning scored into everything" back into "the world is divinely ordered." The image carries the meaning directly, with more precision and more feeling.

### 4. The Parallel That Is Not a List

Three evenly-spaced items is a pattern the reader recognizes before processing the content. Bad:

- "gods, angels, and daimons"
- "a journey to the underworld, a fight with the dragon, a nigredo"

Good alternatives:

**Unequal items:** "gods, angels, daimons — each with its own gravity" (adds a clause that gives weight)

**Pick one, develop it:** "a dissolution so thorough that everything familiar must rot before it can fruit" (instead of listing three stages of alchemy, spend a sentence on one)

**Absorb into sentence structure:** "Fantasy and vision use the same faculty — one directs it downward toward matter, the other upward toward archetypes" (the two items are contrast, not catalog)

**The lineage chain fix:** "from Plotinus through Iamblichus to Jung and Hillman" → "between Plotinus and Hillman — passed from hand to hand by those who knew it"

### 5. The Em-Dash Pivot

The dash lets you state something and then immediately qualify, invert, or deepen it in a single rhythmic unit. Without it, you write two flat declarative sentences.

- "The observer is entangled with what he beholds — participation is the condition of perception."
- "Alchemy is the controlling metaphor for everything that follows. For Corbin, the laboratory was the soul — its furnace, its alembic — and the gold was the encounter with the Angel."
- "The Angel is the goal: the perfected form the soul takes when its transformation is complete. Encountering one's celestial counterpart is encountering God and one's own completed self — the same event, different faces turned toward the same light."

The pattern: `[assertion] — [what this means / what follows / what inverts it]`. The dash forces a relationship between the two halves.

### 6. The Opening That Grabs

The first sentence of an AI block must establish voice immediately. No throat-clearing.

| Weak opening | Strong opening |
|--------------|----------------|
| "The paper opens with alchemy, and the choice is deliberate..." | "Alchemy is the controlling metaphor for everything that follows." |
| "Angela Voss opens with an unexpected angle: alchemy." | "Corbin's alchemy has nothing to do with gold. Forget the furnace." |
| "The cosmology that underlies everything Corbin wrote is three-tiered..." | "Corbin's universe has three floors." |
| "The word 'imaginary' is often used to mean unreal..." | "The word 'imaginary' is wrecked." |
| "The Chaldean Oracles are a collection of fragmented texts..." | "The Chaldean Oracles speak like fragments of a liturgy overheard through fire." |

The strong openings share a quality: they commit. They take a stance. They do not explain what they are about to do — they do it.

## The Voice Register

### What Person Sounds Like

The commentary must sound like a person, not a committee. A person has:
- Opinions ("Corbin never seriously entertained the first.")
- Stakes ("Integration costs something.")
- Surprise ("The daimonic arrives as winged disturbance.")
- Authority without pedantry ("Learning to steer is the entire discipline.")

### What Person Does Not Sound Like

- A museum placard ("The distinction between Aristotelian and Platonic modes of knowing is essential here, because Corbin's project depends on the Platonic conviction...")
- A tour guide ("Voss opens with...", "This section examines...")
- A student essay ("This is important because...", "The key claim is...")
- A TED talk ("The power of now", "living your truth")

### The Register Spectrum

Different subjects call for different textures:

| Subject | Register | Vocabulary |
|---------|----------|------------|
| Corbin / Imagination | Cosmic, alchemical | thrum, scorch, whet, alembic, furnace, seam, lattice |
| Ficino / Beauty | Erotic, ascending | gravid, winged, gate, ladder, honey, transparency |
| Theurgy / Ritual | Operative, initiatic | seal, vessel, current, operative surface, grammar, knot |
| Ibn Arabi / Barzakh | Dialectical, disorienting | mirror, chameleon, horn, light, bewildered, isthmus |
| Chaldean / Fire | Volatile, broken | overheard, scorches, darkly-splendid, stream, ascent |
| Plotinus / The One | Spare, subtractive | pared, stripped, flake, dust, withdrawal, simplicity |
| Goethe / Perception | Organic, morphological | cotyledon, edge, metamorphosis, colour, unfolding |

The register should come from the source material itself. Ficino talks about beauty → use erotic vocabulary. Chaldean Oracles talk about fire → use volatile vocabulary. The register reinforces what the source already does.

## The Four Enemies of Alive Commentary

### 1. Narrritivitis

Commentary that narrates what the text does instead of stating the idea directly:

- "X opens with..." → Just say what the thing IS
- "The paper introduces..." → State the idea
- "This section examines..." → The idea, not the section
- "Voss traces..." → The content, not the tracing

Fix: Delete the narration clause. What remains? If nothing, you didn't have a claim.

### 2. Negat-Assert-Itis

Commentary that clears ground before building. "Not X, but Y." The reader intuits "here comes a correction" before reaching the content.

All 9 disguised forms are the same move:

| Form | Example | Fix |
|------|---------|-----|
| Direct | "Not a creative one, but a spiritual discipline." | "A spiritual discipline — a different category entirely." |
| Fragment | "Not a metaphor. A place." | "A place, with its own weather." |
| Imperative | "Forget the furnace." | Use sparingly (once per essay max) |
| Condition | "Without the Imagination, brushwood and wind." | "With it, the Burning Bush." |
| Substitution | "Instead of studying the map, walk the territory." | "Concept draws the map. The rite walks the territory." |
| Comparison | "The difference between allegory and symbol is..." | "Allegory restates what you already know. Symbol discloses what you could not have known any other way." |
| Staging | "The modern assumption misses the point. Imagination produces perceptions." | "Imagination produces perceptions of a world the senses alone cannot access." |

Fix: State the positive directly. Do not name the wrong view.

### 3. Paraphrase-itis

Commentary that restates what the source just said. If you can remove the AI block and the reader still understands everything, delete it.

The test: does the AI block ADD something the source doesn't say? A mechanism, an image, a consequence, a connection to another idea, a stance?

- Dead: "Ta'wil refers to the process of interiorisation, of restoring the true meaning."
- Alive: "Ta'wil makes integration possible — it trains the soul to pare through surfaces."

### 4. Museum-Guide Neutrality

Commentary where every sentence is 20-35 words with no rhythmic variation. The prose drones.

Fix: Add one fragment, one sentence under 8 words, one long dash-construction. Read aloud. If it sounds like a lecture, rewrite.

## The Ending

The closing AI block is the hardest and the most important. It must not echo the source's closing words. It must not tagline ("Beauty is the method. Spiritus is the medium. Cosmos is the teacher."). It must circle back to the opening image or claim, transformed by what the essay has done.

**Bad endings:**
- Shares key words with the source's last passage
- Sums up rather than opens outward
- Uses "thus," "therefore," "in conclusion"

**Good endings:**
- "The imaginal is reality's deepest register, and the work of attending to it is the oldest form of philosophy."
- "A home scored into the fabric of things before the soul arrived to find it."
- "A cosmology that leaves out the intermediate world is a flat universe where everything is either measurable matter or subjective fantasy. Most people live there. Corbin's work is the way out."

The good endings send the reader back into the world. They do not close. They open.

## Lines That Are Genuinely Alive

From the project's own best work. These lines would feel wrong in any other essay. They are specific, rhythmic, committed, and surprising:

1. **"A brushwood fire to the senses — a theophany that scorches through the ordinary."** — The fragment structure, the unexpected "scorches," the concrete "brushwood." Alive because you didn't see "scorches" coming.

2. **"Learning to steer is the entire discipline."** — Seven words. "Steer" is a concrete metaphor (steering a chariot, a ship, a horse). "Entire discipline" implies this is the WHOLE thing — nothing else needed. The claim is larger than the sentence.

3. **"The imaginal became thinkable."** — Four words. "Thinkable" implies it was literally unthinkable before. A compressed history of philosophy in four words.

4. **"The world is given, and knowing it as given awakens thanks."** — The addition of "knowing it as given" is the whole point. Not just given — known as given. That cognitive act is the Hermetic move.

5. **"Barzakh. The word means barrier, isthmus, something that separates two things without becoming either of them. Ibn Arabi turns this ordinary Arabic word into the name for divine Imagination itself."** — Opens with the word itself, not a framing sentence. "Turns this ordinary Arabic word" is active, not descriptive.

6. **"The Chaldean Oracles speak like fragments of a liturgy overheard through fire."** — "Overheard through fire" is the unexpected phrase. Why overheard instead of heard? Why through fire instead of in fire? Every word chosen for texture, not clarity.

7. **"Knowledge moves toward gratitude the way a river moves toward the sea."** — The simile is familiar (river to sea) but the comparison is not. Knowledge → gratitude. Philosophy as movement toward thanksgiving. The sentence earns its familiarity because the content is fresh.

8. **"The distance between two souls in the spiritual world is measured by difference in inner state. That is the measure."** — The short second sentence repeats the key word ("measure") but changes its meaning. The repetition is not redundancy — it is emphasis.

9. **"Himma closes the gap between conceiving and realizing. In the imaginal world, to desire something with intensity is to bring it into being — the mystic's vision and the artist's creation follow the same law."** — The dash construction connects two domains (mystic and artist) that the reader may not have connected. The commentary adds a relationship the source didn't state.

10. **"A dissolution so thorough that everything familiar must rot before it can fruit."** — The agricultural image ("rot before it can fruit") is unexpected in a discussion of alchemy. The word "fruit" as a verb is the surprise. The sentence sounds like gardening and mysticism at once.

## The Smuggle Test for Your Own Prose

After writing a commentary block, read it and ask:

1. **Did I just restate the source?** If yes, delete or rewrite.
2. **Did I just narrate what the author does?** If yes, delete the narration clause.
3. **Did I just correct a wrong view?** If yes, state the positive directly.
4. **Is every sentence 20-35 words?** If yes, add a fragment.
5. **Does any sentence start with "Not"?** If yes, rewrite.
6. **Does the ending share key words with the source?** If yes, rewrite the ending entirely.
7. **Is there a word in this block that would feel wrong in any other essay?** If no, add one.

## The Essence

The project's own best line of self-criticism captures what alive prose requires:

> *"Could someone who knows nothing about this subject read this and feel something, or just understand something? If they only feel like they understood it, the prose passed the test but failed the point. The imaginal is felt, not understood."*

Commentary on mystical texts must participate in the mode it describes. If you write about the Imagination with no imagination, about theophany with no reverence, about beauty in flat sentences — you have not understood your subject. You have only summarised it.

The aim is not clarity of explanation. It is precision of evocation. The reader should not think "I understand what Corbin meant." They should think "I have been shown something I could not have seen without this essay."
