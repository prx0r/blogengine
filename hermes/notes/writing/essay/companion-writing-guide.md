# Companion Writing Guide — Notes for the Essay Agent

## General Principles

All companions follow the same method: 60% source text, 25% framing, 15% analysis. The source author carries the argument. Commentary is connective tissue only. No summary endings — end with the source's voice or an outward gesture. Greek/Latin/Arabic terms as rhythmic punctuation throughout.

---

## 1. Avicenna and the Visionary Recital (Corbin)

**Essays**: 21 | **Source**: Corbin's commentary + Persian commentary translation

**What the text is**: Corbin's phenomenology of Avicenna's three visionary recitals (Hayy ibn Yaqzan, the Bird, Salaman and Absal). Part I is Corbin's interpretation — dense French phenomenology translated into English. Part II is the Persian commentary on Hayy ibn Yaqzan — more concrete, section-by-section.

**Key themes**: The Angel (Active Intelligence) as personal guide, ta'wil as exegesis of the soul, the Orient/Occident as spiritual geography, the cosmic crypt, the three evil companions, angelic pedagogy, the bird-soul as captive in matter.

**What to watch for**: The OCR is messy — scattered backticks, garbled characters, especially in the title page and footnotes. The actual Corbin text is clean enough to work with. Part II (the Persian commentary) is more concrete and easier to extract from.

**How to write**: Essays 1-7 (the Angel, ta'wil, Orient, hierarchy, pedagogy, daimon) are the highest signal. The bird and Salaman essays are lower. Let the Persian commentary passages carry the argument — they're more vivid than Corbin's theoretical passages. Greek/Latin terms: ishraq, ta'wil, ghurbah, haykal al-nur.

## 2. Ibn Arabi — Fusus al-Hikam (Abrahamov translation)

**Essays**: 11 | **Source**: Abrahamov annotated translation

**What the text is**: 27 chapters, each a "bezel of wisdom" for a prophet. Abrahamov's translation is clear but heavily annotated with footnotes. The actual Ibn Arabi text is interspersed with translator's comments in parentheses.

**Key themes**: The Perfect Human as mirror of divine names, waḥdat al-wujūd (unity of being), the cosmos as imagination, the Breath of the Merciful, the heart as organ of perpetual transformation, the hierarchy of divine names, the fixed entities (a'yān thābita), sainthood vs prophethood.

**What to watch for**: Abrahamov uses round parentheses for his own interpolations — distinguish these from Ibn Arabi's actual text. The footnotes are extensive but should be skipped. Some chapters are very dense (Adam, Muhammad) while others are short (Ezra, John).

**How to write**: The essay structure is thematic, not chapter-by-chapter. Each essay groups 2-3 prophets around a concept. Let Ibn Arabi's own words carry the argument — his style is dense but revelatory. Arabic terms: waḥdat al-wujūd, tajallī, qalb, nafas al-raḥmān, al-insān al-kāmil, a'yān thābita.

## 3. Man of Light in Iranian Sufism (Corbin)

**Essays**: 8 | **Source**: Corbin's lectures on Iranian Sufi phenomenology of light

**What the text is**: Corbin's study of the "man of light" archetype in Suhrawardi, Najm Kobra, Ruzbehan, and others. The phenomenology of colored lights (photisms) in Kubrawi Sufism. The black light, the emerald vision, the seven prophets of your being.

**Key themes**: The Pole (quṭb) as spiritual axis, the North as source of light, the Perfect Nature (ṭab'), the encounter with the guide, the midnight sun, the angel Sraosha, the colored lights as spiritual diagnostics, the black light as theophany of the Essence, the seven inner prophets.

**What to watch for**: Corbin's French philosophical style is dense. The text has scattered OCR artifacts (Syntax Warnings). The Persian/Arabic terms are crucial — preserve them. The Goethe color theory section at the end is a surprising digression.

**How to write**: The high-signal essays are 2 (Man of Light and His Guide), 4 (Angel Sraosha), 5 (Emerald Vision), and 6 (Black Light). Essay 8 (Goethe) can be shorter. Corbin's phenomenological descriptions of colored lights are the heart of the book.

## 4. Plotinus — The Enneads (Gerson translation)

**Essays**: 27 | **Source**: Full modern translation, 49K lines

**What the text is**: Plotinus' complete works — 54 treatises in 6 Enneads. The foundation of Neoplatonism. Modern scholarly translation with extensive footnotes and cross-references.

**Key themes**: The One beyond being, Intellect as self-thinking, the three hypostases (One, Intellect, Soul), the descent and return of the soul, beauty as the radiance of Form, contemplation as the activity of all beings, eternity and time, the daimon, matter as privation, the via negativa, the flight of the alone to the Alone.

**What to watch for**: The text is a modern translation with paragraph numbers (§) — use these for references. The footnotes are extensive and sometimes longer than the text itself. Skip them. Key treatises: I.6 (Beauty), V.1 (Three Hypostases), V.8 (Intelligible Beauty), VI.9 (The One), III.8 (Contemplation), III.4 (Daimon), III.5 (Love), III.7 (Eternity and Time).

**How to write**: Each essay covers 1-2 treatises. The Greek terms are essential: τὸ ἕν, νοῦς, ψυχή, θεωρία, δαίμων, Ἔρως, κάλλος, αἰών. The treatises are dense philosophical arguments — let Plotinus speak in sustained passages, then add a sentence or two of lucid translation. No summary endings.

## 5. Swedenborg — Heaven and Hell (Dole translation)

**Essays**: 20 | **Source**: Dole 2000 translation, clear text with section numbers (§)

**What the text is**: Swedenborg's visionary account of the spiritual world — heaven, hell, angels, spirits, the afterlife. Structured by numbered sections (§2-§615). Clear, simple prose.

**Key themes**: The Lord as God of heaven, the Grand Man (Maximus Homo), the three heavens, angels as human in form, correspondence between heaven and earth, the Word as correspondence, the two kingdoms (celestial and spiritual), the world of spirits, hell as self-love, freedom through equilibrium, "not hard to reach heaven."

**What to watch for**: This is the cleanest text we have — clear prose, numbered sections, no OCR issues. The section numbers (§) make it easy to locate passages. Swedenborg's style is repetitive — the same idea often appears in multiple sections. Vary which sections you use across essays.

**How to write**: Essays 1-7 cover the heavenly material (highest signal), 8-14 cover correspondence and the afterlife, 15-20 cover spirits, hell, and the conclusion. Swedenborg writes in simple, declarative sentences — let him speak. Key terms: correspondence, the Grand Man, the Divine Human, celestial/spiritual kingdoms, the equilibrium.

## 6. Frater Acher — Holy Daimon

**Essays**: 7 | **Source**: Contemporary practical grimoire

**What the text is**: A modern occultist's account of a 28-day Saturn retreat to achieve communion with the holy daimon. Three parts: History (theory), Memory (ritual diary), Practice (exercises). Practical, first-person, vivid.

**Key themes**: The daimon in Platonic and PGM tradition, the 28-day Saturn operation (SatEx), polarity exercises (power, trust, creation/destruction), the serpent and the egg, hitlahavut (raising magical fire), planetary singing, crossing the threshold, systasis (daimonic encounter).

**What to watch for**: This is contemporary English, no OCR issues. The Memory section is a personal diary — immediate and intense. The Practice section has actual ritual instructions. Don't over-explain the rituals; let Acher's own words carry the method.

**How to write**: Essays 1-3 cover theory (history, world, magic), 4 covers the operation, 5-7 cover practices. Essays 5 and 6 were merged (polarities + serpent/egg). Essay 8 was split into 8a (threshold) and 8b (encounter). Practical terminology: hitlahavut, tratak, systasis, voces magicae, LBRP, Saturn Exerzitium.

## 7. Ficino — Platonic Theology Vol 1 (Books I-IV)

**Essays**: 10 | **Source**: I Tatti bilingual Latin/English edition

**What the text is**: Ficino's magnum opus — a systematic proof of the soul's immortality in 18 books. Volume 1 covers Books I-IV: the five levels of being, the soul as the middle link, initial proofs of immortality, the soul's ascent.

**Key themes**: The five levels (body, quality, soul, angel, God), the soul as vinculum naturae totius (bond of all nature), the soul's self-motion, the Platonic wings, the ascent through the levels, angelic mind, the divine Sun.

**What to watch for**: Bilingual Latin/English — extract ONLY English. The introduction and chapter headings are in both languages. The English translation is on facing pages; the OCR mixed them together. Skip anything that looks like Latin (quod, enim, autem, esse).

**How to write**: Essays 1-5 (Books I-II) are thorough. Essays 6-10 (Books III-IV) are thinner — Books III-IV in Vol 1 are transitional. The key concept is the five-level hierarchy and the soul's place in the middle. Latin terms: vinculum naturae, mens angelica, massa corporis, qualitas, anima, angelus, deus.

## Cross-Connections Between Sources

- **Corbin's Avicenna** connects to **Corbin's Man of Light** through the angelology and the Orient/Occident symbolism
- **Corbin's Avicenna** connects to **Ibn Arabi** through the Perfect Nature and angelic pedagogy
- **Plotinus** connects to **Ficino** through the Neoplatonic framework (One, Intellect, Soul)
- **Plotinus on the daimon** (III.4) connects to **Holy Daimon** (Acher cites Plotinus)
- **Swedenborg** connects to **Corbin** through the mundus imaginalis and spiritual geography (Corbin wrote on Swedenborg)
- **Ficino's Platonic Theology** is the direct Renaissance heir to **Plotinus' Enneads**
- **Iamblichus on theurgy** connects to **Holy Daimon** through practical daimonic work
- **Vol 2-6** are not yet written to standard — skip or redo
