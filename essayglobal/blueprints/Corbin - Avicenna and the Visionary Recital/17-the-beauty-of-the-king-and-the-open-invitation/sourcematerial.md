# 17 — The Beauty of the King and the Open Invitation

## Passage 1: The Beauty of the King — Theophanic Vision Beyond Comparison

**Context:** §23 describes the King (God Most High) as so transcending all comparison that His beauty is the veil of His beauty, His manifestation the cause of His occultation. The commentary emphasizes that He is not invisible but that the excess of His light dazzles.

**Full Passage:**
> Know that God Most High is more independent of place than anyone. More than any, He is removed from all localization. This is why the Master says that "the King is the most distant of all," referring to distance with regard to localization in a place. And no one can glorify God in a manner that corresponds to what He is or give Him an attribute that befits Him. Hence the Master says: "Whoever imagines that he can render praise that is proportionate to Him is an idle babbler." His beauty is His entire self; He is the Generous, and His beauty surpasses all beauties. He who seeks to understand Him as He is is smitten with stupor and impotence. This is why the Master says: "If any would meditate on Him, he remains stupefied and dazzled." It is not that He is invisible and that no one can find Him; on the contrary, it is because of His Manifestation, which is indeed real, that no one can apprehend Him, just as, if anyone seeks to look perfectly at the sun, his eye is dazzled by its light. If, then, we cannot apprehend the sun, it is not that it is invisible, but it is because of the very excess of its light that we cannot contemplate it. Lastly, God is not niggardly of His knowledge, giving access to it to one and refusing it to another; it is because of our debility that we cannot know Him perfectly.

**Original terms:** King (Malek / Shah), beauty (husn / jamal), manifestation (zuhur), occultation (batin / satr), stupor (hayrat), theophany (tajalli)

## Passage 2: Those Who Emigrate — Souls Who Leave the Occident for the Kingdom

**Context:** §24 describes the "solitaries" (monadics) who emigrate toward the King. The commentary explains this as the soul's exodus, its hegira toward the original fatherland.

**Full Passage:**
> Know that human beings differ in their intelligence of Knowledge and their perception of philosophical things. There is the case of him who makes use of the two kinds of intellect that we have mentioned, so that the Forms of all things are actualized for him; he knows and he understands the pure spiritual Substances that subsist of themselves without Matter; he understands in what connection they are, one to another. This is to make use of the speculative intellect ['kharad-e dana]. Further, he assimilates virtuous practices and casts far from him evil ways. The three powers of which we have spoken, he makes his servants. He knows what befalls the soul, for good or evil, after the body becomes the prey of dissolution. Then too, he faithfully observes the positive religion, and puts into practice all such things in that Law as come from God. This is the rank of the Sages, of good men, of the pure.

**Original terms:** Solitaries (ajrad / monadics), emigration (hijrat / muhajirun), speculative intellect (kharad-e dana), practical intellect (kharad-e karkun), original fatherland (watan-e asli), Orient (mashriq)

## Passage 3: The Kingdom of the Soul as Destination

**Context:** The commentary describes the soul's journey after death and the joys of the other world. The soul that has pursued Knowledge attains the sweetness of eternity.

**Full Passage:**
> When, then, this body becomes the prey of corruption, the soul of the Sages and the pure easily reaches the other world, for that other world was the object of their researches and their desires; their soul falls into the sweetness and joy of eternity, that joy which no other joy resembles, for in truth the greatest of joys is to attain in perfect fullness to the object of one's desire, especially that joy which has Knowledge as its source. Let us take as an example the case of a man endowed with a noble and great soul that aspires to Knowledge. One question, however, remains hidden from him. When, after expending great effort, he resolves and understands this question, he thereby experiences a joy and delight far higher than can be given by the favor of the world and the possession of what it contains, gold and silver, gardens and orchards, favorites and slaves. Whoever has had this experience will understand what I mean.

**Original terms:** Kingdom (malakut), other world ('alam-e akhirat), joy of eternity (surur-e abadi), Knowledge ('ilm), spiritual Substances (jawakhir-e ruhani)

---

## Passage 4: "If Thou Wilt, Follow Me" — The Angel's Open Invitation

**Context:** §25 is the closing of the Recital of Hayy ibn Yaqzan. The Sage says these final words of invitation. The commentary identifies the Active Intelligence as the Donor of Knowledge and our Guide.

**Full Passage:**
> Know that this Active Intelligence is the Donor of Knowledge and that it is our Guide. It is thereby, then, that what it was created for is realized, and this, finally, is that there should be a progressive approach of it (and through it) to God and, in (and through) this bringing to act, celebration by it of its divine service.

**Original terms:** "If thou wilt, follow me" (in shi'ta fattabi'ni), Active Intelligence ('aql fa''al), Donor of Knowledge (Dehendah-e 'ilm), Guide (hadi / rahnuma), divine service ('ibadat)

## Passage 5: The Journey Remains Incomplete; the Invitation Stays Open

**Context:** Corbin's note explains the meaning of the Angel's words: the invitation opens a boundless perspective. The pilgrimage has no limit; the relation between the soul and the Angel involves reciprocal need.

**Full Passage:**
> The motif of the pilgrimage, of the Quest, is the mainspring of our recitals, Avicennan or Suhrawardian. And it is this motif that gives its experiential, and even dramatic, significance to all this doctrine of the Active Intelligence, which otherwise might at first contact seem to the reader not familiar with it simply a fantastic construction. Not only does the human being depend upon his Angel, his Dator formarum, for all the acts of knowledge that metamorphose him; but reciprocally too, the Angel has need of him to accomplish his own elevation. As we suggested earlier, Tobias and the Angel are here involved in the fate of one and the same pilgrimage. The dominant affirmations must be considered together: first of all, that which postulates the relation of each human soul with this Angel as being the same as the relation of each Anima coelestis with the Cherub from whom it emanates. And then these: "Each time that thou turnest from thy companions, I walk with thee." "Were it not that in conversing with thee, I approach that King. . . ." And finally: "If thou wilt, follow me, come with me toward Him."

**Original terms:** Pilgrimage (ziyarat / safar), Quest (talab), Dator formarum, Tobias and the Angel, reciprocal elevation, Anima coelestis, Cherub (Karubiyun)

---

## Passage 6: The Soul's Freedom to Accept or Refuse

**Context:** The closing note explains that the soul's response to the invitation is a free act — the "pass" of inner initiation, the fervent commitment and act of faith.

**Full Passage:**
> To walk in company with the Angel is likewise what, at the end of the Recital of the Bird, is called "walking in company with the King's Messenger." And this means: freeing oneself to receive the irradiation and the illuminations of the angelic Intelligence; and eo ipso, by enabling it to accomplish in the soul that to which it is ordained by its own being as illuminating Angel, to cause it to fulfill and celebrate its "divine service." To approach the "King" through it but at the same time also to be the instrument through which it approaches the "King." For it, this is to "provoke thine awakening," to make "thee" too a watcher, a filius vigilantis. The end of the recital is extraordinarily rich.

**Original terms:** King's Messenger (Rasul-e Malek / Rasul Karim), awakening (tanabbuh), filius vigilantis, divine service ('ibadat), irradiation (ishraq)
