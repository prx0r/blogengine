# 20 — Epilogue: Symbolism, Presence, and the Hidden Imam

## Passage 1: Avicennism and Imamism — The Esoteric Shi'i Reception

**Context:** The Epilogue begins by showing how Iranian Shi'ite thinkers performed a ta'wil of Avicenna's thought, integrating it with Imamology. The central figure is the Imam as perfect Sage.

**Full Passage:**
> An admirable and fertile example of positive valorization is afforded us by the way in which Avicenna's thought was understood and experienced in Iran, among Shi'ite Avicennans. It is not that here too, among the theologians and jurists, voices were lacking to proclaim their hostility... The dominant concept of Shi'ism, the concept of Imam, proves to have been so great a stimulus to eager speculation and spiritual awareness that perhaps it explains the fact that speculative thought had such a renewal at Ispahan in the seventeenth century, whereas everywhere else in the Islamic world the period of creative philosophy appeared long since to have ended... For an Imamite thinker concerned to arouse sympathy for his hero, it is perhaps a way of facilitating his task to begin by recalling how the great majority of the doctors and jurists of Sunnite Islam pronounced an anathema (takfir) upon Avicenna and his work.

**Original terms:** Imamism (Imamiya), Avicennism (Ibn-Sinagari), ta'wil, Ispahan school, Mir Damad, Magister tertius, anathema (takfir), Shi'ite Avicennans

## Passage 2: The Hidden Imam as the Perfect Man, the Pole of the Spiritual Universe

**Context:** The commentary traces how the "hidden Imam" corresponds to the perfect Sage, the "pole" (qutb) of the spiritual universe. The occultation of the Imam parallels the invisible kingship of the Sage.

**Full Passage:**
> It is rather from the fatality of its temporal failures that Imamism draws its deep meaning, the reason for its permanent protest. It is with the idea of the "hidden Imam," the "great occupation" intensifying the eschatological meaning, that a spiritual Imamism could maintain itself and could provide our mystical theosophists with the occasion for a ta'wil leading it back to the idea of the invisible royalty of the perfect Sage, whose mere secret presence, unknown to the multitude of men, at once suffices and is necessary for the leaven of Wisdom to continue to ferment among them and for the perpetuation of a humanity of which the Imam is the "pole" (qutb). And this "occultation" would much better correspond to the denouement of our Recital of Salaman and Absal — so true is it that when a symbol proves to be a central symbol, it shows an extraordinary polyvalence.

**Original terms:** Hidden Imam (Imam-e gha'ib), pole (qutb), occultation (ghaybat), perfect Sage (hakim-e kamil), eschatological meaning, Wisdom (hikmat), polyvalence of symbols

## Passage 3: Symbolism and Presence — The Symbol as the Presence of What It Reveals

**Context:** The section "Symbolism and Presence" is the philosophical heart of the Epilogue. Corbin argues that the symbol is not allegory but a presence that both manifests and hides — the symbol is the presence of what it reveals.

**Full Passage:**
> On the one hand, there is the full and autonomous reality presented by the intermediate world of the symbolic Imagination; on the other hand, the spontaneity with which that world comes into flower. The change that occurs in the soul, which this world expresses at the same time that it performs it, announces the soul's attainment of its truly personal symbol, and the greatness of the Event resides there... Yet it would not be enough to say that the flowering of symbols comes in answer to an effort to "disengage" the intelligible meaning hidden "behind" each purely sensible reality. Such a schematizing of the process would inevitably miss what constitutes the peculiar reality and the autonomy of the universe of symbols: the symbol is mediator because it is silence, it speaks and does not speak; and, precisely thus, it states what it alone can speak. If one undertakes to achieve its meaning once and for all, in order to take one's stand on the intelligible signification that one substitutes for it, the whole mental dramaturgy of the Avicennan and Suhrawardian recitals vanishes; nothing is left but pallid "allegories."

**Original terms:** Symbol (ramz), symbol as presence, intermediate world ('alam al-mithal), symbolic Imagination (khayal), allegory (tamthil), the Event (al-waqi'a), personal symbol, mental dramaturgy

## Passage 4: The Theophanic Function of the Imagination

**Context:** Corbin explains that the active metaphysical Imagination is the organ of the intermediate world — the world of "Hurqalya" — where visions and symbols are real. The celestial Souls with pure Imagination guarantee the objectivity of this world.

**Full Passage:**
> For it is not what is called the subjective human "fantasy," it is the pleroma of celestial Souls, possessing pure Imagination, free of the senses, that is at once the place and the assurance of what we may call the objectivity of the world of symbols, that whole universe of the Imaginable which will be toponymically typified, among the Ishraqiyun and the Shaikhis, as the world of "Hurqalya." It is "in Hurqalya" that our visionary recitals take place — that is, in the world of the celestial Angels-Souls who preserve the archetypes of prophetic visions and mystical visions. To see events "in Hurqalya" is quite a different thing from seeing them on the sensible and historical plane. To fix them on the latter, conferring on it a pre-eminence such that all occurrent reality depends upon it, is to make an impossibility of the integration — that is, of the inner and personal meaning "in the present" — to which we have referred.

**Original terms:** Hurqalya, active metaphysical Imagination (khayal-e munfasil), celestial Souls (nufus-e falakiya), objectivity of the world of symbols, Ishraqiyun (Illuminationists), Shaikhis, archetypes of prophetic visions

## Passage 5: The Oriental Philosophy as a Living Tradition

**Context:** The Postscript discusses recent studies on Avicenna's "Oriental Philosophy." Corbin affirms that the Orient is both a topographical and a spiritual reality. The Oriental philosophy is not a historical artifact but a living tradition.

**Full Passage:**
> In short, we can only record the concurrence between the positions that we had ourselves adopted and the conclusions that terminate this rich and stimulating study. A topographical meaning for the Orient of Avicenna's "Oriental philosophy"? Certainly, just as in Suhrawardi the Orient accords topographically with that of the Sages of ancient Persia. This meaning is eminently plausible for our Khurasani, living in a period of renaissance notable especially for the formation of neo-Persian prose, to which he himself contributed. But, after all, this meaning would be only episodic and metaphorical (majazi!). Let us recall Hayy ibn Yaqzan's teaching: the Orient beyond the physical cosmos, and the Occident of material nature. Mr. Pines also alludes to Hayy ibn Yaqzan's teaching, and does so to admit that "it is probable that these connotations favored Avicenna's design of presenting himself as the heir or the master of an Oriental philosophy." Nothing better could be said. For our part, the probability has a tincture of certainty.

**Original terms:** Oriental Philosophy (Hikmat al-Mashriqiyin / Falsafe-ye Ishraq), Orient beyond the physical cosmos, Occident of material nature, Hayy ibn Yaqzan's teaching, S. Pines, living tradition, Khurasan

## Passage 6: Corbin's Synthesis of Avicenna, Suhrawardi, Ibn 'Arabi, and the Imam

**Context:** Throughout the Epilogue, Corbin weaves together Avicennan angelology, Suhrawardian illuminationism, Ibn 'Arabi's theosophy, and Shi'ite Imamology into a unified vision of spiritual anthropology.

**Full Passage:**
> If the divinity is indeed what it is, whether our theologies know it or not, every divine epiphany can only have the form of the Angel. It is here that the connection will be made between angelology and Imamology, particularly a subject of meditation in Ismailian Shi'ism (the correspondences between celestial and terrestrial hierarchies). The figure of the Imam, notably in Shi'ite Sufism, will magnetize so much devotion and love that it is possible to speak of an Imamocentrism, in the sense in which Christian spirituality speaks of Christocentrism. But, to be accurate, the comparison would have to take as the homologous term the Gnostic conception of Christos-Angelos rather than that of the theology of the Councils... In brief, in so far as the soul cannot perform an abstractive operation, but receives the illumination that emanates upon it, floods its being, and in-forms in it and through it all that is yet the Formless, its cogitative action is rather a passion, its Cogito is really a Cogitor (to use von Baader's celebrated phrase); to recognize this action of the Active Intelligence or Holy Spirit is the "Oriental knowledge," the knowledge that is the Orient, the origin, of all knowledge.

**Original terms:** Angelology, Imamology, Suhrawardi, Ibn 'Arabi, Christos-Angelos, Imamocentrism, Cogitor (I am thought), Active Intelligence ('aql fa''al), Holy Spirit (Ruh al-Quds), Oriental knowledge ('ilm-e ishraqui)
