# Source Material: Essay 8 — The Prologue: What the Soul Is Made For

## Passage 1: The Recital's Prologue — The Master's Opening Words

**Context:** The Prologue of the Persian Commentary begins with the Master's own words to his brothers, explaining that their persistence has finally overcome his reluctance to set forth the Recital. This opening frames the entire commentary that follows.

**Full Passage:**

> THE MASTER SAYS: "Your persistence, my brothers, in demanding that I set forth the Recital of Hayy ibn Yaqzan for you has finally triumphed over my stubborn determination not to do so; it has untied the bond of my firm resolve to defer and delay. Thus I have found myself ready to come to your aid. May we look to God for help and support!"

---

## Passage 2: The Two Substances — Body as "Mold" (Kalbud) and Soul (Ravin/Nafs)

**Context:** The commentator begins the metaphysical exposition by stating that God created man from two substances: the body which is the "mold" (kalbud), and the soul (ravin/nafs) which constitutes the human being in the proper sense. This establishes the fundamental duality that the entire Recital presupposes.

**Full Passage:**

> Know that God — may He be exalted — created man from two substances: one that is the body (the "mold") and the other that is the soul [ravin], that which in Arabic is called nafs (Anima) and which constitutes the human being in the proper sense. It is the soul that apprehends universals and produces the arts and techniques.

**Original Terms:** *kalbud* (mold/body), *ravin* (soul — Pahlavi *ruvan*), *nafs* (Anima/soul)

---

## Passage 3: The Soul's Native Activity — Knowing God and the Angels

**Context:** The commentator describes the soul's natural constitution: when no obstacle turns it from its proper activity, it seeks out wisdom and aspires to understand all that is knowable. Its native occupation is knowing God and the Angels and understanding their hierarchy — the entire angelological order that connects all beings to God.

**Full Passage:**

> And God has endowed the soul with such a nature and constitution that whenever no obstacle turns it from its proper activity it seeks out the doctrines of wisdom and aspires to understand everything that is an object of knowledge. It occupies itself in knowing God and the Angels; it desires to know their hierarchy, the connection that unites them one to another, and that which links them all together to God Most High. It is entirely absorbed in understanding what must come into being from the World of the Mystery.

**Original Terms:** *ravin/nafs* (soul), knowledge of God, knowledge of the Angels, hierarchy (*martaba*), World of the Mystery (*'alam al-ghayb*)

---

## Passage 4: The Soul Seeks Wisdom When Obstacles Are Absent

**Context:** The commentator emphasizes that the soul's natural inclination toward divine and angelic knowledge is only realized when obstacles — the lower faculties and the cares of governing the body — are removed. This state of freedom from obstacles is the precondition for the soul's true activity.

**Full Passage:**

> Know that one of the obstacles that turn the soul from its natural activity is the other faculties with which the human being is furnished and which were created in him: the irascible faculty, the concupiscible faculty, the imaginative faculty. [...] Another obstacle that turns the soul from its proper activity is the care that keeps it preoccupied with governing its body and ruling the said powers in order to hold them in the straight way. Each time that these powers draw the soul in their direction and constrain it to concern itself with them, the soul is turned from its proper activity, that for which it was created and which consists in understanding the things of the other world, in knowing the True Reality of things and beings.

**Original Terms:** irascible faculty, concupiscible faculty, imaginative faculty, True Reality (*haqiqat*), ultimate causes

## Passage 5: Description of the Pir (Hayy ibn Yaqzan) — Beautiful, Timeless, Youth and Ancient Simultaneously

**Context:** The visionary encounter presents the figure of the Sage — the Pir, Hayy ibn Yaqzan — who combines the features of eternal youth with the gravity of old Sages. He is the Active Intelligence individuated, the Angel who is at once the ancient of days and the eternal youth. The Persian commentator emphasizes this juxtaposition as symbolizing the totality of the human being and its pre-existence.

**Full Passage:**

> THE MASTER SAYS: "Once when I had taken up residence in my city, I chanced to go out with my companions to one of the pleasure places that lie about the same city. Now, as we were coming and going, making a circle, suddenly in the distance appeared a Sage. He was beautiful; his person shone with a divine glory. Certainly he had tasted of years; long duration had passed over him. Yet there was seen in him only the freshness proper to young men; no weakness bowed his bearing; no fault injured the grace of his stature. In short, no sign of old age was to be found in him, save the imposing gravity of old Sages."

**Original Terms:** *Pir* (Sage/spiritual preceptor), Hayy ibn Yaqzan, *farrah/xvarnah* (divine glory/light of glory), "freshness of young men," "gravity of old Sages," *Puer aeternus*

## Passage 6: The Soul as Separate Form — Ruvan/Nafs

**Context:** In the note to the commentary, Corbin glosses the concept of *ruvan* (Pahlavi *ruvan*, Persian *ravin* = Arabic *nafs*) in Mazdean anthropology, noting that this passage poses the notion of the soul as a separate form whose union with the body does not form a unity indispensable to the human person. This conception underlies the entire Avicennan anthropology and angelology.

**Full Passage:**

> For the concept of ruvan (Pahlavi ruvan, Persian ravin = Arabic nafs) in Mazdean anthropology, cf. H. W. Bailey, Zoroastrian Problems in the Ninth-Century Books, p. 92. This passage at once poses the notion of the soul as a separate form, whose union with the body does not form a unity indispensable to the human person.

**Original Terms:** *ruvan* (Pahlavi), *ravin* (Persian), *nafs* (Arabic), separate form, union with the body, human person
