# Avicenna and the Visionary Recital — Complete Blueprint

**Source**: Henry Corbin, *Avicenna and the Visionary Recital*, tr. Willard R. Trask (Bollingen Series LXVI, Princeton, 1960)

**Text structure**: Part I (Corbin's cycle of recitals, 5 chapters) + Epilogue + Postscript + Part II (Persian Commentary on Hayy ibn Yaqzan, 25 sections)

**Essays**: 20, each covering unique source territory with no overlap

---

## Essay 0 — Orientation: Reading the Visionary Recital

**Source territory**: Preface + Part I Ch.1 (§1 Avicennism and Philosophical Situation)

**Source content**: Corbin's preface explains the Iranian context of Avicenna's recitals, the phenomenological method of reading symbols, and why the recitals form an organic trilogy. Ch.1 §1 defines Avicenna's philosophical situation — the "Oriental Philosophy" (*hikmat al-ishraq*), the difference between Latin Avicennism (Scholastic) and Iranian Avicennism (esoteric, theosophical).

**What the source does naturally**: It introduces the reader to the whole project — what a visionary recital is, who Avicenna was in his Iranian context, and how Corbin's phenomenology differs from historicism.

**Passages to extract**:
1. Corbin's method statement: "comprehending symbols is an act that takes place in 'time present'"
2. The three recitals as an organic trilogy
3. Avicennism and its two faces: Latin Scholastic vs Iranian theosophical
4. The meaning of "Oriental Philosophy" and its connection to Suhrawardi's ishraq

## Essay 1 — The Cosmic Crypt: World as Prison, Soul as Stranger

**Source territory**: Part I Ch.2 (§2 The Cosmic Crypt: The Stranger and the Guide)

**Source content**: Corbin analyzes the experience of the world as a crypt — the dome that shelters and imprisons. The soul as stranger (*gharib*), estrangement (*ghurbah*) as the dominant Gnostic feeling. The Recital of Hayy ibn Yaqzan teaches fundamental orientation: Orient and Occident as directions of meaning, not geography. The world as crypt appears across Gnostic traditions (Valentinian, Manichaean, Ismailian). The celestial spheres become a cage. The soul's question is not "what?" but "where?"

**Passages to extract**:
1. The recital teaches orientation — "it becomes possible to answer the question 'where?' by indicating a meaning"
2. The cosmic crypt — dome as shelter and prison
3. The magnificent dome becomes a cage
4. "There was I, sole and solitary, a stranger to the other dwellers at the inn"
5. The celestial spheres as burden rather than unification
6. The planets as terror rather than piety (anti-astral mysticism)
7. The Angels as strangers in the celestial Occident
8. The sense of being a Stranger as the dominant gnostic feeling

## Essay 2 — Ta'wil: The Exegesis of the Soul

**Source territory**: Part I Ch.3 (§3 Ta'wil as Exegesis of the Soul)

**Source content**: Corbin defines ta'wil — the hermeneutic that leads the symbol back to its origin, the exegesis of the soul. The recital is not allegory but symbolic narrative: allegory translates an idea into an image (weakening), while symbol manifests a reality inaccessible otherwise. Ta'wil as the method of reading the recitals: each figure, each event corresponds to a structure of the soul. The soul interprets itself by reading the recital.

**Passages to extract**:
1. Definition of ta'wil: leading back to origin, archetype
2. Symbol vs allegory distinction
3. Ta'wil as exegesis of the soul (not of texts)
4. The recital as the soul's self-interpretation
5. The cosmic crypt as the world that requires ta'wil to become readable

## Essay 3 — The Journey into the Orient: The Trilogy as One Recital

**Source territory**: Part I Ch.4 (§4 The Cycle of Recitals or the Journey into the Orient)

**Source content**: Corbin shows that the three recitals form a single cycle: Hayy ibn Yaqzan (the encounter with the Angel), the Recital of the Bird (the soul's captivity and ascent), Salaman and Absal (the divided soul and its angelic counterpart). The journey into the Orient as the unifying theme. Each recital corresponds to a stage of the soul's journey.

**Passages to extract**:
1. The three recitals as a cycle
2. The journey into the Orient as unifying theme
3. Each recital's place in the soul's itinerary
4. The Orient as spiritual origin, not geographical location

## Essay 4 — The Triple Angelic Hierarchy

**Source territory**: Part I Ch.5 (§5 The Angel, Spirit and Intelligence)

**Source content**: The core of Avicennan angelology. The triple hierarchy: (1) Archangels/Cherubs — pure Intelligences; (2) Angels — moving Souls of the celestial spheres; (3) human souls — "terrestrial angels." The notion that self-consciousness and angelic knowledge are the same act. The soul knows the Angel by knowing itself. The Active Intelligence as the tenth Cherub. The homology between celestial Souls and human souls. The human soul's capacity to be false to its being.

**Passages to extract**:
1. The triple hierarchy stated
2. Self-consciousness as knowledge of the Angel
3. The Active Intelligence (tenth Cherub)
4. Celestial Souls and human souls: homology
5. The human soul can be false to its being (demonic virtuality)
6. The dyad `'aql-nafs` as the structure of each level
7. "It is by awakening to consciousness of itself that the soul knows the Angel"

## Essay 5 — The Cherubs and the Celestial Souls

**Source territory**: Part I Ch.6 (§6 The Archangels-Cherubs or Intelligences) + Ch.7 (§7 The Celestial Angels or Souls)

**Source content**: The first two hierarchies in detail. The Cherubs (Kerubim) as pure Intelligences emanated from the First Intelligence. Each Cherub knows its Principle, itself, and its own being as necessary/possible. From the "median dimension" of each Cherub emanates a celestial Soul (Anima coelestis) — the moving principle of a sphere. The drama in heaven: how far can an Angel descend? The distinction between Cherubs and Souls maps onto the distinction between intellect and soul in the human being.

**Passages to extract**:
1. The Cherubs as pure Intelligences
2. The three "dimensions" of each Cherub's intellection
3. Emanation of the celestial Soul from the Cherub's median dimension
4. The Anima coelestis and its sphere
5. The "drama in heaven" — the risk of incarnation
6. Parallels with Enochic angelology, the Watchers
7. Avicenna's angelology vs Koranic angelology vs Averroistic angelology

## Essay 6 — Angelic Pedagogy and Individuation

**Source territory**: Part I Ch.8 (§8 Angelic Pedagogy and Individuation)

**Source content**: Corbin's most original synthesis. The Angel is not just a cosmological principle but a personal guide. Angelic pedagogy: the Angel teaches the soul by being what it is. The problem of individuation: how can a multitude of human souls relate to one Active Intelligence? The answer: each soul's relation to the Angel constitutes its unique individuality. The "Perfect Nature" (*tab'* or *al-insan al-kamil*) as the personal Angel. The dyad of intellect and soul in each human mirrors the dyad of Cherub and celestial Soul. Individuation through the Angel.

**Passages to extract**:
1. The problem of the one Intelligence and the many souls
2. Individuation through the Angel — each soul a unique relation
3. The Perfect Nature as the personal Angel
4. The dyad structure repeated at every level
5. The Angel as "celestial Self"
6. Pedagogy as theophany — the Angel teaches by revealing

## Essay 7 — The Number of the Spheres and the Oriental Philosophy

**Source territory**: Part I Ch.9 (§9 The Number of the Celestial Spheres) + Ch.10 (§10 Latin Avicennism and Iranian Avicennism)

**Source content**: The cosmological architecture: how many spheres? The debate between astronomers and philosophers. The Ninth Sphere, the Tenth Intelligence. Latin Avicennism: what the Scholastics made of Avicenna (the "Avicenna of the Latins"). Iranian Avicennism: what Avicenna meant in his own cultural context — the Oriental Philosophy as a living esoteric tradition.

**Passages to extract**:

3. Latin Avicennism vs Iranian Avicennism

## Essay 8 — The Prologue: What the Soul Is Made For

**Source territory**: Part II §1 (Prologue)

**Source content**: The Persian commentary opens. God created man from two substances: body (the "mold," *kalbud*) and soul (*ravin/nafs*). The soul's proper activity: knowledge of God and the Angels, understanding their hierarchy. Obstacles turn the soul from this activity. The soul as a form separate from the body. The description of the Pir (Hayy ibn Yaqzan): beautiful, timeless, with the features of a youth and the gravity of old Sages.

**Passages to extract**:
1. The two substances: body as mold, soul as form
2. The soul's native activity: knowing God and the Angels
3. The soul seeks wisdom when obstacles are absent
4. Description of the Pir: youth and ancient simultaneously
5. Ruvan/nafs — the soul as separate form

## Essay 9 — The Encounter: Obstacles and the Active Intelligence

**Source territory**: Part II §2 (The Encounter with the Angel)

**Source content**: The soul's three faculties — irascible, concupiscible, imaginative — as obstacles. These powers draw the soul from its proper activity. When governed, they become servants and the soul is "at home." The Active Intelligence as the Angel through whom knowledge becomes actual. The Angel knows all things instantly, not potentially. Condition of the human being: knowledge is virtual until the Angel's influx makes it actual.

**Passages to extract**:
1. The three faculties as obstacles
2. The soul at home in its city
3. The Active Intelligence as the cause of actual knowledge
4. The Angel's knowledge: instant, actual, comprehensive
5. All knowledge depends on the Angel's influx
6. "From far off I saw a Sage"

## Essay 10 — The Salutation: Ishraq and the Angel's Light

**Source territory**: Part II §3 (The Salutation)

**Source content**: The Angel takes the initiative — salutes the soul before the soul can speak. The salutation as *ishraq* (auroral illumination). The Active Intelligence does not withhold its light; it shines on all equally. Reception depends on the receiver's capacity. The Angel's influx as the awakening from the "lethargy of Nescience." The commentator's analogy: straw buried in damp ground, warmed by the sun, becomes fit to generate a scorpion — the fitness determines the reception of form.

**Passages to extract**:
1. The Angel salutes first — "he took the initiative"
2. Ishraq: the salutation as sunrise of the Intelligence
3. The influx reaches all equally — each according to capacity
4. The straw-scorpion analogy for reception of form
5. The soul wakes from Nescience
6. "His influx was added to me, so that I understood the divine sciences"

## Essay 11 — The Name of the Angel: Vivens Filius Vigilantis

**Source territory**: Part II §4 (Beginning of the Initiation: The Name and Person of the Angel)

**Source content**: The Angel reveals his name: Hayy ibn Yaqzan — Living, son of Awake/Vigilant. His country: the Celestial Jerusalem (*al-Bait al-Muqaddas*). His profession: forever journeying through the universe in knowledge. His face turned toward his father (the First Intelligence). The name as essence: "Living" means death cannot touch him; "son of Awake" means he is wakefulness itself, vigilant, exempt from sleep. The Celestial Jerusalem as the archangelic pleroma — neither situated nor situatable in a sensible place.

**Passages to extract**:
1. "My name is Vivens; my lineage, filius Vigilantis"
2. The meaning of the two names: life, wakefulness
3. "My country is the Celestial Jerusalem"
4. "My profession is to be forever journeying"
5. The face turned toward the Father
6. The keys to every kind of knowledge
7. The pleroma as non-spatial
8. Connection to Enochic Watchers, the Egregoroi

## Essay 12 — Physiognomy: How the Angel Knows the Soul

**Source territory**: Part II §5 (Physiognomy)

**Source content**: The Angel demonstrates the science of physiognomy — reading inner nature from outer appearance. The Angel's knowledge is comparable to physiognomy: he knows exactly what each soul is and what it can become. The science of physiognomy as the science of the invisible revealed through the visible. This is the epistemological foundation of the entire imaginal project: appearances are not deceptive but revelatory when read by the one who knows.

**Passages to extract**:
1. The Angel's penetration in physiognomy
2. "It reveals to thee what every man conceals of his own nature"
3. The Angels know each human being exactly
4. Physiognomy as angelic science
5. The visible reveals the invisible — foundation of imaginal epistemology

## Essay 13 — The Two Ways and the Three Evil Companions

**Source territory**: Part II §6 (The Two Ways for the Soul) + §7-8 (The Soul's Three Evil Companions, How to Treat the Three Companions) + §9 (The Conditions of the Journey)

**Source content**: The two ways for the soul — descent or return. The three evil companions: irascible faculty (anger, violence), concupiscible faculty (lust, gluttony), imaginative faculty (fantasy, delusion). How to treat each: not by destroying them but by governing them. Each has a proper function when subordinated. The conditions of the journey: what the soul needs to make the return — purification, discipline, the active Imagination as either angel or demon. The active Imagination as the crucial faculty: it can be the organ of prophecy or the organ of fantasy.

**Passages to extract**:
1. The two ways for the soul

4. The imaginative companion: fantasy and its governance
5. How to treat each companion
6. The conditions required for the journey

The celestial spheres as stations of ascent: each sphere, each heaven, corresponds to a degree of proximity to the Orient. Toward the Orient: the clime of elementary forms and the forms of species — where matter becomes receptive to Form through the mediation of the celestial Angels.

## Essay 19 — Salaman and Absal: The Divided Soul

**Source territory**: Part I Ch.19-21 (The Two Versions of the Recital, The Hermetistic Version, The Avicennan Version)

**Source content**: The most enigmatic recital. Salaman as the soul bound to the world, caught in earthly attachments. Absal as the angelic counterpart, the spiritual double. The hermetistic version: alchemical symbolism, the birth of the soul from two parents. The Avicennan version: philosophical allegory of the soul's dual nature. The divided soul, the angelic pair, mystical death, and the "journey without return."

**Passages to extract**:
