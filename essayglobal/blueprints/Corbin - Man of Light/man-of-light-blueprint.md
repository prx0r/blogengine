# Corbin — The Man of Light in Iranian Sufism: Blueprint

**Source**: Henry Corbin, *The Man of Light in Iranian Sufism*, tr. Nancy Pearson (Omega, 1994)
**Text**: 7,969 lines extracted, 501KB

**Essays**: 8, each covering a distinct theme with no overlap

---

## Essay 1 — The Pole That Holds the World Together

**Territory**: I. Orientation (§1 The Pole of Orientation, §2 The Symbols of the North) — lines 180-686

**Core theme**: Corbin opens by establishing the "pole" (qutb) as the spiritual axis around which all orientation revolves. The North is not a direction but a state — the place of origin, the unseen source of light. The symbols of the North in Hermeticism, Iranian gnosis, and Suhrawardi's Oriental theosophy.

**Passages to extract**: The Pole as spiritual axis, the North as source of light, the symbolism of the bear constellations, the "Occident" and "Orient of the soul," the stranger from the North.

## Essay 2 — The Man of Light and His Guide

**Territory**: II. The Man of Light and His Guide — lines 687-1757

**Core theme**: The "man of light" archetype — the inner guide, the celestial self, the personal angel who appears as a figure of light. Suhrawardi's recital of the Occidental Exile. The Perfect Nature (tab') as the individual Angel. The encounter with the guide as the condition of spiritual vision.

**Passages to extract**: The man of light as the daimon/angel, the Perfect Nature, the Occidental Exile recital, the encounter with the guide, the guide as the soul's own celestial counterpart.

## Essay 3 — Midnight Sun: The Pole as Celestial Vision

**Territory**: III. Midnight Sun and Celestial Pole (§1 The Cosmic North, §2 Visions of the Pole in Ruzbehan) — lines 1758-2722

**Core theme**: The mystical geography of the pole. The "midnight sun" as theophany — light that shines in the darkest place. Ruzbehan Baqli's visions of the pole as the encounter with the divine form. The celestial pole as the point where heaven touches earth.

**Passages to extract**: The midnight sun as symbol, the cosmic north in Oriental theosophy, Ruzbehan's visions, theophany at the pole.

---

## Essay 4 — The Angel Sraosha

**Territory**: III.§3 (The Pole as the Abode of the Angel Sraosha) — lines 2722-3120 approximately

**Core theme**: The Angel Sraosha (Sraosha) — the angel of listening, of inspiration, of the celestial pole. In Zoroastrian and Iranian Islamic angelology, Sraosha is the being who hears prayers and transmits revelation. His abode is the pole itself. The angelology of the pole.

**Passages to extract**: Sraosha's identity, function, abode, relation to the pole, the angel of listening.

---

## Essay 5 — The Emerald Vision: Najmoddin Kobra and the Colored Lights

**Territory**: IV. Visio Smaragdina (§1 Najmoddin Kobra, §2 Light and Spiritual Warfare, §3 The Trilogy of the Soul) — lines 2723-4370

**Core theme**: Najmoddin Kobra's "emerald vision" — the green light that appears at the beginning of the spiritual path. The phenomenology of colored lights (photisms) in Kubrawi Sufism. "Light and spiritual warfare" — the combat between light and darkness within the soul. The three stages of the soul: darkness, colored light, pure light.

**Passages to extract**: The emerald vision, colored lights as diagnostic signs, the struggle with darkness, the trilogy of the soul's transformation, each color as a spiritual station.

---

## Essay 6 — The Black Light

**Territory**: V. The Black Light (§1 Light without Matter, §2 Doctrine of Photisms, §3 The Rose Garden) — lines 4371-5315

**Core theme**: The black light — the most mysterious of all the photisms. Light that is not from this world, darkness that is more luminous than brightness. The doctrine of photisms in Najm Razi. The black light in Mahmud Shabistari's *Rose Garden of Mystery*. The black light as theophany of the Essence beyond all attributes.

**Passages to extract**: Light without matter, the doctrine of photisms, the black light as the highest unveiling, Shabistari's poetry on black light, light beyond light.

---

## Essay 7 — The Seven Prophets of Your Being

**Territory**: VI. The Seven Prophets of Your Being (§1-2, up to The World of Colors) — lines 5316-6407

**Core theme**: The seven prophets as the seven faculties/organs of the soul. Each prophet corresponds to a spiritual center: Muhammad (the heart), Abraham (the spirit), etc. The inner prophetology of the human being. The "seven prophets of your being" as the key to spiritual anthropology.

**Passages to extract**: The seven prophets as soul faculties, inner prophetology, each prophet's spiritual organ, the correspondence between outer prophecy and inner constitution.

---

## Essay 8 — The World of Colors and the Goethean Spectrum

**Territory**: VI.§2-3 (The World of Colors and the Man of Light, The "Physiological" Colors According to Goethe) — lines 5800-6407

**Core theme**: The phenomenology of color as spiritual perception. Goethe's color theory as a Western parallel to the Kubrawi phenomenology of photisms. The "physiological" colors as the bridge between body, soul, and spirit. The man of light as the one who sees all colors in their spiritual significance.

**Passages to extract**: The world of colors as theophany, Goethe's color theory as parallel to Kubrawi photisms, physiological colors, the man of light as the perfect perceiver.
