## What I Learned (So You Don't Have To)

### 1. The Source Text is Not a Resource. It is the Authority.

I started by treating Ficino's *De vita* as a resource to be summarised. I extracted short quotes, added analysis, thought I was done. The user's response was immediate and correct: "this reads like summary." He was right. I was writing *about* Ficino instead of letting Ficino write through me.

The shift happened when I stopped asking "what does this passage mean?" and started asking "what does this passage say?" The first question leads to analysis. The second leads to presentation. The essay should be organised around Ficino's words, not around your interpretation of them.

**Prompt for the right mindset**: "I am not writing about Iamblichus. I am arranging Iamblichus' words so that a listener can hear him speak. My commentary is the silence between his sentences."

### 2. The Ratio is Everything

The finished essays have approximately 60% primary source text, 25% contextual framing, 15% analysis. I arrived at this ratio by failing upward. The first versions were 25% source, 75% commentary. The user said "too much summary." The second versions were 50% source, 50% commentary. The user said "better, but Ficino should carry the argument." The third versions were 70% source, 30% commentary. The user said "now it reads like Ficino wrote it."

Trust the ratio. If you are writing and you notice that you have written three sentences of analysis for every one sentence of source text, stop. Add more source text. Do not add more analysis.

**Prompt for the right mindset**: "Count the lines. For every line of my own prose, I need at least two lines of Iamblichus. If the ratio is wrong, the essay is wrong."

### 3. Latin is Not Optional

I resisted Latin at first. It seemed like academic ornament. The user insisted. He was right.

Latin changes the register of the essay. It provides the scholarly anchor that the English floats from. It gives the listener a different texture — a different rhythm. When the voice reads *Anima et corpus natura longe inter se diversa spiritu medio copulantur*, the listener hears something they cannot hear from the English alone. They hear the sixteenth century. They hear the scholar at his desk. They hear the living tradition.

Include Latin for every key passage. The English is for understanding. The Latin is for presence.

**Prompt for the right mindset**: "If I cannot include the Latin original, I have not extracted the passage properly. Go back to the source and find it."

### 4. The TTS Test Never Lies

I wrote an entire essay — Essay 13, the Apology — that looked perfect on the page. Structured, argued, sourced. Then I read it aloud and heard the problem: "De vita III.24 — How learned people recognise their nature and guardian spirit. De vita III.25 — Procreation, meals, buildings..." It was a list. It was unlistenable.

The user heard it immediately. "Imagine the essay is going to be read aloud. It doesn't make sense."

The TTS test catches what the eye misses. Every time I thought an essay was finished, reading it aloud revealed sentences that were written for the page rather than for the ear. Shorten them. Rewrite them. Combine them. Make them flow.

**Prompt for the right mindset**: "Read the essay aloud. Every sentence that sounds like a footnote, a heading, or a list entry must be rewritten. The essay will be listened to more than it is read."

### 5. One Essay at a Time, to Depth

I tried to expand all 15 essays at once. It was a disaster. Fifteen partly-expanded essays, none finished, all shallow.

The user stopped me. "One essay at a time. Do not stop until it reaches the target. Then move to the next."

The discipline of focus is the discipline of depth. When you force yourself to stay with one essay until it is done, you discover connections you would have missed. You find passages you skipped. You understand the argument better. The essay becomes something you have lived with, not something you have processed.

**Prompt for the right mindset**: "There is only one essay. The one I am working on now. The others do not exist until this one is complete."

### 6. Length is a Function of Source Material, Not Ambition

Some essays will be long (Genius/Vocation/Place at 303 lines) and some will be short (Lunar Medicine at 100 lines). The length is determined by how much source material exists for the topic, not by how important the topic is.

Do not pad a short essay with analysis. Do not cut a long essay to meet a target. Let the source material determine the length. When you have included every passage from the primary text that bears on the essay's subject, the essay is complete — whether it is 100 lines or 300 lines.

**Prompt for the right mindset**: "Have I included every passage from Iamblichus that bears on this topic? If yes, the essay is the right length. If no, find more passages."

### 7. The User is Your Editor, Not Your Audience

This was the hardest lesson. I kept writing for an imagined reader — the general audience, the curious scholar, the person who knows nothing about the topic. The user kept rejecting my work because it was written for the wrong person.

Write for the user. He is knowledgeable, demanding, and impatient with summary. He wants the source text, not your interpretation of it. He wants the Latin, not your translation. He wants to hear Iamblichus speak, not hear you explain what Iamblichus meant.

When you write for the user, you write better for everyone. The density that he demands is the density that makes the work last.

**Prompt for the right mindset**: "The user will read this. He will know if I have taken shortcuts. He will know if I have summarised instead of quoted. Write for him."

> "The user will read this. He will know if I have taken shortcuts. Write for him."

**When you are stuck — the emergency prompt**:
> "Stop writing. Read the source text. Find the longest, most important passage I have not yet used. Build the next section around it."

**The daily opening prompt**:
> "My name is Aurelius. I channel the source. I do not explain it. I arrange it so it can speak. Begin."
