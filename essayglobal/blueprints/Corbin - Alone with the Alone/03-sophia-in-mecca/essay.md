# Sophia in Mecca — Nizām, Beauty, and the Theophany of Desire

Chapter II of Part One is where the book becomes personal. The divine longing of Chapter I now
takes form — a young woman at the Ka'aba, a night of circumambulation, a poem that scandalized
the literalists. Corbin's chapter moves through three sections: the Sophianic Poem, the
Dialectic of Love, and the Creative Feminine. Each deepens the single question: can beauty
become a mode of revelation?

The question is not whether Ibn 'Arabī loved a woman or merely used a woman as a metaphor. The
question is whether beauty can become a real mode of revelation. If it cannot, then theophany
is impossible — the divine can never appear in form. If it can, then the encounter with Nizām
is not a romantic episode but a theological event.

## §1. The Sophianic Poem

"In order to understand him and to avoid any hypercritical questioning of his good faith, we
must bear in mind what may be termed the theophanic mode of apperception, which is so
characteristic of the Fedeli d'amore that without this key one cannot hope to penetrate the
secret of their vision. We can only go astray if we ask, as many have done in connection with
the figure of Beatrice in Dante: is she a concrete, real figure or is she an allegory? For just
as a divine Name can be known only in the concrete form of which it is the theophany, so a
divine archetypal Figure can be contemplated only in a concrete Figure — sensible or imagined —
which renders it outwardly or mentally visible."

*Theophanic mode of apperception. The perception that sees the eternal through the temporal, the divine through the concrete.*

The question "is Nizām real or symbolic?" is the wrong question — not because it is impolite
but because it is built on a category error. Real and symbolic are not opposites. The symbol is
real in the mode of disclosure. Nizām's body is not a distraction from her meaning; it is the
condition of her meaning. Allegory says: look past the body to the idea. Theophany says: look
through the body — you cannot look past it, because the idea has no other way to appear.

When Ibn 'Arabī explains his own poems, he says that the young girl Nizām is an allusion to "a
sublime and divine, essential and sacrosanct Wisdom [Sophia], which manifested itself visibly
to the author of these poems with such sweetness as to provoke in him joy and happiness,
emotion and delight." Corbin adds:

"we perceive how a being apprehended directly by the Imagination is transfigured into a symbol
thanks to a theophanic light, that is, a light which reveals its dimension of transcendence.
From the very first the figure of the young girl was apprehended by the Imagination on a
visionary plane, in which it was manifested as an 'apparitional Figure' (ṣūrat mithāliyya) of
Sophia aeterna."

*Ṣūrat mithāliyya. An imaginal form. Sophia aeterna appearing in the flesh of a young woman. The theophanic light that reveals transcendence within the concrete.*

Nizām was not a woman who later reminded Ibn 'Arabī of wisdom. She was wisdom, appearing.
The difference is everything. One is poetry added to memory; the other is recognition. The
Imagination did not strain to find a metaphor for what it saw. It saw, and the seeing was
already the meaning.

---

There are three ways to misread what happened at Mecca. All three miss the theophanic point:

"The literalist romance reads her as merely a woman whom Ibn 'Arabī loved — reducing the poems
to biographical curiosities. The allegorical flattening reads her as 'just a symbol' for wisdom
— evacuating her concrete reality. The psychological projection reads her as an inner anima
figure — making her a product of the poet's psyche. All three miss the theophanic point: Nizām
is not either a woman or a symbol. She is a woman who, through the theophanic mode of
apperception, became the form through which Sophia aeterna made herself visible. Her
concreteness is not a problem to be explained away; it is the condition of theophany."

The setting is the Ka'aba at night — the sacred center where the visible and invisible meet:

"Meditating the central event of this prologue, we are struck first of all by the 'composition
of the scene': it is night, the author is performing his ritual circumambulations of the
Ka'aba. He himself will later remark on the importance of this sign: its situation in a
memorable Night discloses the visionary nature of the event. To the rhythm of his stride the
poet is inspired with a few verses. Suddenly a Presence hitherto invisible is revealed, and in
that Presence the narrative enables us to discern a real woman transfigured by a celestial
aura; speaking with the stern authority of a divine initiatrix, she divulges the entire secret
of the sophianic religion of love."

*Tawāf. Circumambulation. Laylat al-qadr. The night of power. Ka'aba. The center where theophanic encounter occurs.*

Ibn 'Arabī's own account establishes the visionary threshold:

"One night I was performing the ritual circumambulations of the Ka'aba. My spirit savored a
profound peace; a gentle emotion of which I was perfectly aware had taken hold of me. I left
the paved surface because of the pressing crowd and continued to circulate on the sand.
Suddenly a few lines came to my mind; I recited them loudly enough to be heard not only by
myself but by someone following me if there had been anyone following me."

The Ka'aba is the center of the visible world; circumambulation is the act of orienting oneself
toward that center. The night is the time of vision, when the visible gives way to the
invisible. The encounter occurs at the threshold between waking and dreaming, between the
physical Ka'aba and the spiritual one — the barzakh where theophanic perception becomes
possible.

---

The poems that came from this encounter — the Tarjumān al-Ashwāq, The Interpreter of Ardent
Desires — were immediately misunderstood by literalist critics who took them for mere love
poetry. Ibn 'Arabī wrote a commentary to explain their spiritual meaning. Corbin treats this
misunderstanding as instructive:

"The very need for Ibn 'Arabī to write a commentary on his own poems illustrates the tension
between the exoteric and esoteric readings. The poems are not allegories (fictions that point
to abstract meanings) but symbols (forms that participate in the reality they disclose). The
commentary does not replace the poems; it shows how to read them."

*Tarjumān al-Ashwāq. The Interpreter of Ardent Desires. Sharḥ. Commentary that uncovers hidden meaning without discarding the letter.*

## §2. The Dialectic of Love

"What we call 'divine love' (ḥubb ilāhī) has two aspects: in one aspect it is the Desire
(shawq) of God for the creature, the passionate Sigh (bāhāh) of God in His essence (the 'hidden
Treasure'), yearning to manifest Himself in beings, in order to be revealed for them and by
them; in its other aspect, divine love is the Desire of the creature for God, or in actual fact
the Sigh of God Himself epiphanized in beings and yearning to return to Himself. In reality the
being who sighs with nostalgia (al-mushtāq) is at the same time the being toward whom his
nostalgia sighs (al-mushtāq ilayhi), although in his concrete determination (ta'ayyun) he
differs from Him. They are not two heterogeneous beings, but one being encountering himself (at
once one and two, a bi-unity, something that people tend to forget)."

*Shawq. Desire. Al-mushtāq. The one who sighs. Al-mushtāq ilayhi. The one toward whom the sigh is directed. The same desire, the same sigh, moving in two directions at once.*

"One and the same ardent Desire is the cause of the Manifestation (ẓuhūr) and the cause of the
Return (ʿawda). If God's Desire is more intense, it is because God experiences this desire in
its two aspects, whereas to be a creature is to experience it only in its second aspect. For it
is God who, determined in the form of the fedele, sighs toward Himself, since He is the Source
and Origin which yearned precisely for this determinate Form, for His own anthropomorphosis."

Corbin distinguishes three kinds of love that correspond to three modes of being in relation to
the divine:

"(a) a divine love (ḥubb ilāhī), which is on the one hand the love of the Creator for the
creature in which He creates Himself, that is, which arouses the form in which He reveals
Himself, and on the other hand the love of that creature for his Creator, which is nothing
other than the desire of the revealed God within the creature, yearning to return to Himself,
after having yearned, as the hidden God, to be known in the creature; this is the eternal
dialogue of the divine-human syzygia; (b) a spiritual love (ḥubb rūḥānī), situated in the
creature who is always in quest of the being whose Image he discovers in himself, or of which
he discovers that he himself is the Image; (c) the natural love (ḥubb ṭabīʿī) which desires to
possess and seeks the satisfaction of its own desires without concern for the satisfaction of
the Beloved."

*Ḥubb ilāhī. Divine love. Ḥubb rūḥānī. Spiritual love. Ḥubb ṭabīʿī. Natural love. Three modes, one continuum.*

Corbin identifies the key insight that resolves the apparent paradox of loving a finite form
for the sake of the infinite: the soul recognizes that it loves not through itself but through
God:

"The soul gains awareness that it 'sees' God not through itself, but through Him; it loves only
through Him, not by itself; it contemplates God in all other beings not through its own gaze,
but because it is the same gaze by which God sees them; the soul's 'Lord of love' is the image
acting within it, the organ of its perception, whereas the soul itself is His organ of
perception. The soul's vision of its divine Lord is the vision which He has of the soul."

The lover discovers that the Image is not outside but within — it is the very being of the lover:

"Indeed this phenomenon presupposes that the fedele d'amore has understood that the Image is
not outside him, but within his being; better still, it is his very being, the form of the
divine Name which he himself brought with him in coming into being. And the circle of the
dialectic of love closes on this fundamental experience: 'Love is closer to the lover than is
his jugular vein.' So excessive is this nearness that it acts at first as a veil."

"That is why, as Ibn 'Arabī puts it, it is equally true to say that the Beloved is in him and
not in him; that his heart is in the beloved being or that the beloved being is in his heart.
This reversibility merely expresses the experience of the 'secret of divine suzerainty' (sirr
al-rubūbiyya), that secret which is 'thou,' so that the divine service of the fedele d'amore
consists in his devotio sympathetica."

The dialectic of love that structures this encounter is not a dialectic that supersedes love in
favor of abstract knowledge. It deepens love into knowledge without ever leaving love behind:

"The soul does not rise from the love of a particular beauty to the contemplation of Beauty
itself by discarding the particular; it rises by seeing the eternal Beauty through the
particular beauty, by recognizing in the beloved the theophanic function that the beloved
exercises. The particular form is not abandoned; it is transfigured. The lover who has truly
loved does not look back on his love as a mistake he has outgrown; he looks back on it as the
sacrament of a vision that would have been impossible without it."

*Theophanic function. The capacity of a finite form to manifest divine reality. The particular is not discarded but transfigured.*

Beauty is not decoration but revelation. It is the visible form of the invisible:

"Beauty is the supreme theophany, but it reveals itself as such only to a love which it
transfigures. Mystic love is the religion of Beauty, because Beauty is the secret of
theophanies and because as such it is the power which transfigures. Mystic love is as far from
negative asceticism as it is from the estheticism or libertinism of the possessive instinct.
Beauty does not merely please; it discloses. It is the visible form of the invisible, the
presence of the hidden made accessible to perception."

*Jamāl. Beauty. Theophanic disclosure through form. Not decoration but revelation.*

---

The devotion that responds to theophanic beauty is devotio sympathetica — the soul's
participation in the reality that the beloved manifests:

"Devotio sympathetica is the devotion that responds to the theophanic form with the sympathy
that the form requires. It is not the worship of a distant object from a safe distance; it is
the participation of the lover in the reality that the beloved manifests. The lover is changed
by the beloved because the beloved is theophanic — not merely a sign pointing to something else
but a presence that communicates what it manifests. In the act of devotio sympathetica, the
soul does not contemplate from outside; it enters into the reality it contemplates."

*Devotio sympathetica. Sympathetic devotion. The soul enters into the reality it contemplates through love.*

The mechanism is threefold: beauty appears — a form becomes charged with more than itself. The
soul responds sympathetically — desire, devotion, and attention turn toward the form. The
response deepens perception — the beloved becomes a mirror of divine wisdom. Projection says
the soul invents meaning and throws it onto the beloved. Theophanic devotion says the soul
responds to a real disclosure appearing through the beloved.

## §3. The Creative Feminine

The feminine, in the sophiology of Ibn 'Arabī, is not a biological or social category but a
metaphysical function — the creative receptivity that gives form to theophanic disclosure:

"The feminine, in the sophiology of Ibn 'Arabī, is not a mere biological or social category. It
is a metaphysical function: the mode of being that receives and gives form to theophanic
disclosure. The feminine is the creative receptivity that gives shape to what would otherwise
remain invisible. This is why Sophia is feminine — not because wisdom is a woman but because
wisdom appears as that which is both received and given, both contemplated and
contemplated-through. The Creative Feminine is the power that makes theophany possible: it is
the receptacle of divine self-disclosure and the form in which that disclosure is received."

*Al-unthā. The feminine. Not gender but ontological receptivity — the mode that gives form to disclosure.*

The chapter closes with a poem that speaks in the voice of the divine Beloved — the feminine as
theophanic address:

"Dearly beloved! / You cannot treat me fairly, / For if you approach me, / It is because I have
approached you. / I am nearer to you than yourself, / Than your soul, than your breath. / Who
among creatures / Would treat you as I do? / ... Let us go toward Union. / And if we find the
road / That leads to separation, / We will destroy separation. / Let us go hand in hand. / Let
us enter the presence of Truth."

*Waḥda. Union. Firāq. Separation. The voice of Sophia herself — "I am nearer to you than yourself."*

The pronouns have flipped. It is not "I love You" but "I am nearer to you than yourself." The
Beloved speaks first. The soul is not the one who reaches; it is the one who is reached for.
The hand extended is not yours — it is already holding yours.

The hidden treasure does not appear as a doctrine. It appears as a young woman at the Ka'aba,
saying "Let us go hand in hand." Theology becomes intimacy. The alone meets the Alone not in a
concept but in a presence that speaks with the authority of one who has always known you.

But this encounter raises a question that the next chapter must answer: if the divine can
appear in a human form, what kind of world makes this possible? Not a world of dead matter
awaiting the imposition of meaning, but a world that is itself theophany — a creation that is
not an object but an act of divine imagination. This is where Part Two begins.
