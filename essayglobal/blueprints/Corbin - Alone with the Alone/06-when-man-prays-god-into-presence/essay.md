# When Man Prays God Into Presence — The Method of Theophanic Prayer

Chapter V of Part Two is where the entire book becomes practical. The heart has been identified
as the organ of theophanic perception (Essay 5); now the act that activates this organ must be
understood. Prayer is not petition to a distant God. It is the method by which the soul,
through the Imagination, enters into the presence of the God who has chosen to manifest Himself
in the forms of the imaginal world. The chapter moves through three sections: the Method of
Theophanic Prayer, Homologations, and Divine Responses.

## §1. The Method of Theophanic Prayer

Corbin grounds theophanic prayer in the idea of a sharing of roles between God and the soul —
the same sharing that the ellipse of compassion described in Essay 2:

"This idea of a sharing of roles in the manifestation of being, in the eternal theophany, is
fundamental to Ibn 'Arabī's notion of prayer; it inspires what we have termed his method of
prayer and makes it a 'method of theophanic prayer.' The notion of sharing presupposes a
dialogue between two beings, and this living experience of a dialogical situation confutes any
theoretical attempt to reduce the unity of dialogue to an existential monism; the truth is,
rather, that the unity of being conditions the dialogical situation."

The modern mind hears "prayer" and thinks "petition" — a beggar at a king's gate, asking for favors. Corbin shatters this picture entirely:

"For prayer is not a request for something: it is the expression of a mode of being, a means of
existing and of causing to exist, that is, a means of causing the God who reveals Himself to
appear, of 'seeing' Him, not to be sure in His essence, but in the form which precisely He
reveals by revealing Himself by and to that form. This view of Prayer takes the ground from
under the feet of those who, utterly ignorant of the nature of the theophanic Imagination as
Creation, argue that a God who is the 'creation' of our Imagination can only be 'unreal' and
that there can be no purpose in praying to such a God. For it is precisely because He is a
creation of the imagination that we pray to him, and that He exists."

*Sharing of roles. Dialogue. Prayer as the form in which God appears to and through the soul.*

"Prayer is the highest form, the supreme act of the Creative Imagination. By virtue of the
sharing of roles, the divine Compassion, as theophany and existentiation of the universe of
beings, is the Prayer of God aspiring to issue forth from His unknownness and to be known,
whereas the Prayer of man accomplishes this theophany because in it and through it the 'Form of
God' (ṣūrat al-Ḥaqq) becomes visible to the heart, to the Active Imagination."

God prays toward the soul as much as the soul prays toward God. The two prayers are one movement:

"God prays for us (yuṣallī ʿalaynā), which means that He epiphanizes Himself insofar as He is
the God whom and for whom we pray (that is, the God who epiphanizes Himself for us and by us).
We do not pray to the Divine Essence in its hiddenness; each faithful (ʿabd) prays to his Lord
(Rabb), the Lord who is in the form of his faith."

The method unfolds in three phases — corresponding to the three moments of the Fātiḥa, the sura
that opens the Koran:

"He distinguishes three successive moments which correspond to the phases of what we may call
his 'method of prayer' and provide us with a good indication of how he put his spirituality
into practice. First, the faithful must place himself in the company of his God and 'converse'
with Him. In an intermediate moment the orant must imagine (takhayyul) his God as present in
his Qibla, that is, facing him. Finally, in a third moment, the faithful must attain to
intuitive vision (shuhūd) or visualization (ru'ya), contemplating his God in the subtile center
which is the heart, and simultaneously hear the divine voice vibrating in all manifest things."

*Three phases: conversation, imaginative presence, visionary contemplation. The entire arc of the companion in three moments.*

The Fātiḥa itself, when prayed theophanicly, becomes a dialogue between the soul and God:

"The sura 'which opens' the Koran is composed of seven verses. As meditated by our shaykh, its
liturgical action breaks down into three phases; the first (that is, the first three verses) is
the action of the faithful toward or upon his Lord; the second (the fourth verse) is a
reciprocal action between the Lord and his faithful; the third (the three last verses) is an
action of the Lord toward and upon his faithful. In each of these verses, the Divine Presence,
to which the faithful makes himself present, and which he makes present to himself, is attested
by a divine response, vibrating as though in an undertone. This response is not a poetic or
rhetorical fiction, in which the Godhead is arbitrarily 'made to speak.' The divine response
merely records the event of Prayer from the viewpoint of the being to whom it is addressed."

Prayer is not petition to a distant God. It is the method by which the soul, through the
Imagination, enters into the presence of the God who has chosen to manifest Himself in the
forms of the imaginal world:

"Prayer is not a request addressed to an absent God; it is the activation of a presence that is
already there but hidden. The one who prays does not speak into a void; he speaks from within
the theophanic relation that constitutes his very being."

*Ṣalāt. Tawajjuh. Prayer as presence, as orientation, as turning of the whole heart.*

Theophanic prayer is creative because it participates in the divine Imagination by which all
things exist:

"When the soul prays with the full concentration of the heart (himma), it gives form to the
divine presence it invokes. This does not mean the soul creates God; it means the soul gives
form to its relation to God — and that form, because it is formed in the imaginal world, is
real. Prayer is the act by which the soul, in the 'ālam al-mithāl, brings forth the theophanic
form that corresponds to its faith, its love, and its longing. The God who appears in prayer is
not a God invented by the soul but the God who has chosen to appear according to the capacity
of the soul that receives Him."

Prayer is co-imagining with God. The soul does not address an absent deity but enters into the
divine self-disclosure that is always already occurring. The form that appears in prayer is
real — not as a material object but as an event in the imaginal world. This is why prayer
changes things: not because it persuades a reluctant God but because it brings the soul into
deeper participation in the theophany that creation already is.

## §2. Homologations

The creativity of prayer is connected to the cosmic meaning of prayer perceived by Proclus in
the heliotrope. Corbin develops two forms of homologation — the soul as Imām of its own
microcosm, and the body's postures as cosmic gestures:

"In one of these homologations, the man in prayer represents himself as the Imām of his own
microcosm. In another the ritual gestures of Prayer are likened to the acts of the Creation of
the universe. These homologations presuppose the meaning of Prayer as creative; they prepare,
ground, and justify its visionary denouement, since precisely as new creation it signifies new
epiphany."

First, the soul as Imām:

"The mystic himself is invested in the dignity of the Imām in relation to his own universe, his
microcosm. He is the Imām for 'the angels who pray behind him,' in ranks like the faithful in a
mosque, but invisible. But the condition of this personal divine service is precisely solitude.
'Every orant (muṣallī) is an Imām, for the Angels pray behind the worshiper when he prays
alone.'"

*Imām. The one who stands before. The soul as the guide of its own universe in prayer.*

Second, the body's postures as cosmic homologations:

"It is based on the attitudes of the body prescribed in the course of ritual Prayer: erect
stance (qiyām), profound inclination (rukūʿ), prosternation (sujūd)."

"A movement of pure thought transfers the universe of beings from its state of occultation to
the manifest state of concrete existence which constitutes theophany. In this visible world,
the movements of natural beings can be reduced to three categories. And the ritual of Prayer
embraces all these movements: (a) The ascending, vertical movement which corresponds to the
faithful's erect stance. This is the movement of the growth of man, whose head rises toward the
heavens. (b) The horizontal movement, which corresponds to the orant's state at the moment of
the profound inclination. This is the direction in which animals grow. (c) The inverse,
descending movement, corresponding to the prosternation. This is the movement of the plant,
sinking its roots in depth. Thus Prayer reproduces the movements of the creatural universe; it
is itself recurrence of Creation and new Creation."

*Qiyām. Rukūʿ. Sujūd. Standing, bowing, prostration. The body traces the arc of all created being — ascending, horizontal, descending.*

"Homologations are the correspondences that structure the theophanic universe. The position of
the body in prayer — standing, bowing, prostrating — is not arbitrary ritual; it is the
homologation of the soul's relation to God expressed through the body. The standing is the
soul's attentiveness; the bowing is its humility; the prostration is its total surrender. In
the same way, the directions of prayer, the times of prayer, the words of prayer — all are
homologations, earthly correspondences of heavenly realities. The sympathetic universe means
that every act of the praying soul corresponds to something in the divine order — not as a
symbol represents an idea but as a mirror reflects a face."

*Muwāfaqāt. Homologations. Qiyām. Rukū'. Sujūd. Standing, bowing, prostration — the body tracing the arc of the soul.*

The body in prayer is a theophanic sign. The standing is not a posture but an attendance; the
bowing is not a gesture but a humility; the prostration is not a movement but a surrender. Each
corresponds to something in the divine order — not by arbitrary convention but by the structure
of the sympathetic universe.

## §3. Divine Responses

Corbin reveals the deepest structure of prayer — the alternation of the Names "the First" and
"the Last" between God and the soul:

"Under the inspiration of his personal philology, Ibn 'Arabī stresses a homonymy which, far
from being a mere play on words, is one of those profoundly significant homonymies due to the
polyvalence of certain Arabic roots. Thus the word muṣallī comes to signify no longer 'he who
prays' but 'he who comes after,' who 'is later than.' This homonymy throws a sudden new light
on the relationship between Prayer of God and Prayer of man, serving to determine in what sense
God and His faithful are by turns muṣallī, that is to say, receive by turns the divine Names
'the First' (al-Awwal) and 'the Last' (al-Ākhir)."

*Muṣallī. He who prays and he who comes after. The Names "First" and "Last" pass between God and the soul in prayer.*

"Thus when God is the muṣallī, 'He who prays' and who 'comes last,' He manifests Himself to us
under His Name of 'the Last' (al-Ākhir), that is to say, the Revealed, since His manifestation
depends on the existence of the faithful. The 'God who prays toward us' is precisely the
manifested God, He is the God whom the faithful creates in his heart, either by his meditations
and reflections or by the particular faith to which he adheres. To this aspect therefore
belongs the God who is designated technically as the 'God created in the faiths,' the God who
determines Himself according to the capacity of the receptacle which receives Him."

The secret of divine responses is that the soul and God alternate being "First" and "Last" —
each takes the role of the other:

"This is the case envisaged by the words of Junayd, the great mystic: 'The color of the water
is that of the vessel which contains it.' In this sense, the 'God who prays toward us' 'is
later than' our being; He is posterior to it, dependent on it; He is the God whom our theopathy
establishes as theos, because the Worshiped presupposes the existence of the worshiper to whom
He shows Himself ('by knowing Him, I give Him being'). In this sense He is therefore the
'Last,' the 'Manifested.' Here the divine Names 'the First' and 'the Hidden' are appropriate to
the faithful."

The divine response to prayer is the alternation itself — the discovery that the soul and God
exchange the roles of First and Last:

"The divine response to prayer is not a separate act that occurs after the prayer is finished.
The response is the transformation of the prayer itself into theophanic presence. When the soul
prays with full attention, the prayer becomes the place of encounter — the barzakh where the
human and the divine meet. God's response is the deepening of the prayer into vision, the
gradual unveiling of the One who was always present in the act of invocation. The response is
not a message sent from afar but the discovery that the One addressed was already present in
the act of addressing."

*Ijāba. Divine response. Barzakh. The place of encounter where prayer becomes presence.*

The response is not separate from the prayer. Prayer is itself theophanic encounter; the divine
response is the deepening of that encounter from invocation to presence. This is why theophanic
prayer does not ask for things; it asks for presence — and the asking is already the beginning
of the receiving.

The heliotrope from Chapter I returns here as the emblem of all prayer:

"The prayer of the heliotrope is the prayer of all being — the turning of each thing toward its
source. The heliotrope does not ask the sun for anything; it turns toward the sun, and in that
turning it receives the light that it needs. All prayer, in its deepest form, is this
heliotropic turning — not a petition but an orientation, not a request but a presence. The soul
that prays theophanic prayer becomes a heliotrope of the divine — turning not toward a distant
sun but toward the Sun that is always present, always shining, always giving itself in the act
of illumination."

*Ḥarakat al-ḥubb. The movement of love. The heliotropic turning of all being toward its source.*

Prayer is not asking; it is turning. The soul does not draw God down by its petitions; it turns
itself toward the divine presence that is always already there. The heliotrope does not ask the
sun to come closer; it turns itself toward the light that is already shining. The turning and
the receiving are one act.

The method of theophanic prayer is the discipline of this turning — the concentration of the
whole heart on the divine presence that surrounds and sustains it. The body turns, the heart
turns, the soul turns — and in the turning, the One who was always present becomes present-to.
This is the prayer that does not ask for things but for presence. And in the asking, the
presence is received.

But if prayer can become presence, then presence can become vision. The final chapter asks the
question that the entire book has been preparing: can God be seen in form without the form
becoming an idol? Prayer opens into vision. The response deepens into theophany. And the
theophany raises the final question: what is the face that cannot be seen?
