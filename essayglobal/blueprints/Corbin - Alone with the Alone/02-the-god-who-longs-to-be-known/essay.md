# The God Who Longs to Be Known — Divine Passion, Compassion, and the Birth of Theophany

Corbin's first chapter after the Introduction does not begin with a doctrine of God. It begins
with a plant. The heliotrope, turning toward the sun, is the emblem of everything that follows:
sympathy between visible and invisible, the coincidence of action and passion, the prayer that
is not petition but orientation. Chapter I moves through three sections — the Prayer of the
Heliotrope, the "Pathetic God," and Unio Mystica as Unio Sympathetica — each one deepening the
claim that the origin of manifestation is not power but longing.

There is a way of speaking about God that makes creation feel like an administrative decision:
God wills; the world appears. Corbin's Ibn 'Arabī gives us something stranger. The origin of
manifestation is not command but longing. Not lack in the crude sense, but hidden plenitude
desiring disclosure.

## §1. The Prayer of the Heliotrope

Corbin opens the chapter with Proclus's hymn to the heliotrope — a plant whose turning toward
the sun is not mere tropism but a prayer, a participation in the sympathy that binds all levels
of being:

"Proclus writes: 'Just as in the dialectic of love we start from sensuous beauties to rise
until we encounter the unique principle of all beauty and all ideas, so the adepts of hieratic
science take as their starting point the things of appearance and the sympathies they manifest
among themselves and with the invisible powers. Observing that all things form a whole, they
laid the foundations of hieratic science, wondering at the first realities and admiring in them
the latest comers as well as the very first among beings; in heaven, terrestrial things
according both to a causal and to a celestial mode and on earth heavenly things in a
terrestrial state. Example: the heliotrope and its prayer.'"

*Hieratic science. The knowledge of sympathies between visible and invisible. The heliotrope as the emblem of all prayer.*

This is not decorative poetry. Proclus is making a claim about the structure
of reality: there is a knowledge that precedes reasoning, a direct perception
of sympathy between things. The heliotrope does not calculate its turning — it
turns because it participates in the sun's own movement. The plant's action
and the sun's attraction are one event seen from two sides. This is what
Corbin means by hieratic science: a knowing that is also a being-known, a
perception that is also a participation.

"What other reason can we give for the fact that the heliotrope follows in its movement the
movement of the sun and the selenotrope the movement of the moon, forming a procession within
the limits of their power, behind the torches of the universe? For, in truth, each thing prays
according to the rank it occupies in nature, and sings the praise of the leader of the divine
series to which it belongs, a spiritual or rational or physical or sensuous praise; for the
heliotrope moves to the extent that it is free to move, and in its rotation, if we could hear
the sound of the air buffeted by its movement, we should be aware that it is a hymn to its
king, such as it is within the power of a plant to sing."

The heliotrope is the emblem of the entire chapter. Its turning toward the sun is at once
action (its own movement) and passion (being drawn by the sun). This coincidence of action and
passion is the structure of sympathy, and the structure of divine-human relation. The
heliotrope does not ask; it turns. And in turning, it receives.

Corbin draws out the parallel between the heliotrope's prayer and the dialectic of love:

"This passage by a philosopher and poet endowed with a hieratic sense of Beauty, strikes us as
an exemplary text eminently suited to preface the themes which will here be at the center of
our meditation. It establishes a connection between the 'dialectic of love' and hieratic art,
which are grounded on the same principle: the essential community between visible and invisible
beings. 'On earth,' Proclus goes on to say, 'suns and moons can be seen in an earthly state and
in the heavens all the plants, stones, animals in a heavenly state, living spiritually.' This
common essence, which is distributed among several beings, is not perceived through argument
proceeding from effect to cause; it is the perception of a sympathy, of a reciprocal and
simultaneous attraction between the manifest being and his celestial prince."

The plant's heliotropism — its turning toward the sun — is at once action and passion: "its
action (that is to say, its tropos, its 'conversion') is perceived as the action (that is, the
attraction) of the Angel or celestial prince whose name for that very reason it bears. Its
heliotropism (its 'conversion' toward its celestial prince) is thus in fact a keliopathy (the
pathos, the passion, of the celestial prince attracting it)."

The plant moves, but the sun also moves — not in space, but in the order of being. One reaches,
the other draws. The reaching and the drawing are the same event. This coincidence is the
signature of theopathy everywhere: your prayer and God's attraction to your prayer are one
movement. You thought you were reaching. You were also being reached for.

## §2. The "Pathetic God"

"We must here introduce a term which will play an essential part in our exposition: the term
'theopathy.' Literally it means a state of being affected by God, or a state of being affected
in God, but it also means a state in which God Himself is affected by the passion of His
creature. Theopathy is the pathos of God and the pathos of man experienced in a single act of
sympathy. It is not that man projects his own emotions onto God; it is that God Himself has
chosen to be affected by the longing of His creature. The 'pathetic God' is not an
anthropomorphic projection; He is the God who, in the depths of His own being, experiences the
desire to be known, the longing for manifestation, the compassion that brings worlds into
existence."

*Theopathy. The pathos of God. The divine capacity for passion that is not weakness but the inner structure of self-disclosure.*

The word is deliberately shocking. Western theology has spent two millennia protecting God from passion — passion implies change, and change implies imperfection. Aristotle's Unmoved Mover cannot be moved by anything, least of all by love for creatures. Ibn 'Arabī and Corbin say: what if the capacity to be moved is not a defect but the very thing that makes manifestation possible? What if God's passion is not a wound but an abundance? Theopathy means God is not a stone wall against which prayer echoes; God is an ear, a lung, a womb that expands to receive what it has itself created.

The grammar of the divine name itself enacts this theology. The name of God in Arabic can be
read as deriving from a root meaning "to sigh, to desire, to feel compassion":

"Considering the word ulūhīya (the Godhead) as an ideogram which, by introducing a trifling
orthographic sign (a tashdīd, which doubles the n) we can read as al-hānnīya. We then have an
abstract noun denoting state, mode of being, formed from the verbal noun of the root h-n-n
meaning to desire, to sigh, to feel compassion. Thus the true name of the Divinity, the name
which expresses His hidden depths, is not the Infinite and All-Powerful of our rational
theodicies. Nothing can better bear witness to the feeling for a 'pathetic God,' which is no
less authentic than that disclosed by a phenomenology of prophetic religion."

*Al-hānnīya. The Sighing One. The God whose name means desire, sighing, compassion. The grammar of the divine name enacts the theology.*

The famous "hidden treasure" hadith is the seed from which the entire metaphysics grows. Corbin
presents it with a precision that reveals its full depth — the hadith in which the Godhead
reveals the secret of His passion:

"'I was a hidden Treasure and I yearned to be known. Then I created creatures in order to be
known by them.' With still greater fidelity to Ibn 'Arabī's thought, let us translate: 'in
order to become in them the object of my knowledge.' This divine passion, this desire to reveal
Himself and to know Himself in beings through being known by them, is the motive underlying an
entire divine dramaturgy, an eternal cosmogony. This cosmogony is neither an Emanation in the
Neoplatonic sense of the word nor, still less, a creatio ex nihilo. It is rather a succession
of manifestations of being, brought about by an increasing light, within the originally
undifferentiated God; it is a succession of tajalliyāt, of theophanies."

Imagine a name that is the thing it names. When you say "fire," you are not burned — the name points to the fire, it is not the fire. But the divine Names are different: "The Names, which are the divine Essence itself, because, though not identical with the divine
Essence as such, the attributes they designate are not different from it, have existed from all
eternity: these Names are designated as 'Lords' (Arbāb), who often have all the appearance of
hypostases though they cannot strictly be defined as such. We know them only by our knowledge
of ourselves (that is the basic maxim). God describes Himself to us through ourselves. Which
means that the divine Names are essentially relative to the beings who name them, since these
beings discover and experience them in their own mode of being."

*Arbāb. Lords. The divine Names as the forms through which the hidden God becomes known. Ḥaḍarāt. Presences. The states in which the Godhead reveals Himself.*

"Their aspiration is itself nothing other than the nostalgia of the divine Names yearning to be
revealed. And this nostalgia of the divine Names is nothing other than the sadness of the
unrevealed God, the anguish He experiences in His unknownness and occultation."

"And from the inscrutable depths of the Godhead this sadness calls for a 'Sigh of Compassion'
(Nafas Raḥmānī). This Sigh marks the release of the divine Sadness sym-pathizing with the
anguish and sadness of His divine names that have remained unknown, and in this very act of
release the Breath exhales, arouses to active being, the multitude of concrete individual
existences by which and for which these divine names are at last actively manifested. Thus in
its hidden being every existent is a Breath of the existentiating divine Compassion, and the
divine Name Allāh becomes purely and simply equivalent to al-Raḥmān, the Compassionate."

*Nafas al-Raḥmān. The Sigh of the Compassionate. The Breath that releases divine sadness into being. Allāh = al-Raḥmān. God is the Compassionate.*

The distance between creator and creature collapses. If every existent is a breath of the Sigh,
then you are not a product of God's will but a syllable of God's speech — spoken not because
God needed to say something but because God needed to sigh.

From this longing flows compassion — not as a moral attribute but as the very structure of
existence:

"Raḥma, usually translated as mercy or compassion, has in the Sufi vocabulary an ontological
meaning. It is not merely a moral quality that God exercises toward His creatures; it is the
very act by which beings are given existence. The root r-ḥ-m, from which raḥma derives, is
related to the word for womb (raḥim). Mercy is the generative matrix of being. To exist is
already to be touched by mercy. The world does not arise from a neutral act of fabrication but
from the compassionate expansion of the divine toward that which is not yet — the mercy that
wills the other into being."

*Raḥma. Raḥim. Mercy. Womb. To be is to be-mercied. Existence is compassion made substantial.*

## §3. Unio Mystica as Unio Sympathetica

"The unio mystica with which we are concerned is not a fusion or absorption in which the
individual disappears. It is a unio sympathetica — a union in which the lover and the Beloved
remain distinct while their essences are intertwined in sympathy. The lover does not cease to
be the lover; the Beloved does not cease to be the Beloved. But their life is one life, their
breath one breath, their desire one desire — not because one has swallowed the other but
because each is the condition of the other's being. This is the true meaning of the 'alone with
the Alone' — not a solitude of two isolated beings but a solitude shared, a presence that is
mutual and complete."

*Unio sympathetica. Unus ambo. One from two, without confusion or absorption.*

Corbin gives the structure of this union the geometry of an ellipse — two foci, distinct but
inseparable:

"This is not the movement of a dialectical pendulum, oscillating between two terms. It is
rather a movement describing the area of His Compassion in an ellipse, one focus of which is
the being of God for and through me, while the other is my being for and through Him, in other
words, the area enclosing the two of us, the area in which He is for me in proportion to my
capacity for Him and in which my knowledge of Him is His knowledge of me."

The union is mutual and reciprocal. Each focus constitutes the other:

"This perfume He exhales is the Breath of His Compassion which emancipates beings enclosed in
their unburgeoned virtuality; it is this perfume that all breathe and that is the nourishment
of their being. But because in their secret being they are this Compassion itself, the
Compassion does not move only in the direction from the Creator to the creature whom He feeds
with his existentiating Breath; it also moves from the creature toward the Creator, so that the
created universe is the theophany of His Names and attributes, which would not exist if the
creature did not exist. The same idea is formulated in different ways: 'If He has given us life
and existence by His being, I also give Him life by knowing Him in my heart.'"

God and the gnostic coinhere. The compassion moves in both directions. The creature gives God
life in the heart by knowing Him — which is the same movement by which God gives the creature
existence. This is the structure of the unio sympathetica.

The God who longs to be known — who breathes the world into being from the root h-n-n, to
desire, to sigh, to feel compassion — enters into sympathetic union with the soul that knows
Him. But this longing cannot remain abstract. It must take form. It must become visible,
personal, beautiful. The next chapter shows how this happens: through a young woman at the
Ka'aba, through the figure of Sophia, through beauty that discloses without ceasing to be
beauty.
