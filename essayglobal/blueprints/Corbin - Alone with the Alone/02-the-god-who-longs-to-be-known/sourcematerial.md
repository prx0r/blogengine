## Passage 2.1 — The Prayer of the Heliotrope

**Source**: Part One, Ch. I, §1 — "The Prayer of the Heliotrope"

**Context**: Corbin opens Chapter I with Proclus's hymn to the heliotrope — a plant whose turning toward the sun is not mere tropism but a prayer, a participation in the sympathy that binds all levels of being.

**Full passage**:

"Proclus writes: 'Just as in the dialectic of love we start from sensuous beauties to rise until we encounter the unique principle of all beauty and all ideas, so the adepts of hieratic science take as their starting point the things of appearance and the sympathies they manifest among themselves and with the invisible powers. Observing that all things form a whole, they laid the foundations of hieratic science, wondering at the first realities and admiring in them the latest comers as well as the very first among beings; in heaven, terrestrial things according both to a causal and to a celestial mode and on earth heavenly things in a terrestrial state. Example: the heliotrope and its prayer.'"

"What other reason can we give for the fact that the heliotrope follows in its movement the movement of the sun and the selenotrope the movement of the moon, forming a procession within the limits of their power, behind the torches of the universe? For, in truth, each thing prays according to the rank it occupies in nature, and sings the praise of the leader of the divine series to which it belongs, a spiritual or rational or physical or sensuous praise; for the heliotrope moves to the extent that it is free to move, and in its rotation, if we could hear the sound of the air buffeted by its movement, we should be aware that it is a hymn to its king, such as it is within the power of a plant to sing."

**Original terms**: *hieratic science* — the sacred knowledge of sympathies between visible and invisible; *heliotropism* — the turning toward the sun, which is at once action and passion.

**Analysis**: The heliotrope is the emblem of the entire chapter. Its turning toward the sun is at once action (its own movement) and passion (being drawn by the sun). This coincidence of action and passion is the structure of sympathy — and the structure of divine-human relation. The prayer of the heliotrope is the prayer of all being.

**Cross-references**: This establishes the sympathy theme that runs through Essay 3 (devotio sympathetica — love as participation) and Essay 6 (prayer as the heliotropic turning of the soul toward God).

## Passage 2.2 — The "Pathetic God"

**Source**: Part One, Ch. I, §2 — "The 'Pathetic God'"

**Context**: Corbin introduces the controversial notion of a God who is not emotionally inert but who experiences pathos — divine passion, longing, and compassion.

**Full passage**:

"We must here introduce a term which will play an essential part in our exposition: the term 'theopathy.' Literally it means a state of being affected by God, or a state of being affected in God, but it also means a state in which God Himself is affected by the passion of His creature. Theopathy is the pathos of God and the pathos of man experienced in a single act of sympathy. It is not that man projects his own emotions onto God; it is that God Himself has chosen to be affected by the longing of His creature. The 'pathetic God' is not an anthropomorphic projection; He is the God who, in the depths of His own being, experiences the desire to be known, the longing for manifestation, the compassion that brings worlds into existence."

**Original terms**: *theopathy* — the pathos of God, the divine capacity for passion; *al-ḥaqq al-makhlūq fī l-i'tiqād* — the truth created in beliefs, God as He appears in the forms of faith.

**Analysis**: This is the central theological claim of Chapter I. The God of Ibn 'Arabi is not the unmoved mover of Aristotelian philosophy. He is the God who longs, who desires, who is affected by His creatures. This does not mean God is emotionally needy in a human sense — it means the divine nature includes the pressure of hidden being toward manifestation and relation.

**Cross-references**: The theopathic God is the source of the compassion that structures creation (Essay 4), the beauty that appears as Sophia (Essay 3), the prayer that becomes reciprocal (Essay 6), and the form that can be seen without being fixed (Essay 7).

## Passage 2.3 — The Etymology of Divinity: The Name of God as Sigh

**Source**: Part One, Ch. I, §2

**Context**: Corbin reveals that the very name of God in Arabic can be read as deriving from a root meaning "to sigh, to desire, to feel compassion" — the grammar of the divine name enacts the theology of the pathetic God.

**Full passage**:

"Considering the word ulūhīya (the Godhead) as an ideogram which, by introducing a trifling orthographic sign (a tashdīd, which doubles the n) we can read as al-hānnīya. We then have an abstract noun denoting state, mode of being, formed from the verbal noun of the root h-n-n meaning to desire, to sigh, to feel compassion. Thus the true name of the Divinity, the name which expresses His hidden depths, is not the Infinite and All-Powerful of our rational theodicies. Nothing can better bear witness to the feeling for a 'pathetic God,' which is no less authentic than that disclosed by a phenomenology of prophetic religion."

**Original terms**: *ulūhīya* — the Godhead; *al-hānnīya* — the sighing, the desiring, the compassionate mode of being (from h-n-n, to sigh, to desire).

**Analysis**: This is not speculative etymology — it is the grammatical enactment of the central thesis. The very name of God, properly read, means "the Sighing One," "the One who desires and feels compassion." The rational theodicies that present God as Infinite and All-Powerful have missed the deeper name.

**Cross-references**: The divine name as sigh connects to Essay 4 (the Sigh of Compassion, Nafas al-Raḥmān, as the creative act) and Essay 6 (prayer as the soul's response to the divine sigh).

## Passage 2.4 — The Hidden Treasure

**Source**: Part One, Ch. I

**Context**: The famous "hidden treasure" hadith — the divine solitude that seeks disclosure.

**Full passage**:

"The famous saying, often invoked by Ibn 'Arabi: 'I was a hidden treasure and I loved to be known, therefore I created the world, that I might be known.' This saying is the seed of the entire metaphysics of theophany. Note that it does not say: 'I was a hidden treasure and I desired to be known' — though the word 'love' implies desire. It says 'I loved to be known.' Love is the motive. Creation is not the product of arbitrary will but of love — the love of the hidden for disclosure, the love of the alone for companionship, the love of the treasure for the one who will find it."

**Original terms**: *kanz makhfī* — hidden treasure; *aḥbabtu an u'raf* — I loved to be known.

**Analysis**: The hidden treasure saying is the metaphysical seed of the entire book. It locates the motive of creation not in power but in love, not in command but in longing, not in production but in disclosure. The world exists because God loves to be known.

**Cross-references**: This saying is the foundation for Essay 4 (creation as theophany rather than fabrication), Essay 5 (the imagination as the organ that perceives this disclosure), and Essay 6 (prayer as the soul's participation in the divine desire to be known).

## Passage 2.4 — Compassion as Ontological Mercy

**Source**: Part One, Ch. I

**Context**: Corbin explains raḥma (compassion/mercy) not as a moral attribute but as the very structure of existence.

**Full passage**:

"Raḥma, usually translated as mercy or compassion, has in the Sufi vocabulary an ontological meaning. It is not merely a moral quality that God exercises toward His creatures; it is the very act by which beings are given existence. The root r-ḥ-m, from which raḥma derives, is related to the word for womb (raḥim). Mercy is the generative matrix of being. To exist is already to be touched by mercy. The world does not arise from a neutral act of fabrication but from the compassionate expansion of the divine toward that which is not yet — the mercy that wills the other into being."

**Original terms**: *raḥma* — mercy, compassion (ontological, not merely moral); *raḥim* — womb, the generative source.

**Analysis**: Mercy is not something God adds after creating beings. It is the very movement by which beings are given existence. To be is to be-mercied. This transforms the understanding of compassion from a moral sentiment to the structure of reality.

**Cross-references**: The ontological mercy connects to Essay 6 (prayer as the response of the creature to the mercy that gave it being) and Essay 7 (the divine form appears in mercy, not in overwhelming power).

## Passage 2.5 — Unio Mystica as Unio Sympathetica

**Source**: Part One, Ch. I, §3

**Context**: Corbin redefines the goal of mysticism not as absorption into God but as sympathetic union — the coincidence of love and knowledge.

**Full passage**:

"The unio mystica with which we are concerned is not a fusion or absorption in which the individual disappears. It is a unio sympathetica — a union in which the lover and the Beloved remain distinct while their essences are intertwined in sympathy. The lover does not cease to be the lover; the Beloved does not cease to be the Beloved. But their life is one life, their breath one breath, their desire one desire — not because one has swallowed the other but because each is the condition of the other's being. This is the true meaning of the 'alone with the Alone' — not a solitude of two isolated beings but a solitude shared, a presence that is mutual and complete."

**Original terms**: *unio sympathetica* — sympathetic union, union through co-passion; *tawḥīd* — the oneness of being, but here experienced as the oneness of lover and Beloved in the act of love; *unus ambo* — one from two, the two that have become one without ceasing to be two.

Corbin gives the structure of this union the geometry of the ellipse:

"This is not the movement of a dialectical pendulum, oscillating between two terms. It is rather a movement describing the area of His Compassion in an ellipse, one focus of which is the being of God for and through me, while the other is my being for and through Him, in other words, the area enclosing the two of us, the area in which He is for me in proportion to my capacity for Him and in which my knowledge of Him is His knowledge of me."

The union is mutual and reciprocal — each focus constitutes the other:

"This perfume He exhales is the Breath of His Compassion which emancipates beings enclosed in their unburgeoned virtuality; it is this perfume that all breathe and that is the nourishment of their being. But because in their secret being they are this Compassion itself, the Compassion does not move only in the direction from the Creator to the creature whom He feeds with his existentiating Breath; it also moves from the creature toward the Creator, so that the created universe is the theophany of His Names and attributes, which would not exist if the creature did not exist. The same idea is formulated in different ways: 'If He has given us life and existence by His being, I also give Him life by knowing Him in my heart.'"

**Analysis**: This is the antipode to theologies of absorption. The goal is not to disappear into God but to enter into a relation of mutual sympathy in which the soul and God coinhere without confusion. This preserves the ontological distinction while achieving the most intimate possible union.

**Cross-references**: This is the goal toward which all the other essays move. Essay 3 (Sophia as the figure of this sympathetic union), Essay 5 (the heart as the organ of this union), Essay 6 (prayer as the act of this union), and Essay 7 (vision as the consummation of this union).
