## Passage 4.1 — The Part Two Prologue: Imagination as Magical Intermediary

**Source**: Part Two, Prologue (opening)

**Context**: Corbin opens Part Two with a quote identifying Imagination as the "magical intermediary between thought and being" — the key to understanding creation as theophany.

**Full passage**:

"'The notion of the imagination, magical intermediary between thought and being, incarnation of thought in image and presence of the image in being, is a conception of the utmost importance, which plays a leading role in the philosophy of the Renaissance and which we meet with again in the philosophy of Romanticism.' This observation, taken from one of our foremost interpreters of the doctrines of Boehme and Paracelsus, provides the best possible introduction to the second part of the present book. We wish to stress on the one hand the notion of the Imagination as the magical production of an image, the very type and model of magical action, or of all action as such, but especially of creative action; and, on the other hand, the notion of the image as a body (a magical body, a mental body), in which are incarnated the thought and will of the soul. The Imagination as a creative magical potency which, giving birth to the sensible world, produces the Spirit in forms and colors; the world as Magia divina 'imagined' by the Godhead — that is the ancient doctrine."

**Original terms**: *Magia divina* — divine magic, the creative power by which God imagines the world into being; *Imaginatio* — the active, creative Imagination (distinguished from fantasy).

**Analysis**: The Prologue establishes the pedigree of the creative Imagination tradition — from the Renaissance through Paracelsus and Boehme to Romanticism. The Imagination is not a human fantasy but a cosmic power: the "magical intermediary between thought and being" that gives form to thought and being to form.

**Cross-references**: This anchors the entire Part Two project and connects directly to Essay 5 (the human heart as the organ that participates in this creative Imagination).

## Passage 4.2 — Part Two Prologue: The Degradation of Imagination

**Source**: Part Two, Prologue

**Context**: Corbin opens Part Two by diagnosing the modern loss of the intermediate world — which makes Ibn 'Arabi's cosmology unintelligible.

**Full passage**:

"There has ceased to be an intermediate level between empirically verifiable reality and unreality pure and simple. All indemonstrable, invisible, inaudible things are classified as creations of the Imagination, that is, of the faculty whose function it is to secrete the imaginary, the unreal. In this context of agnosticism the Godhead and all forms of divinity are said to be creations of the imagination, hence unreal. What can prayer to such a Godhead be but a despairing delusion? I believe that we can measure at a glance the enormity of the gulf between this purely negative notion of the Imagination and the notion of which we shall be speaking if, anticipating our analyses of the ensuing texts, we answer as though taking up the challenge: well, precisely because this Godhead is a Godhead, it is real and exists, and that is why the Prayer addressed to it has meaning."

**Original terms**: *khayāl* — imagination (in the positive, theophanic sense, not fantasy); *mundus imaginalis* — the intermediate ontological world that modern thought has lost.

**Analysis**: The Prologue diagnoses the modern condition: the loss of the imaginal world and the reduction of imagination to fantasy. This is why Ibn 'Arabi's world is so difficult for modern readers — they lack the ontological category that makes it intelligible.

**Cross-references**: This recovers the threefold world from Essay 1 and shows why it matters for understanding creation, prayer, and vision. Essays 5, 6, and 7 all presuppose this recovered imaginal.

## Passage 4.2 — God Imagines the Universe Into Being

**Source**: Part Two, Prologue

**Context**: Corbin introduces the central thesis of Part Two: God Himself possesses Imagination and creates through it.

**Full passage**:

"On both sides we encounter the idea that the Godhead possesses the power of Imagination, and that by imagining the universe God created it; that He drew this universe from within Himself, from the eternal virtualities and potencies of His own being; that there exists between the universe of pure spirit and the sensible world an intermediate world which is the world of 'Idea-Images' as the Sufis put it, the world of 'supersensory sensibility,' of the subtile magical body, 'the world in which spirits are materialized and bodies spiritualized'; that this is the world over which the Imagination holds sway."

**Original terms**: *takhayyul* — the act of imagining, which in God is creative; *'ālam al-mithāl* — the world of similitudes, the intermediate imaginal world.

**Analysis**: The radical claim: creation is God's Imagination. The universe is not a product of arbitrary will or mechanical emanation. It is the divine imagining reality into being. The imagination is not a human faculty for fantasy — it is the cosmic power by which the hidden becomes manifest.

**Cross-references**: This is the foundation for Essay 5 (human imagination participates in the divine imagination), Essay 6 (prayer as co-imagining with God), and Essay 7 (theophanic vision as perception of divine imagination).

## Passage 4.3 — The Sigh of Compassion

**Source**: Part Two, Ch. I — "The God from Whom All Being Is Created"

**Context**: Corbin develops the doctrine of creation as theophany through the image of the Divine Sigh — Nafas al-Raḥmān.

**Full passage**:

"Sadness is not the 'privilege' of the creature; it is in the Creator Himself, it is indeed the motif which, anticipating all our deductions, makes the primordial Being a creative Being; it is the secret of His creativity. And His creation springs, not from nothingness, from something other than Himself, from a not-Him, but from His fundamental being, from the potencies and virtualities latent in His own unrevealed being. Accordingly, the word tanaffus also connotes 'to shine,' 'to appear' after the manner of the dawn. The Creation is essentially the revelation of the Divine Being, first to Himself, a luminescence occurring within Him; it is a theophany. Here there is no notion of a creatio ex nihilo opening up a gulf which no rational thought will ever be able to bridge."

"The Divine Breathing exhales what our shaykh designates as Nafas al-Raḥmān or Nafas Raḥmānī, the Sigh of existentiating Compassion; this Sigh gives rise to the entire 'subtile' mass of a primordial existentiation termed 'Cloud' ('amā'). Which explains the following hadith: 'Someone asked the Prophet: Where was your Lord before creating His Creation? — He was in a Cloud; there was no space either above or below.'"

**Original terms**: *Nafas al-Raḥmān* — the Sigh of the Compassionate, the divine exhalation that brings creation into being; *'amā'* — the Cloud, the primordial subtile substance from which all forms emerge; *tajallī* — theophany, divine self-disclosure; *tanaffus* — breathing, sighing, which also connotes the dawning of light.

**Analysis**: This is the cosmological counterpart of Essay 2's divine longing. Creation is not an act of fabrication but an act of compassion — the compassionate sigh of the divine that exhales the universe from its own being. The Cloud is the intermediate substance in which all forms are latent, the imaginal matrix from which all things emerge.

**Cross-references**: This is the cosmological foundation for the entire companion. The Sigh of Compassion is the divine longing from Essay 2 made creative. The Cloud is the imaginal world from Essay 1. Theophanic creation makes possible the heart's perception (Essay 5), prayer (Essay 6), and vision (Essay 7).

## Passage 4.4 — The God Manifested by Imagination

**Source**: Part Two, Ch. II — "The God Manifested by Imagination"

**Context**: Corbin develops the reciprocal structure of theophany: God manifests through imagination and is received by imagination.

**Full passage**:

"The God who is manifested by Imagination is not a God created by human fantasy. He is the God who chooses to manifest Himself through the forms that the Imagination perceives. The Imagination does not invent God; it receives Him in the mode appropriate to its own nature — which is the mode of form, image, symbol, and encounter. The God manifested by Imagination is the God who discloses Himself in the 'ālam al-mithāl, the world where spirits take body and bodies become spiritual. This is the God who can be seen — not with the eyes of the body, not with the intellect alone, but with the imaginative faculty that is the organ of theophanic perception."

**Original terms**: *al-ḥaqq al-makhlūq fī l-khayāl* — the truth created in imagination, God as He appears in the imaginal world; *tajallī* — theophany, the act of divine self-disclosure.

**Analysis**: This is the central theological claim of Part Two. There is a mode of divine presence that is neither purely spiritual (inaccessible to form) nor purely material (confined to the sensible). It is the imaginal mode — God appearing in and through the forms of the intermediate world. This is the God who is seen in vision, encountered in prayer, and contemplated in beauty.

**Cross-references**: Bridges Essay 2 (divine longing for manifestation) to Essay 5 (the heart as the organ that receives this manifestation) and Essay 7 (vision of God in form).

## Passage 4.5 — The Recurrence of Creation

**Source**: Part Two, Ch. III — §4, "The Recurrence of Creation"

**Context**: Corbin explains Ibn 'Arabi's doctrine of the perpetual renewal of creation at each instant.

**Full passage**:

"Creation is not a past event; it is a perpetual act. At every instant the universe is re-created by the divine breath. There is no 'nature' that persists on its own between acts of creation; there is only the continuous theophanic act by which the world is held in being. The Sufis express this by saying that at each moment the creature dies and is reborn. What seems to be the persistence of things over time is actually the rapid succession of theophanic recreations. The world does not endure; it is resumed."

**Original terms**: *tajdīd al-khalq* — the renewal of creation, the perpetual recreation of the world at each instant; *al-khalq al-jadīd* — the new creation, the constant renewal of being.

**Analysis**: This doctrine destroys the ordinary notion of a stable, self-subsistent world. There is no substrate of matter that persists through time; there is only the continuous act of divine self-disclosure. The world is not an object that exists once and then endures; it is an event that must be re-enacted at every moment.

**Cross-references**: This doctrine is essential for Essay 6 (prayer enters into the moment of recreation) and Essay 7 (vision is possible because creation is not fixed but continuously renewed).

## Passage 4.6 — The Twofold Dimension of Beings

**Source**: Part Two, Ch. III — "The Twofold Dimension of Beings"

**Context**: Corbin explains that every being has both a fixed essence and a continuously renewed form — which is why theophanic perception is possible.

**Full passage**:

"Every being has two dimensions: one fixed, which is its eternal essence in the divine knowledge; one mobile, which is its continuously renewed form in creation. The first is its unchanging archetype; the second is its theophanic manifestation at each instant. The soul, too, has this twofold dimension: it has an eternal aspect that is never entirely caught in generation, and a temporal aspect that experiences the succession of forms. The spiritual life consists in awakening to the eternal dimension — not by escaping the temporal but by perceiving the eternal through the temporal."

**Original terms**: *'ayn thābita* — fixed essence, the eternal archetype of each being in divine knowledge; *tajallī* — theophany, the continuous self-disclosure of the divine in form.

**Analysis**: This twofold dimension explains both the stability and the fluidity of beings. Each thing has a fixed identity (its archetype in divine knowledge) but its manifestation is continuously renewed. The spiritual path is the awakening to the eternal dimension within the temporal.

**Cross-references**: This prepares Essay 5 (the heart perceives the eternal archetype through the temporal form), Essay 6 (prayer addresses the eternal dimension), and Essay 7 (vision perceives the eternal form).

## Passage 4.7 — The "God Created in the Faiths"

**Source**: Part Two, Ch. III — "The 'God Created in the Faiths'"

**Context**: Corbin discusses the controversial idea that God appears differently in different faith traditions — not because God changes but because the capacity of the receiver determines the form.

**Full passage**:

"The God who appears in the various faiths is not a God who changes. He is the same God, but He appears according to the capacity of the heart that receives Him. This is the meaning of Ibn 'Arabi's famous saying: 'The God created in the faiths.' It does not mean that the faithful create their own God by projecting their beliefs. It means that the divine self-disclosure takes form according to the receptacle that receives it — and the receptacle is the heart of the believer, shaped by the tradition in which it has been formed. All the forms are real; none is exhaustive. The error is not in possessing a form of God but in mistaking that form for God as He is in Himself, beyond all form."

**Original terms**: *al-ḥaqq al-makhlūq fī l-i'tiqād* — the truth created in beliefs, God as He appears in the forms of faith; *tajallī* — theophany, which varies according to the capacity of the receiver.

**Analysis**: This is one of the most important and misunderstood ideas in the book. It does not mean that all religions are equally valid in a relativistic sense. It means that divine self-disclosure is always received according to the capacity of the receiver — and that capacity is formed by the spiritual tradition in which the receiver stands. Each form is a real theophany; none is the final form.

**Cross-references**: This is the theological foundation for Essay 3 (Nizām as theophany within one tradition), Essay 7 (the tension between form and idolatry), and the entire project of esoteric hermeneutics from Essay 1.
