# Creation Is Not an Object — The Universe as Theophany

Part Two of *Alone with the Alone* opens with a diagnosis and a challenge. The diagnosis: the
modern world has lost the intermediate ontological region — the imaginal — and with it the
capacity to understand theophany, symbol, and prayer. The challenge: Corbin proposes to recover
it through the doctrine that creation is theophany — the divine imagining reality into being.
The Part Two Prologue sets the stage; Chapters I-III develop the logic: the Sigh of Compassion,
the God Manifested by Imagination, the Recurrence of Creation, and the God Created in the
Faiths.

The diagnosis is stark. Something has been amputated from reality, and we have grown so
accustomed to the missing limb that we no longer feel the ghost of it:

"There has ceased to be an intermediate level between empirically verifiable reality and
unreality pure and simple. All indemonstrable, invisible, inaudible things are classified as
creations of the Imagination, that is, of the faculty whose function it is to secrete the
imaginary, the unreal. In this context of agnosticism the Godhead and all forms of divinity are
said to be creations of the imagination, hence unreal. What can prayer to such a Godhead be but
a despairing delusion? I believe that we can measure at a glance the enormity of the gulf
between this purely negative notion of the Imagination and the notion of which we shall be
speaking if, anticipating our analyses of the ensuing texts, we answer as though taking up the
challenge: well, precisely because this Godhead is a Godhead, it is real and exists, and that
is why the Prayer addressed to it has meaning."

Corbin reaches back past the Enlightenment, past the Reformation, to a tradition that knew what
the imagination was before it was demoted: a magical intermediary, the power by which thought
becomes image and image becomes being:

"'The notion of the imagination, magical intermediary between thought and being, incarnation of
thought in image and presence of the image in being, is a conception of the utmost importance,
which plays a leading role in the philosophy of the Renaissance and which we meet with again in
the philosophy of Romanticism.' ... We wish to stress on the one hand the notion of the
Imagination as the magical production of an image, the very type and model of magical action,
or of all action as such, but especially of creative action; and, on the other hand, the notion
of the image as a body (a magical body, a mental body), in which are incarnated the thought and
will of the soul. The Imagination as a creative magical potency which, giving birth to the
sensible world, produces the Spirit in forms and colors; the world as Magia divina 'imagined'
by the Godhead — that is the ancient doctrine."

## Prologue: The Diagnostic

If you have followed the argument this far, you have already been prepared for the shock. The
creation of the world is not a manufacturing job. It is an act of imagination — and not a human
one:

"On both sides we encounter the idea that the Godhead possesses the power of Imagination, and
that by imagining the universe God created it; that He drew this universe from within Himself,
from the eternal virtualities and potencies of His own being; that there exists between the
universe of pure spirit and the sensible world an intermediate world which is the world of
'Idea-Images' as the Sufis put it, the world of 'supersensory sensibility,' of the subtile
magical body, 'the world in which spirits are materialized and bodies spiritualized'; that this
is the world over which the Imagination holds sway."

*Takhayyul. The act of imagining, which in God is creation. 'Ālam al-mithāl. The world of similitudes.*

## Ch.I: The Sigh of Compassion

Creation is not fabrication. It is theophany — the divine breathing the universe into being
from His own compassionate substance. Corbin traces the motive back to the hidden treasure:

"To begin with: a Divine Being alone in His unconditioned essence, of which we know only one
thing: precisely the sadness of the primordial solitude that makes Him yearn to be revealed in
beings who manifest Him to Himself insofar as He manifests Himself to them. That is the
Revelation we apprehend. We must meditate upon it in order to know who we are. The leitmotiv is
not the bursting into being of an autarchic Omnipotence, but a fundamental sadness: 'I was a
hidden Treasure, I yearned to be known. That is why I produced creatures, in order to be known
in them.' This phase is represented as the sadness of the divine Names suffering anguish in
nonknowledge because no one names them, and it is this sadness that descended in the divine
Breath (tanaffus) which is Compassion (Raḥma) and existentiation (ījād)."

The divine Breath — the Sigh of Compassion — gives rise to the Cloud, the primordial imaginal
substance that is both Creator and Creature:

"This Cloud, which the Divine Being exhaled and in which He originally was, receives all forms
and at the same time gives beings their forms; it is active and passive, receptive and
existentiating (muḥaqqiq); through it is effected the differentiation within the primordial
reality of being. As such, it is the absolute unconditioned Imagination (khayāl muṭlaq).
Primordial Cloud, absolute or theophanic Imagination, existentiating Compassion are equivalent
notions, expressing the same original reality: the Divine Being from whom all things are
created — which amounts to saying the 'Creator-Creature.' For the Cloud is the Creator, since
it is the Sigh He exhales and since it is hidden in Him; as such the Cloud is the invisible,
the 'esoteric' (bāṭin). And it is the manifested creature (ẓāhir). Creator-Creature
(khāliq-makhlūq): this means that the Divine Being is the Hidden and the Revealed, or also that
He is the First (al-Awwal) and the Last (al-Ākhir)."

*Khayāl muṭlaq. Absolute Imagination. Khāliq-makhlūq. Creator-Creature. Bāṭin and ẓāhir. The same reality, seen from two sides.*

"To the initial act of the Creator imagining the world corresponds the creature imagining his
world, imagining the worlds, his God, his symbols. Or rather, these are the phases, the
recurrences of one and the same eternal process: Imagination effected in an Imagination
(takhayyul fī takhayyul), an Imagination which is recurrent just as — and because — the
Creation itself is recurrent. The same theophanic Imagination of the Creator who has revealed
the worlds, renews the Creation from moment to moment in the human being whom He has revealed
as His perfect image and who, in the mirror that this Image is, shows himself Him whose image
he is. That is why man's Active Imagination cannot be a vain fiction, since it is this same
theophanic Imagination which, in and by the human being, continues to reveal what it showed
itself by first imagining it."

"To perceive all forms as epiphanic forms (maẓāhir), that is, to perceive through the figures
which they manifest and which are the eternal hexeities, that they are other than the Creator
and nevertheless that they are He, is precisely to effect the encounter, the coincidence,
between God's descent toward the creature and the creature's ascent toward the Creator. The
'place' of this encounter is not outside the Creator-Creature totality, but is the area within
it which corresponds specifically to the Active Imagination, in the manner of a bridge joining
the two banks of a river. The crossing itself is essentially a hermeneutics of symbols (ta'wīl,
ta'bīr)."

You cannot think your way into this. You can only taste it — dhawq, the word Sufis use for
knowing by direct experience. It is the difference between reading a recipe and eating the
meal. The Active Imagination is the mouth.

Creation is not a past event but a perpetual act. At every instant the universe is re-created:

"Sadness is not the 'privilege' of the creature; it is in the Creator Himself, it is indeed the
motif which, anticipating all our deductions, makes the primordial Being a creative Being; it
is the secret of His creativity. And His creation springs, not from nothingness, from something
other than Himself, from a not-Him, but from His fundamental being, from the potencies and
virtualities latent in His own unrevealed being. Accordingly, the word tanaffus also connotes
'to shine,' 'to appear' after the manner of the dawn. The Creation is essentially the
revelation of the Divine Being, first to Himself, a luminescence occurring within Him; it is a
theophany. Here there is no notion of a creatio ex nihilo opening up a gulf which no rational
thought will ever be able to bridge."

"The Divine Breathing exhales what our shaykh designates as Nafas al-Raḥmān or Nafas Raḥmānī,
the Sigh of existentiating Compassion; this Sigh gives rise to the entire 'subtile' mass of a
primordial existentiation termed 'Cloud' ('amā'). Which explains the following hadith: 'Someone
asked the Prophet: Where was your Lord before creating His Creation? — He was in a Cloud; there
was no space either above or below.'"

*Nafas al-Raḥmān. The Sigh of the Compassionate. 'Amā'. The Cloud. The primordial imaginal matrix.*

The Cloud is not a thing God made. It is the thing God is doing. It is the Sigh still
vibrating, the Breath still moving, the compassionate exhalation that has not yet reached its
end — and will not, because there is no end. You are breathing it now.

## Ch.II: The God Manifested by Imagination

The God who appears through this creative Imagination is not a God created by human fantasy. He
is the God who chooses to manifest in the mode appropriate to the imaginal world:

"The God who is manifested by Imagination is not a God created by human fantasy. He is the God
who chooses to manifest Himself through the forms that the Imagination perceives. The
Imagination does not invent God; it receives Him in the mode appropriate to its own nature —
which is the mode of form, image, symbol, and encounter. The God manifested by Imagination is
the God who discloses Himself in the 'ālam al-mithāl, the world where spirits take body and
bodies become spiritual. This is the God who can be seen — not with the eyes of the body, not
with the intellect alone, but with the imaginative faculty that is the organ of theophanic
perception."

*Al-ḥaqq al-makhlūq fī l-khayāl. The truth created in imagination. God as He appears in the imaginal mode.*

## Ch.III: Recurrence and the God Created in the Faiths

"Creation is not a past event; it is a perpetual act. At every instant the universe is
re-created by the divine breath. There is no 'nature' that persists on its own between acts of
creation; there is only the continuous theophanic act by which the world is held in being. The
Sufis express this by saying that at each moment the creature dies and is reborn. What seems to
be the persistence of things over time is actually the rapid succession of theophanic
recreations. The world does not endure; it is resumed."

*Tajdīd al-khalq. The renewal of creation. Al-khalq al-jadīd. The new creation at every instant.*

This perpetual creation means every being has two dimensions — one fixed in divine knowledge,
one continuously renewed in manifestation:

"Every being has two dimensions: one fixed, which is its eternal essence in the divine
knowledge; one mobile, which is its continuously renewed form in creation. The first is its
unchanging archetype; the second is its theophanic manifestation at each instant. The soul,
too, has this twofold dimension: it has an eternal aspect that is never entirely caught in
generation, and a temporal aspect that experiences the succession of forms. The spiritual life
consists in awakening to the eternal dimension — not by escaping the temporal but by perceiving
the eternal through the temporal."

*'Ayn thābita. Fixed essence. The eternal archetype in divine knowledge.*

Corbin develops the doctrine in full — the new creation at every instant:

"This is one of the key terms in the theosophical system of Ibn 'Arabī; the idea of recurrent
creation, new creation (khalq jadīd) calls the very nature of creation into question. We have
already seen that there is no place in Ibn 'Arabī's thinking for a creatio ex nihilo, an
absolute beginning preceded by nothing. The existentiation of a thing which had no existence
before, a creative operation which took place once and for all and is now complete — is for him
a theoretical and practical absurdity. Creation as the 'rule of being' is the pre-eternal and
continuous movement by which being is manifested at every instant in a new cloak."

"Nevertheless, we never cease to see what we are seeing; we do not notice that there is
existentiation and passing away at every moment, because when something passes away, something
like it is existentiated at the same moment. We look upon existence, our own for example, as
continuous, past-present-future, and yet at every moment the world puts on a 'new creation,'
which veils our consciousness because we do not perceive the incessant renewal. At every breath
of the 'Sigh of Divine Compassion' (Nafas al-Raḥmān) being ceases and then is; we cease to be,
then come into being. In reality there is no 'then,' for there is no interval."

*Khalq jadīd. New creation. No interval between ceasing and being. The world is continuously resumed.*

---

Does God wear a different face in every tradition? The question sounds like
relativism. Ibn 'Arabī's answer is stranger — it is the doctrine of the receptacles:

"The God who appears in the various faiths is not a God who changes. He is the same God, but He
appears according to the capacity of the heart that receives Him. This is the meaning of Ibn
'Arabī's famous saying: 'The God created in the faiths.' It does not mean that the faithful
create their own God by projecting their beliefs. It means that the divine self-disclosure
takes form according to the receptacle that receives it — and the receptacle is the heart of
the believer, shaped by the tradition in which it has been formed. All the forms are real; none
is exhaustive. The error is not in possessing a form of God but in mistaking that form for God
as He is in Himself, beyond all form."

*Al-ḥaqq al-makhlūq fī l-i'tiqād. The truth created in beliefs. Theophany according to capacity.*

The world is not an object fabricated by a distant maker and set adrift. It is the continuous
theophany of the divine Names, the compassionate sigh made visible, the Cloud ever-taking-form.
Creation is disclosure. The universe is not something God made; it is something God is doing —
and doing at every instant, in every form, through every heart that receives Him.

But if the world is theophany — divine imagination made visible — then what faculty perceives
it? The Prologue warned that the Imagination has been degraded into fantasy. The next chapter
answers: the heart. The heart is the organ of the imaginal world, the subtle faculty that
perceives the theophanic depth of what is. And it is not a passive receiver — it is creative.
