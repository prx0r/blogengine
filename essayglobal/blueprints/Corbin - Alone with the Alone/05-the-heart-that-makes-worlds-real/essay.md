# The Heart That Makes Worlds Real — Imagination, Mirrors, and the Subtile Organ

This is the center of the book — Chapter IV of Part Two, the longest and
densest chapter in *Alone with the Alone*. Everything that came before leads
here; everything that follows flows from it. The chapter moves through three
sections: the Field of the Imagination, the Heart as a Subtile Organ, and the
Science of the Heart. Each builds toward the same claim: the heart is not a
metaphor for emotion but the subtle organ of spiritual perception, the faculty
by which the soul perceives the imaginal world and participates in the divine
imagination.

Everything hinges on a single distinction: is the imagination a faculty of
fantasy that produces unreality, or is it the organ of a world? The answer
determines whether the universe is a theophany or a void.

## §1. The Field of the Imagination

"Ibn 'Arabī distinguishes an imagination conjoined to the imagining subject
and inseparable from him (khayāl muttaṣil) and a self-subsisting imagination
dissociable from the subject (khayāl munfaṣil). In the first case we must
distinguish between the imaginations that are premeditated or provoked by a
conscious process of the mind, and those which present themselves to the mind
spontaneously like dreams. The Imagination separable from the subject, on the
other hand, has an autonomous and subsisting reality sui generis on the plane
of the intermediary world, the world of Idea-Images."

*Khayāl muttaṣil. Khayāl munfaṣil. Conjoined and separable. One lives and
dies with you; the other subsists in the world of Idea-Images, whether you
are there to perceive it or not.*

Pause here. The imagination is not in your head. It is in the world — not
your private fantasy but a real region of being. When the Angel Gabriel
appeared to the Prophet in the form of a beautiful youth, the Companions
saw only a handsome young man. The image itself was real, subsisting in the
imaginal world; the Prophet's faculty simply perceived what was already there.
This is not psychology. It is cosmology.

"Even the Imagination conjoined to, and inseparable from, the subject is in
no sense a faculty functioning arbitrarily in the void, secreting 'fantasies.'
When the form of the Angel, for example, 'projects itself' into a human form,
this act takes place on the plane of the autonomous Imagination (munfaṣil),
which then raises the Image to the plane of the conjoint Imagination. Thus
there is only one autonomous Imagination, because it is absolute Imagination
(Khayāl muṭlaq), that is to say, the Primordial Cloud which constitutes the
universe as theophany."

This solves a problem that has haunted Western philosophy since Plato: if
there is a world of eternal Forms (intelligible) and a world of decaying
matter (sensible), where do images belong? They are not purely intelligible
(they have shape, color, form) and they are not purely material (they cannot
be touched, weighed, or measured). Ibn 'Arabī gives them their own ontological
region — the barzakh, the isthmus between two worlds. And that region has its
own faculty: the active Imagination.

"There are five of these Presences, namely, the five Descents (tanazzulāt);
these are determinations or conditions of the divine Ipseity in the forms of
His Names. The first Ḥaḍra is the theophany of the Essence in the eternal
latent hexeities. The second and the third Ḥaḍarāt are respectively the
angelic world of Spirits and the world of Souls. The fourth Ḥaḍra is the
world of Idea-Images (ʿālam al-mithāl), typical Forms in the 'subtile matter'
state. The fifth Ḥaḍra is the sensible and visible world (ʿālam al-shahāda),
of dense material bodies."

*Ḥaḍarāt. Presences. Five levels of being, each one the mirror and marriage
of the level above it.*

Think of it as a staircase with five landings. At the top, the divine Essence
beyond all form. One step down, the world of Spirits — pure intelligence.
Another step, the world of Souls. Another, the world of Idea-Images — where
the Angel has a form though not a body. And finally, the material world you
are touching right now. Each step down is a "marriage" that produces the next
level. Nothing is unrelated. Everything is connected by desire, by the prayer
of each level for the one above.

"Consequently each of these Ḥaḍarāt or Descents is also designated as a
'marriage' (nikāḥ), whose fruit is the Presence which follows it. For this
reason each lower Presence is the image and correspondence (mithāl), the
reflection and mirror of the next higher. Thus everything that exists in the
sensible world is a reflection, a typification, of what exists in the world of
Spirits, and so on."

This is the architecture of the soul's universe. Every level reflects the one
above it. Every lower Presence is the mirror of the next higher. The whole
ladder is held together by desire — the heliotrope's turning toward the sun,
the lover's longing for the Beloved, each level's prayer for the one above.

"As we have seen, the Primordial Cloud, the divine existentiating Sigh of
Compassion, is the intermediary between the Divine Essentia abscondita and
the manifest world of multiple forms; similarly the world of Idea-Images, the
world of apparitional forms and of bodies in the subtile state ('ālam
al-mithāl) to which our imaginative faculty specifically relates, is the
intermediary between the world of pure spiritual realities, the world of
Mystery, and the visible, sensible world. Dream is intermediary between the
real and waking consciousness. The Prophet's vision of the Angel Gabriel in
the form of Dāḥya al-Kalbī, an Arab youth known for his beauty, the images
seen in mirrors — these are intermediary realities. And because they are
intermediary, they culminate in the notion of the symbol, for the
intermediary 'symbolizes with' the worlds it mediates."

*Khayāl. Barzakh. 'Ālam al-mithāl. The Imagination is the faculty of the
between — neither spirit nor matter, but the bridge that makes them one.*

A symbol, in this framework, is not a metaphor. A metaphor says "this stands
for that." A symbol says "this is that, appearing in a mode that your ordinary
perception cannot grasp." The Burning Bush is not a metaphor for divine
presence; it is divine presence appearing in the mode of fire that does not
consume. The Angel Gabriel appearing as Dāḥya al-Kalbī is not a metaphor for
angelic encounter; it is an angelic encounter appearing in the mode of human
beauty. The symbol is the theophany itself, not a sign pointing to it.

"The science of the Imagination is theogony when it meditates on the Primordial
Cloud, the theophanies of the 'God from whom all being is created'; it is
again theogony when it meditates on the theophanies of the 'God created in
the faiths,' since these are still manifestations and occultations of the
divine Names. But here it is also cosmology, since it is knowledge of being
and of the universe as theophany. It is again cosmology when it thematizes
the intermediary world perceived by our imaginative faculty, the world in
which occur visions, apparitions, and in general all the symbolic histories
which reveal only their material aspect to perception or sensory
representation. It is the science of the theophanies that are dispensed
specifically to mystics, and of all the related thaumaturgies; it gives
existence to the Improbable, to what reason rejects, and above all to the fact
that the Necessary Being, whose pure Essence is incompatible with all form, is
nevertheless manifested in a form belonging to the 'Imaginative Presence.' It
has the specific power to cause the impossible to exist, and this power is put
into effect by Prayer."

*'Ilm al-khayāl. The science of the Imagination — theogony, cosmology, and
prayer all at once. The queen science of the theophanic universe.*

What Corbin is describing is not a subset of psychology or art criticism. It
is the most comprehensive science there is. It studies how God manifests
(theogony), how the world is structured (cosmology), and how the soul
participates in both (prayer). The modern division of knowledge into
theology, physics, and psychology would strike Ibn 'Arabī as a catastrophic
mistake — as if someone had cut a single organism into three pieces and
expected each piece to live.

"The science of the Imagination is also the science of mirrors, of all
mirroring 'surfaces' and of the forms that appear in them. As the science of
the speculum, it takes its place in speculative theosophy, in a theory of the
vision and manifestations of the spiritual, and draws the ultimate
consequences from the fact that though forms appear in mirrors, they are not
in the mirrors."

A mirror reflects your face, but your face is not inside the mirror. The
form is real — you see it, it has color and shape and movement — but it is
not located in the glass. This is exactly the mode of being of theophanic
forms. They are really present, but not materially contained. When the Angel
appears in human form, the form is present — the Prophet sees it, speaks to
it — but the Angel is not "inside" the form the way a man is inside his body.
The form is a mirror in which the Angel appears, not a container in which the
Angel is trapped.

## §2. The Heart as a Subtile Organ

"In Ibn 'Arabī as in Sufism in general, the heart (qalb), is the organ which
produces true knowledge, comprehensive intuition, the gnosis (ma'rifa) of God
and the divine mysteries, in short, the organ of everything connoted by the
term 'esoteric science' ('ilm al-Bāṭin). It is the organ of a perception which
is both experience and intimate taste (dhawq). Of course, and of this we are
reminded at every turn, this 'heart' is not the conical organ of flesh,
situated on the left side of the chest, although there is a certain connection,
the modality of which, however, is essentially unknown."

*Qalb. Ma'rifa. Dhawq. The heart is the organ of direct spiritual perception.
Its knowledge is not conceptual; it is tasted.*

When we say "I know this in my heart," we are closer to the Sufi meaning than
we realize — though we have sentimentalized what they meant with precision.
The heart does not emote; it tastes. Ma'rifa is not information about God; it
is the direct experience of divine reality, the way tasting sugar is different
from reading the chemical formula for sucrose. You cannot argue someone into
ma'rifa any more than you can argue someone into tasting sweetness.

"Here we might also speak of its 'theandric' function, since its supreme
vision is of the Form of God (ṣūrat al-Ḥaqq) — this because the gnostic's
heart is the 'eye,' the organ by which God knows Himself, reveals Himself to
Himself in the forms of His epiphanies. It is also true to say that the
gnostic, as Perfect Man, is the seat of God's divine consciousness and that
God is the seat and essence of the gnostic's consciousness. If it were
necessary to draw a diagram, the situation would be far better represented by
the two foci of an ellipse than by the center of a circle."

*Ṣūrat al-Ḥaqq. The Form of God. Insān kāmil. Perfect Man. The two foci of
the ellipse — distinct but inseparable, each constituting the other.*

This is the most vertiginous claim in the entire book. The heart is not just
an organ that perceives God; it is the organ in which God perceives Himself.
The gnostic's heart is God's eye. When the gnostic sees God, it is God seeing
Himself through the gnostic's vision. The ellipse image is essential here: two
foci, neither reducible to the other, but neither functioning without the
other. You are not dissolved into God; you become the focus through which God
contemplates His own beauty.

"The heart is called qalb because of its constant turning and transformation
(taqlīb). It is never in the same state twice. This is not a weakness but its
essential nature — the heart is the organ that corresponds to the continuous
renewal of creation. Just as creation is re-enacted at each instant, the heart
receives a new theophany at each instant. The heart that would fix itself in
one form would cease to be a heart. Its fluidity is its capacity for infinite
reception."

*Qalb. From q-l-b: to turn, to change. The heart's very name enacts its
function — constant turning, never fixed.*

The name says it all. The Arabic root q-l-b means to turn, to flip, to
transform. The heart is the organ of perpetual metamorphosis. If creation is
renewed at every instant — if the Sigh of Compassion breathes the world into
being anew with each breath — then the organ that perceives this creation must
itself be capable of continuous transformation. A heart that froze in one
state would be like an eye that could only see one color. The heart's
fluidity is not instability; it is the condition of seeing a God who is
always new.

"Himma is the power of the heart, the concentrated spiritual energy that gives
efficacy to the heart's vision. It is not mere wishing; it is the force that
actualizes in the imaginal world what the heart perceives. The gnostic whose
heart has been purified can, by himma, bring about real effects in the
intermediate world — not by commanding external reality but by giving such
concentrated form to his vision that it becomes substantiated in the 'ālam
al-mithāl. Himma is the heart's creative power, the point at which
contemplation becomes operation."

*Himma. Concentrated spiritual energy. The point at which seeing becomes
doing, contemplation becomes creation.*

This is not magic in the modern sense of manipulating the physical world
through willpower. Himma operates in the imaginal world, not the material
one. If you fix your himma on a vision of the Angel, the Angel becomes
substantiated in the imaginal realm — not because you created the Angel (the
Angel already exists) but because your concentration made the Angel's presence
perceptible in the mode of the imaginal. Himma is the difference between a
vague wish and a vision so concentrated that it has weight.

## §3. The Science of the Heart

"Ibn 'Arabī divides men into three classes: (a) the disciples of the science
of the heart, those who possess the psycho-spiritual organ which mystic
physiology designates as the 'heart' (aṣḥāb al-Qulūb); these are the mystics,
and more particularly the perfect among the Ṣūfīs; (b) the disciples of the
rational intellect (aṣḥāb al-ʿUqūl); these are the Mutakallimūn, the
scholastic theologians; (c) simple believers (mu'minūn). Under normal
circumstances a simple believer can develop into a mystic through spiritual
training; but between mystics and rational theologians there is an
unbridgeable gulf."

*Aṣḥāb al-Qulūb. Disciples of the heart. Aṣḥāb al-ʿUqūl. Disciples of the
intellect. Between them, a gulf that no argument can cross.*

This is not intellectual snobbery. It is a claim about different modes of
knowing. The rational theologian proves, refutes, and defines. His God is a
conclusion reached through argument. The disciple of the heart sees, tastes,
encounters. His God is a presence met through vision. These are not two ways
of reaching the same destination; they are two different destinations
altogether. The theologian's God is an "absent" — a being inferred from
evidence. The mystic's God is a presence encountered directly.

"The scholastic theologian formulates a dogma; he proves, he refutes, but he
is not an eye-witness (shāhid); argumentation and dialectic have no need of
vision and consequently cannot lead to it. The God of whom those who are not
eye-witnesses speak is an 'absent'; they have not seen each other. And for
this reason no dogmatist's God can help him against someone else's God; the
antagonists can neither defeat nor convince each other. In short, the science
of the heart transmutes dogma by disclosing its limit; the authoritative
statement which closed off the horizon because it said everything it had to
say and nothing more, is transmuted into a symbol which shows (maẓhar)
something else by summoning up other tajallī, other visions."

This is the most liberating — and the most threatening — claim in the science
of the heart. Dogma is a closed door. It says everything it has to say and
stops. A symbol is an open window. It shows you something and then invites
you to see beyond it. The science of the heart does not destroy dogma; it
transforms dogma into symbol. It takes the statement "God is merciful" and
shows you the mercy in the rain, in the mother's care for her child, in the
forgiveness you did not deserve. The statement closes; the symbol opens.

"The science of the heart culminates in the science of prayer. For what is
prayer but the heart's highest act — the concentration of all its powers in
the direction of the divine presence? The heart that has been purified and
turned toward God becomes the place of theophanic encounter. In prayer, the
heart does not merely speak to God; it enters into the reality that it
addresses. The prayer of the heart is the point at which the science of the
Imagination becomes operative — for prayer is the act by which the soul,
through the heart, participates in the divine imagination that creates and
sustains the world."

Everything leads to this. The science of the heart is not theoretical. It
exists for the sake of prayer — not petitionary prayer that asks for things,
but theophanic prayer that enters the presence it addresses. The heart that
has been purified, that has learned to turn (qalb), that has concentrated its
himma, becomes the place where the divine form appears. The imagination that
the modern world has degraded into fantasy is, in truth, the power by which
the hidden becomes manifest, by which the soul sees God, by which prayer
moves the real.

The science of the heart culminates in the science of prayer. The next chapter
takes up prayer directly — not as petition to a distant God but as the method
by which the soul, through the Imagination, enters into the presence of the
God who has chosen to appear in form. If the heart is the organ, prayer is the
act.
