## Passage 5.1 — The Field of the Imagination: Intermediary Function

**Source**: Part Two, Ch. IV, §1 — "The Field of the Imagination"

**Context**: Corbin defines the Imagination not as fantasy but as the faculty of the intermediate world — the organ that mediates between the sensible and the intelligible.

**Full passage**:

"As we have seen, the Primordial Cloud, the divine existentiating Sigh of Compassion, is the intermediary between the Divine Essentia abscondita and the manifest world of multiple forms; similarly the world of Idea-Images, the world of apparitional forms and of bodies in the subtile state ('ālam al-mithāl) to which our imaginative faculty specifically relates, is the intermediary between the world of pure spiritual realities, the world of Mystery, and the visible, sensible world. Dream is intermediary between the real and waking consciousness. The Prophet's vision of the Angel Gabriel in the form of Dāḥya al-Kalbī, an Arab youth known for his beauty, the images seen in mirrors — these are intermediary realities. And because they are intermediary, they culminate in the notion of the symbol, for the intermediary 'symbolizes with' the worlds it mediates."

**Original terms**: *'ālam al-mithāl* — the imaginal world, the intermediate world of idea-images; *khayāl* — imagination (the active, theophanic faculty, not fantasy); *barzakh* — isthmus, intermediary, that which both separates and connects two orders.

**Analysis**: The Imagination is not one faculty among others; it is the faculty of the barzakh — the intermediate region that connects and separates the sensible and the intelligible. This is why it is the organ of theophanic perception: it perceives the forms in which the spiritual takes body and the body becomes spiritual.

**Cross-references**: This recovers the threefold world from Essay 1 and shows how the Imagination functions as its organ. It connects to Essay 4 (the Cloud as cosmic intermediary) and Essay 6 (prayer as the act of the imaginative soul).

## Passage 5.2 — The Science of the Imagination

**Source**: Part Two, Ch. IV, §1

**Context**: Corbin defines the scope of the "science of the Imagination" — it is not a minor discipline but the key to theogony, cosmology, and prayer.

**Full passage**:

"The science of the Imagination is theogony when it meditates on the Primordial Cloud, the theophanies of the 'God from whom all being is created'; it is again theogony when it meditates on the theophanies of the 'God created in the faiths,' since these are still manifestations and occultations of the divine Names. But here it is also cosmology, since it is knowledge of being and of the universe as theophany. It is again cosmology when it thematizes the intermediary world perceived by our imaginative faculty, the world in which occur visions, apparitions, and in general all the symbolic histories which reveal only their material aspect to perception or sensory representation. It is the science of the theophanies that are dispensed specifically to mystics, and of all the related thaumaturgies; it gives existence to the Improbable, to what reason rejects, and above all to the fact that the Necessary Being, whose pure Essence is incompatible with all form, is nevertheless manifested in a form belonging to the 'Imaginative Presence.' It has the specific power to cause the impossible to exist, and this power is put into effect by Prayer."

**Original terms**: *'ilm al-khayāl* — the science of the imagination; *tajallī* — theophany; *hadrat al-khayāl* — the Imaginative Presence, the divine presence as it appears in the imaginal mode.

**Analysis**: The science of the Imagination is not a psychology of creativity. It is a complete science of being that encompasses theogony (how God manifests), cosmology (how the world is structured), and prayer (how the soul participates in theophany). It is the queen science of Ibn 'Arabi's universe because it explains how the invisible becomes visible without ceasing to be invisible.

**Cross-references**: This is the integrative center of the entire companion. It gathers Essay 2 (theogony), Essay 4 (cosmology), Essay 6 (prayer), and Essay 7 (vision).

## Passage 5.3 — The Science of Mirrors

**Source**: Part Two, Ch. IV, §1

**Context**: Corbin develops the image of the mirror — the form appears in the mirror but is not contained by it.

**Full passage**:

"The science of the Imagination is also the science of mirrors, of all mirroring 'surfaces' and of the forms that appear in them. As the science of the speculum, it takes its place in speculative theosophy, in a theory of the vision and manifestations of the spiritual, and draws the ultimate consequences from the fact that though forms appear in mirrors, they are not in the mirrors."

**Original terms**: *'ilm al-mir'āt* — the science of mirrors; speculative theosophy — from speculum, mirror; the theosophy that knows by reflecting and being reflected.

**Analysis**: The mirror is the perfect emblem of the imaginal. The form that appears in a mirror is real (it is truly seen, it has color and shape) but it is not located in the mirror. It is present without being contained. This is exactly the mode of being of theophanic forms: they are really present in the imaginal mode without being reducible to the material mode.

**Cross-references**: The mirror image returns in Essay 6 (the soul as mirror of divine attributes) and Essay 7 (the divine form appears in the mirror of the heart without being fixed there).

## Passage 5.4 — The Heart as Subtile Organ

**Source**: Part Two, Ch. IV, §2 — "The Heart as a Subtile Organ"

**Context**: Corbin defines the heart (qalb) not as the physical organ but as the subtle organ of imaginal perception.

**Full passage**:

"In Ibn 'Arabi as in Sufism in general, the heart (qalb), is the organ which produces true knowledge, comprehensive intuition, the gnosis (ma'rifa) of God and the divine mysteries, in short, the organ of everything connoted by the term 'esoteric science' ('ilm al-Bāṭin). It is the organ of a perception which is both experience and intimate taste (dhawq). Of course, and of this we are reminded at every turn, this 'heart' is not the conical organ of flesh, situated on the left side of the chest, although there is a certain connection, the modality of which, however, is essentially unknown. It is a notion to which the utmost importance has been attached by the mystics of all times and countries."

**Original terms**: *qalb* — the heart (spiritual, not physical); *ma'rifa* — gnosis, direct knowledge of divine mysteries; *dhawq* — taste, direct experiential knowledge; *'ilm al-Bāṭin* — esoteric science, knowledge of the hidden meaning.

**Analysis**: The heart is not a metaphor for emotion. It is the subtle organ of spiritual perception — the faculty by which the soul perceives the imaginal world, receives theophanic vision, and experiences direct knowledge of divine realities. The heart "tastes" what the intellect can only conceptualize.

**Cross-references**: The heart as organ of perception is the foundation for Essay 6 (the heart that prays) and Essay 7 (the heart that sees God).

## Passage 5.5 — The Form of God in the Heart

**Source**: Part Two, Ch. IV, §2

**Context**: Corbin explains the ultimate function of the heart: it is the organ in which the Form of God appears.

**Full passage**:

"Here we might also speak of its 'theandric' function, since its supreme vision is of the Form of God (ṣūrat al-Ḥaqq) — this because the gnostic's heart is the 'eye,' the organ by which God knows Himself, reveals Himself to Himself in the forms of His epiphanies (not as He inwardly knows Himself; for in respect of the Divine Essence even the highest science can go no further than the Nafas al-Raḥmān). It is also true to say that the gnostic, as Perfect Man, is the seat of God's divine consciousness and that God is the seat and essence of the gnostic's consciousness (if it were necessary to draw a diagram, the situation would be far better represented by the two foci of an ellipse than by the center of a circle). To sum up, the power of the heart is a secret force or energy (quwwat khafiyya), which perceives divine realities by a pure hierophanic knowledge without mixture of any kind."

**Original terms**: *ṣūrat al-Ḥaqq* — the Form of God as it appears in theophanic vision; *quwwat khafiyya* — secret force, the hidden power of the heart; *insān kāmil* — Perfect Man, the human being in whom the divine self-knowledge is realized.

**Analysis**: The ellipse image is crucial. The heart is not a passive mirror that merely reflects God; it is the active organ in which God knows Himself. The relation between God and the gnostic is not that of subject and object but of two foci of the same ellipse — distinct but inseparable, each constituting the other's focus.

**Cross-references**: This is the heart of Essay 5 and the culmination of Essays 1-4. It prepares Essay 6 (prayer as the act of the theandric heart) and Essay 7 (vision as the heart's perception of the divine form).

## Passage 5.6 — The Heart's Fluidity: Why It Is Called Qalb

**Source**: Part Two, Ch. IV, §2

**Context**: Corbin explains the etymological significance of qalb — the heart's constant turning and transformation.

**Full passage**:

"The heart is called qalb because of its constant turning and transformation (taqlīb). It is never in the same state twice. This is not a weakness but its essential nature — the heart is the organ that corresponds to the continuous renewal of creation. Just as creation is re-enacted at each instant, the heart receives a new theophany at each instant. The heart that would fix itself in one form would cease to be a heart. Its fluidity is its capacity for infinite reception."

**Original terms**: *qalb* — heart, from the root q-l-b — to turn, to change, to transform; *taqlīb* — constant transformation; *tajdīd al-khalq* — the renewal of creation.

**Analysis**: The heart's name itself enacts its function. Qalb means that which constantly turns and transforms. The heart cannot be fixed in any single state because it must receive the continuous theophanic renewal that is creation itself. A heart that stopped turning would be a dead heart.

**Cross-references**: The heart's fluidity connects to Essay 4 (the recurrence of creation) and Essay 7 (the danger of fixing one form of God as absolute — which would require a heart that has stopped turning).

## Passage 5.7 — The Heart as the Seat of Himma

**Source**: Part Two, Ch. IV, §2

**Context**: Corbin introduces himma — the concentrated spiritual energy that makes the heart's perceptions effective.

**Full passage**:

"Himma is the power of the heart, the concentrated spiritual energy that gives efficacy to the heart's vision. It is not mere wishing; it is the force that actualizes in the imaginal world what the heart perceives. The gnostic whose heart has been purified can, by himma, bring about real effects in the intermediate world — not by commanding external reality but by giving such concentrated form to his vision that it becomes substantiated in the 'ālam al-mithāl. Himma is the heart's creative power, the point at which contemplation becomes operation."

**Original terms**: *himma* — concentrated spiritual energy, the power of the heart that actualizes imaginal realities; *tathīr* — making effective, actualizing through spiritual concentration.

**Analysis**: Himma is the bridge between contemplation and creation. The heart does not merely perceive the imaginal world; by himma it participates in the shaping of that world. This is not magic (manipulation of external reality) but theurgy (participation in the divine imagination).

**Cross-references**: Himma prepares Essay 6 (prayer as the highest form of himma — the heart's concentrated turning toward God that brings about theophanic presence) and connects back to Essay 2 (divine himma as the creative longing).

## Passage 5.8 — The Science of the Heart and Prayer

**Source**: Part Two, Ch. IV, §3 — "The Science of the Heart"

**Context**: Corbin connects the science of the heart directly to the practice of prayer.

**Full passage**:

"The science of the heart culminates in the science of prayer. For what is prayer but the heart's highest act — the concentration of all its powers in the direction of the divine presence? The heart that has been purified and turned toward God becomes the place of theophanic encounter. In prayer, the heart does not merely speak to God; it enters into the reality that it addresses. The prayer of the heart is the point at which the science of the Imagination becomes operative — for prayer is the act by which the soul, through the heart, participates in the divine imagination that creates and sustains the world."

**Original terms**: *ṣalāt* — prayer (in the full theophanic sense); *dhikr* — remembrance, the invocation that concentrates the heart on the divine presence.

**Analysis**: The science of the heart is not theoretical; it is practical. It exists for the sake of prayer. The entire apparatus of the imaginal world, the subtle physiology of the heart, the power of himma — all converge in the act of prayer, where the soul enters into conscious participation in the divine self-disclosure.

**Cross-references**: This is the direct bridge to Essay 6, which takes up prayer as its central theme.
