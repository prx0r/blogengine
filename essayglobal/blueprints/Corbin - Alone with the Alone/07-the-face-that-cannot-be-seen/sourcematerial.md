## Passage 7.1 — The Hadith of the Vision

**Source**: Part Two, Ch. VI, §1 — "The Hadith of the Vision"

**Context**: Corbin opens with the famous hadith in which the Prophet says he has seen his Lord in the form of a young man — a tradition that scandalizes literalists but contains the key to theophanic vision.

**Full passage**:

"The hadith of the vision — 'I have seen my Lord in the form of a young man' — is one of the most controversial traditions in Islam. The literalists reject it as anthropomorphism. The rationalists explain it away as metaphor. For Ibn 'Arabi, it is neither. It is a report of an actual vision that occurred in the imaginal world, where the divine appears in forms appropriate to the capacity of the seer. The form of the young man is not God as He is in Himself — that would be impossible, for God in Himself is beyond all form. But it is God as He manifests Himself to the visionary in the 'ālam al-mithāl, the world where the spiritual takes body and the body becomes spiritual."

**Original terms**: *ru'yat Allāh* — vision of God; *ḥadīth al-ru'ya* — the tradition of the vision; *tashbīh* — anthropomorphism (the error of taking the theophanic form for God as He is in Himself); *tanzīh* — incomparability (God's transcendence beyond all form).

**Analysis**: The hadith of the vision is the theological crux of the entire book. It asserts that God can be seen — but only in the imaginal mode, not in His Essence. The form is real (it is truly seen) but not exhaustive (God is more than the form). The tension between tashbīh and tanzīh — between comparing God to form and declaring God incomparable — is resolved by the imaginal: God appears in form without being contained by form.

**Cross-references**: This resolves the tension created in Essay 4 (the God created in the faiths) and Essay 5 (the Form of God in the heart). It is the theological culmination of the entire companion.

## Passage 7.2 — Vision According to Capacity

**Source**: Part Two, Ch. VI, §1

**Context**: Corbin explains that every vision of God is according to the capacity of the seer — which is why there are many forms and no final form.

**Full passage**:

"Every vision of God is determined by the capacity of the one who sees. The divine form that appears in the vision is not God limiting Himself to one form; it is the form that the seer's heart is capable of receiving. This is why the same God appears differently to different seers, and why the same seer may see different forms at different stages of his spiritual development. The form is never the whole of God; it is always the form that the heart can bear. The error is not in seeing a form but in stopping at the form, in mistaking the theophany for the God who manifests through it."

**Original terms**: *ṣūra* — form, theophanic image; *qābiliyya* — capacity, receptivity; *tajallī* — theophany, which varies according to the capacity of the receiver.

**Analysis**: The principle that vision is according to capacity protects both the reality of the vision and the transcendence of God. The form is real — it is truly seen, it is genuinely a theophany. But it is not exhaustive; God is more than any form that appears. The danger is not in seeing but in stopping.

**Cross-references**: This deepens the principle from Essay 4 (the God created in the faiths) and applies it to personal vision. It also connects to Essay 5 (the heart's fluidity — because the heart changes, the vision changes).

## Passage 7.3 — The Danger of Fixing Form: Idolatry

**Source**: Part Two, Ch. VI, §1

**Context**: Corbin identifies the spiritual danger — fixing one form of God as the only form, which is the essence of idolatry.

**Full passage**:

"Idolatry is not the use of form in worship; it is the fixation of form — the refusal to let the form remain transparent to the divine beyond it. The idolater is not the one who sees God in a stone or an image; the idolater is the one who sees God only in that stone or that image, who refuses to recognize the divine presence in any other form. The theophanic vision is the opposite of idolatry: it sees the form as a window, not as a wall. The idolater builds a wall around his form; the visionary looks through the form to the infinite beyond it."

**Original terms**: *shirk* — idolatry, the association of God with a limited form taken as absolute; *tajallī* — theophany, which keeps the form transparent.

**Analysis**: Idolatry is not having a form of God; it is refusing to let that form be surpassed. The theophanic tradition is the discipline of seeing through forms without abolishing them. Each form is a true theophany; none is the final theophany.

**Cross-references**: This is the practical danger that the entire companion warns against. Essay 1 (esotericism as depth-reading) is the method that prevents fixation. Essay 3 (Nizām as theophany, not idol) is the example. Essay 5 (the heart's fluidity) is the organ that prevents fixation.

## Passage 7.4 — Around the Mystic Ka'aba

**Source**: Part Two, Ch. VI, §2 — "Around the Mystic Ka'aba"

**Context**: Corbin ends the book where it began — at the Ka'aba — but now the Ka'aba has become the heart's inner center, the place of theophanic vision.

**Full passage**:

"The mystic Ka'aba is not the stone building in Mecca; it is the heart's center, the place where the divine presence is encountered. The pilgrimage to the physical Ka'aba is the homologation of the soul's journey to its own center. When the soul has completed its circumambulation — when it has turned itself entirely toward the divine — it arrives at the place where vision is possible. The mystic Ka'aba is the heart made into a place of theophany, the innermost sanctuary where God appears in the form that love can see."

**Original terms**: *Ka'aba* — the cube, the physical and spiritual center; *Bayt Allāh* — the House of God, both the physical temple and the heart of the gnostic; *tawāf* — circumambulation, the soul's turning around the divine center.

**Analysis**: The book that began with spiritual topography (Essay 1) ends at the mystic Ka'aba — the center toward which all spiritual geography is oriented. The physical pilgrimage to Mecca becomes the symbol of the inner journey. The Ka'aba of stone and the Ka'aba of the heart are not two different things but the same thing seen at different levels.

**Cross-references**: This closes the circle with Essay 1 (spiritual topography) and Essay 3 (the encounter at the Ka'aba). The structure of the entire companion mirrors the circumambulation of the Ka'aba — a movement around a center that is both the source and the goal.

## Passage 7.5 — The Two Paradoxical Truths: Vision and Refusal

**Source**: Part Two, Ch. VI (concluding passage before Epilogue)

**Context**: Corbin states the two simultaneous truths that the vision of God requires — the divine refusal and the prophetic attestation.

**Full passage**:

"The outcome of what, by way of contrasting it with the traditional idea of the 'combat with the Angel,' I have characterized in certain earlier studies as a 'combat for the Angel': a homologation of the infinite in the finite, of the divine totality in the microcosm of the Perfect Man, and these two simultaneous but paradoxical truths — the divine refusal: 'Thou shalt not see me' and the prophetic attestation: 'I have contemplated my God in the most beautiful of forms.'"

**Original terms**: *lā tarānī* — "Thou shalt not see me" (the divine refusal, God's transcendence beyond all form); *ra'aytu rabbī fī aḥsani ṣūra* — "I have seen my Lord in the most beautiful of forms" (the prophetic attestation, the reality of theophanic vision).

**Analysis**: These two truths must be held together. The divine refusal prevents the reduction of God to form; the prophetic attestation prevents the denial of theophanic vision. The imaginal is the space in which both are true: the form is seen, but God is not contained by it.

**Cross-references**: This is the culminating paradox of the entire companion. It resolves the tension between tashbīh and tanzīh that runs through Essays 1, 4, and 5.

## Passage 7.6 — The Epilogue: Theophanic Imagination and Being

**Source**: Epilogue

**Context**: Corbin concludes the entire work by returning to the ontological status of the Imagination — it is not a human faculty but the very power of being.

**Full passage**:

"The Imagination is not a human faculty that produces unrealities. It is the power by which being itself manifests. The divine Imagination is the creative act by which the hidden becomes manifest. The human Imagination, when purified and activated, participates in this divine act — not by creating new beings from nothing but by perceiving the theophanic forms through which beings disclose themselves. To imagine, in the highest sense, is to let being appear. The active Imagination is the organ of the real — not because it invents reality but because it is the place where reality reveals its depth."

**Original terms**: *khayāl* — Imagination (theophanic, creative); *wujūd* — being, existence; *tajallī* — theophany, the self-disclosure of being.

**Analysis**: The Epilogue gathers the entire argument: the Imagination is not fantasy but theophany. It is not a human power that produces unrealities but the cosmic power by which the real manifests. The active Imagination is the organ of the real — the faculty that perceives the depth-dimension of all that exists.

Corbin closes with a poignant reflection on the distance between the modern world and the world of the Spirituals whose vision he has been describing:

"What we experience as an obsession with nothingness or as acquiescence in a nonbeing over which we have no power, was to them a manifestation of divine anger, the anger of the mystic Beloved. But even that was a real Presence, the presence of that Image which never forsook our Sufis."

The Epilogue ends with a poem that speaks in the voice of the soul to the Beloved — a soul that has received vision even in the face of refusal:

"If the sword of your anger puts me to death, / My soul will find comfort in it. / If you impose the cup of poison upon me, / My spirit will drink the cup. / ... For even though you refused me your love, / You have given me a vision of You / Which has been the confidant of my hidden secrets."

**Cross-references**: This is the final synthesis of all seven essays. The Imagination that opened Essay 1 as the organ of the intermediate world returns here as the very power of being. The circle is complete. The book that began with the invitation to become a disciple of Khiḍr ends with the soul that has received the vision and can say: "You have given me a vision of You."
