# The Face That Cannot Be Seen — Vision, Form, and the Danger of Idolatry

The final chapter of *Alone with the Alone* — Chapter VI — and the Epilogue bring the entire
book to its point. Prayer has become presence (Essay 6); now presence must be understood as
vision. But vision carries a danger: the form that reveals can also fix, the window can become
a wall. The chapter moves through two sections — the Hadith of the Vision and Around the Mystic
Ka'aba — and closes with the Epilogue, which gathers the whole argument into a single poem.

The final tension of the book gathers in a single question: can God be seen in form without the
form becoming an idol?

## §1. The Hadith of the Vision

The question lands with the weight of a body
falling. Can God be seen? The Prophet said yes — "I have seen my Lord in the form of a
young man" — and the theologians have never recovered from the scandal of that
sentence. Ibn 'Arabī picks up the broken pieces:

"The hadith of the vision — 'I have seen my Lord in the form of a young man' — is one of the
most controversial traditions in Islam. The literalists reject it as anthropomorphism. The
rationalists explain it away as metaphor. For Ibn 'Arabī, it is neither. It is a report of an
actual vision that occurred in the imaginal world, where the divine appears in forms
appropriate to the capacity of the seer. The form of the young man is not God as He is in
Himself — that would be impossible, for God in Himself is beyond all form. But it is God as He
manifests Himself to the visionary in the 'ālam al-mithāl, the world where the spiritual takes
body and the body becomes spiritual."

*Ru'yat Allāh. Vision of God. Tashbīh and tanzīh. Comparison and incomparability, held together.*

The vision is real — the form is truly seen. But the vision is not exhaustive — God is more
than the form that appears. The tension between tashbīh (comparing God to form) and tanzīh
(declaring God incomparable) is resolved by the imaginal: God appears in form without being
contained by form.

"Every vision of God is determined by the capacity of the one who sees. The divine form that
appears in the vision is not God limiting Himself to one form; it is the form that the seer's
heart is capable of receiving. This is why the same God appears differently to different seers,
and why the same seer may see different forms at different stages of his spiritual development.
The form is never the whole of God; it is always the form that the heart can bear. The error is
not in seeing a form but in stopping at the form, in mistaking the theophany for the God who
manifests through it."

*Qābiliyya. Capacity. The heart's receptivity determines the form of theophany.*

The principle protects both the reality of the vision and the transcendence of God. The form is
real — it is truly seen, it is genuinely a theophany. But it is not exhaustive; God is more
than any form that appears. The danger is not in seeing but in stopping.

## §2. Idolatry as Fixation

Idolatry is not the use of form in worship. It is the fixation of form — the refusal to let the
form remain transparent:

"Idolatry is not the use of form in worship; it is the fixation of form — the refusal to let
the form remain transparent to the divine beyond it. The idolater is not the one who sees God
in a stone or an image; the idolater is the one who sees God only in that stone or that image,
who refuses to recognize the divine presence in any other form. The theophanic vision is the
opposite of idolatry: it sees the form as a window, not as a wall. The idolater builds a wall
around his form; the visionary looks through the form to the infinite beyond it."

*Shirk. Idolatry. Fixing the divine in a single form and refusing to let it be surpassed.*

The theophanic tradition is the discipline of seeing through forms without abolishing them.
Each form is a true theophany; none is the final theophany. The heart that has learned to see
God in one form must be ready to see God in another — and another, and another, without ever
mistaking the window for the view.

---

The two simultaneous truths that the vision requires are the divine refusal and the prophetic
attestation. Both must be held together:

"The outcome of what, by way of contrasting it with the traditional idea of the 'combat with
the Angel,' I have characterized in certain earlier studies as a 'combat for the Angel': a
homologation of the infinite in the finite, of the divine totality in the microcosm of the
Perfect Man, and these two simultaneous but paradoxical truths — the divine refusal: 'Thou
shalt not see me' and the prophetic attestation: 'I have contemplated my God in the most
beautiful of forms.'"

*Lā tarānī. Thou shalt not see me. Ra'aytu rabbī fī aḥsani ṣūra. I have seen my Lord in the most beautiful of forms.*

The divine refusal prevents the reduction of God to form; the prophetic attestation prevents
the denial of theophanic vision. The imaginal is the space in which both are true: the form is
seen, but God is not contained by it.

## §3. Around the Mystic Ka'aba

Corbin narrates the culminating vision of Ibn 'Arabī's spiritual life — the encounter with the
Evanescent Youth at the Ka'aba:

"While circumambulating the Temple he improvised aloud certain verses resonant with the
melancholy of his doubts. Suddenly there emerged from the shadows the feminine Figure who was
to be for him the earthly manifestation of Sophia aeterna. The visionary event that I should
like to evoke in conclusion of the present volume forms both the prelude and the mystic source
of Ibn 'Arabī's great book *Spiritual Conquests of Mecca*. Our brief reference to it here will
concern the identity of the Apparition as a visualization of the Image in which, because this
'Form of God' is his origin and end, his eternal companion, Ibn 'Arabī's whole personal being
is fulfilled."

The Temple itself becomes alive and transparent:

"Becoming alive and transparent, the Temple reveals the secret it concealed, the 'Form of God'
which is the Self (or rather the Figure which eminently personifies it) and makes it known as
the Mystic's divine Alter Ego. And the denouement is this: the period of circumambulation
around the Temple comes to an end, and together the two 'companions' enter the Temple."

The encounter takes the form of a dialogue between the human self and the divine Alter Ego:

"Ibn 'Arabī is engaged in circumambulating the Ka'aba. Before the Black Stone he encounters the
mysterious being whom he recognizes and designates as 'the Evanescent Youth, the Silent
Speaker, him who is neither living nor dead, the composite-simple, the enveloped-enveloping,'
all terms signifying the coincidentia oppositorum."

*The Evanescent Youth. The Silent Speaker. The coincidentia oppositorum. The form that is both the soul's origin and its end.*

The Youth reveals himself as the mystic's transcendent Self — the being who is both the soul's origin and its companion:

"'I am knowledge, I am he who knows and I am what is known.' Thus the being who is the mystic's transcendent self, his divine Alter Ego, reveals himself, and the mystic does not hesitate to recognize him, for in the course of his quest he has heard the command: 'Look toward the Angel who is with you and who accomplishes the circumambulations beside you.' He has learned that the mystic Ka'aba is the heart of being. It has been said to him: 'The Temple which contains Me is your heart.' The mystery of the Divine Essence is no other than the Temple of the heart, and it is around the heart that the spiritual pilgrim circumambulates."

"'Accomplish the circumambulations and follow my footsteps,' the Youth now commands him. Then we hear an amazing dialogue, the meaning of which seems at first to defy all human expression. For how indeed is it possible to translate what two beings who are each other can say to each other: the 'Angel' who is the divine self, and his other self, the 'missionary' on earth, when they meet in the world of 'Imaginative Presence'?"

The visionary is no longer alone before an inaccessible God:

"The visionary is no longer the solitary self, reduced to his mere earthly dimension in the face of the inaccessible Godhead, for in encountering the being in whom the Godhead is his companion he knows that he himself is the secret of the Godhead (sirr al-rubūbiyya), and it is their 'syzygia,' their twoness which accomplishes the circular processional: seven times, the seven divine Attributes of perfection in which the mystic is successively invested."

The ritual culminates in entry into the Temple:

"And then comes the denouement: 'Enter into the Temple with me,' the Mystic Youth commands. The hermeneut of the Mystery no longer contents himself with looking from outside; he enters the sanctuary. The circumambulation is complete. The face has been seen."

The book ends where it began — at the Ka'aba. But now the Ka'aba has become the heart's inner
center, the place where theophanic vision occurs:

"The mystic Ka'aba is not the stone building in Mecca; it is the heart's center, the place
where the divine presence is encountered. The pilgrimage to the physical Ka'aba is the
homologation of the soul's journey to its own center. When the soul has completed its
circumambulation — when it has turned itself entirely toward the divine — it arrives at the
place where vision is possible. The mystic Ka'aba is the heart made into a place of theophany,
the innermost sanctuary where God appears in the form that love can see."

*Ka'aba. Bayt Allāh. Tawāf. The House of God in the heart, around which the soul turns.*

The book that began with spiritual topography ends at the mystic Ka'aba — the center toward
which all spiritual geography is oriented. The physical pilgrimage to Mecca is the outer form
of the inner journey. The Ka'aba of stone and the Ka'aba of the heart are not two different
things but the same thing seen at different levels.

## Epilogue: The Vision and the Distance

Corbin closes the Epilogue with a reflection on the distance between the modern world and the
world of the Spirituals whose vision he has been describing:

"What we experience as an obsession with nothingness or as acquiescence in a nonbeing over
which we have no power, was to them a manifestation of divine anger, the anger of the mystic
Beloved. But even that was a real Presence, the presence of that Image which never forsook our
Sufis."

The final poem speaks in the voice of the soul to the Beloved — a soul that has received vision
even in the face of refusal:

"If the sword of your anger puts me to death, / My soul will find comfort in it. / If you
impose the cup of poison upon me, / My spirit will drink the cup. / When on the day of
Resurrection / I rise from the dust of my tomb, / The perfume of your love / Will still
impregnate the garment of my soul. / For even though you refused me your love, / You have given
me a vision of You / Which has been the confidant of my hidden secrets."

The book that began with the invitation to become a disciple of Khiḍr ends with the soul that
has received the vision and can say: "You have given me a vision of You." The form was seen,
the face was encountered, the theophany was real. And because the form was a window and not a
wall, the vision opens not onto possession but onto infinite depth — the face that cannot be
seen because it has been seen, and in being seen, has disclosed that it can never be exhausted.
