# The Map, the Veil, and the Method — How Corbin Teaches Us to Read Ibn 'Arabī

In the Introduction to *Alone with the Alone*, Henry Corbin does what he asks his reader to do:
he changes the ground before the argument begins. The book does not open with a thesis
statement about Ibn 'Arabī's doctrine. It opens with a map — between Andalusia and Iran — and a
figure — Khiḍr, the Green Guide — and a warning: the reader who cannot dissociate spirituality
from its social frame will not understand what follows. Corbin's Introduction is not
background. It is an initiation.

The work falls into two parts — "Sympathy and Theopathy" and "Creative Imagination and Creative
Prayer" — but the Introduction belongs to neither. It is the threshold. Before Ibn 'Arabī's
doctrines can be understood, the reader's categories have to be changed: geography becomes
spiritual topography, biography becomes symbolic itinerary, esotericism becomes the necessary
mode of interpretation, and imagination begins to be recovered as a faculty of real disclosure
rather than fantasy. The three sections of the Introduction — the spiritual topography, the
symbolic curve of the life, and the situation of esotericism — each perform a distinct
transformation. The essay that follows walks through them in order.

## §1. Between Andalusia and Iran: The Spiritual Topography

"For them the world is 'objectively' and actually threefold: between the universe that can be
apprehended by pure intellectual perception (the universe of the Cherubic Intelligences) and
the universe perceptible to the senses, there is an intermediate world, the world of
Idea-Images, of archetypal figures, of subtile substances, of 'immaterial matter.' This world
is as real and objective, as consistent and subsistent as the intelligible and sensible worlds;
it is an intermediate universe 'where the spiritual takes body and the body becomes spiritual,'
a world consisting of real matter and real extension, though by comparison to sensible,
corruptible matter these are subtile and immaterial. The organ of this universe is the active
Imagination; it is the place of theophanic visions, the scene on which visionary events and
symbolic histories appear in their true reality."

*'Ālam al-mithāl. Mundus imaginalis. Barzakh. The intermediate world, the isthmus between the sensible and the intelligible.*

We grew up in a two-story house: matter downstairs, spirit upstairs. Corbin opens a door in the stairwell and shows us a whole floor we never knew existed. The imaginal is not the imaginary — the imaginary is what you make up, the imaginal is what you discover. It has its own furniture, its own weather, its own laws. It is where prophets go when they see what others cannot. To refuse this third floor is to refuse Ibn 'Arabī before he has spoken a word.

A dream proves it to you. In a dream you run, you touch, you weep — and when you wake, the road you ran on is nowhere, the hand you touched is empty. Was it nothing? If it was nothing, why did it leave a trace? The imaginal is that trace made objective. It is where the Angel appears as a young man, where the Burning Bush burns without consuming, where Moses hears a voice that is not an echo of his own mind.


## §2. The Reader as Disciple of Khiḍr

"We have used the word spirituality by design, fully aware of how misplaced it may seem. It
concerns the most secret and most profound life of the soul; but more often than not age-old
habits make it impossible for us to dissociate this personal life from its social frame, lead
us to regard it as dependent on the mediation of an 'ecclesiastical reality' — so much so that
detachment from this reality appears equivalent to the irrevocable loss of spirituality itself.
To those who are unable to effect this dissociation, the spirituality of an Ibn 'Arabī will
have little to say. To those who seek an encounter 'alone with the Alone,' those who are
capable of being like him the 'disciples of Khiḍr' and for whom no conformism prevails over the
personal imperative — to those Ibn 'Arabī and his companions in the Orient still have something
to say."

*Khiḍr. The Green One. Walāyah. Initiation through personal encounter, not institutional succession.*

Corbin is not writing a textbook. He is calling a reader into being — the one who has felt the unease of belonging to a tradition without being contained by it. If your spirituality requires a building, a ceremony, a certificate of membership, this book will feel like a threat. If your spirituality has already outgrown its container, it will feel like a homecoming.


## §2. The Norm of One's Own Orthodoxy

"Ibn 'Arabī is one of those powerful and rare spiritual individuals who are the norm of their
own orthodoxy and of their own time, because they belong neither to what is commonly called
'their' time nor to the orthodoxy of 'their' time. What by a historical convention is termed
'their' time is not really their time. Accordingly, to affect to believe that such masters are
nothing more than representatives of a certain 'tradition' is to forget their considerable
personal contribution, is to neglect the perfect assurance with which an Arab of Andalusia like
Ibn 'Arabī, or Iranians like Abū Ya'qūb Sejestānī, Suhrawardī, Semnānī, Mullā Ṣadrā of Shīrāz
proclaim that such and such an idea, developed on such and such a page of their books, can be
found nowhere else, because it is their discovery of their personal experience."

*Taḥqīq. Personal verification. Truth realized through spiritual experience, not received from external authority.*

"Some writers have employed the word 'syncretism.' This is the summary, insidious, and facile
kind of explanation that appeals to a dogmatic mind alarmed at the operations of a thinking
which obeys only the imperatives of its internal norm but whose personal character does not
impair its rigor. To content oneself with such an explanation is to confess one's failure,
one's inability to gain so much as an intimation of this norm which cannot be reduced to a
school or other collective conformism."

---

"Ibn 'Arabī was surnamed the Platonist, the 'son of Plato' (Ibn Aflāṭūn). This clarifies
certain co-ordinates of the spiritual topography which we are here trying to establish.
Anticipating the projects of Gemistos Pletho and Marsilio Ficino, this oriental Platonism, this
Zoroastrian Neoplatonism of Iran escaped the rising tide of Aristotelianism which invaded the
Latin Middle Ages and for several centuries determined not only their philosophy but also their
world feeling. Accordingly, when in Cordova the young Ibn 'Arabī attended the funeral of
Averroes, the great master of medieval Aristotelianism, the melancholy scene becomes
transfigured into a symbol which we shall do well to consider attentively."

*Ishrāq. Illumination. The Oriental wisdom that Suhrawardi revived and Ibn 'Arabī embodied.*

Corbin is redrawing the map of philosophy. We divide thinkers into East and West as though those were neutral geographies. For Corbin, "East" does not mean Asia — it means the Orient of light, where the sun rises on the soul. When Ibn 'Arabī is called "Platonist," it is not about borrowing Greek ideas; it is about a living current of wisdom that survived in Iran while the Latin West went Aristotelian. The difference between Aristotelianism and this Oriental Platonism is the difference between a philosophy that argues from texts and a philosophy that sees from illumination. The funeral of Averroes is the funeral of the first kind.

The Averroes funeral is the condensed symbol of the entire Introduction. The young Ibn 'Arabī
watched the great Aristotelian's body loaded onto a beast — his books on one side, his coffin
on the other to balance the weight. Ibn 'Arabī's comment reverses the symbolism: the books are
not the master.

---

"The curve and symbols of the life of a spiritual master such as Ibn 'Arabī are not intended
for that biographical curiosity which delights in the exceptional. Contemplating the curve of
this life should lead us to reflect on the meaning and function of the lives of the great
spiritual figures of Islam. Because the man who is the object of our study is in the highest
sense a prophet of the inner man, such a study may serve as an introduction to a spiritual
typology. Let us say that the curve of his life, its characteristic ups and downs, its
encounters and its ruptures, is the model, the type, of a spiritual destiny — that of the
Fedele d'Amore."

*Fedele d'Amore. The faithful of love. One whose spiritual path is through love and beauty.*

The life is not separate from the teaching. It is the teaching's first visible form. The
encounters, journeys, visions, and writings are not biographical curiosities — they are the
doctrine made visible in time.

Think of a great musician's biography. You can list every city they toured, every contract they signed, every review they received — and still miss the point if you never understood what their music was doing. Corbin reads Ibn 'Arabī's life the same way: the outer events are not the story. They are the visible waveform of an invisible melody. The "curve" of the life is the shape of that melody. Reading it this way means you stop asking "what happened?" and start asking "what wanted to happen through him?"

"The figure of Khiḍr, the 'Green One,' the mysterious prophet who initiates those who are worthy into the secret knowledge, haunts the spirituality of Islam. He is the 'invisible guide' who appears at the moment of need, who leads the soul beyond the letter of the law to the living spring of life. To be a disciple of Khiḍr is to belong to that spiritual lineage which transcends all institutional forms, all dogmatic formulations, all confessional boundaries. It is to seek knowledge not from books alone but from the direct encounter with the living source. Ibn 'Arabī is pre-eminently a disciple of Khiḍr."

*Khiḍr. Walāyah. The transmission that bypasses institutions and flows through personal encounter.*

---

The center of the Introduction — and the hermeneutic key to the entire companion — is the situation of esotericism.

"Esotericism is one of the essential dimensions of the spiritual life of Islam. To speak of the
'situation of esotericism' is to define at the same time its function and necessity, that is,
the need to distinguish between the literal, apparent, or exoteric meaning (ẓāhir) and the
hidden, interior, or esoteric meaning (bāṭin) of the data of Revelation, and indeed of all
reality. The ḥarf or letter of Revelation is the outward form (ẓāhir) which rewards the
'realists of the letter.' The law (Sharī'at) is the aspect of obedience. The esoteric meaning
(bāṭin) is the truth perceived by spiritual vision, a vision which is also a transfiguration of
the whole being; it is the direct and intuitive vision of the spiritual reality hidden in the
letter, the unveiling of the true meaning. The discipline that enables us to attain this
unveiling, this hermeneutic, is ta'wīl."

*Ẓāhir. Bāṭin. Ta'wīl. The outer, the inner, the return to the origin.*

Think of a poem you have read a hundred times that suddenly, one day, meant something completely different. The words did not change. You did. That gap between what the words say and what they reveal — that is the space esotericism names. Ẓāhir is not wrong; it is the surface, the necessary shore from which you must launch. Bāṭin is not a secret code hidden in the text; it is the depth that only becomes visible when the reader has been transformed enough to see it. The heresy is not reading the surface — the heresy is stopping there.

"This 'return' to the origin, the source, the true meaning — which is also the truth of one's
own being — is the operation of ta'wīl. Ta'wīl is, etymologically, to lead something back to
its origin, to its archetype. It is the hermeneutic that consists in leading the literal and
apparent meaning back to its hidden and spiritual meaning, the letter to the spirit. This is
the essential method of esotericism. Ta'wīl is to the spiritual world what dialectic was to the
world of the Greeks — the instrument by which the soul is led back to the truth of its own
essence."

"It is essential to distinguish between symbol and allegory. Allegory is a rational operation;
it consists in the translation, by means of more or less appropriate transpositions, of an
abstract idea into a concrete image. It is a weakening of the idea, a concession to the
weakness of the understanding. Whereas the symbol corresponds to a mode of being that is
inaccessible otherwise than through the symbol itself; it is the manifestation of a reality
that would not be accessible without this manifestation. The symbol is not a sign that points
away from itself to something else — it is the presence of what it reveals."

*Ramz. Symbol. The presence of what it reveals. Not a sign that points away but a form that makes present.*

This distinction governs every major concept in the companion: Sophia is not an allegory of
wisdom but a symbol in which wisdom appears. The world is not an allegory of divine power but a
theophanic symbol. Prayer is not an allegory of the soul's longing but the actual presence of
the divine in the soul's act. The divine form is not an allegory of the invisible but the
visible presence of the invisible in the mode appropriate to vision.

---

"Unlike common knowledge, which is effected by a penetration of the sense impressions of the
outside world into the interior of the soul, the work of prophetic inspiration is a projection
of the inner soul upon the outside world. The active Imagination guides, anticipates, molds
sense perception; that is why it transmutes sensory data into symbols. The Burning Bush is only
a brushwood fire if it is merely perceived by the sensory organs. In order that Moses may
perceive the Burning Bush and hear the Voice calling him 'from the right side of the valley' —
in short, in order that there may be a theophany — an organ of trans-sensory perception is
needed."

*Theophanic perception. The organ that transmutes sensory data into symbols, brushwood fire into theophany.*

"This theophanic perception is accomplished in the 'ālam al-mithāl, whose organ is the
theophanic Imagination. ... Since the Imagination is the organ of theophanic perception, it is
also the organ of prophetic hermeneutics, for it is the imagination which is at all times
capable of transmuting sensory data into symbols and external events into symbolic histories.
Thus the affirmation of an esoteric meaning presupposes a prophetic hermeneutics; and this
hermeneutics postulates an organ capable of perceiving theophanies, of investing visible
figures with a 'theophanic function.' This organ is the active Imagination."

"We shall try to show in what sense this Imagination is creative: because it is essentially the
active Imagination and because its activity defines it essentially as a theophanic Imagination.
It assumes an unparalleled function, so out of keeping with the inoffensive or pejorative view
commonly taken of the 'imagination,' that we might have preferred to designate this Imagination
by a neologism and have occasionally employed the term Imaginatrix."

*Imaginatrix. The theophanic Imagination. Not fantasy but the organ of disclosure, the faculty that lets being appear.*

We habitually treat the imagination as a kind of lie — "you just imagined it" means it was not real. Corbin and Ibn 'Arabī invert this completely. The imagination is not the opposite of reality; it is the organ through which deeper reality becomes perceptible. A brushwood fire is a brushwood fire to ordinary sight, but to the eye that sees through active Imagination, the same fire becomes the Burning Bush — not because the fire changed, but because the seer grew capable of the perception the fire demanded. The active Imagination does not make things up. It makes things present.

---

The Introduction closes by gathering all these threads into a single thesis that announces the
work to come:

"But the organ of theophanic perception, that is, of the perception through which the encounter
between Heaven and Earth in the mid-zone, the 'ālam al-mithāl, takes place, is the active
Imagination. It is the active Imagination which invests the earthly Beloved with his
'theophanic function'; it is essentially a theophanic Imagination and, as such, a creative
Imagination, because Creation is itself theophany and theophanic Imagination. From this idea of
Creation as theophany arises the idea of a sophiology, the figure of Sophia aeterna as she
appears in the theosophy of Ibn 'Arabī."

"The purpose of an introduction as of a prelude is to announce, to give an intimation of, the
themes of a work. It is thus to be hoped that certain of our leitmotivs have been set forth
with some clarity in the foregoing pages. In concluding our introduction, we shall make no
attempt to summarize the book itself, but merely indicate the link between its two parts."

*No summary. Only the link between Part One (Sympathy and Theopathy) and Part Two (Creative Imagination and Creative Prayer).*

This is the most disarming move in the entire Introduction. Most academic writing front-loads its thesis: here is what I will prove, here is the plan. Corbin refuses that convention. An introduction, he says, should not summarize the book; it should tune the reader's instrument so that the book can be heard. The difference is between handing someone a map of a symphony and tuning their ear before the concert begins. If the Introduction feels like an initiation rather than an argument, that is not a flaw — it is the method.

Only after this conversion of reading can the book's stranger claims become intelligible: that
God longs to be known (Essay 2), that beauty can be Sophia (Essay 3), that creation is
theophany (Essay 4), that the heart makes worlds perceptible (Essay 5), that prayer is mutual
creation (Essay 6), and that divine form may appear without becoming an idol (Essay 7).

The map has been drawn. The veil has been named — the ẓāhir that both conceals and reveals the
bāṭin. The method has been given — ta'wīl, the return to origin, the spiritual hermeneutic that
leads the soul back to the truth of its own essence. The reader who has understood why Corbin
begins with the threefold world, why he reads biography as symbolic itinerary, why he insists
on the esoteric as depth rather than secrecy, and why the active Imagination is the organ of
theophanic perception — that reader is ready for what comes next.

What comes next is the engine of the entire book. The Introduction has prepared the reader to
ask: if the world is threefold, if the Imagination is its organ, if esotericism is depth and
not secrecy — then why does manifestation happen at all? The answer, in Chapter I, begins with
a heliotrope turning toward the sun, and a God whose name means to sigh.
