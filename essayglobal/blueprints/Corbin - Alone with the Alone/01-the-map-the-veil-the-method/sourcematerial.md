## Passage 1.1 — The Intermediate World and the Active Imagination

**Source**: Introduction (opening pages)

**Context**: Corbin opens by describing the threefold structure of the world that the reader must accept before Ibn 'Arabi becomes intelligible. This is not background — it is the ontological framework for everything that follows.

**Full passage**:

"For them the world is 'objectively' and actually threefold: between the universe that can be apprehended by pure intellectual perception (the universe of the Cherubic Intelligences) and the universe perceptible to the senses, there is an intermediate world, the world of Idea-Images, of archetypal figures, of subtile substances, of 'immaterial matter.' This world is as real and objective, as consistent and subsistent as the intelligible and sensible worlds; it is an intermediate universe 'where the spiritual takes body and the body becomes spiritual,' a world consisting of real matter and real extension, though by comparison to sensible, corruptible matter these are subtile and immaterial. The organ of this universe is the active Imagination; it is the place of theophanic visions, the scene on which visionary events and symbolic histories appear in their true reality."

**Original terms**: *'ālam al-mithāl* — the world of similitudes, the imaginal world; *mundus imaginalis* — the intermediate world that is neither purely intellectual nor purely sensible.

**Analysis**: This is the ontological ground of the entire book. Without accepting this threefold universe — the intelligible, the imaginal, and the sensible — Ibn 'Arabi's claims about imagination, theophany, prayer, and vision will be misunderstood. The imaginal is not the imaginary. It is a real ontological region with its own matter (subtile, immaterial) and its own mode of perception (active Imagination).

**Cross-references**: This framework is presupposed by every subsequent essay. Essay 5 develops the organ of this world (the heart/Imagination). Essay 4 shows how creation itself is theophanic manifestation within this structure.

## Passage 1.2 — The Reader's Categories Must Be Changed

**Source**: Introduction

**Context**: Corbin states directly that the reader's ordinary habits of thought are the obstacle.

**Full passage**:

"We have used the word spirituality by design, fully aware of how misplaced it may seem. It concerns the most secret and most profound life of the soul; but more often than not age-old habits make it impossible for us to dissociate this personal life from its social frame, lead us to regard it as dependent on the mediation of an 'ecclesiastical reality' — so much so that detachment from this reality appears equivalent to the irrevocable loss of spirituality itself. To those who are unable to effect this dissociation, the spirituality of an Ibn 'Arabi will have little to say. To those who seek an encounter 'alone with the Alone,' those who are capable of being like him the 'disciples of Khiḍr' and for whom no conformism prevails over the personal imperative — to those Ibn 'Arabi and his companions in the Orient still have something to say."

**Original terms**: *khiḍr* — the mysterious prophet-guide who initiates into esoteric knowledge; *alone with the Alone* — the solitary encounter between the soul and the divine.

**Analysis**: Corbin is not merely describing Ibn 'Arabi — he is selecting his reader. The book requires a capacity for dissociation from collective religious forms. This is the hermeneutic conversion that the blueprint identifies as the essay's job.

**Cross-references**: Sets up the personal encounter that becomes central to Essay 3 (Sophia/beauty as personal theophanic form) and Essay 6 (prayer as personal relation).

## Passage 1.3 — Spiritual Topography

**Source**: Introduction §1 — "Between Andalusia and Iran: A Brief Spiritual Topography"

**Context**: Corbin does not begin with doctrine but with geography — because geography itself is already metaphysical.

**Full passage**:

"Ibn 'Arabi was surnamed the Platonist, the 'son of Plato' (Ibn Aflaṭūn). This clarifies certain co-ordinates of the spiritual topography which we are here trying to establish. Anticipating the projects of Gemistos Pletho and Marsilio Ficino, this oriental Platonism, this Zoroastrian Neoplatonism of Iran escaped the rising tide of Aristotelianism which invaded the Latin Middle Ages and for several centuries determined not only their philosophy but also their world feeling. Accordingly, when in Cordova the young Ibn 'Arabi attended the funeral of Averroes, the great master of medieval Aristotelianism, the melancholy scene becomes transfigured into a symbol which we shall do well to consider attentively."

**Original terms**: *Ishrāq* — illumination, the dawning light; *ishrāqīyūn* — the Platonists of Persia, disciples of Suhrawardi's philosophy of illumination.

**Analysis**: Corbin reads geography as a map of spiritual orientation. Andalusia and Iran are not just locations — they represent two poles of Islamic thought: the Aristotelian West and the Platonic East. Ibn 'Arabi's journey from Andalusia to the East is a symbolic passage from rational philosophy to theosophical illumination.

**Cross-references**: The theme of spiritual orientation returns in Essay 7 (the mystic Ka'aba as the center toward which all spiritual journeying moves).

## Passage 1.4 — At Averroes' Funeral

**Source**: Introduction §1

**Context**: Corbin tells the famous anecdote of Ibn 'Arabi's encounter with Averroes as a young boy — a symbolic passing of the torch from rational philosophy to spiritual illumination.

**Full passage**:

"The scene was as follows. As Averroes's body was being placed on a beast of burden, his coffin was being loaded on one side while the books of the master were being loaded on the other, to balance the weight. Ibn 'Arabi's comment reverses the symbolism: On one side the master, on the other his books. But the books, he says, are not the master, and I am hard put to know whether the master will arrive at his destination, if indeed his books do. This precocious observation and gesture reveal a sovereign freedom of spirit, and an emancipation from the cult of books and of dogmatic authority. Perhaps the future Platonist, the 'son of Plato,' thus bade farewell to the great Aristotelian master on his last journey, a master whom he valued but whose path he himself would not follow."

**Original terms**: *taḥqīq* — verification, the personal realization of truth as opposed to the mere possession of knowledge from books.

**Analysis**: This anecdote is a condensed symbol of the entire Introduction. The funeral of Averroes marks the end of a certain kind of philosophy — philosophy as book-learning, as rational demonstration, as adherence to authority. Ibn 'Arabi's path will be that of personal spiritual verification.

**Cross-references**: The theme of knowledge through personal experience vs. received authority recurs throughout the companion. Essay 5 (the heart as organ of knowledge) and Essay 6 (prayer as personal relation to God) both depend on this distinction.

## Passage 1.5 — Biography as Symbolic Curve

**Source**: Introduction §2 — "The Curve and Symbols of Ibn 'Arabi's Life"

**Context**: Corbin does not write a conventional biography. He reads the life as a symbolic pattern.

**Full passage**:

"The curve and symbols of the life of a spiritual master such as Ibn 'Arabi are not intended for that biographical curiosity which delights in the exceptional. Contemplating the curve of this life should lead us to reflect on the meaning and function of the lives of the great spiritual figures of Islam. Because the man who is the object of our study is in the highest sense a prophet of the inner man, such a study may serve as an introduction to a spiritual typology. Let us say that the curve of his life, its characteristic ups and downs, its encounters and its ruptures, is the model, the type, of a spiritual destiny — that of the Fedele d'Amore, to use the term which, with a special meaning of our own, we borrow from the 'faithful of love' of Dante's circle."

**Original terms**: *Fidèles d'Amour* — the "faithful of love," those whose spiritual path is through love and beauty.

**Analysis**: Corbin treats the life as a pre-doctrinal map. The encounters, journeys, visions, and writings of Ibn 'Arabi are not biographical curiosities — they are the doctrine's first visible form. The life teaches the reader how the doctrine appears in time.

**Cross-references**: This symbolic reading of biography prepares the reader for Essay 3, where the encounter with Nizām is read not as romance but as theophany.

## Passage 1.6 — Esotericism Is Not Secrecy

**Source**: Introduction §3 — "The Situation of Esotericism"

**Context**: This is the center of Essay 1. Corbin defines esotericism as a necessary hermeneutic, not as hidden trivia.

**Full passage**:

"Esotericism is one of the essential dimensions of the spiritual life of Islam. To speak of the 'situation of esotericism' is to define at the same time its function and necessity, that is, the need to distinguish between the literal, apparent, or exoteric meaning (ẓāhir) and the hidden, interior, or esoteric meaning (bāṭin) of the data of Revelation, and indeed of all reality. The ḥarf or letter of Revelation is the outward form (ẓāhir) which rewards the 'realists of the letter.' The law (Sharī'at) is the aspect of obedience. The esoteric meaning (bāṭin) is the truth perceived by spiritual vision, a vision which is also a transfiguration of the whole being; it is the direct and intuitive vision of the spiritual reality hidden in the letter, the unveiling of the true meaning. The discipline that enables us to attain this unveiling, this hermeneutic, is ta'wīl."

**Original terms**: *ẓāhir* — exterior, apparent, exoteric sense; *bāṭin* — interior, hidden, esoteric sense; *ta'wīl* — spiritual hermeneutic, the return of a thing to its origin and deeper meaning; *Sharī'at* — the religious law, the exoteric form of revelation.

**Analysis**: This is the hermeneutic key to the entire companion. Esotericism here does not mean secret knowledge for the initiated few. It means the recognition that reality has depth — that every phenomenon has an outer face and an inner meaning. The error is to mistake the surface for the totality.

**Cross-references**: The ẓāhir/bāṭin distinction governs every major concept in the companion: the world is theophany (Essay 4), imagination is perception of the inner meaning of things (Essay 5), prayer is the inner dialogue of the soul with God (Essay 6), the divine form is the inner reality of theophanic vision (Essay 7).

## Passage 1.7 — Ta'wīl as Spiritual Hermeneutic

**Source**: Introduction §3

**Context**: Corbin defines ta'wīl as the method by which esoteric meaning is uncovered.

**Full passage**:

"This 'return' to the origin, the source, the true meaning — which is also the truth of one's own being — is the operation of ta'wīl. Ta'wīl is, etymologically, to lead something back to its origin, to its archetype. It is the hermeneutic that consists in leading the literal and apparent meaning back to its hidden and spiritual meaning, the letter to the spirit. This is the essential method of esotericism. Ta'wīl is to the spiritual world what dialectic was to the world of the Greeks — the instrument by which the soul is led back to the truth of its own essence."

**Original terms**: *ta'wīl* — spiritual hermeneutic, the return to origin.

**Analysis**: Ta'wīl is not interpretation in the modern sense (imposing meaning on a text). It is a process of leading the soul back to the origin from which it came. The text, the world, and the self all have an outer form that must be returned to its inner truth.

**Cross-references**: Ta'wīl is the method that enables the Sophiology of Essay 3 (reading the human beloved as theophanic form), the cosmology of Essay 4 (reading the world as divine self-disclosure), and the prayer of Essay 6 (reading human speech as divine response).

## Passage 1.8 — The Symbol Is Not Allegory

**Source**: Introduction §3

**Context**: Corbin distinguishes the symbol from allegory — the symbol participates in what it reveals, while allegory only points to it.

**Full passage**:

"It is essential to distinguish between symbol and allegory. Allegory is a rational operation; it consists in the translation, by means of more or less appropriate transpositions, of an abstract idea into a concrete image. It is a weakening of the idea, a concession to the weakness of the understanding. Whereas the symbol corresponds to a mode of being that is inaccessible otherwise than through the symbol itself; it is the manifestation of a reality that would not be accessible without this manifestation. The symbol is not a sign that points away from itself to something else — it is the presence of what it reveals."

**Original terms**: *ramz* — symbol; *ta'mith* — allegorization.

**Analysis**: This is one of the most important passages in the entire Introduction. The symbol is not a literary device — it is an ontological event. Something becomes present through the symbol that could not be present any other way. This is the basis for understanding theophany, the imaginal world, and the divine form.

**Cross-references**: The symbol/allegory distinction is presupposed by Essay 3 (Nizām is a symbol, not an allegory, of Sophia), Essay 5 (the imagination perceives symbols, not fantasies), and Essay 7 (the divine form is a symbol, not an idol).

## Passage 1.9 — The Active Imagination as the Organ of the Imago

**Source**: Introduction (concluding passage)

**Context**: Corbin concludes the Introduction by laying out the thesis of Part Two.

**Full passage**:

"But the organ of theophanic perception, that is, of the perception through which the encounter between Heaven and Earth in the mid-zone, the 'ālam al-mithāl, takes place, is the active Imagination. It is the active Imagination which invests the earthly Beloved with his 'theophanic function'; it is essentially a theophanic Imagination and, as such, a creative Imagination, because Creation is itself theophany and theophanic Imagination. From this idea of Creation as theophany arises the idea of a sophiology, the figure of Sophia aeterna as she appears in the theosophy of Ibn 'Arabi."

**Original terms**: *khayāl* — imagination (in the technical sense, not fantasy); *'ālam al-mithāl* — the imaginal world; *theophanic function* — the capacity of a finite form to manifest the divine.

**Analysis**: Corbin ties together the entire Introduction in this passage: the threefold world requires the active Imagination as its organ of perception; this Imagination is theophanic (it perceives divine self-disclosure) and creative (it participates in the theophany it perceives); and from this arises the sophiology of Part One.

**Cross-references**: This is the bridge to every subsequent essay. Essay 2 develops the divine longing that drives theophany. Essay 3 shows theophany through beauty. Essay 4 develops creation as theophany. Essay 5 develops the organ of theophanic perception.

## Passage 1.10 — The Disciples of Khiḍr

**Source**: Introduction §2

**Context**: Corbin introduces the figure of Khiḍr, the mysterious prophet who initiates into esoteric knowledge.

**Full passage**:

"The figure of Khiḍr, the 'Green One,' the mysterious prophet who initiates those who are worthy into the secret knowledge, haunts the spirituality of Islam. He is the 'invisible guide' who appears at the moment of need, who leads the soul beyond the letter of the law to the living spring of life. To be a disciple of Khiḍr is to belong to that spiritual lineage which transcends all institutional forms, all dogmatic formulations, all confessional boundaries. It is to seek knowledge not from books alone but from the direct encounter with the living source. Ibn 'Arabi is pre-eminently a disciple of Khiḍr."

**Original terms**: *Khiḍr* — the Green One, the immortal prophet-guide; *walāyah* — spiritual initiation, the transmission of esoteric knowledge through personal encounter rather than institutional succession.

**Analysis**: Khiḍr is the archetype of the spiritual master who initiates outside the established channels. To be a disciple of Khiḍr is to seek knowledge through personal spiritual encounter rather than through conformity to established authority. This is the spiritual type that Ibn 'Arabi represents.

**Cross-references**: The Khiḍr figure connects to Essay 3 (the encounter with Nizām as a khiḍr-like theophanic meeting), Essay 6 (prayer as personal relation that transcends institutional forms), and Essay 7 (vision as direct encounter that cannot be fixed by dogma).

## Passage 1.11 — The Burning Bush and the Active Imagination as Prophetic Hermeneutics

**Source**: Introduction §3 — "The Situation of Esotericism"

**Context**: Corbin explains that esotericism requires a specific organ of perception — the active Imagination — and gives the Burning Bush as the paradigm case.

**Full passage**:

"The intelligentia spiritualis brings about the union between prophetic religion and mystic religion. From this complex derives a threefold preoccupation with the method, organ, and source of this hermeneutics. ... For the organ which the spiritual perception of symbols presupposes, it motivates the most characteristic chapters of Shi'ite and of Sufi theosophy, dealing with themes that can be subsumed under the title 'prophetic psychology.'"

"Unlike common knowledge, which is effected by a penetration of the sense impressions of the outside world into the interior of the soul, the work of prophetic inspiration is a projection of the inner soul upon the outside world. The active Imagination guides, anticipates, molds sense perception; that is why it transmutes sensory data into symbols. The Burning Bush is only a brushwood fire if it is merely perceived by the sensory organs. In order that Moses may perceive the Burning Bush and hear the Voice calling him 'from the right side of the valley' — in short, in order that there may be a theophany — an organ of trans-sensory perception is needed."

"This theophanic perception is accomplished in the 'ālam al-mithāl, whose organ is the theophanic Imagination. ... Since the Imagination is the organ of theophanic perception, it is also the organ of prophetic hermeneutics, for it is the imagination which is at all times capable of transmuting sensory data into symbols and external events into symbolic histories. Thus the affirmation of an esoteric meaning presupposes a prophetic hermeneutics; and this hermeneutics postulates an organ capable of perceiving theophanies, of investing visible figures with a 'theophanic function.' This organ is the active Imagination."

**Original terms**: *intelligentia spiritualis* — spiritual understanding, the faculty of esoteric hermeneutics; *ta'wīl* — spiritual hermeneutic, the return to origin; *'ālam al-mithāl* — the imaginal world.

**Analysis**: This passage ties together the entire Introduction. Esotericism requires a method (ta'wīl), an organ (the active Imagination), and a source (the prophetic revelation). The Burning Bush is the paradigm: without the organ of trans-sensory perception, there is no theophany — only a brushwood fire. The Imagination is not decorative; it is constitutive of revelation itself.

**Cross-references**: This is the direct bridge to Essay 5 (the heart as the organ of theophanic perception) and Essay 7 (vision as the consummation of theophanic perception). The Burning Bush example also connects to Essay 3 (Nizām as theophanic form — without theophanic apperception, she is "only" a woman).

## Passage 1.12 — The Norm of Their Own Orthodoxy

**Source**: Introduction §1

**Context**: Corbin introduces Ibn 'Arabi as a spiritual individual who cannot be understood through ordinary categories.

**Full passage**:

"Ibn 'Arabi is one of those powerful and rare spiritual individuals who are the norm of their own orthodoxy and of their own time, because they belong neither to what is commonly called 'their' time nor to the orthodoxy of 'their' time. What by a historical convention is termed 'their' time is not really their time. Accordingly, to affect to believe that such masters are nothing more than representatives of a certain 'tradition' is to forget their considerable personal contribution, is to neglect the perfect assurance with which an Arab of Andalusia like Ibn 'Arabi, or Iranians like Abū Ya'qāb Sejestānī, Suhrawardī, Semnānī, Mullā Ṣadrā of Shīrāz proclaim that such and such an idea, developed on such and such a page of their books, can be found nowhere else, because it is their discovery of their personal experience."

**Original terms**: *taḥqīq* — personal verification, the spiritual realization of truth through experience rather than received authority.

**Analysis**: Corbin establishes from the first pages that Ibn 'Arabi cannot be reduced to his historical context. He is not a "representative" of a tradition but a norm unto himself — a spiritual individual whose authority comes from personal realization, not institutional position.

**Cross-references**: This establishes the personal/spiritual authority theme that reappears in Essay 6 (prayer as personal relation) and Essay 7 (vision as personal encounter).

## Passage 1.13 — The Imagination as Theophanic, Not Fantasy

**Source**: Introduction §1

**Context**: Corbin directly addresses the tension between spirituality and imagination — and explains why this Imagination is not fantasy but theophany.

**Full passage**:

"We shall try to show in what sense this Imagination is creative: because it is essentially the active Imagination and because its activity defines it essentially as a theophanic Imagination. It assumes an unparalleled function, so out of keeping with the inoffensive or pejorative view commonly taken of the 'imagination,' that we might have preferred to designate this Imagination by a neologism and have occasionally employed the term Imaginatrix. ... Here, however, we shall be dealing with an effort to utilize the image and the Imagination for spiritual experience. The inner, structural reasons for this will become apparent when we consider the themes themselves; they are already foreshadowed by the belief in the existence and ontological consistency of an intermediate world."

**Original terms**: *Imaginatrix* — Corbin's neologism for the active, theophanic Imagination; *mundus imaginalis* — the intermediate world that gives the Imagination its ontological ground.

**Analysis**: Corbin is aware that his use of "Imagination" will be misunderstood. He coins "Imaginatrix" to signal that this is not the passive, reproductive imagination of modern psychology but an active, creative, theophanic power. The justification is ontological: the intermediate world is real, and the Imagination is its organ.

**Cross-references**: This passage directly prepares Essay 5 (the heart as the organ of this Imagination) and connects to the entire Part Two project.
