# Theurgy Is Not Coercion

Porphyry's most damaging objection to theurgy is simple: if the gods are superior beings, and if theurgy involves invoking them and receiving responses, it looks like the human operator is commanding the god. This would collapse the hierarchy that Iamblichus has spent the entire work establishing.

"It very much perplexes me to understand how superior beings, when invoked, are commanded by those that invoke them, as if they were their inferiors."

The objection must be allowed to bite before it can be answered. If theurgy means human beings ordering gods around, then everything Iamblichus has said about the hierarchy of beings is false.

But Iamblichus answers by distinguishing the twofold character of the priest:

"In all theurgical operations the priest sustains a twofold character; one, indeed, as man, and which preserves the order possessed by our nature in the universe; but the other, which is corroborated by divine signs, and through these is conjoined to more excellent natures, and is elevated to their order by an elegant circumduction, this is deservedly capable of being surrounded with the external form of the Gods. Conformably, therefore, to a difference of this kind, the priest very properly invokes, as more excellent natures, the powers derived from the universe, so far as he who invokes is a man; and again, he commands these powers, because through arcane symbols, he, in a certain respect, is invested with the sacred form of the Gods."

The objection is not answered by denying that ritual has power. It is answered by showing that the power is not the priest's own.

Iamblichus then distinguishes the kinds of powers that can legitimately be commanded — the lower, irrational daemons that are "without judgment, and allotted only one power." These are not gods but blind natural forces, closer to physical functions than to conscious beings:

"As therefore, it is the province of a sword to cut, and to do nothing else than this, thus also of the spirits which are distributed in the universe, according to the partible necessity of nature, one kind divides, but another collects, things which are generated. ... He, therefore, who turns from their natural course things which contribute to the universe in an orderly manner, and illegitimately performs a certain thing, in this case receives the injury arising from that which he uses badly."

The same principle applies to human reason commanding these lower spirits:

"For it happens that spirits are commanded who do not use a reason of their own, and have not the principle of judgment. Nor does this occur irrationally. For our dianoia naturally possessing the power of reasoning about and judging of things as they are, and comprehending in itself many powers of life, is accustomed to command the most irrational spirits, and such as derive their perfection from one energy alone. Hence, it invokes these as more excellent natures, because it endeavours to attract to particulars from the whole world, in which we are contained, things which contribute to wholes. And it commands them as inferior natures, because frequently certain parts of things in the world [such as our reasoning power] are more pure and perfect than things which extend themselves to the whole world."

This is not coercion of the gods. It is the proper use of reason over blind natural forces — a very different operation.

---

Iamblichus' first answer is a directional reversal.

The vulgar view says: ritual drags gods down. Iamblichus says: true invocation raises humans up. Invocations elevate and purify the soul, liberating it from generation and joining it to divine principle. Invocation is successful not because the god is moved by human force but because the soul is reconfigured toward the divine.

This reversal is the key to the entire defence. The theurgist does not command the god. The theurgist purifies himself so that the god can approach. The agency is not human but divine.

---

Iamblichus then addresses the problem of threatening language in ritual texts.

"The theurgist, through the power of arcane signatures, commands mundane natures, no longer as man, nor as employing a human soul; but as existing superior to them in the order of the Gods, he makes use of greater mandates than pertain to himself, so far as he is human. This, however, does not take place as if he effected every thing which he vehemently threatens to accomplish; but he teaches us by such a use of words the magnitude and quality of the power which he possesses through a union with the Gods, and which he obtains from the knowledge of arcane symbols."

But where threat-language does appear, it is never directed at the gods themselves:

"No one, however, threatens the Gods, nor is such a mode of invocation addressed to them. Hence with the Chaldeans, by whom words used to the Gods alone are preserved distinct and pure, no threats are employed. But the Egyptians, mingling dæmoniacal words with divine signatures, sometimes employ threats."

*Οὐδεὶς ἀπειλεῖ τοῖς θεοῖς.*

Blunt and clear. If threat-language exists in ritual texts, it applies to lower, divided, irrational powers — not to gods or the highest orders. This preserves the hierarchy. The gods are never threatened because they cannot be coerced.

The crucial passage is the transformation of the theurgist's agency:

"The theurgist, through the power of arcane signatures, commands mundane natures, no longer as man, nor as employing a human soul; but as existing superior to them in the order of the Gods, he makes use of greater mandates than pertain to himself, so far as he is human."

*Οὐκέτι ὡς ἄνθρωπος, οὐδὲ χρώμενος ψυχῇ ἀνθρωπίνῃ.*

The priest does not command as Tom, or as a powerful magician, or as an egoic agent. He acts through divine order, signs, and union. His ritual authority is not personal.

As human: inferior. As theurgist: participating in divine symbols. As united to gods: acting from a higher order.

This is the difference between theurgy and goetia. Theurgy is not human power over gods. It is divine order acting through a prepared human and its own symbols.

Ritual power comes "through a union with the Gods" and knowledge of "arcane symbols." Again: the power is not egoic. It is derivative. The theurgist does not generate power. He participates in a power that comes from above.

---

The central question is not "does ritual have power?" but "whose power is operating?" If the power is the theurgist's own, then theurgy is coercion. If the power is divine, operating through the theurgist, then theurgy is participation.

The priest who acts in theurgy acts "no longer as man." He has been taken up into the divine order. His words are not his own. His actions are not his own. His power is not his own. He has become a vehicle for divine operation.

This is why theurgy cannot be reduced to magic in the modern sense. Magic assumes a human operator who manipulates forces. Theurgy assumes a divine operator who uses a human instrument. The difference is absolute.

The distinction between theurgy and goetia is essential for understanding Iamblichus' project. Goetia attempts to manipulate spiritual powers for human ends. Theurgy submits human agency to divine order. Goetia treats the gods as resources. Theurgy treats them as sources.

This distinction is not always visible from the outside. A goetic rite and a theurgic rite may look similar. They may use similar words, similar gestures, similar materials. The difference is not in the external form but in the internal orientation.

The goetic practitioner approaches the rite as a technique for producing results. The theurgic practitioner approaches it as a participation in divine order. The goetic practitioner trusts the technique. The theurgic practitioner trusts the god.

This is why Iamblichus insists that the theurgist acts "no longer as man." The personal ego of the practitioner must be suspended for the divine operation to occur. As long as the practitioner is acting from his own will, his own desire, his own power, the rite remains at the human level. It is only when the practitioner's will is taken up into the divine will that the rite becomes theurgic.

The transformation is not automatic. It requires purification, preparation, and the assistance of the divine order itself. The theurgist cannot simply decide to act "no longer as man." He must become someone who can be taken up into divine operation.

The transformation of agency that occurs in theurgy is not a metaphor. The theurgist who acts "no longer as man" is not pretending to be something he is not. He has been taken up into a higher mode of operation, in which his ordinary human faculties are suspended and replaced by divine operation.

This is why theurgy cannot be learned from books. Books can describe the rites, the words, the symbols. But the transformation of agency requires more than knowledge. It requires participation. It requires the active presence of the divine order.

The theurgist must be initiated. He must receive the transmission of the tradition. He must be purified and prepared. The transformation does not happen automatically when the right words are spoken. It happens when the divine order takes up the theurgist into its own operation.

This is why Iamblichus insists on the authority of tradition. The theurgist does not invent his practice. He receives it from those who have gone before him, who have received it from those who went before them, back to the original revelation that established the tradition.

The tradition is the vehicle of the transmission. It carries the power that transforms ordinary human agency into theurgic operation. The theurgist who stands within the tradition stands within that transmission. He is connected to the source from which the power flows.

The charge that theurgy is coercion arises from a misunderstanding of what theurgy actually is. The critic sees the external form of the rite — words spoken, gestures performed, materials offered — and assumes that the theurgist is trying to manipulate the gods through these actions.

But the external form is not the essence of theurgy. The essence is the transformation of the theurgist's agency from human to divine. The words, gestures, and materials are the means by which this transformation is accomplished, not the means by which the gods are commanded.

This is why the same external action can be either theurgy or goetia depending on the inner orientation of the practitioner. If the practitioner is acting from his own will, the action is goetia. If the practitioner's will has been taken up into the divine will, the same action is theurgy.

The distinction is not visible from the outside. It can only be known from within, through the transformation of the practitioner's own consciousness.

This is why Iamblichus insists on the necessity of purification and preparation. The theurgist must become someone who can be taken up into the divine order. He must purify his will, his desire, his intention. He must prepare himself to act "no longer as man" but as a vehicle for divine operation.

The preparation is not optional. It is the condition of theurgy.

Iamblichus' defence of theurgy against the charge of coercion rests on a single principle: the theurgist does not act from his own power. The power that operates in theurgy is divine. The theurgist is the instrument through which the power flows, not the origin from which it proceeds.

This principle has two consequences. First, it removes the scandal of command. The theurgist does not command the gods because the theurgist is not acting as a human agent. He is acting as a vehicle for divine operation. The command, if it can be called that, is not a human command but a divine one.

Second, it establishes the necessity of purification. The theurgist cannot act as a vehicle for divine operation unless he is purified. The purification removes the obstacles — personal will, personal desire, personal ambition — that would prevent the divine from operating through him.

The purification is not a moral achievement. It is a technical requirement. The theurgist does not become a better person in order to be rewarded by the gods. He becomes a more transparent instrument so that the divine can operate through him without distortion.

This is why theurgy requires discipline. The theurgist must learn to set aside his own will, his own desires, his own agenda. He must become someone who can be taken up into the divine order without resistance.

The transformation is not easy. It requires years of practice, guidance, and purification. But it is the condition of theurgy.

Iamblichus extends this distinction to the problem of evil and false appearances in ritual. Evil daemons can imitate the form of good ones, and the theurgist must be able to distinguish them:

"if what we a little before said, concerning images and evil dæmons, who assume the appearance of Gods and good dæmons, is true, an abundant evil-producing tribe, about which a contrariety of this kind usually happens, will from hence appear to flow. For an evil dæmon requires that his worshipper should be just, because he assumes the appearance of one belonging to the divine genus; but he is subservient to what is unjust, because he is depraved."

The criterion is not the external form of the rite but the source from which the power flows — and the effect it produces:

"in things just and unjust, the beautiful and the just are to be alone ascribed to Gods and good dæmons; but such dæmons as are naturally depraved, perpetrate what is unjust and base. And that, indeed, which consents and accords with itself, and always subsists with invariable sameness, pertains to more excellent natures; but that which is hostile to itself, which is discordant, and never the same, is the peculiarity in the most eminent degree of dæmoniacal dissension."

The distinction between theurgy and goetia is not always visible from the outside. A theurgic rite and a goetic rite may use similar words, similar gestures, similar materials. The difference is not in the external form but in the internal disposition of the practitioner and in the source from which the power flows.

The theurgic practitioner acts from divine union. The goetic practitioner acts from personal will. The theurgic practitioner seeks to participate in the divine order. The goetic practitioner seeks to manipulate the divine order for personal ends.

The similarity of external forms is a source of confusion. It leads critics to assume that theurgy is just a more respectable form of magic. Iamblichus insists that the difference is not one of respectability but of ontology. Theurgy and goetia are different operations because they proceed from different sources.

The source of theurgy is the divine order itself. The source of goetia is the human will operating independently. The two cannot be reduced to each other because their sources are irreducibly different.

This is why Iamblichus spends so much effort on the transformation of the theurgist's agency. The theurgist who acts "no longer as man" has crossed a threshold that separates theurgy from every form of human magic. He no longer operates from his own power. He has been taken up into the divine operation.

The transformation is the guarantee that theurgy is not coercion. A power that comes from the gods cannot coerce the gods. It can only manifest their will.

The charge of coercion arises from a misunderstanding of what invocation is. The critic assumes that invocation is a technique for getting the gods to do what the human operator wants. But invocation is not a technique. It is a relationship.

The relationship is not between a human subject and a divine object. It is between a soul that is already in relation to the gods and the gods who are already present to the soul. Invocation makes this relationship explicit. It does not create it.

This is why Iamblichus grounds theurgy in the innate knowledge of the gods. The soul that invokes the gods does not call them from a distance. It calls them from within the relation that already exists. Invocation is the expression of that relation, not the creation of it.

The critic who sees invocation as coercion is looking at the relationship from outside. He sees a human being speaking words and assumes that the words are intended to manipulate. But from inside the relationship, the words are not manipulation. They are communication.

The theurgist who invokes the gods is not trying to get something from them. He is expressing his relation to them. The response he receives is not a product of his technique but a gift of their presence.

The difference between theurgy and magic is the difference between relationship and technique. Theurgy is relationship. Magic is technique. The theurgist relates. The magician manipulates. The distinction is absolute.

The transformation of the theurgist's agency is the central mystery of theurgy. How can a human being act "no longer as man"? How can the personal ego be suspended and replaced by divine operation?

Iamblichus' answer is that the transformation is achieved through participation. The theurgist participates in the divine order through the rites, the symbols, and the names that the tradition provides. This participation gradually transforms the theurgist's mode of operation.

At first, the theurgist acts as a human being performing rites. He speaks the words, makes the gestures, offers the materials. The action is his own.

As he continues, something begins to change. The words begin to speak themselves through him. The gestures begin to move through him. The offering begins to offer itself through him.

At the highest stage, the theurgist no longer acts as a separate agent. He has been taken up into the divine operation. The god acts through him. His agency has been transformed.

This transformation is not the result of effort. It is the result of participation. The theurgist does not make himself divine. He allows the divine to act through him.

The transformation is the gift of the gods, not the achievement of the theurgist. The theurgist prepares. The gods complete.

The defence of theurgy against the charge of coercion is essential for the coherence of the entire theurgic project. If theurgy were coercion, it would violate the hierarchy that makes it possible. The gods cannot be coerced because they are superior. The theurgist cannot coerce because he acts "no longer as man."

The defence preserves the hierarchy. The gods remain superior. The theurgist remains human, but his agency is taken up into the divine order. The hierarchy is not violated. It is fulfilled.

This is why the defence of theurgy is not an afterthought in Iamblichus' system. It is the condition that makes the system coherent. Without it, theurgy would be a contradiction: lower beings commanding higher ones. With it, theurgy is participation: lower beings being taken up into the operation of higher ones.

The theurgist who understands this can practice with confidence. He knows that his practice does not violate the order of reality. He knows that his practice fulfils that order.

The charge of coercion is answered not by argument alone but by the transformation of the theurgist's agency. The theurgist who acts "no longer as man" is the living proof that theurgy is not coercion. His transformed agency is the evidence.

Iamblichus' Book IV provides the argument. The theurgist's practice provides the proof.

Theurgy is the art of divine operation. It is not something the human being does alone. It is something the divine does through the human being who has been prepared to receive it.

This is why the theurgist must be purified. The purification removes the obstacles that would prevent the divine from operating through him. The purified theurgist is a transparent instrument through which the divine can act.

The purification is not a moral achievement. It is a technical requirement. The theurgist does not become a better person in the moral sense. He becomes a more effective instrument in the theurgic sense.

The effectiveness of theurgy does not depend on the theurgist's personal qualities but on his capacity to be taken up into the divine operation. This capacity is developed through practice, guidance, and the grace of the gods.

Iamblichus' Book IV describes this capacity and how it is developed. It explains why theurgy is not coercion and how the theurgist's agency is transformed.

The theurgist who understands this can practice with confidence. He knows that his practice is not a violation of the hierarchy but its fulfilment. He knows that he is not commanding the gods but participating in their order.

This knowledge is the theurgist's protection against the charge of coercion. It is also his protection against the temptation to use theurgy for personal power.

Iamblichus' defence of theurgy is not only for external critics. It is also for the practitioner himself. The theurgist must know that what he is doing is legitimate. He must know that he is not engaging in coercion or manipulation. He must know that his practice is a form of participation, not a technique of control.

This knowledge protects the theurgist from the corruptions that can attend spiritual practice. The theurgist who thinks he is commanding the gods will become arrogant. The theurgist who thinks he is manipulating spiritual forces will become manipulative. The theurgist who knows he is participating will remain humble.

The defence of theurgy is therefore a safeguard for the practitioner's soul. It keeps him oriented correctly. It reminds him that the power is not his own but the gods'.

Iamblichus' Book IV provides this defence. It answers the objections of the critics and protects the integrity of the practitioner.

The theurgist who understands this defence can practice with confidence and humility. He knows that his practice is legitimate and that his power is derivative. He acts "no longer as man" but as a vehicle for divine operation.

This is the theurgic path as Iamblichus describes it: a path of participation, purification, and ultimately union with the gods. It is not a path of power but a path of love.

Theurgy is the art of divine participation. It is not something the human being accomplishes alone. It is something the divine accomplishes through the human being who has been prepared.

This is why theurgy cannot be reduced to technique. Technique can prepare the theurgist. It can provide the forms, the words, the gestures. But the power that operates through these forms is not produced by them. It comes from the gods.

The theurgist who understands this will not trust in technique alone. He will trust in the gods. He will use the techniques faithfully but will know that the power is not his own.

This trust is not passivity. The theurgist must act. He must speak the words, make the gestures, offer the materials. But his action is not the source of the power. His action is the means by which he opens himself to the power that comes from above.

Iamblichus' Book IV describes this paradoxical combination of action and receptivity. The theurgist acts, yet the power is not his own. He speaks, yet the words are not his own. He offers, yet the gift is not his own.

The theurgist who understands this paradox will practice with confidence. He will not be paralysed by the thought that his action is insufficient. He will act, knowing that the gods will complete what he begins.

The transformation of agency in theurgy occurs through specific means. The theurgist does not simply decide to act "no longer as man." He uses the arcane symbols that the tradition provides — symbols that carry divine power and prepare the soul for divine operation.

These symbols are not arbitrary. They are forms that have been transmitted through the tradition because they participate in the realities they represent. The theurgist who uses them is not inventing a technique. He is receiving a gift.

The symbols include sacred names, geometric figures, material substances, and ritual gestures. Each symbol has a specific power. Each prepares a specific aspect of the soul for divine operation.

The sacred name invokes the presence of the god. The geometric figure configures the space for divine reception. The material substance provides a physical anchor for divine power. The ritual gesture aligns the body with the divine order.

Together, these symbols constitute the technology of theurgic transformation. They are not the power itself, but they are the means by which the soul is opened to the power.

The theurgist who uses these symbols with understanding will find that his agency gradually transforms. What begins as a deliberate performance becomes a spontaneous operation. What begins as human action becomes divine action.

This transformation is the answer to the charge of coercion. The theurgist does not command the gods because the theurgist is no longer acting as a separate agent. His will has been taken up into the divine will.

The symbols are the instruments of this transformation. Tradition transmits them. The theurgist uses them. The gods accomplish the transformation.
