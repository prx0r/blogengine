**Length tier**: Medium (325 lines)

## Passage 1.1 — Hermes and Sacred Language

**Source**: *On the Mysteries* I.1

**Context**: Porphyry's letter asked about the nature of the gods, daemons, divination, dreams, sacrifice, theurgy, sacred names, fate, and the personal daemon. His stance was that of a philosopher examining these phenomena from outside, applying reason to tradition. Iamblichus' response begins not with an argument but with an invocation of Hermes, the god of language.

**Iamblichus' own words**: "Hermes, the God who presides over language, was formerly very properly considered as common to all priests; and the power who presides over the true science concerning the Gods is one and the same in the whole of things."

**Greek key terms**: Ἑρμῆς (Hermes), λόγος (logos/logos — language, reason, account), ἱερατική (hieratikē — priestly science), θεολογία (theologia — theology/the science of divine things).

**Analysis**: The book begins with language because the entire work will depend on names, invocations, symbols, sacred speech, "barbarous" names, and the impossibility of translating sacred names without loss. Hermes marks the threshold between ordinary discourse and operative divine language. This is not decorative mythology — it is epistemological placement. The first god named is not a creator or a saviour but the god of communication itself, because the question of how to speak about the gods is the first question that must be answered.

Hermes is the god who presides over all priestly language. The science of divine things — theology in the ancient sense, not the academic discipline — is one and the same through the whole of things. This is a claim about the unity of sacred knowledge across cultures and traditions. The same science that the Egyptian priests possess is the science that Hermes governs.

**Cross-references**: This passage directly prepares Essay 7 (Mud, Lotus, Sacred Names), where Iamblichus discusses the power of barbarous names and the impossibility of translating sacred names without loss of power. The Hermes of Book I returns in Book VII when Iamblichus defends the untranslatability of sacred language. The opening is not a single gesture but the first appearance of a theme that recurs throughout.

**Essay use**: Opening. Establishes that the work begins not with doctrine but with the conditions of sacred speech. The question is not "what are the gods?" but "how can we speak about them?"

## Passage 1.2 — The Priestly Persona

**Source**: *On the Mysteries* I.1

**Context**: Having invoked Hermes, Iamblichus must establish who is speaking. He does not write in his own name as a philosopher. He writes as Abammon, an Egyptian prophet.

**Iamblichus' own words**: "Admit it to be me who discourses with you in writing, or some other prophet of the Egyptians, for this is of no consequence."

**Greek key terms**: προφήτης (prophētēs — prophet, spokesman, interpreter), Αβάμμων (Abammon), Αἰγύπτιος (Aigyptios — Egyptian).

**Analysis**: The identity of the speaker is less important than the level of speech. Iamblichus answers through a priestly mask — Abammon — not as a philosopher speaking from a neutral academic nowhere. The response must come from within the sacred tradition. This is not literary play but epistemological placement. Porphyry wrote as a philosopher standing outside the tradition. Iamblichus answers from inside the tradition, speaking as one who participates in what he describes.

The phrase "or some other prophet of the Egyptians" is telling. The specific individual does not matter. What matters is that the answer comes from the priestly lineage, from the tradition of Egyptian prophecy. The speaker is a placeholder for the tradition itself.

**Cross-references**: This establishes the speaking position that all subsequent essays inherit. When Essays 2-8 discuss hierarchy, epiphany, divination, sacrifice, symbols, and union, they do so from within the tradition, not as external observers. The critique of external standpoints (e.g., the philosopher who examines religion as an object) is implicit in every essay.

**Essay use**: Method. Establishes the speaking position from which the rest of the work proceeds.

## Passage 1.3 — The Method Rule

**Source**: *On the Mysteries* I.2

**Context**: Having established who speaks, Iamblichus states the rule for how the questions will be answered.

**Iamblichus' own words**: "Such questions, indeed, as are theological, we shall answer theologically; such as are theurgic, theurgically; but such as are philosophical, we shall, in conjunction with you, philosophically explore."

**Greek key terms**: θεολογικῶς (theologikōs — theologically), θεουργικῶς (theourgikōs — theurgically), φιλοσοφικῶς (philosophikōs — philosophically).

**Analysis**: This is the controlling passage for the whole companion. Bad readings of *On the Mysteries* collapse everything into one lens: psychology reduces rites to inner states; anthropology reduces them to cultural performance; occultism treats them as techniques for power; philosophy treats them as metaphysical symbols; Jungianism treats gods as archetypes; rationalism treats everything as superstition.

Iamblichus' own method is plural but ordered. Theological things require theology. Theurgic things require theurgy. Philosophical things require philosophy. The method must fit the subject. He does not reject philosophy — he assigns it to its proper level. But he refuses to let philosophy police the entire sacred field.

**Cross-references**: The method rule governs every subsequent essay. Essay 2 (hierarchy) is primarily theological. Essay 3 (epiphany) is phenomenological/theological. Essay 4 (divination) is theological with natural-philosophical elements. Essay 5 (theurgy) is practical and defensive. Essay 6 (sacrifice) is cosmological. Essay 7 (symbols) is symbolic/theological. Essay 8 (fate/daemon/union) is synthetic. None reduces the sacred to the secular. None mistakes the instrument for the source.

**Essay use**: Core — the method that governs every subsequent essay.

## Passage 1.4 — Innate Knowledge of the Gods

**Source**: *On the Mysteries* I.3

**Context**: Porphyry's letter had asked as if the existence of the gods were a hypothesis to be tested. Iamblichus responds with a statement about the nature of the soul itself.

**Iamblichus' own words**: "An innate knowledge of the Gods is co-existent with our very essence... prior to reason and demonstration."

**Greek key terms**: ἔμφυτος γνῶσις (emphyton gnōsis — innate knowledge), συνυπάρχουσα (synyparchousa — co-existing), οὐσία (ousia — essence), πρὸ λόγου (pro logou — before reason), πρὸ ἀποδείξεως (pro apodeixeōs — before demonstration).

**Analysis**: This is the first doctrinal bomb of the work. The gods are not a proposition waiting for argumentative permission. The soul is already in relation to divinity before proof. The relation is co-existent with the soul's very essence — not acquired through reasoning, not demonstrated through argument, but present from the beginning.

The gods are not a conclusion we reach after thinking. They are a presence we discover when we stop thinking in the mode of detachment and begin to think from within participation. This is the foundation on which everything else in *On the Mysteries* rests. Theurgy works because the soul is already oriented toward the gods, already participating in their order, already held within the knowledge that precedes reason and demonstration.

**Cross-references**: This passage is presupposed by every other essay. Essay 2's hierarchy assumes the soul can know different levels of being. Essay 3's epiphanies assume the soul can recognise divine presence. Essay 4's divination assumes the soul can receive divine communication. Essay 6's sacrifice assumes the soul can be purified and returned to its source. Essay 8's union assumes the soul can ascend to the gods because it already participates in their order.

**Essay use**: Closing — the foundation on which everything else rests.

## Supplementary Passages

**Porphyry's lost letter**: Porphyry's letter to Anebo has not survived, but its contents can be reconstructed from Iamblichus' response. He asked: what distinguishes gods from daemons? How does divination work? Are divine dreams different from ordinary dreams? Does theurgy involve coercion? What do sacrifices accomplish? Why barbarous names? Can fate be escaped? What is the personal daemon? These questions map directly onto Essays 2-8.

**Hermes Trismegistus context**: Hermes was the legendary author of the Hermetic writings, believed by Renaissance Platonists to contain the ancient wisdom of Egypt. By opening with Hermes, Iamblichus claims continuity with a tradition that his readers would have recognised as authoritative.

**The priestly mask**: Iamblichus writes as "Abammon, master of the priests of Egypt." The name is not a disguise but a declaration. The answers come from within the priestly tradition, not from a philosopher who has studied it.

**Taylor translation notes**: Thomas Taylor's translation renders the method rule with a clarity that later translations sometimes lose. The three adverbs — "theologically," "theurgically," "philosophically" — preserve the Greek adverbial forms.

**Connections to later Platonism**: Proclus, Damascius, and the later Neoplatonic commentators all cite I.3's innate-knowledge passage as the foundation of their theology of religious knowledge.
