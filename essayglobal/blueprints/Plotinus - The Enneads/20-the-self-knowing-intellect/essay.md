# ennead 20: the self-knowing intellect

treatise 20 of the enneads: the self-knowing intellect
plotinus said the highest self-knowledge is not thinking about yourself in a mirror — it is being your own thinking, fully present to yourself without the gap between subject and object.

plotinus wrote this as treatise 20 of the enneads. here is what he said and why it still matters.

How does Intellect know itself? The question seems simple, but it contains a paradox. If knowing requires a distinction between knower and known, then self-knowledge would require Intellect to split itself into subject and object. But Intellect is simple. How can it know itself without ceasing to be itself?

> For Intellect to know itself, it cannot be divided into a thinker and a thought. It must be both at once, in a single act. Intellect's self-knowledge is not a turning of the mind upon itself as upon an object. It is the identity of thinker and thought.

*Ταὐτὸν νοῦς καὶ νοητά.* Intellect and the intelligible are the same. This identity is not a claim about consciousness but about the structure of reality. Intellect is not a subject that has objects. It is the act in which subject and object are one.

This is where Plotinus solves a paradox that still haunts philosophy: if the knower and the known are separate, how can knowledge ever be certain? Plotinus's answer: at the highest level, they are not separate.

Plotinus works through the paradox carefully:

> In fact, if it knows itself in this way, it will think itself as that which is being seen and not as that which sees, so that it will neither know all of itself nor know itself as a whole. For what it saw was what was seen; it did not see the seeing. And in this way, it will be something else, and not itself, that it is seeing.

> ...the seeing must be identical with the seen, that is, Intellect must be identical with the intelligible, because if they are not identical, there will not be truth, for that which possesses things different from Beings, will have an impression, which is not truth. The truth, therefore, should not be about something different from itself; rather, what it says is what it is. In this way, therefore, Intellect and that which is intelligible are one; and this is primary Being and indeed primary Intellect which has the Beings, or rather which is identical with them.

> If Intellect were to think itself as something other than itself, it would be thinking something else, not itself. Self-knowledge requires that the thinker be the thought. Intellect achieves this by being the content of its own thinking.

The human parallel: when you think about yourself, you are both the thinker and the thought. But human self-knowledge is incomplete — we always catch ourselves from behind, as it were. Intellect's self-knowledge is perfect, because Intellect is transparent to itself.

> Intellect does not need to look for itself. It does not need to discover itself. It is always already present to itself. Its self-knowledge is not an achievement but its very nature.

*Παρουσία.* Presence. Intellect is present to itself not as an object of reflection but as the very act of being aware. It does not think about itself after the fact. It is itself in the thinking.

Plotinus likens Intellect's self-knowledge to light seeing light:

> But there is, then, nothing of these things in the intelligible world; rather, seeing and the object of sight are together in it, and the object of sight is the same sort of thing as the seeing, and the seeing is the same sort of thing as the object of sight. Who, then, will say what sort of thing it is? The one who sees. But it is Intellect that sees. Even in the sensible world, seeing, since it is light or, rather, united with light, sees light, for it sees colours. But in the intelligible world, seeing is not by means of something different; rather, it is by means of itself, because it is not of what is outside it. Intellect thus sees one light with another, not by means of another. Light, therefore, sees another light; therefore, it sees itself.

> The One is beyond the distinction between knower and known — beyond the need for self-knowledge, exceeding any lack that knowledge would fill. The One does not need to know itself because it is not a self in the sense that we understand it. It is the source of all selves, all knowledge, all distinction.

Intellect knows itself. The One transcends self-knowledge — an excess of simplicity, not a defect. The One is too simple for self-knowledge. Intellect, being the first determination, the first distinction, is also the first self-knowledge.

Thinking itself requires this duality:

> Let us now say again — for the examination is 'not about some trivial matter' — and it should be reiterated that this Intellect needs to see itself, or rather that it has the seeing of itself, first by being a many, next by belonging to something else, and from necessity being capable of seeing, and of seeing that other thing, and its seeing is its substantiality. For seeing must be of some other thing that exists, and if that does not exist, seeing is in vain. So, it must be more than one, in order that there should be seeing, and the seeing must coincide with the seen, and that which is seen by it must be a multiplicity within a totality.

> If, though, something does not direct itself towards another, it will be stable. But when there is total stability, it will not think. So, that which thinks must, whenever it thinks, be a duality, and either one of these is outside the other or both are in the identical thing, and the intellection always of necessity involves difference and identity.

> To know oneself is to be Intellect. To be beyond self-knowledge is to be the One. The soul that reaches Intellect knows itself as Intellect. The soul that reaches the One passes beyond knowledge into a kind of touch — a presence that is not thought.

The path of return, then, is also a path of increasing self-knowledge. The soul that does not know itself is dispersed. The soul that knows itself as soul is still dependent on what is above it. The soul that knows itself as Intellect has reached its goal. And when it goes further, it passes beyond knowledge itself, into the simple presence that is the source of all knowing. Self-knowledge is not introspection. It is becoming what you are. And what you are, at the deepest level, is not a self that knows — but knowing itself.
