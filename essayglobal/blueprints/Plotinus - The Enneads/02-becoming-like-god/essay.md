# ennead 2: becoming like god

treatise 2 of the enneads: becoming like god
plotinus said the goal of life is not happiness and it is not virtue — it is becoming like god by becoming simple. the way up is the way in, and the way in is to strip everything away.

plotinus wrote this as treatise 2 of the enneads. here is what he said and why it still matters.

The goal of human life is assimilation to God — an act of becoming that makes the soul like what it contemplates, beyond mere happiness or worship.

> Since evil is here below and necessarily pervades this region, while the soul wants to flee from evil, it must flee from here. What, then, is this flight? It is becoming like God, as Plato says. Becoming like God is becoming just and holy, with the help of wisdom.

*Ὁμοίωσις θεῷ.* Becoming like God. The phrase is from the *Theaetetus*, but Plotinus gives it a precise metaphysical meaning. To become like God is not to imitate God's power or knowledge but to share in God's mode of being — to become simple, pure, self-sufficient, beyond the reach of passion.

> Virtue is the means of this assimilation. But not all virtue. The civic virtues — temperance, courage, practical wisdom — order the soul in relation to the body and to other human beings. They are necessary but not sufficient. They make the soul gentle and harmonious, but they do not make it divine.

*Πολιτικαὶ ἀρεταί.* Civic virtues. They are the foundation. They tame the passions, order the life, make the soul fit for society — the foundation of the path, with the goal lying beyond.

Plotinus describes the soul in its primal state before the virtues become necessary:

> The starting point for their evil is, then, audacity, generation, primary difference, and their willing that they belong to themselves. Since they appeared actually to take pleasure in their autonomy, and to have made much use of their self-motion, running in the opposite direction and getting as far away from home as possible, they came not to know even that they themselves were from the intelligible world. They were like children who at birth are separated from their fathers and, being raised for a long time far away, are ignorant both of themselves and of their fathers.

> Above the civic virtues are the purificatory virtues. These separate the soul from the body — not by harming the body but by refusing to share its passions, its fears, its desires. The purificatory virtues release the soul from attachment to the sensible world.

*Καθαρτικαὶ ἀρεταί.* Purificatory virtues. They relativize the body — they do not reject it. The soul learns that it is not the body, does not need the body to be itself, can live without the body's concerns.

> The purificatory virtues are not about behavior. They are about attention. The soul that practices purification stops attending to the body's voice and starts attending to its own. It gathers itself from dispersion, collects itself from the many places it has scattered itself.

The result is liberation — not asceticism. The soul that has purified itself can think, can love, can be — without the constant interference of bodily concerns.

> The purified soul is like the intelligible world: tranquil, self-sufficient, without need. It does not seek happiness. It is happiness. It does not strive for virtue. It is virtue — not as an achievement but as a nature.

And above the purificatory virtues are the paradigmatic virtues — the Forms of virtue in Intellect itself. These are what the soul becomes when it reaches Intellect — not virtues that the soul possesses.

> In Intellect, there are not virtuous souls. There is virtue itself — the Form of justice, the Form of wisdom, the Form of temperance. The soul that reaches Intellect does not practice virtue. It is virtue, because it has become a Form.

The path has three stages: the civic virtues harmonize the soul, the purificatory virtues liberate it, and the paradigmatic virtues deify it. At each stage, the soul becomes more like what it contemplates.

> If the soul is to become like God, it must become what God is: not this or that good thing but the Good itself. It must strip away everything that is not itself. It must become simple.

*Ἅπλωσις.* Simplification. The soul becomes one by stripping away the many. It lets go of attachments, opinions, passions, desires — not by fighting them but by outgrowing them. What remains is the soul in its simplicity, the soul as it was before it became entangled, the soul that is like the One because it has become one.

This is where Plotinus's genius becomes undeniable: the path to the highest is not through accumulating more but through becoming less — and in that lessness, you find everything.

This is the flight: a gathering of the self — not a movement through space. The soul does not go anywhere. It becomes what it always was. And what it always was is what it is trying to become: simple, free, whole. This is not escape. It is return — and the return is already happening the moment you stop clinging.
