# 06-contemplation-makes-the-world

**Territory**: III.8 (30) — On Nature, Contemplation, and the One

---

## Context: The Playful Preface

Plotinus opens by proposing a "game": that all things — not only rational beings but animals, plants, and the earth itself — aim at contemplation (θεωρία, *theoria*). The absurdity is deliberate. It forces us to see that production, action, and even play are forms of seeing.

> If, before attempting to be serious, we were actually to begin by playing and say that all things aim at contemplation and look to this goal, not only rational but also non-rational animals and nature in plants and the earth which produces them, and that all things achieve it as far as they can in their natural state, but contemplate and achieve it in different ways, and some in a genuine manner, others by acquiring an imitation and image of it, would anyone put up with the oddity of the statement? ... Are we, too, then contemplating right now when we are playing? In fact, both we and all who play are contemplating or at least desire this when we are playing. And, as it happens, whether it is a child or a man that plays or is serious, he is going to be playing or he is being serious for the sake of contemplation; and every action is going to involve a serious tendency to contemplation. (§1)

Greek key terms: **θεωρία** (*theoria*), **ἐνέργεια** (*energeia*), **τέλος** (*telos*)

---

## Context: Nature Speaks in Silence

Plotinus gives nature (φύσις, *physis*) a voice. This is one of the most famous passages in the Enneads: nature answers the question "why do you produce?" by telling us to be silent as she is silent. Her production is not an action but a vision that overflows.

> And if someone were to ask nature why it produces, if it were willing to listen and answer the questioner it would say, 'You should not ask but understand and fall silent yourself, as I am silent and not accustomed to speak. Understand what, then? That what comes to be is my vision, in my silence, an object of contemplation that comes to be by nature, and that since I come to be from this sort of contemplation, it is necessary for me to have a contemplation-loving nature. And my contemplating produces an object of contemplation, just as geometricians draw lines as they contemplate. But without my drawing, while I contemplate, the lines of bodies come to exist as though falling out of me. And my experience is the same as that of my mother and those who begat me. For they, too, are a result of contemplation and my birth has come about without them doing anything, but since they are greater expressed principles and contemplate themselves, I have come to be.' (§4)

Greek key terms: **φύσις** (*physis*), **σιωπή** (*siōpē*, silence), **θεώρημα** (*theōrēma*, object of contemplation), **λόγος** (*logos*, expressed principle)

---

## Context: Action as the Shadow of Contemplation

If nature's quiet vision is the paradigm, then action (πρᾶξις, *praxis*) is a symptom of contemplative weakness. We act when we cannot see. Action is the fallout of a gaze that could not hold its object.

> Human beings, too, when they are weak in contemplation, produce action as a shadow of contemplation and reason. For their faculty of contemplation is not adequate for them due to weakness of soul and being unable to grasp adequately the object of their vision and because of this not being filled [by it], yet still desirous of seeing it, they are carried towards action so that they can see [with their eyes] what they cannot see with their intellect. (§4)

> Indeed, everywhere we will find that production and action are a weakened form of contemplation or a consequence of contemplation; a weakness where a person has nothing in mind beyond what has been made, a consequence where he has something prior to this to contemplate which is superior to what has been produced. For why would anyone go after the image of what is genuine as their first choice, if he can contemplate what is genuine? And less intelligent children are also evidence of this; not being capable of study and theory, they turn to crafts and manual work. (§4)

Greek key terms: **πρᾶξις** (*praxis*), **ἀσθένεια** (*astheneia*, weakness), **σκιά** (*skia*, shadow), **θεωρία** (*theoria*)

---

## Context: The Goal of Action Is Still Contemplation

Even when we act, we are trying to see. Action circles back to vision, because the soul seeks to possess the good, and possession is vision.

> Action, therefore, is for the sake of contemplation and for an object of contemplation. And so, contemplation is the goal even for those who are acting, and what they are unable to obtain in a straight line, in a way, they seek to grasp by a circuitous route. For whenever they succeed in achieving the object of their desire, which they want to come to be, not because they want to be ignorant of it, but rather to know and see it present in their soul, in this case, it is clear that it lies there as something to be contemplated. ... Action, then, turns back again to contemplation. For what else could that be which someone receives in his soul, which is itself an expressed principle, than a silent expressed principle? And all the more silent the more [the soul possesses it within]. For then it holds its peace and seeks nothing since it has been filled. And contemplation in such a person lies within because he is confident in its possession. And as the confidence becomes clearer, the contemplation, too, becomes stiller. (§6)

Greek key terms: **ἡσυχία** (*hēsychia*, stillness), **λόγος** (*logos*), **πλήρωσις** (*plērōsis*, filling)

---

## Context: The Hierarchy of Contemplation — From Nature to Intellect

Contemplation intensifies as it ascends. Nature's silent vision gives way to Soul's discursive activity, then to Intellect's self-identity where the seer and the seen are one.

> This is our account of the matter. But when contemplation ascends from nature to Soul and from Soul to Intellect, the acts of contemplation are even more fully appropriated by, that is, more unified with, the contemplators. In the case of the virtuous person's soul, that which is known approaches becoming identical with the substrate which contemplates, inasmuch as it hastens to Intellect. In Intellect, it is clear that the two are already one not by appropriation, as in the case of the best soul, but in Substantiality because 'thinking and Being are identical'. (§8)

> This life, however, is clearer and is the primary Life and primary Intellect, and these are one. And so, the first life is intellection, and the second life is a second kind of intellection, and the last life is a final form of intellection. And so, all life is of this kind and is intellection. ... So, if the truest life is life with intellection, and this is identical with the truest intellection, then the truest intellection is alive, and contemplation and the object of the highest kind of contemplation are alive and are life, and the two are together one. (§8)

Greek key terms: **νοῦς** (*nous*, Intellect), **νόησις** (*noēsis*, intellection), **ταὐτόν** (*tauton*, identical), **τὸ αὐτὸ νοεῖν καὶ εἶναι** (thinking and being are the same — Parmenides B3)

---

## Context: Beyond Intellect — The One

Contemplation culminates not in a vision but in a presence that exceeds vision. The One is not an object of thought but the source from which thought itself flows.

> Intellect, then, is beautiful and the most beautiful of all, situated in pure light and pure radiance, encompassing the nature of Beings – of which this our world is a shadow and an image. ... But if Intellect is beautiful, who is the One in its beauty beyond all? For the One did not have what was to come; but what is it that made Intellect beautiful? Or what, apart from the One, gave beauty to it? ... For if the One is a producer, its product does not come from it by chance, but insofar as it can produce. But Intellect is the offspring and product of the One. (§11)

> For the One is not everything, but by making everything, it made everything; it is all things, though not as something among them, but in another way. For there is no 'there' in the One; and not 'there' either, but it holds everything together without being held together. (§10)

Greek key terms: **τὸ Ἕν** (*to Hen*, the One), **τὸ Ἀγαθόν** (*to Agathon*, the Good), **δύναμις πάντων** (*dynamis pantōn*, potency of all things)
