# ennead 6: happiness does not come and go

treatise 6 of the enneads: happiness does not come and go
plotinus said happiness cannot grow with time — a single moment of true happiness is as complete as all of eternity. quality, not quantity. depth, not duration.

plotinus wrote this as treatise 6 of the enneads. here is what he said and why it still matters.

Happiness is the soul's stable possession of the Good — not an emotion, not a feeling that comes and goes with circumstances — and nothing external can take it from the soul that has it.

> Happiness is not found in the satisfaction of desires. For desire is the mark of need, and the happy person is not in need. Happiness is self-sufficiency — not the self-sufficiency of the isolated self but the self-sufficiency that comes from possessing the Good.

*Εὐδαιμονία.* Happiness. Literally, "having a good daimon." The word itself points to the connection between happiness and the soul's true nature. The happy person is the one whose daimon is good — which is to say, the one whose soul is aligned with its source.

The happy life depends entirely on what the soul is, not on its circumstances:

> If indeed the happy life is to be determined by the presence of a natural disposition, we mean, of course, a perfection among beings. Or rather, it is an activity — a life that is well-directed and is active in its own proper sphere, a life that is supremely real and belongs to Intellect. And such a life is wisdom — not a wisdom acquired through reasoning but the wisdom that belongs to Intellect itself, which is ever-present and never fails.

> The happy life is the life that is lived according to Intellect. Not the life of pleasure, for pleasure passes. Not the life of honor, for honor depends on others. Not the life of wealth, for wealth can be lost. The life according to Intellect is the only life that depends on the soul itself.

The argument is simple: if your happiness depends on what you have, you are merely lucky — not truly happy. And luck can change. True happiness must depend on what you are — not on what you have.

> Does happiness increase with time? Can a person be happier after fifty years of virtue than after five? If happiness is the possession of the Good, and the Good is not subject to time, then time cannot affect it. A moment of true happiness is as complete as eternity.

*Αὔξησις.* Increase. The common assumption is that more time means more happiness. But if happiness is a quality of the soul's relationship to the Good, then duration has nothing to do with it. A lifetime of virtue is not more virtuous than a moment of pure virtue. The quality is the same.

This is a thunderbolt: if the quality of happiness does not increase with time, then you do not need more years to be happy. You need more depth.

> The wise person is happy even in the Bull of Phalaris. Not because pain is not painful but because pain does not touch the soul's deepest self. The soul that has the Good possesses it regardless of the body's condition.

The Bull of Phalaris: a brazen bull in which victims were roasted alive. If the wise person can be happy there, then happiness is truly independent of circumstances. The point is not that suffering is unreal. The point is that the soul's core is untouched by it.

> Happiness is not the absence of pain. It is the presence of the Good. And the Good is present to the soul that has turned toward it. Nothing external can interrupt that presence. The soul that has seen the Good carries the vision with it, even through suffering.

It is the recognition that the soul has depths that the body cannot reach — not stoic indifference. The body suffers, but the soul — if it has chosen the Good — remains in contact with what is beyond all suffering. The happy person is not the one who feels good but the one who is good. And being good is a state that fortune cannot touch. The happy person is not lucky. He is real — and reality, unlike luck, cannot be taken away.
