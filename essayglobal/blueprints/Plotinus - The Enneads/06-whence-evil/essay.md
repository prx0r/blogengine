# ennead 7: whence evil?

treatise 7 of the enneads: whence evil?
plotinus said evil is not a real thing — it is a shadow. and a shadow proves the light exists.

plotinus wrote this as treatise 7 of the enneads. here is what he said and why it still matters.

Evil is the absence of form — the limit of emanation, the darkness that remains when light withdraws — not a principle opposed to the Good, not a dark god, not a rival power, not something that exists in its own right.

> If there is evil, where does it come from? It cannot come from the Good, for the Good gives only good. And it cannot be self-existent, for then there would be two principles, and the Good would not be the only source. Evil must be a kind of non-being — not absolute non-being but relative non-being, the absence of form in what should have form.

*Μὴ ὄν.* Non-being. Not nothing but the lack of determination, the indefiniteness that resists form. Matter is the ultimate subject of this lack — pure potentiality that never fully becomes actual.

Plotinus explains in his treatise on providence:

> In general, then, evil must be considered to be a lack of goodness, but the lack of goodness must be here because the good is in another. This other [matter], then, in which the good is, since it is other than the good, produces the lack. For it is not good. For this reason, 'evils will not be done away with'; both because some things are less than others in respect of the nature of good and these other things which have the cause of their real existence from the Good are different from it and have actually become what they are because of their distance from it.

> And, as we have just stated, evils did not come about for these reasons; rather, when they do occur, the cosmic expressed principle makes use of them for some needful purpose; and that this is the sign of the greatest power, to be able to make good use even of evils and be capable of utilizing what has become formless to fashion into other forms.

> Matter is the substrate of evil. Not because matter is evil in itself but because it is the absence of all form. It is what remains when the light of the Good is withdrawn. It is the darkness that is necessary for light to be seen.

Matter is the necessary limit of emanation — not an independent principle, making Plotinus no dualist. The Good overflows, and its overflow produces all levels of reality. But emanation cannot go on forever. At some point, the light fades, and what remains is pure potentiality — matter.

This is the move that separates Plotinus from every dualist who ever lived: evil is not a rival to the Good. It is the Good's own limit, the edge of its radiance.

> Evil is necessary for the perfection of the whole. Not as a positive element but as the contrast against which the Good appears. A painting cannot be all light; it needs shadow. A melody cannot be all sound; it needs silence. The cosmos cannot be all form; it needs matter.

This is not a justification of suffering. It is an explanation of structure. Evil exists not as a positive force but as a deficiency — the failure of a thing to realize its own form. A disease is not a positive entity; it is the absence of health. A vice is not a positive power; it is the absence of virtue.

> Evil is not something that attacks the Good from outside. It is the Good's own shadow — the limit that makes the Good visible. To be wholly good is to be the Good itself. Everything less than the Good is, to that extent, evil.

The human task, then, is not to fight evil as an external enemy. It is to realize form in ourselves — to become what we are, to actualize our potential, to reduce the deficiency that makes us vulnerable to evil.

> The soul becomes evil when it turns away from Intellect and attaches itself to the body. It becomes good when it turns back. Evil is not a substance; it is a direction. The same soul can be evil or good depending on where it looks.

The flight from evil is therefore a return — not a war. The soul escapes evil by attending to what is better — it does not defeat evil by struggling against it. Evil loses its power when it is ignored — not when it is conquered.

> The one who knows the Good cannot be harmed by evil. For evil is not a power that can touch the soul that has turned toward the Good. It is only the absence that remains where the Good is not present. And where the Good is present, evil cannot enter.

This is why Plotinus can say that evil has no positive existence. It is real as an absence, as a lack, as a limit — but it is not a being. The only being is from the Good. And the soul that returns to the Good returns to what is real. Evil has no power of its own. It has only the power we give it by attending to it. Turn toward the Good, and the shadow vanishes — not because it was destroyed but because it was never there to begin with.
