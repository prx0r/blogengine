# ennead 15: the powers of the soul

treatise 15 of the enneads: the powers of the soul
plotinus solved the mind-body problem in the third century — and you have never heard the solution. the soul has exactly five powers, from plant growth to pure intellect, each one containing the ones below it. the entire cosmos is compressed into a single soul: yours.

plotinus wrote this as treatise 15 of the enneads. here is what he said and why it still matters.

The soul has powers — faculties through which it acts at different levels of reality. This is where Plotinus's genius as a systematizer shows itself. Before him, Plato had talked about the tripartite soul. After him, everyone from Augustine to Freud would offer their own models. But Plotinus's framework is the cleanest: five powers, from the lowest to the highest, each one a transformation of the one before. Growth. Perception. Imagination. Discursive reason. Intellect. The same life, at different depths.

> The soul is one, but it acts through many powers. When it thinks, it is at the level of Intellect. When it reasons, it is at the level of discursive thought. When it perceives, it is at the level of sense. When it grows, it is at the level of nature. The same soul does all these things, but at different depths.

Each power is not a separate thing. The soul's unity is the unity of a life that expresses itself in many ways — not like the unity of a machine with many parts. The same life that thinks also perceives, but it perceives only when it turns toward the body.

> Perception is the soul's attention to the body's affections. It is not the body's act but the soul's act through the body. The body receives the impression; the soul judges it. Perception is the soul's interpretation of what the body undergoes.

*Αἴσθησις.* Sense-perception. Not a passive reception but an active judgment. The soul does not receive sensations like a wax tablet receiving an impression. It actively interprets the body's states. To perceive is to judge — to say "this is red," "this is hard," "this is painful."

Notice what Plotinus has done here: perception is not passive. You are not a sponge. You are an active interpreter of reality at every level.

Plotinus delves deeper into how intellection is apprehended:

> For the person who will remember, there will be nothing to stop the sense-datum from being an imaginative representation, and remembering and retaining the memory will belong to the faculty of imaginative representation, which is a different thing. For this is the point at which the sense-perception terminates, and what was seen is present to it when the sense-perception is no longer there.

> Perhaps, for example, we might postulate the reception into the faculty of imaginative representation of a verbal expression of a thought. For the thought has no parts, and when it has not yet, in a way, proceeded to the outside, it remains unnoticed within, but the verbal expression, by unfolding it and bringing it forth from the thought to the faculty of imaginative representation, exhibits the thought as if in a mirror, and this is how there is apprehension of it, and persistence of it, and memory.

> Imagination (phantasia) is the intermediary between sense and intellect. It receives the images from perception and presents them to reason. It also receives the traces of intellectual activity and presents them in imagistic form.

*Φαντασία.* Imagination. The middle faculty. It is what allows the soul to think about what it has perceived and to perceive what it has thought. When you imagine a triangle, you are using phantasia. When you understand the triangle's essence, you have moved beyond it.

> Memory belongs to the imagination, not to Intellect. For Intellect does not need to remember — it is always present to all things. Memory is the retention of images, and it belongs to the faculty that deals with images.

*Μνήμη.* Memory. The soul retains what it has experienced. But this retention is not a storage of impressions. It is a power — the power to recall what the soul has undergone, and in recalling, to learn from it.

The soul's memory determines its orientation:

> When it has departed from the intelligible world and is unable to bear the oneness, but has become attracted to a state of its own, wanting to be other than it was, and in a way poking its head out, it is in consequence of this, so it seems, that it acquires memory. The memory of the things in the intelligible world continues to restrain it from falling, but that of things here carries it in this direction, while that of the things in heaven holds it there, and, in general, according as the focus of its memory alters, there it is and comes to be. Now, remembering is either thinking or imagining, but its imagination does not consist in possessing something; rather, its disposition is such as the things it sees; and if it sees sensibles, its level of descent will correspond to the amount of them it sees. Because it possesses all things in a secondary way, and not in a perfect way, it becomes all things, and being a boundary entity, and having its location in such a place, it is borne in either direction.

> Discursive reason is the soul's characteristic activity. It moves from premise to conclusion, from cause to effect, from one thought to the next. This is not the mode of Intellect, which sees all things at once. It is the mode of soul, which sees things in sequence because it is not fully present to itself.

*Διάνοια.* Discursive reason. The soul's way of knowing in time. It cannot hold all things together, so it moves through them one by one. But this sequential movement is the soul's nature — not a defect — and the soul can rise above it by gathering itself into the unity of Intellect.

> Each power is a mode of the soul's attention. The soul becomes what it attends to. When it attends to Intellect, it becomes intellectual. When it attends to sense, it becomes perceptual. The soul is all things, but it is them in the mode of its attention.

The soul is a universe. It contains all powers within itself, from the highest to the lowest. Its task is not to reject the lower powers but to order them — to let Intellect guide reason, reason guide imagination, imagination guide perception, perception guide the body. The soul's freedom is its power to choose where to attend. This is the beauty of Plotinus's model: you already have every level. You grow, perceive, imagine, reason, and think — not as five separate things but as one life expressing itself in an unbroken hierarchy from matter to spirit. The entire cosmos is inside you, compressed into a single soul. You do not need to acquire new powers to reach the highest. You already have every level. You only need to attend at the level you want to become.
