# ennead 25: the multiplicity of ideas

treatise 25 of the enneads: the multiplicity of ideas
plotinus said the one does not choose to produce the forms — it is too full to hold itself in. the infinite richness of reality is not a decision. it is an overflow of joy.

plotinus wrote this as treatise 25 of the enneads. here is what he said and why it still matters.

How do the Forms come from the One? If the One is absolutely simple, how does it produce the multiplicity of Forms? The answer is that the One's power is infinite — and infinity, when it turns toward itself, produces determination.

> The One does not calculate how many Forms to produce. It does not deliberate. The Forms are not the result of a choice but the expression of the One's infinite power. In the One they are one; in Intellect they are many.

The generation of the Forms is the eternal structure of reality — not an event in time. The One overflows, and its overflow is Intellect. Intellect, in turning toward the One, receives the One's power in a mode that is not the One's — a mode of multiplicity.

> Intellect, in its turning, receives the One's power. But it cannot receive it as the One — for the One is beyond reception. It receives it as a multiplicity. Each Form is a distinct way of participating in the One's power. The Forms are the One's power refracted through Intellect's self-awareness.

*Δύναμις.* Power. The One is power itself — not the power to do this or that but the power of all things. Intellect is the first act of that power, the first determination. And the Forms are the determinate shapes that power takes when it becomes self-aware.

This is the beauty of Plotinus's system: the One is not a miser guarding its treasure. It is a fountain that cannot stop flowing because it has too much to give.

Plotinus argues from the nature of Intellect itself:

> But why are the animals in the intelligible world? What are they in god? Rational animals are there, so be it. But the vast number of non-rational animals — what is holy about them? And why not the opposite? Because it is clear that the One-Being also has to be many, since it is posterior to that which is absolutely One. Otherwise it would not be posterior to the One but would be the One itself. And since it is posterior to that, it was not possible for it to exceed that One in unity; it had to fall short of it. Since the best was the One, it had to be more than one. For multiplicity lies in deficiency.

> So, what prevents it from being a Dyad? In fact, it was not possible for either of the two parts in the Dyad to be absolutely one; rather they had to be at least two, and so, too, their parts in turn.

> Next, there was Motion and Stability in the primary Dyad, there was Intellect and Life in it — that is, perfect Intellect and perfect Life. So, it was not as one Intellect, but as all Intellect, that is Intellect containing all the individual intellects; Intellect as many as these are, and more. And it was alive not as one soul, but as all souls, containing more power to produce the individual souls. And it was a 'complete Living Being', containing not only the human being in itself; otherwise, human being would be only in the sensible world.

> For this reason, Intellect is said to be one-many. It is one because it is the single act of turning toward the One. It is many because its content — the Forms — is inexhaustible. Each Form contains the whole, but in its own mode.

The Forms are numberless — not a finite set. The One's power is infinite, and Intellect's reception of it is infinite. There are as many Forms as there are ways of participating in the One — which is to say, numberlessly many.

> Among the Forms are the Forms of all living beings — not only of the noble but also of the humble. There are Forms of animals, of plants, of elements. Nothing that exists in the sensible world is without its paradigm in Intellect. For the sensible world is the image of the intelligible, and every image has its original.

Even the lowest beings have their Forms — the worm, the weed, the stone — all have their archetypes in Intellect because they participate in being, not because they are perfect, and being flows from the Forms.

Plotinus explains how the non-rational in our world exists as an expressed principle in Intellect:

> One should bear in mind that the many lives are, in a way, motions, and the many acts of intellection need not be the same: both lives and acts of intellection are different. The distinctions differ in luminosity and clarity, first, second, and third, depending on the proximity to the first principles. This is why some of these acts of intellection are gods, others a second kind, which has here the designation 'rational', and the one coming after that is called 'non-rational'. In the intelligible world, what is called here 'non-rational' is an expressed principle, and what is without Intellect is Intellect; for it is Intellect that is thinking Horse — and the intellection of Horse is Intellect.

> The Forms are not static. They are alive — each is a mode of Intellect's self-awareness. Intellect does not contain the Forms as a jar contains water. It is the Forms, in the act of knowing itself. The Forms are Intellect's own life.

And at the summit of this hierarchy, the Good itself does not think:

> The Good does not think, and so does not think itself, as the Peripatetics claim. The Good is not, has no predicates and does not think itself. Thinking and Substantiality require Difference, so that the Good cannot think itself, on pain of not being simple. Since the Good is perfectly one, primary and independent, it cannot think, since thinking requires an object.

> The hierarchy: all beings are for the sake of the Good. Intelligibles follow the Good directly, Soul in Intellect produces the sensible things.

And the purpose of the Forms is not to be contemplated but to be the content of contemplation. Intellect does not produce the Forms for any end beyond itself. It produces them because it is what it is — the eternal self-vision of the One's power.

> The multiplicity of Ideas is the glory of Intellect — the inexhaustible richness of being's self-awareness, not a problem to be solved. The One, in its simplicity, contains all multiplicity in a mode beyond multiplicity. Intellect, in its multiplicity, expresses all that the One is, in a mode that can be known.

The Forms are the face of the One turned toward the many. To see the Forms is to see the One — not as it is in itself but as it appears to Intellect. And for Intellect, that appearance is the whole of reality. The multiplicity of Ideas is not a fragmentation of the One. It is the One's glory, refracted into light — and every color of that light is beautiful.
