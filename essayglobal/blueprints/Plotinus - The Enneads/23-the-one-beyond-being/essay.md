# ennead 23: the one beyond being

treatise 23 of the enneads: the one beyond being
plotinus said the source of everything is not a thing at all — and if you think you have understood it, you have missed it. the one is not an object of knowledge. it is the silence in which all knowledge happens.

plotinus wrote this as treatise 23 of the enneads. here is what he said and why it still matters.

All beings are beings because they are one. Take away unity, and you take away being itself. An army without unity is a mob; a choir without unity is noise; a soul without unity is dispersed into nothing. Unity is the condition of there being anything at all — not one property among others.

> All beings are beings due to unity — all those which are primary beings, and all those which are in any way said to be among beings. For what could actually be if it were not one? For if things are deprived of the one, which is what we say they are, they are not those things. For an army does not exist unless it is one, nor a chorus, nor a herd. A house or a ship? They do not exist unless they are one. For a house is one, and a ship is one; if they lose their oneness, they are no longer a house or a ship.

*Τὸ ἕν.* The One. It is not a being among beings. It is the source from which being itself flows.

Plotinus draws out the full consequences of this dependency:

> All beings are beings due to unity, both those beings that are primarily Beings and those that are said to be among beings in any way. For what could be, if it were not a unity? For if you take away the unity which they are said to be, then they are not those things. For an army does not exist unless it is a unity, nor a choir, or a herd, if it is not a unity. Nor a house or a ship, if it does not have unity, since a house or a ship is a unity, and if it loses its unity, then it is no longer a house or a ship. So, there cannot be continuous magnitudes unless unity is present in them; when divided, they alter their existence, insofar as their unity is destroyed. And indeed so, too, with the bodies of plants and animals, since each is a unity, if they lose their unity when they are divided into a multiplicity, they lose the substantiality they had, in that they are no longer what they were.

> What could be lost through the removal of unity? For if things are one, they are; if they are not one, they are not. This is why it is said of the One that it is the principle of all things — not the one that is predicated of something else, but the One that is prior to all predication.

The One is the One before one-and-many, the source before the distinction between unity and multiplicity — not a thing, not a being, not even one in the sense that we can count it.

> The One is not a being. For being has the shape of the One, but the One is shapeless and without form. For if it had form, it would be a particular being, not the universal source of all beings. For this reason, the One is in fact all beings and none of them. It is all because all things come from it; it is none because it is prior to all.

*Ἀνείδεος.* Formless. The One has no shape because every shape is a limitation, and the One is unlimited — not as an infinite expanse but as the pure power of all things, before any of them exist.

Plotinus clarifies that even thinking must be taken away from the One:

> Indeed, one should take away from it both thinking and being with itself and the intellection of itself and of other things. For it should not be ranked with something that thinks, but with the intellection itself. The intellection does not think; it is the cause for something else thinking, and the cause is not identical with what is caused. The cause of everything is none of these things. So, this should not be called 'good', which is what it bestows on other things. Rather, it is the Good in another way, above the other goods.

> If you become unfocused in your view, since the One is none of these things, you should rely on them, and use them to form your vision. Do not form your vision by diverting your thought elsewhere. It does not lie somewhere bare of other things, it is always present to anyone with the power to touch it, and it is not present to anyone without this power.

> The One is not Intellect. For Intellect is already a multiplicity, since it thinks itself and is many in its thinking. The One does not think. Thinking would introduce duality: thinker and thought. The One is prior to that division.

How, then, can we speak of it? We cannot. All language about the One is negation. Not this, not that. We strip away every determination until nothing remains — and in that nothing, the One is present.

> We must take away everything. We must not add anything. We must be careful not to attribute anything to it, not even being. For being is something that comes after it. It is from the One that being proceeds.

*Ἀφαίρεσις.* Taking away. The method of the via negativa is not addition but subtraction. We do not build up a picture of the One; we strip away every picture until the silence that remains is the One's own presence.

This is the method that leads beyond all method: you do not find the One by adding beliefs. You find it by subtracting everything that is not it — until nothing is left but presence.

> The One is prior to place — for place is something that comes after it and encloses what is in it. The One is prior to time — for time measures change, and the One does not change. The One is not in anything at all — for it is the source of all things, and the source is not in what flows from it.

The One is present — more present than anything else, because everything else has its being by participating in the One — not merely absent.

> The One is present to all things as their source, yet it is not present as something contained. For how could what is absolutely simple be contained by what is composite? It is present as power: the power of all things, the potency from which everything proceeds and into which everything returns.

*Δύναμις πάντων.* The power of all things. The One is not a being that has power. It is power itself — not the power to do this or that but the power from which all doing and all being proceed.

How does Intellect come from such a One? Through the superabundance of power:

> If that which is first is perfect, that is, the most perfect of all things and the first power, it must be the most powerful of all things, and the other powers imitate it as much as they are able. In the case of other things, we see whatever comes to perfection, generating, and not holding back so as to remain self-contained, but rather making something else. This is the case not only for things that have choice, but also for things that grow without choice — and even for things without souls, which give of themselves to the extent that they are able. For example, fire warms, and snow chills, and drugs which act on something else according to their own nature. Everything imitates the principle according to its capacity by tending towards eternity and goodness.

> How, then, could that which is most perfect and that which is the first Good remain in itself as if it were grudging of itself, or without power — that which is the productive power of all things? How would it still be a principle? Something actually must come to be from it if indeed other things are to exist beside it.

And the soul's encounter with this One is presence — not knowledge.

> The experience is not a seeing but a kind of touch, a contact without words, without distance. The soul touches the One and knows that it has touched something beyond knowledge. It cannot say what it has touched, because speech belongs to the realm of division and the One is indivisible.

This is the famous *flight of the alone to the Alone*.

> This is the life of gods and of godlike and blessed human beings: a liberation from all that is alien here below, a flight beyond all earthly pain, a life that is beyond all touch of earthly things, a passing into the alone of the Alone.

*Μόνου πρὸς μόνον.* The alone to the Alone. The soul goes to the One by becoming one itself — by stripping away all multiplicity, all division, all thought, until only the simple presence remains — not as to a distant place. And in that presence, the soul finds not a thing but the source of all things, not a being but the power of being, not a thought but the silence from which all thought arises. And this silence is not absence. It is abundance so complete that it cannot be spoken. The alone to the Alone: this is not a doctrine. It is an invitation.

This is the One. And it cannot be spoken.
