# Source Material — Essay 27: Being Is Everywhere Whole

## Territory: VI.4-5 (22-23) — That Being, One and Identical, Is Simultaneously Everywhere Whole

---

### VI.4.1 — The Puzzle of Omnipresence Without Division

**Context:** The treatise opens with the fundamental puzzle: how can something without parts or magnitude be present everywhere at once? The soul is present to the whole cosmos without being divided.

> Is soul present everywhere to the universe, because the body of the universe has a determinate size, and because soul is naturally divided in bodies? In fact, no, soul in itself is everywhere, and not just where it would be extended to by body. Indeed, does body not find that soul is everywhere prior to body? The result is that wherever body happens to be placed, it finds that soul is already there prior to body's being placed in any part of the universe, and the whole body of the universe is placed in soul which is already there.

> But if soul extends so far that, prior to reaching a body of a determinate size, it filled up all extension, how will it not have a magnitude? Or in what manner would it exist in the universe prior to the universe coming to be, when the universe was not? How would anyone accept that the soul, said to be without parts and without magnitude, is everywhere, if it does not have magnitude?

**Greek terms:** ψυχή (psychē) — soul; πανταχοῦ (pantachou) — everywhere; ἀμερής (amerēs) — without parts; μέγεθος (megethos) — magnitude; σῶμα (sōma) — body

---

### VI.4.2-3 — The True Universe Is in Nothing; Being Is in Itself

**Context:** The intelligible universe, the primary Being, is not in a place — it is in nothing, for there is nothing prior to it. Everything posterior to it is in it. It is not divided by being present to many things.

> Certainly, there is both the true universe and the imitation of the universe, that is, the nature of the visible universe. The true universe is, then, in nothing, for there is nothing prior to it. Anything posterior to this is at once necessarily in the true universe, if indeed it is at all, and is completely dependent on that; it cannot either persist or move without it.

> The true universe, being all, is not such that it falls short of itself; rather, it has both completed itself and is equal to itself. And where the universe is, it is there, for it is itself the universe.

> And there is nothing to be astonished at if that which is everywhere is in being and in itself. For that which comes to be everywhere is already in unity. For in positing being in the sensible world, we also posit that that which is everywhere is there. Because we place being in the sensible realm, we place 'everywhere' there, too. And since we think that the sensible is large, we are puzzled how that nature is extended in something of such a largeness.

**Greek terms:** κόσμος ἀληθινός (kosmos alēthinos) — true universe; μίμημα (mimēma) — imitation; τόπος (topos) — place; οὐσία (ousia) — Being; πανταχοῦ (pantachou) — everywhere

---

### VI.4.7 — The Hand and the Luminous Sphere: Images of Undivided Presence

**Context:** Two key images to aid understanding: the hand that controls a whole body without being divided into it, and a luminous sphere where light from the centre fills the whole without being split.

> A hand, however, could well control a whole body, for example, a piece of wood many cubits in length, or something else, and, while controlling the thing entirely, it is nonetheless not separated into parts equal to what is in its control. The power may seem to extend as far as what is grasped, but nonetheless the hand is limited by its own quantity, not by that of the body that it lifts and controls.

> And what if one were actually to make a small luminous mass, in a way, the centre of a larger transparent spherical body, so that the light from the inside is revealed in all the surrounding parts without any radiance reaching the outer mass from anywhere else? Will we not then say that the inside remains unaffected, but has extended itself to every part of the exterior of the mass while remaining fixed itself, and that light seen in the small mass has taken hold of the exterior?

**Greek terms:** χείρ (cheir) — hand; φῶς (phōs) — light; σφαῖρα (sphaira) — sphere; κέντρον (kentron) — centre; δύναμις (dynamis) — power

---

### VI.4.8 — The Indivisible Cannot Be Divided

**Context:** Since Being has no magnitude, it cannot be divided. When we say it is 'in many things', we are attributing the affection of the many to the one, mistakenly thinking it has become many.

> For how will you divide something which does not have magnitude? If then, something which has magnitude in any way participates in that which does not have magnitude, it would not participate in it by that being divided. Otherwise, it will again have magnitude.

> Whenever you say that the true universe is in many things, you are not saying that it has become many. Instead, you are attributing the affection belonging to the many things to that one, because you see it simultaneously in many things. But the words 'in the many' should not be taken to indicate that it has come to belong either to each of the many things or to all of them, but that it belongs to itself and it exists itself and that it does not leave itself behind.

**Greek terms:** ἀμερής (amerēs) — indivisible; διαίρεσις (diairesis) — division; μέγεθος (megethos) — magnitude; πάθος (pathos) — affection; αὐτὸ ἐν ἑαυτῷ (auto en heautō) — itself in itself

---

### VI.4.12 — Voice Everywhere Whole; Soul Present as Voice in Air

**Context:** The analogy of the voice: one voice is present as a whole to every ear in the air without being divided. Soul is likewise present wholly everywhere it is present.

> The voice is everywhere in the air, not as one voice divided, but as a one which is everywhere whole. And in the case of sight, if the air has the shape it has by being affected, then it has it not as divided. Wherever sight is placed, there it has the shape.

> If in this case, the whole voice is not spread through all the air, such that this part of it is conjoined with part of the air, and this part divided together with that, why be incredulous if the one soul is not spread out, having been divided, but is present wholly everywhere it is present, and is everywhere the soul of the universe, without having been divided.

**Greek terms:** φωνή (phōnē) — voice; ἀήρ (aēr) — air; ἀκοή (akoē) — hearing; ὅλη (holē) — whole; ψυχή (psychē) — soul

---

### VI.4.14 — We Are Not Cut Off

**Context:** The famous passage affirming that before our birth into bodies, we were in the intelligible world — and even now we are not cut off from it. The true Human Being has approached us, and we remain parts of the whole.

> And we — who are we? Are we what approaches and comes to be in time? In fact, before that coming to be came to be, we were in the intelligible world, since we were both other human beings, and some indeed gods, pure souls, and intellect united with the whole of Substance, parts of the intelligible neither bounded off nor cut off, but by belonging to the whole. For even now we are not cut off; actually, the Human Being has approached that human being, despite his wishing to be another. And having found us — for we are not outside the true cosmos — he has wrapped himself around us and added himself to that human being, who was each of us then.

**Greek terms:** οὐκ ἀποτέτμημεθα (ouk apotetmēmetha) — we are not cut off; νοῦς (nous) — intellect; ψυχή (psychē) — soul; πᾶσα οὐσία (pasa ousia) — the whole of Substance; ἄνθρωπος (anthrōpos) — Human Being

---

### VI.4.15 — The City and the Assembly; Vice and Virtue in the Soul

**Context:** The image of a city assembly: the better part of the soul remains quiet, while the disorderly mob of appetites and passions creates turmoil. Virtue is the rule of the better part.

> Just as in an assembly when the elders of the people occupy their seats in quiet deliberation, but the disorderly mob, demanding food and complaining of the other things it has actually suffered, throws the whole assembly into unseemly tumult. And then, whenever word reaches them from a wise man, such people are quiet, the multitude settles into a measured order, and the worse sort does not gain the upper hand.

> It is also the vice of a human being, since he has in him a mob of pleasures, appetites, and fears which take over, when a human being gives himself over to such a mob. But whoever will enslave such a rabble and run up to that human being, which he once was, and live in accordance with him, he is also that human being, giving to the body such things as he may give, as though he were giving to someone other than himself.

**Greek terms:** δῆμος (dēmos) — mob; γερουσία (gerousia) — council of elders; ταράσσω (tarassō) — to disturb; ἀρετή (aretē) — virtue; κακία (kakia) — vice

---

### VI.5.1-2 — The Notion of God Is of Something Omnipresent

**Context:** The second book of the treatise begins with the common notion of god that we all possess: a god who is omnipresent, the same in each being. The Good and Being belong to each being as such.

> We all have the notion of a god which is omnipresent, and the same in each being; the Good, and Being belong to each being as such. The intelligible has to be treated using its proper principles and leaving aside body. True unity remains in itself; other things participate in it insofar as they are able. It comes to the same thing to believe in god and to believe there is one being which is identical everywhere.

**Greek terms:** θεός (theos) — god; πανταχοῦ παρεῖναι (pantachou pareinai) — to be present everywhere; ἀγαθόν (agathon) — the Good; ὄν (on) — Being; μετέχειν (metechein) — to participate
