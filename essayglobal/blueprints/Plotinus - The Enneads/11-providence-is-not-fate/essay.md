# ennead 11: providence is not fate

treatise 11 of the enneads: providence is not fate
plotinus said fate is what you call the pattern when you do not understand it. providence is what you call the same pattern when you do. the difference is not in the cosmos — it is in you.

plotinus wrote this as treatise 11 of the enneads. here is what he said and why it still matters.

The universe is governed by providence, not by blind fate or chance. Evil exists within the providential order as part of a whole that is beautiful. The logos of the universe weaves all things — even evils — into the pattern of the good.

> If the universe is governed by providence, what is its nature? It cannot be the intervention of a deity who adjusts events from outside. Providence is the inherent rationality of the whole — not a plan imposed from without — the logos that makes the cosmos a cosmos at all.

*Πρόνοια.* Providence. Forethought. But not forethought as prediction. Providence is the intelligible structure of the whole — the way every part is related to every other, the way the whole orders itself from within.

Plotinus opens by clearing the ground:

> If we said that the universe came into being at a particular time without pre-existing, we would have posited in our definition the identical kind of providence as we said occurs in the case of particulars, a sort of divine foreseeing and calculative reasoning as to how this universe would be and how it might be as good as possible. But since we claim that this universe always existed and there never was a time when it was not, we would be correct and logical in saying that universal providence consists in the existence of the universe in accordance with Intellect and that Intellect is prior to it, not being prior in time but because its existence depends on Intellect and Intellect is prior by nature and is its cause.

> Providence is not the same as fate. Fate is the compulsion that follows from external causes. Providence is the internal order that results from the nature of the whole. Fate binds. Providence liberates — because to understand the logos of the whole is to consent to it.

Plotinus distinguishes sharply between fate and providence. Fate is the mechanism of cause and effect, the chain of external events that seems to determine everything. Providence is the meaning of that chain — the intelligible pattern that the mechanism serves.

This is the distinction that liberates: you are not a puppet of fate. You are a participant in providence. The difference is whether you see the meaning or only the mechanism.

The logos flows from Intellect like a seed unfurling:

> So, Intellect, while giving something of itself to matter, fashioned everything while remaining undisturbed and quiet. And what it gives is the expressed principle which flows from Intellect, for that which flows from Intellect is an expressed principle; and it is always flowing as long as Intellect remains present among Beings.

> But just as in the expressed principle in a seed everything is together in the identical place, and nothing is vying or at odds with or hindering anything else, yet once something develops into a mass, different parts could be in different places and one part could actually hinder another, and one thing could consume something else, so, too, this universe has actually arisen from a single Intellect and the expressed principle which flows from it and has been divided, of necessity some things friendly and well-disposed, others hostile and at war, some willingly, others even unwillingly harming each other, some things destroyed to effect the generation of others; and yet the universe imposes a single harmony on the things that are producing and undergoing these effects as each of them utters its own notes and the expressed principle effects harmony on them and a unified order on the totality.

> Evil exists within the providential order, but not as a positive element. It exists as the necessary shadow of the Good — not because the Good intends it but because the Good's light, when it reaches the limit of emanation, produces darkness.

The problem of evil is solved not by denying evil but by seeing it in context. A play contains conflicts and disasters. But the play as a whole is beautiful. The individual suffering is real, but the whole that includes it is ordered.

Plotinus develops the analogy at length:

> This expressed principle, then — we must dare to state it, for we might just manage to hit on the right description — is not pure intellect nor is it 'intellect itself' or even the nature of pure soul, but depends on the latter and is an illumination coming from both Intellect and Soul — Soul disposed according to Intellect — which generated this expressed principle as a life in quiet possession of an expressed principle.

> So, this expressed principle, which has come from a single Intellect and a single life, each of which is complete, is neither a single life nor a single Intellect nor complete in every aspect; nor does it give itself whole and entire to those to which it does give itself. And by setting the parts in opposition to each other and making them deficient, it has fashioned a structure and process which are characterized by war and struggle, and this is the way in which it is one single whole, even if it is not one single thing. For although it is at war with itself through its parts, it is one and harmonious just as the plot of a play might be; the plot of the play is one though it contains in itself many conflicts. And so, the play brings the elements of conflict into a single harmony by producing a kind of overarching symphonic narrative of the conflicts.

> In the universe, however, the conflict of disparate elements comes from a single expressed principle. And so, it would be better to compare it to the harmony which is produced from conflicting notes.

> The viewer of a play knows that the actor's suffering is not the final truth. The actor, too, knows this — he plays his role without being destroyed by it. The wise person is like the good actor: he plays his role fully but knows that he is more than his role.

It is a relocation of identity — not a denial of suffering. The soul that identifies with its role suffers. The soul that knows itself as a participant in the whole is free — free in suffering, not free from suffering.

> Providence does not remove all difficulties. It weaves them into the pattern. The soul that resists the pattern suffers doubly — once from the difficulty itself and once from the resistance. The soul that consents to the pattern suffers only once.

And the highest freedom is the freedom to consent to what is necessary — not the freedom to choose otherwise — to see that the logos of the whole is good, and that one's place in it, however painful, contributes to the beauty of the whole.

> The logos weaves all things: the good and the evil, the just and the unjust, the beautiful and the ugly. It weaves them into a single fabric that is beautiful as a whole. The part cannot see the whole, but the whole is what it is. And the part that consents to the whole shares in its beauty. Freedom is not the power to escape the pattern. It is the power to see that the pattern is good — and to choose it, not because you must, but because you see that it is beautiful.
