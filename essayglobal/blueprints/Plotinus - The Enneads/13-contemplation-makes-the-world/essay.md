# ennead 13: contemplation makes the world

treatise 13 of the enneads: contemplation makes the world
plotinus said the world exists because nature is staring at itself so intensely that it spills over into visible form. the cosmos is not made. it is seen into being.

plotinus wrote this as treatise 13 of the enneads. here is what he said and why it still matters.

Everything that is, from the highest Intellect to the lowest plant, is engaged in contemplation. Nature does not act. It contemplates — and its contemplation produces the sensible world.

> We might say that nature, too, is a contemplative principle, engaged in contemplation, and that what it produces comes from its contemplative activity. For nature does not act by pushing and pulling. It acts by being what it is — a vision that, in its seeing, brings forth what it sees.

*Θεωρία.* Contemplation. Not a passive looking but an active bringing-forth. Nature does not labor. It does not calculate. It sees, and in seeing, it produces. The plant is not striving to grow. It is contemplating its own form, and the growth is the visible trace of that contemplation.

This is the most radical claim in the entire Enneads: to be is to see. Everything that exists is a degree of contemplation.

Plotinus gives nature a voice to explain:

> And if someone were to ask nature why it produces, if it were willing to listen and answer the questioner it would say, 'You should not ask but understand and fall silent yourself, as I am silent and not accustomed to speak. Understand what, then? That what comes to be is my vision, in my silence, an object of contemplation that comes to be by nature, and that since I come to be from this sort of contemplation, it is necessary for me to have a contemplation-loving nature. And my contemplating produces an object of contemplation, just as geometricians draw lines as they contemplate. But without my drawing, while I contemplate, the lines of bodies come to exist as though falling out of me.'

> Nature's contemplation is silent. It does not reason. It does not deliberate. It simply is what it is, and in being what it is, it produces what follows from its being. The production is not an act of will but an act of being.

What is true of nature is true of everything. Action is weakened contemplation. The person who acts is the person whose contemplation has become so dim that it must express itself outwardly.

> Human beings, too, when they are weak in contemplation, produce action as a shadow of contemplation and reason. For their faculty of contemplation is not adequate for them due to weakness of soul and being unable to grasp adequately the object of their vision and because of this not being filled [by it], yet still desirous of seeing it, they are carried towards action so that they can see [with their eyes] what they cannot see with their intellect.

> Indeed, everywhere we will find that production and action are a weakened form of contemplation or a consequence of contemplation; a weakness where a person has nothing in mind beyond what has been made, a consequence where he has something prior to this to contemplate which is superior to what has been produced. For why would anyone go after the image of what is genuine as their first choice, if he can contemplate what is genuine? And less intelligent children are also evidence of this; not being capable of study and theory, they turn to crafts and manual work.

> All action is for the sake of contemplation. The active person acts in order that he may see what he has not been able to see in contemplation itself. He acts because his contemplation is incomplete.

The warrior fights, the builder builds, the politician governs — because they cannot see clearly enough, not for the sake of the action itself. The one who truly contemplates has no need of action. His vision is its own fulfillment.

The hierarchy rises: from nature's silent contemplation, through the soul's discursive contemplation, through Intellect's eternal self-contemplation, to the One, which is beyond contemplation because it is beyond the distinction between seer and seen.

> But when contemplation ascends from nature to Soul and from Soul to Intellect, the acts of contemplation are even more fully appropriated by, that is, more unified with, the contemplators. In the case of the virtuous person's soul, that which is known approaches becoming identical with the substrate which contemplates, inasmuch as it hastens to Intellect. In Intellect, it is clear that the two are already one not by appropriation, as in the case of the best soul, but in Substantiality because 'thinking and Being are identical'.

> This life, however, is clearer and is the primary Life and primary Intellect, and these are one. And so, the first life is intellection, and the second life is a second kind of intellection, and the last life is a final form of intellection. And so, all life is of this kind and is intellection.

> Nature contemplates, and what it contemplates is the expressed principle within it. The soul contemplates, and what it contemplates is above it — Intellect and its forms. Intellect contemplates, and what it contemplates is itself, for it is identical with its own thinking. And above Intellect is the One, which does not contemplate because it does not need to — it is the goal of all contemplation.

At each level, contemplation produces by its own excess. Nature's contemplation produces the sensible world. The soul's contemplation produces its own life and activity. Intellect's contemplation produces the Forms. And the One's super-abundance produces all of reality.

> How does nature produce? By remaining what it is, by being a contemplative principle, by possessing a silent vision. Its production is not an act of making but a kind of irradiation — like light that radiates from the sun without the sun deciding to radiate. Nature shines, and the world appears.

This is the deepest meaning of contemplation: it is what we are — not something we do. Everything that is, is a degree of contemplation. The plant contemplates dimly, the animal contemplates through sense, the human contemplates through reason, the intellect contemplates through self-presence. To be is to see.

And the highest seeing is the simplest: the vision that has no object because it is the source of all objects. The One does not see anything because it is everything. Its seeing is its being, and its being is the overflow that makes all other seeing possible. You are not an exception to this. You are a degree of contemplation — and the quality of your seeing determines the quality of your being. Look at what is highest, and you become it.
