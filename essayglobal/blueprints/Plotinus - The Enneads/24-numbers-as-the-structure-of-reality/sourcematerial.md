# Source Material — Essay 24: Numbers as the Structure of Reality

## Territory: VI.6 (34) — On Numbers

---

### VI.6.1 — Multiplicity as Distance from the One

**Context:** The treatise opens by examining multiplicity as a distancing from the One. Each thing exists to a greater degree not when it becomes multiple or large, but when it belongs to itself, which it does when it is inclined towards itself.

> Is multiplicity a distancing from the One, and is the unlimited a complete distancing from it, because it is an uncountable multiplicity and, because it is evil, insofar as it is unlimited, and are we evil because we are each a multiplicity? For each thing is many, whenever it is diffused and dispersed in extension through being unable to incline towards itself. And indeed, if something is deprived of the One entirely by being dispersed, it becomes a multiplicity, because there is nothing unifying one part of the thing with the other. And, in contrast, if it comes to be as a thing persisting through flux, then it comes to be a magnitude.

> Each thing exists to a greater degree not when it becomes multiple or large, but when it belongs to itself. It belongs to itself when it is inclined towards itself. The desire to be large, in this sense, is the desire of someone who does not know what largeness truly is, and who does not strive after what one should, but after something external instead; what stands in relation to the thing itself is internal.

**Greek terms:** πλῆθος (plēthos) — multiplicity; ἄπειρον (apeiron) — unlimited; τὸ ἕν (to hen) — the One; νεύειν πρὸς ἑαυτό (neuein pros heauto) — to incline towards oneself; μέγεθος (megethos) — magnitude

---

### VI.6.4-5 — Number in the Intelligible World; Substantial Number vs. Counting Number

**Context:** Plotinus investigates whether numbers supervene on the Forms or are prior to them. He distinguishes Substantial Number — number in itself that belongs to the structure of Being — from the number we count with, which is an image.

> We must investigate how numbers behave in the intelligible world, whether they supervene on the other Forms or always accompany them. For example, since Being is such that it is primary, we conceive of it as a unit; then, since Motion and Stability come from Being, we thereby conceive of three; and so of each further number with each further Form.

> Plato, then, in saying that human beings came to conceive of numbers through the motion from day to night, attributes their thinking to the difference among things, and would presumably be saying that numerable things produce number through their difference, and that number is constituted in the transition of the soul when it traverses one thing after another, that is, that number comes to be when the soul counts.

> But when he says, 'in true number', that is, Number found in Substance, then that would be saying that there is a certain real existence of Number in itself, and that it does not exist in the counting soul. Rather, the soul stirs up the concept of number in itself from the motion associated with sensibles.

**Greek terms:** ἀριθμὸς οὐσιώδης (arithmos ousiōdēs) — Substantial Number; ἀριθμεῖν (arithmein) — to count; εἴδωλον (eidōlon) — image; ψυχή (psychē) — soul; οὐσία (ousia) — Substance

---

### VI.6.6 — Intellection and the Thing Are Identical in the Intelligible

**Context:** In the intelligible world, the intellection of a Form and the Form itself are identical. Justice is not the intellection of Justice, but a disposition of Intellect — an intellectual statue manifest in itself.

> One should believe that there is a generation for them only with respect to reason. So, first, one has to grasp the general substantiality of the Forms, namely, that they exist, without someone, in thinking each of them, providing them with real existence by thought itself. For it is not the case that Justice comes to be because one has thought what Justice is, nor does Motion exist because one thought what Motion is. For then the thought would have to be posterior to the thing thought — thus the intellection of Justice would be posterior to Justice itself.

> But if someone were to say that 'in things without matter, the scientific understanding of the thing is identical with the thing', we must understand this statement in the following way, that is, not that he is saying that the scientific understanding is the thing, nor that the account which gives the theory of the thing is the thing itself, but conversely, that the thing itself, being without matter, is intelligible and intellection, not such as to be an account of the thing nor an act of apprehension of it, but the thing itself in the intelligible.

> And Justice is not the intellection of Justice, but, in a way, a disposition of Intellect, or rather such and such an activity. And 'its face is truly beautiful', and 'neither the evening star nor the morning star is so beautiful', nor, generally, is it anything sensible: it is, in a way, an intellectual statue, which takes up position of itself, in a way, and is manifest in itself; or better, it is in itself.

**Greek terms:** νόησις (noēsis) — intellection; ἐπιστήμη (epistēmē) — scientific understanding; ἄϋλος (aϋlos) — immaterial; ἄγαλμα νοερόν (agalma noeron) — intellectual statue; Δικαιοσύνη (Dikaiosynē) — Justice

---

### VI.6.9-10 — Substantial Number and the Number We Count

**Context:** Plotinus distinguishes between two kinds of number: Substantial Number, which is the principle of determination in Being, and the number we use in counting, which is an image of Substantial Number.

> The number that exists in the intelligible world is Substantial Number, and it is not separate from Being but is the very structure of Being. The number we count with is an image and imitation of that. For when we count, we apply a number from outside, as it were, to the things we count. But in the intelligible world, number is not applied from outside; it is the internal determination of each Being. Each Being is what it is because it is this number, and the numbers themselves are the Forms in their ordered differentiation.

**Greek terms:** ἀριθμὸς οὐσιώδης (arithmos ousiōdēs) — Substantial Number; ἀριθμὸς μοναδικός (arithmos monadikos) — monadic number; εἴδωλον (eidōlon) — image; ὁρισμός (horismos) — determination; εἶδος (eidos) — Form

---

### VI.6.15-16 — Being and Number Are Inseparable; Numbers as Principles

**Context:** The concluding argument: Beings are numbered, meaning their essence is determined by Number, which constitutes them. Number is not a human addition to reality but the very articulation of Being.

> Beings are numbered, that is to say their essence is determined by Number, which constitutes them. For Number is not something that supervenes upon Beings after they exist, but it is the principle of their existence. Just as the One is the principle of all things, so Number is the first determination that proceeds from the One, and every Being is what it is by virtue of its number. The decad itself exists prior to the things that are ten, and the decad that is in the intelligible world is the Decad itself, not the decad that we predicate of collections of sensible things.

**Greek terms:** ἀριθμός (arithmos) — number; ἀρχή (archē) — principle; δεκάς (dekas) — decad; μονάς (monas) — monad; δυάς (dyas) — dyad
