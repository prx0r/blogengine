# ennead 1: the self that is not the body

treatise 1 of the enneads: the self that is not the body
plotinus said you are not your body — you are the soul that uses it, the way a musician plays a guitar. break the instrument and the music does not die.

plotinus wrote this as treatise 1 of the enneads. here is what he said and why it still matters.

We are not our bodies. It is a claim about what we are right now, while we are alive — not a claim about an afterlife. The "we" — the true self — is the soul using the body as an instrument.

> What is the living being? If we say that it is the compound of soul and body, then the living being is something between soul and body, a third thing that results from their union. But what are "we"? Are we the living being? Or are we the soul that uses the living being?

*Ἡμεῖς.* We. Plotinus begins his collected works with this question — not "what is the soul?" but "what are we?" The answer is not the compound. The answer is the soul.

Plotinus elsewhere clarifies what this means for our nature:

> Whether each one of us is immortal, or, on the contrary, is destroyed entirely, or if some parts of the individual depart into dispersion and destruction, while other parts persist forever — those which constitute the self — this is something that one might discover if one conducted the enquiry with a view to our nature. A human being is certainly not something simple, but there is in him a soul, and he also has a body, whether in the role of an instrument for us, or connected to us in some other way.

> The dominant part of us, and that which constitutes the human being himself, if indeed it is this, is related to the body as form to matter, or as user to instrument. In either case, the soul is the self.

> Pleasures and pains, fears and appetites — who experiences them? If the soul is unaffected, as we claim, then it must be the living being that experiences them. The "we" is not the living being but that which uses the living being.

The body feels. The soul knows. The confusion arises because we identify with what we experience. But the experiencer is not the same as the instrument of experience.

> It is the soul that uses the body as an instrument. The soul is not in the body as a thing in a place. It is present as a principle of organization, as a source of life. The soul is not contained by the body. It contains the body.

*Χρῆσις.* Use. The soul uses the body the way a musician uses an instrument. The musician is not the instrument. When the instrument breaks, the musician does not break. The soul is separable from the body, not as a thing that can be removed but as a principle that transcends its instrument.

Pause here: this is the claim that rewires everything. If the experiencer is not the instrument of experience, then nothing the body undergoes can touch the core of who you are.

Plotinus argues that the soul must be more than a body to think:

> If sense-perception is the soul's apprehending of sensibles by employing the body, it would not be the case that thinking also is apprehending by means of the body, or it will be identical with sense-perception. If then, thinking is apprehending without involving the body, far more so should that which will be doing the thinking not be a body.

> Furthermore, if sense-perception is of sensibles, and intellection is of intelligibles — if they are not willing to concede that, yet there will at any rate be acts of thinking of some intelligibles and apprehensions of objects without magnitude — how, then, being a magnitude, will it think what is not a magnitude, and by means of that which is divisible think of that which is indivisible?

> When the soul uses the body for sense-perception, it is not the body that perceives but the soul through the body. The body is the organ, the soul is the user. The perception belongs to the soul, even if it seems to happen in the body.

This has immediate consequences for how we understand ourselves. If I am not my body, then my identity is not tied to my bodily states. I am not my hunger, my fear, my pleasure. I am the one who experiences these — and the one who can choose not to identify with them.

> The "we" is double. There is the "we" that is the soul alone, and there is the "we" that is the soul in relation to the body. The first is what we truly are. The second is what we appear to be. The task of philosophy is to transfer our sense of self from the second to the first.

It is a correction of identity — not a denial of embodiment. The soul recognizes that it was never the body to begin with — it does not need to leave the body to become itself.

> The soul that knows itself knows that it is not the body. It knows that it is something prior to the body, something that does not depend on the body for its existence. And in this knowledge, it is already free — not free from the body but free in itself, regardless of what happens to the body.

The first step of the return, then, is a recognition — not an action. The soul must stop identifying with what it is not. It must discover that the "I" that asks the question "what am I?" is already more than the body it finds itself in. And this is the discovery that changes everything: you do not need to escape the body to be free. You only need to remember who you are — and in that remembering, you are already free.
