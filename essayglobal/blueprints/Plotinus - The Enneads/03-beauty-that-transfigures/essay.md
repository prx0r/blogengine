# ennead 4: beauty as the shock of recognition

treatise 4 of the enneads: beauty as the shock of recognition
plotinus said beauty is not symmetry and it is not proportion — beauty is the soul recognizing its own origin. the shock of the beautiful is the shock of recognition.

plotinus wrote this as treatise 4 of the enneads. here is what he said and why it still matters.

Beauty is the radiance of the Form shining through matter — not symmetry, proportion, color, or any property of bodies — and the soul that sees it knows it by recognition, not by measurement.

> Beauty is found for the most part in what is seen, but it also reaches the ears through the composition of words and in music too, for rhythms and melodies are beautiful. And if people advance beyond sense-perception to higher ways of life, there are also beauties of conduct, of ways of life, and also of theorems and of virtue. Are there, in fact, even more beautiful things than these?

Plotinus opens his earliest treatise with the widest possible aperture. Beauty is in sights and sounds, in conduct and character, in theorems and virtues — not one thing but many. The question is what makes anything beautiful at all — not simply what is beautiful.

> What is it, then, that makes us imagine that bodies are beautiful? What is it that draws the gaze of those who see them and turns them towards themselves and makes them enjoy the sight? If we can discover this, perhaps we can also use it as a stepping-stone to see the rest.

*Τὸ κάλλος.* Beauty — the thing's own form manifesting, not a quality added to a thing. A stone is beautiful because it is what it is — and that "what it is" is form, not matter — not because it is painted.

The beauty of craft reveals this clearly:

> The stone which has acquired the beauty of form by craft does not appear beautiful by being stone — for in that case the other would be similarly beautiful — but by the form which the craft imported. So, the matter did not have this form, but it was in the one conceptualizing it even before it came to be in the stone. And it was in the creator not insofar as he had eyes or hands, but because he partook of the craft. The beauty was, therefore, in the craft, and it was far superior there.

> Beauty is for the most part in what is seen. But the sense of hearing, too, can have beautiful sounds in the combination of words, and also in music — all rhythms and melodies are beautiful. And when we go up from sense perception, there are also beautiful ways of life, actions, characters, intellectual activities — and there is the beauty of virtue.

Beauty is a ladder. It begins with bodies, ascends through souls, through virtues, through intellectual activities, to Beauty itself. Each step is the recognition of form at a higher level.

> How then is Beauty? When the formative power masters the shapelessness of matter, it brings form, which is an image of what is prior. The form is a reasoned principle. It shapes and molds that which it brings into being, making it one thing out of many parts. It brings it into a unified whole, producing unity by its own power. Since it is one, the thing that is shaped is as beautiful as the form allows it to be.

The form is a *λόγος* — a reasoned principle. Beauty is the unity that form imposes on multiplicity. The more unified a thing is, the more beautiful it is. Matter is ugly because it is formless — pure multiplicity without the unity that form provides.

But the soul recognizes beauty not by analysis but by a kind of shock.

> When the soul encounters the beautiful, it feels a shock — it is excited, shaken, stirred, filled with desire, with love, with a kind of happy agitation. It is affected in this way by all beauties, but especially by the beauty of the highest kind.

*Ἔκπληξις.* Shock. The soul does not calculate that a thing is beautiful. It is struck. And the reason is that the soul recognizes the form as something akin to itself.

This is the insight that makes aesthetics into a spiritual path: beauty is not a property of things. It is the soul's memory of home.

Plotinus describes the soul's response to higher beauty:

> But such a sight must be reserved for those who see it with that in the soul by which it sees such things, and seeing it are delighted and shocked and overwhelmed much more than in the previous cases, inasmuch as we are now speaking of those who have already got hold of true beauties. For these are the states one should be in regarding something which is beautiful: astonishment, and sweet shock, and longing, and erotic thrill, and pleasurable excitement.

> Beauty is what partakes of the formative power from the Intelligible, in the same way as everything ugly is what has not been subdued by the formative power. The soul feels a kinship with the form, recognizes it as something of its own order, and welcomes it.

The soul recognizes beauty because the soul itself is form. The shock of beauty is the soul's recognition of its own origin.

The ascent: first, the practice of turning inward.

> Let him who can, follow and go inside. Let him leave the sight of his eyes behind and not turn back to the beauties of bodies. When he sees beauties in bodies, let him run, not toward them, but toward the beauty of souls, of customs, of laws, of studies, and of all the other things that are beautiful in themselves. Let him produce virtues. Let him see what beauty virtue gives to a soul.

This is the work. You ascend by seeing through bodies — not by rejecting them — recognizing that what you loved in the body was the form shining through it, never the body itself.

The final stage is a vision, and it requires that the seer become what he sees.

> We must close our eyes and change our sight to another kind of vision and wake up that faculty which everyone has but few use. What does that inner vision see? When it is just awakened, it cannot look at the brilliance. So the soul must be led to the sight of what is beautiful, but it must first become beautiful itself, if it intends to see beauty.

*Καλός.* Beautiful. The seer must become beautiful — literally. The soul that wants to see Beauty itself must make itself beautiful by becoming form, by shedding the ugliness of attachment to matter, by gathering itself into unity.

Plotinus gives the method:

> How, then, can you see the kind of beauty that a good soul has? Go back into yourself and look. If you do not yet see yourself as beautiful, then be like a sculptor who, making a statue that is supposed to be beautiful, removes a part here and polishes a part there so that he makes the latter smooth and the former just right until he has given the statue a beautiful face. In the same way, you should remove superfluities and straighten things that are crooked, work on the things that are dark, making them bright, and not stop 'working on your statue' until the divine splendour of virtue shines in you, until you see 'Self-Control enthroned on the holy seat'.

> If you have become this and have seen it and find yourself in a purified state, you have no impediment to becoming one in this way nor do you have something else mixed in with yourself, but you are entirely yourself, true light alone, neither measured by magnitude nor reduced by a circumscribing shape nor expanded indefinitely in magnitude but being unmeasured everywhere, as something greater than every measure and better than every quantity. If you see that you have become this, at that moment you have become sight, and you can be confident about yourself, and you have at this moment ascended here, no longer in need of someone to show you. Just open your eyes and see, for this alone is the eye that sees the great beauty. For no eye has ever seen the sun without becoming sun-like, nor could a soul ever see Beauty without becoming beautiful. You must first actually become wholly god-like and wholly beautiful if you intend to see god and Beauty.

> The soul, when it loves beauty, must become beautiful. When it comes to the sight of it, it will be full of the vision. If the sight is of Beauty itself, then the soul will be filled with that which it has always sought. It will possess it, not merely look at it as something external, but in a way become it.

The end of the ascent is not a vision of beauty but a union with it. The soul does not see the beautiful. It becomes beautiful, and in that becoming, it knows what beauty always was: the soul's own form, the soul's own origin, the soul's own home.

And this is why the shock of beauty is the shock of recognition. The soul is not seeing something foreign. It is remembering itself. And that is why beauty moves you to tears: because in the presence of the beautiful, you are not seeing something new. You are remembering something ancient — something you have always known but forgotten. Beauty is the shock of remembering who you are.
