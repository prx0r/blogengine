# Source Material — Essay 26: The One's Freedom

## Territory: VI.8 (39) — On the Voluntary, and the One's Wishing

---

### VI.8.1-3 — What Is 'Up to Us'? Freedom as Self-Determination

**Context:** The treatise begins by investigating what we mean by 'what is up to us' (to eph' hēmin) in human beings, before extending the concept to Intellect and the One. True freedom is found in the activity of Intellect, not in action.

> Can one so much as raise the question whether, in the case of the gods, too, there is anything that is up to them, or is it really only appropriate to look for such a thing amongst the frailties and ambiguous powers of human beings, whereas we should grant omnipotence to gods, so that it is not merely something that is up to them, but everything?

> In fact, we should investigate these things, too, and we should dare to investigate these things both in the case of the primary Beings and in the case of what is above all, how anything is up to it, even if we agree that it is omnipotent.

> For this reason, we do not designate the actions of bad people, who do many things according to their imaginative representations, as being up to them or voluntary. But we will designate as autonomous those who, due to intellect, are free of the affections of the body. In explaining what is up to us by reference to the most beautiful principle, we will grant that the activity of intellect and the premises arising from it are truly free.

**Greek terms:** τὸ ἐφ᾽ ἡμῖν (to eph' hēmin) — what is up to us; τὸ αὐτεξούσιον (to autexousion) — autonomy; βούλησις (boulēsis) — will; προαίρεσις (proairesis) — choice; νοῦς (nous) — intellect

---

### VI.8.4-6 — Intellect's Freedom; Activity and Substance Are Identical

**Context:** Intellect is free because its activity and substantiality are identical. It is not enslaved to its nature because it is its nature. Freedom in the full sense belongs to the activity of Intellect, not to action.

> Still, one should investigate how something occurring in accordance with desire will fall under the autonomy of the agent, because desire drives the agent towards something external, and contains a deficiency. The thing desiring is driven, even if it is driven towards the Good.

> Moreover, a problem about Intellect itself arises: since it acts by nature based on what it is by nature, should it be said to be free and are things up to it when it is not up to it not to produce some result?

> One can speak of slavery to one's own nature if you distinguish between the thing enslaved and what it is enslaved to. For how could a simple nature, that is, one activity, which is not different potentially and actually, not be free? For you could not say that it acts according to its nature, such that its substantiality is one thing and its activity is another, if indeed in the intelligible world existence and acting are identical. If then, the activity is not because of another thing nor up to another thing, how can it not be free?

> Theoretical intellect, that is, primary Intellect is up to itself, since its function is never up to something else. On the contrary, it reverted entirely towards itself and its function, lying itself in the Good with no deficiency and fulfilled, living in a way in conformity to will. Its will is intellection, but it is called 'will' because its activity accords with Intellect.

**Greek terms:** ἐνέργεια (energeia) — activity; οὐσία (ousia) — substantiality; δύναμις (dynamis) — potency; βούλησις (boulēsis) — will; νόησις (noēsis) — intellection

---

### VI.8.7-8 — The Reckless Argument; The Good Is Beyond Chance and Necessity

**Context:** The 'reckless argument' claims the Good is not free because it does not control what it is. Plotinus refutes this: the Good is not by chance, not under necessity, but is prior to both. It is what it is not by constraint but by being the best.

> This argument uses a different premise, and claims that, since the nature of the Good is disposed as it is by chance, and is not master of what it is, and since it is not what it is on account of itself, it will have neither freedom nor what is up to it, whether or not it produces the effects which it is forced to produce or not to produce.

> This argument is certainly repellent and without support; it does away with the nature of the voluntary and of autonomy, and the conception of what is up to us, in that, on this assumption, it would be fruitless to say these things, being mere sounds referring to non-existent things.

> We see that autonomy is not an accident of the Good; rather, we see that it has this autonomy by stripping away the contraries from things with autonomy in other respects. In transferring lesser attributes from lesser beings to the Good, on account of our inability to grasp those things which should be said of it, we would like to say the following about it; yet we are in no position to find anything to say about it, let alone anything properly applicable to it.

**Greek terms:** τύχη (tychē) — chance; ἀνάγκη (anankē) — necessity; αὐτεξούσιον (autexousion) — autonomy; ἀφαίρεσις (aphairesis) — stripping away; πρῶτον (prōton) — first

---

### VI.8.9-10 — The One Is Not Accidentally What It Is; It Is Its Own Ground

**Context:** The One cannot be accidentally what it is, for it is prior to all accidents. It is not what it 'happens to be' but what it 'had to be' — not by external necessity but by its own nature which is the source of all necessity.

> What you should actually say in reply to this kind of thought is that it is not possible that any chance thing can be the Good, since it is principle of everything, not because any chance thing is worse, but because it is not good in some respect and not in others, like a deficiency; no, the principle of everything must be better than everything posterior to it so that the Good is something bounded. I mean by 'bounded', that it is unique, and is not what it is by necessity. For there is no necessity in it; necessity is in the things following the principle, and even in them it has no force.

> So, if 'it is accidentally' does not apply even to Being — for something belongs to being accidentally, if it is accidentally, but Being itself is not accidentally — then neither does Being come to be what it is accidentally, nor is it happenstance that Being is what it is, nor did it get to be the way it is from another; rather, its nature itself truly is to be Being. How can anyone apply in thought 'it is accidentally what it is' to something that transcends Being? For it has generated Being, which itself is not accidentally what it is.

**Greek terms:** συμβεβηκός (symbebēkos) — accident; ἀνάγκη (anankē) — necessity; ἐπέκεινα οὐσίας (epekeina ousias) — beyond Being; ἀρχή (archē) — principle; μονάς (monas) — solitary

---

### VI.8.13-16 — The Good Is What It Wills to Be; Self-Identity as Freedom

**Context:** The Good's substantiality and its will are identical. It does not 'have' freedom — it is freedom. Its self-identity is the ground of all freedom in things that derive from it.

> The Good is will entirely; it cannot produce itself other than it does. There is identity between the substantiality of the Good and its will. To contemplate the Good, one has to do away with all other predicates. It is master of itself, being what it wants, and even more so distributing what it wants to Beings, being itself greater than any wanting, and itself positing wanting as posterior to itself. Neither, then, did it want to be 'what it is' such that it would follow on something else, nor has any other god made it what it is.

**Greek terms:** θέλησις (thelēsis) — willing; ταὐτότης (tautotēs) — identity; οὐσία (ousia) — substantiality; ἐξουσία (exousia) — mastery; δύναμις (dynamis) — power

---

### VI.8.21 — The One's Wishing; Freedom as Self-Constitution

**Context:** The concluding movement of the treatise: the One is what it is because it wills to be what it is. Its existence and its activity are one, and this unity is the ultimate meaning of freedom.

> So, the One is what it is by itself, and it is what it wills to be. For its being and its willing are not two, but one. And its being is not determined by anything else, nor is its willing. It is, therefore, self-constituted, and its freedom is not an attribute added to it but is identical with its being.

**Greek terms:** αὐτό (auto) — itself; βούλησις (boulēsis) — will; τὸ εἶναι (to einai) — being; ταὐτότης (tautotēs) — identity; ἐλευθερία (eleutheria) — freedom
