# ennead 26: the one's freedom: beyond will and necessity

treatise 26 of the enneads: the one's freedom: beyond will and necessity
plotinus said the one is not free because it can choose to be something else — it is free because there is nothing else it could possibly be. the highest freedom is not the power to choose. it is the impossibility of being less than yourself.

plotinus wrote this as treatise 26 of the enneads. here is what he said and why it still matters.

Is the One free? The question seems impossible. Freedom belongs to agents who can choose otherwise. The One simply is — beyond choosing. And yet the One is beyond compulsion — beyond the distinction between freedom and necessity.

> The One does not act by necessity, for necessity implies something external that constrains. Nor does it act by will, for will implies a lack that must be fulfilled. The One is beyond both. Its freedom is its self-identity: it is what it is, and what it is is good.

*Ἐλεύθερον.* Free. But free in a sense that transcends our understanding of freedom. Our freedom is the power to choose among alternatives. The One's freedom is the power to be itself — which is not a choice because there is nothing else it could be.

Plotinus begins his investigation by asking what we mean by 'what is up to us':

> For this reason, we do not designate the actions of bad people, who do many things according to their imaginative representations, as being up to them or voluntary. But we will designate as autonomous those who, due to intellect, are free of the affections of the body. In explaining what is up to us by reference to the most beautiful principle, we will grant that the activity of intellect and the premises arising from it are truly free.

> The One is not free because it can do otherwise. It is free because it is entirely self-caused. Nothing outside it determines it. It determines itself — not by an act of will but by the simplicity of its own nature.

*Αὐτεξούσιον.* Self-determined. The One is the cause of itself, not in the sense that it brought itself into being but in the sense that its being is not dependent on anything else. Everything that exists depends on the One. The One depends on nothing.

Plotinus extends the inquiry to Intellect itself:

> Still, one should investigate how something occurring in accordance with desire will fall under the autonomy of the agent, because desire drives the agent towards something external, and contains a deficiency. The thing desiring is driven, even if it is driven towards the Good.

> Moreover, a problem about Intellect itself arises: since it acts by nature based on what it is by nature, should it be said to be free and are things up to it when it is not up to it not to produce some result?

> One can speak of slavery to one's own nature if you distinguish between the thing enslaved and what it is enslaved to. For how could a simple nature, that is, one activity, which is not different potentially and actually, not be free? For you could not say that it acts according to its nature, such that its substantiality is one thing and its activity is another, if indeed in the intelligible world existence and acting are identical. If then, the activity is not because of another thing nor up to another thing, how can it not be free?

> If we say that the One wills the good, we are speaking improperly. For the One does not will the good. It is the Good. Will is the movement of what lacks toward what it needs. The One lacks nothing. Its activity is not a striving but an overflow.

The One's production of Intellect is an overflow of fullness — not a choice, not a decision to create. The overflow is a consequence of nature, not an act of will. Yet it is uncompelled, because compulsion is the constraint of what is not full. The One is so full that it cannot help but overflow, and this "cannot help" is the highest freedom — not a limitation.

This is the insight that reframes freedom entirely: the One does not act because it must or because it chooses. It acts because it is — and its being is activity.

Plotinus refutes the claim that the Good's nature is accidental:

> What you should actually say in reply to this kind of thought is that it is not possible that any chance thing can be the Good, since it is principle of everything, not because any chance thing is worse, but because it is not good in some respect and not in others, like a deficiency; no, the principle of everything must be better than everything posterior to it so that the Good is something bounded. I mean by 'bounded', that it is unique, and is not what it is by necessity. For there is no necessity in it; necessity is in the things following the principle, and even in them it has no force.

> So, if 'it is accidentally' does not apply even to Being — for something belongs to being accidentally, if it is accidentally, but Being itself is not accidentally — then neither does Being come to be what it is accidentally, nor is it happenstance that Being is what it is, nor did it get to be the way it is from another; rather, its nature itself truly is to be Being. How can anyone apply in thought 'it is accidentally what it is' to something that transcends Being? For it has generated Being, which itself is not accidentally what it is.

> The freedom of the One is the freedom of absolute self-possession. The One is not pushed from outside. It is not pulled from inside. It simply is, and what it is is the source of all that is. Its freedom is its being.

For human beings, freedom is analogous. We are free when we are most ourselves — not when we can choose anything — when our actions flow from our true nature, not from compulsion, addiction, or external pressure.

> The virtuous person is free because his actions flow from his character. The wise person is free because his knowledge flows from his nature. And the One is free because its being flows from itself. Freedom is not the absence of determination but the identity of determination with nature.

The highest freedom is the impossibility of being otherwise — because what one is, is perfect — not the power to do otherwise. The One is the Good — it does not choose it. And that is the deepest freedom there is.

Plotinus concludes:

> So, the One is what it is by itself, and it is what it wills to be. For its being and its willing are not two, but one. And its being is not determined by anything else, nor is its willing. It is, therefore, self-constituted, and its freedom is not an attribute added to it but is identical with its being.

And you, when you are most yourself — when your actions flow from your deepest nature without compulsion — participate in that freedom. You are not free despite your nature. You are free through it.
