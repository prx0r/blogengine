# ennead 17: the soul does not die

treatise 17 of the enneads: the soul does not die
plotinus said the soul cannot die — not because a god promises it, but because it has no parts. what is simple cannot be unmade. what has no pieces cannot fall apart.

plotinus wrote this as treatise 17 of the enneads. here is what he said and why it still matters.

The soul is a self-moving substance — not a harmony of the body, not the body's life that would end when the body ends — and because it moves itself, it cannot cease to be.

> The soul is not a harmony, for a harmony is something that results from the arrangement of parts. But the soul exists before the body and can exist without it. The soul is a substance, not a property.

The argument against the harmonic theory: if the soul were a harmony of bodily parts, it could not oppose the body. But the soul does oppose the body — it restrains desires, corrects perceptions, judges the body's impulses. What is merely a result cannot judge its cause.

Plotinus refutes the materialist conception directly:

> Since life is of necessity present to soul, it is necessary that, in the case of this [supposed] body which is the soul, if it is composed out of two or more bodies, either or each of them must possess life innately, or one of them would possess it and the other not, or neither or none of them. After all, if life were actually present to any one of them, then this very one would be soul.

> We must, therefore, have something that can provide the life, whether this provision is made to matter itself or to any other sort of body, which originates from outside, and transcends any corporeal nature. After all, no body would even exist if the soul's power did not exist. For body is in flux, and its nature is subject to constant motion, and it would rapidly self-destruct, if all things were bodies, even if one of them were given the name of soul.

> The soul also cannot be a body. For bodies are composite and divisible, but the soul is simple and indivisible. A body can be divided, and when divided, it ceases to be what it was. But the soul remains one in its acts, even when it is present to many parts of the body.

The soul is present to the whole body at once. The same soul sees through both eyes, hears through both ears, moves both hands. It is not distributed across the body like a physical thing. It is present as a whole in every part.

This is the argument that has never been refuted: if the soul has no parts, nothing in it can separate. And what cannot separate cannot die.

> The soul is immortal because it participates in Intellect, which participates in the One. It does not have life as a property. It is life itself — not the life that comes and goes but the life that is the source of all living.

*Ζωή.* Life. Not biological life but the life that is being itself. The soul is alive not because it is in a body but because it is an expression of the primal life that flows from the One.

The soul is the self-moving source of all motion:

> The other nature, however, that which has its existence from itself, is all that is really Being which neither comes to be nor is destroyed; indeed, all other things will pass away, and never return again, if that suffered destruction which preserves them, other things, and, above all, this universe as a whole which is preserved and endowed with order by means of soul. For this serves as 'principle of motion', providing motion to all other things, while is itself moved by itself, bestowing life on the ensouled body, while possessing it of itself, which it never loses, inasmuch as it has it from itself.

> For it is certainly not the case that everything enjoys a life sourced from outside — that would lead to an infinite regress — but there must be some nature that is primarily alive, which must necessarily be 'indestructible and immortal', inasmuch as it serves as a principle of life for all other things as well.

> If the soul were destructible, all things would eventually cease to be, because the soul is the principle of life for the body. But the intelligible world is eternal, and the soul belongs to it. The soul cannot cease to be any more than Intellect can cease to think.

Plotinus invites us to examine the purified soul:

> Since we are indeed agreed that the whole world of the divine and really real is endowed with 'a life of goodness and intelligence', we must consider next after this, taking our start from our own soul, what would be its nature. Let us for this purpose focus on a soul that has not, in the body, taken to itself non-rational appetites and passions and made itself the receptacle of other emotions, but one that has cleansed itself of these, and as far as possible has nothing in common with the body.

> Regarding a thing of this sort, who endowed with good sense would dispute that it is immortal? It is, after all, something endowed of itself with life, which it is not capable of losing. How indeed could soul be destroyed, since life is not added to it from outside, nor does it have it in the way that heat is present to fire?

The strongest argument: everything that perishes does so by being separated into its parts. But the soul has no parts — it is simple, with nothing in it that can come apart.

> The body dies because its parts separate. The soul does not die because it has no parts. It is one, and what is one cannot be destroyed. The soul's immortality is a consequence of its simplicity.

And because the soul is immortal, the death of the body is not an end. It is a transition — a release from the instrument that the soul has been using. The musician is not destroyed when the instrument breaks. The soul is not destroyed when the body dies.

> What happens after death is determined by what the soul has become during life. The soul does not go to a place. It goes to a state. And that state is the natural result of what the soul has made of itself. The soul that has become pure goes to the pure. The soul that has become attached goes to what it is attached to.

The soul's immortality is a guarantee of continuity — not a guarantee of bliss. The soul persists, and its persistence is shaped by its choices. The soul that has purified itself continues in purity. The soul that has entangled itself continues in entanglements. But the soul continues. You are not a candle that can be blown out. You are the light itself — and light does not die. It only changes what it illuminates.
