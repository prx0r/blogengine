## Key Passages

### 1. Who Ficino Is

**Source**: Letter to Canigiani (1476), quoted in Voss thesis
**De vita** III.23 — natural bent and genius
**De vita** Gen. Proem — three-book structure

Ficino describes himself as a "doctor of body and soul" (letter to Canigiani). The *De vita* was written by a priest, physician, Platonist, astrologer, and scholar — all simultaneously.

Voss (1992, p.255): "It must not be forgotten that Ficino was both a priest and a physician, and that his wide range of activities included interaction and communication with the ordinary Florentine citizen, whether in Church or at their bedside."

### 2. Spiritus as the Key Mediator

**Source**: **De vita** I.2

The core passage on spiritus. Ficino compares scholars to craftsmen who care for their tools, then says scholars neglect the instrument by which they grasp the world.

Latin anchors:
- *instrumentum* — the instrument
- *spiritus* — the spirit
- *vapor sanguinis* — vapour of blood

The chain: stomach/liver → blood → heart refines blood into spirit → spirit rises to brain → serves senses → senses serve reason.

Analysis: Spiritus is not modern "spirit" in the vague sense. It is a subtle physiological-psychological medium. Appears in all three books in different keys: physiological (Book I), preservative (Book II), cosmic (Book III).

### 3. The Scholar as Test Case

**Source**: **De vita** I.2, I.3–4

The scholar is the human being whose soul reaches upward but whose body is damaged by that upward effort. See Essay 2 for full treatment of I.3–4 on the bodily cost of study.

**De vita** I.4 — "circumference to the centre" — the soul withdraws inward during contemplation, and this inward motion physiologically produces the melancholic condition.

**De vita** I.4 — philosophy half-kills the body: "the more difficult the work, the more the body is disjoined and becomes almost half-alive."

### 4. Astrology is Not Fatalism

**Source**: **De vita** III.16–20 (rays and reception)
**De vita** III.23 (natural bent, star, genius)
**De vita** II.20 (septenary years — life not absolutely fixed)

Voss (1992, Introduction): Ficino's astrology is "closer to diagnosis, timing, and remediation. The planets incline, intensify, burden, or support; the art is to receive and balance influence rather than passively submit."

**De vita** II.20 — Ficino appeals to Pietro d'Abano: natural lifespan can be moved nearer or farther by stars and material things; doctors and astrologers both matter.

### 5. Book III is Dangerous

**Source**: **De vita** III.26 + Apology

The Apology rests on four pillars: medicine, astrology, life of the world, Magi at Christ's birth. Ficino walks a line between natural magic and Christian legitimacy.

**De vita** III.1 — Zoroaster called correspondences "divine lures" and Synesius called them "magical baits."

### 6. How to Read the Remedies

**Source**: **De vita** I.13–25 (entire pharmacology section)

The remedies are historical medicine as metaphysical evidence. The point is not "take Ficino's pills" but: thought depends on a bodily-spiritual medium that can be altered by food, herbs, blood, air, taste, smell, sleep, and imagination.

## Post-Essay Review

### Initial vs Required
The initial sourcematerial.md for this orientation essay listed concepts (spiritus, the scholar as test case, astrology as non-fatalism) with short descriptions and chapter references. For example: "**De vita** I.2 — The core passage on spiritus. Ficino compares scholars to craftsmen..." This was a description of the passage, not the passage itself.

The finished essay required the actual words: "Painters care for their brushes. Soldiers care for their weapons. Musicians care for their instruments. Yet scholars neglect the instrument by which they measure and grasp the whole world. That instrument is the spirit, produced from subtle blood, refined by the heat of the heart, rising to the brain..." with the Latin original: *Anima et corpus natura longe inter se diversa spiritu medio copulantur...*

### Lesson
An orientation essay needs even MORE source material, not less, because it must establish the primary text's voice before any analysis begins. The reader needs to hear Ficino speaking before they hear the commentator explaining.
