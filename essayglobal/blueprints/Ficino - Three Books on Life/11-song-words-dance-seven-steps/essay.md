# Song, Words, Dance, and the Seven Steps

The essay is not about "planetary music" as a vague concept. It is about embodied imitation — the human being becoming a performative correspondence of the heavens.

*De vita* III.21 opens with Ficino's advice to observe celestial positions and imitate them through speech, song, movement, dance, moral behaviour, and action. The sequence is not arbitrary. It descends from the most inward to the most outward: the music of the soul becomes the music of the body.

"The first music takes place in reason, the second in fantasy, and the third in words. Thence follows song, and after that the movement of the fingers in sound. Lastly the movement of the whole body in gymnastics or dancing. Thus the music of the soul is led by steps to all the limbs of the body."

*Prima musica in ratione est, secunda in phantasia, tertia in verbis. Deinde cantus, postea motus digitorum in sono. Ultimo totius corporis motus in gymnasio aut saltatione.*

Reason, fantasy, words, song, gesture, dance. The soul's interior harmony descends through stages into embodied expression. Each stage is a mode of participation in the same celestial order.

In his letter to Canigiani, Ficino describes the mechanism by which sound moves the soul:

"For sound and song arise from consideration in the mind, the impulse of fantasy and the desire of the heart, and in disturbing the air and lending measure to it they vibrate the airy spirit of the listener, which is the link between body and soul. Thus sound and song easily arouse the fantasy, affect the heart, and reach the inmost recesses of the mind; they still, and also set in motion, the humours and the limbs of the body."

*Nam quum cantus sonusque ex cogitatione mentis, et impetu phantasiae, cordisque affectu proficiscatur, atque una cum aere facto et temperato, aereum audientis spiritum pulset, qui animae corporisque nodus est, facile phantasiam movet, afficitque cor et intima mentis penetralia penetrat. Corporis quoque humores, et membra sistit, et movet.*

The chain is: mind produces the conception. Fantasy gives it form. The heart gives it desire. The voice gives it sound. The sound disturbs the air. The disturbed air touches the listener's spirit. The listener's spirit arouses fantasy, affects the heart, and reaches the mind. Sound is the carrier wave for spiritual influence.

*Mens concipit. Phantasia format. Cor desiderat. Vox sonat. Aer movetur. Spiritus auditoris tangitur.*

Ficino cites Pythagoras and Empedocles as examples of magi who used serious music to calm unruly passions and different modes to stimulate lazy minds. He modelled his own practice on these precedents. He himself often sang to the lyre, as he writes, "to banish vexations of both soul and body, and to raise the mind to the highest considerations and to God as much as I may."

*Ipse saepe ad lyram cantabat, ut animae et corporis molestias depelleret, mentemque ad altissimas considerationes et ad Deum erigeret.*

*De vita* III.22 completes the musical theory:

"Celestial gifts enter bodies through rightly prepared spirit. Higher gifts reach souls through rays, affection, free will, and prayer-like orientation."

*Dona coelestia corpora intrant per spiritum rite praeparatum. Dona altiora animas attingunt per radios, affectionem, liberum arbitrium, et orationem.*

The ascent has three levels. Prepared spirit receives bodily gifts. Rays, affection, and free will receive soul gifts. Prayer, study, and conduct produce greater likeness.

"Some powers are harmful to some, favourable to others. Celestial influence is not one-size-fits-all."

*Quaedam potentiae aliis nocivae sunt, aliis favent. Influxus coelestis non est uniformis.*

The same music that helps one person may harm another. Celestial influence must be individually discerned. Ficino's astrology and his music both require the same skill: knowing the nature of the one who receives.

"Spirit, which is the airy vapour of our blood and the link between body and soul, is tempered and nourished by airy smells, by sounds, and by song. In nature a union is made from soul, body and spirit. To the Egyptian priests medicine, music and the mysteries were one and the same study."

*Spiritus autem qui aereus sanguinis vaporem, et quasi quidam animae corporisque nodus, aereis quoque odoribus sonisque et cantibus temperatur, et aliter... Apud naturam unum quiddam est ex anima et corpore, spirituque compositum. Apud Aegyptios sacerdotes una eademque facultas erat, pharmacis fidibus, mysteriis occupata.*

Medicine, music, and the mysteries were one study. To sing is to participate in cosmic order. The human voice, rightly tuned, becomes an instrument of celestial harmony.

The seven steps toward celestial things that Ficino describes in III.21-22 are the practical application of the musical theory. Each step is a mode of accommodation — a way of making the human being capable of receiving a specific celestial quality. The steps are not arbitrary. They follow the same logic as the ladder from the One to material reception: each step prepares the one that follows.

*Septem gradus ad res coelestes sunt applicatio practica theoriae musicalis. Quodlibet gradus est modus accommodationis.*

The art of celestial music is the art of making the soul capable of receiving the quality it desires through the medium of sound. The singer who sings a hymn to the Sun while the Sun is rising, who uses the right mode and the right words and the right intention, will receive solar influence because his voice has made his spirit conformable to the Sun.

*Ars musicae coelestis est ars faciendi animam capacem recipiendi qualitatem quam desiderat per medium soni.*

To sing is to become like what you sing. To sing a solar hymn is to become solar. To sing a Jovial hymn is to become Jovial. The voice is not merely an instrument of expression. It is an instrument of transformation.
