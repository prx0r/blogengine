# Food, Putrefaction, Gold, Milk, and Blood

Book II of the *Liber de vita* treats aging as an ecology of intake, decay, and preservation. The deeper argument is about preservation against putrefaction, incorruptibility as a material-spiritual ideal, and vital transfer as the mechanism by which life passes through matter.

*De vita* II.6 opens the material ecology: life is a continuous replacement problem. The body is always spending its substance and must be replenished through food. Eating is not lifestyle. It is the daily rebuilding of life itself.

*Corpus semper substantiam suam expendit et repleri debet. Cibus non est vitae genus sed ipsa vita reficienda.*

The body is always spending its substance and must be replenished. Food is not a lifestyle choice but the very rebuilding of life.

*De vita* II.7 names the enemy: putrefaction. Putrefaction is the breakdown of ordered life against corruption. Bad foods and bad regions accelerate this breakdown. Putrefaction matters because old age is not abstract decline. It is the breakdown of the body's capacity to preserve spirit. The enemy is not time but decay.

*Putredo est dissolutio vitae ordinatae. Senectus est defectus spiritus conservandi. Hostis non tempus est sed putredo.*

Putrefaction is the dissolution of ordered life. Old age is the failure to preserve spirit. The enemy is not time but putrefaction.

The concept of putrefaction links Book II to the alchemical tradition. In alchemy, putrefaction is the first stage of transformation — the breakdown of the old form so that a new form can emerge. Ficino is not using the word casually. He is drawing on a tradition in which decay and renewal are two phases of the same process.

*Putredo in alchemia est prima transformatio. Ficinus hanc traditionem sequitur.*

The foods and regions that accelerate putrefaction are those that are already close to decay themselves. Old meat, stale bread, stagnant water — these carry the seeds of corruption into the body. The foods that resist putrefaction are those that are fresh, warm, and alive. Ficino's dietary advice is not arbitrary. It follows from his understanding of life as a struggle against dissolution.

*Cibi qui putredinem accelerant sunt qui iam prope corruptionem sunt. Cibi qui putredini resistunt sunt recentes, calidi, vivi.*

The body that eats decaying food is eating death. The body that eats living food is eating life. This is not a metaphor. It is the physiological foundation of Ficino's longevity practices.

*Putredo in alchemia est prima transformatio. Ficinus hanc traditionem sequitur.*

---

*De vita* II.8 and II.9 give specific guidance for the elderly diet: wine, wheat, spices, and cordials that preserve warmth and moisture. The logic is consistent with Book I: the instrument must be maintained through what enters it.

*Vinum, triticum, aromata, cordialia. Haec calorem et humorem conservant.*

The elderly diet is designed to counteract the natural drying and cooling of the body. Wine warms. Wheat nourishes. Spices stimulate. Cordials restore. Each element of the diet addresses a specific aspect of the aging process.

*De vita* II.10 introduces a substance that stands outside the ordinary economy of decay:

"Gold is incorruptible, solar, bright, noble, stable, anti-putrefactive."

*Aurum est incorruptibile, solare, splendidum, nobile, stabile, antiputredinosum.*

Gold images permanence. It does not rust, rot, or decay. In Ficino's symbolic-medical world, gold belongs both to the preservation of the body and to the solarisation of the spirit that Book III will describe. The same gold that preserves the aging body will later become a vehicle for celestial reception.

*Aurum est imago incorruptibilitatis. Idem aurum quod corpus in II conservat in III vehiculum receptionis coelestis fit.*

Gold is not merely a symbol. Ficino believed that gold could be ingested in a form that would transfer its incorruptibility to the body. The spirit of gold, separated from its gross matter through sublimation, could be taken as medicine. This is not magic. It is applied alchemy — the belief that the qualities of a substance can be transferred to the one who ingests it.

*Spiritus auri per sublimationem separatus ut medicina sumi potest. Non est magia sed alchemia applicata.*

The gold that preserves the body in Book II is the same gold that will become a vehicle for celestial reception in Book III. In *De vita* III.14, gold appears again as a substance belonging to the Sun. The solarisation of the spirit that Book III describes — the process of making the spirit solar through the reception of celestial influence — is prefigured in Book II by the ingestion of gold, which makes the body more like gold: incorruptible, stable, solar.

*Aurum in II corpus conservat. Aurum in III spiritum solarem facit. Idem aurum, idem principium.*

The same gold, the same principle. The continuity between Book II and Book III is visible in the simplest substance.

*De vita* II.11 extends the logic of preservation into vital transfer. Human milk and blood can carry life, youth, and substance from one body to another. This is not a practical recommendation. It is evidence of how far Ficino's theory of vital transfer goes.

Ficino's discussion of human milk and blood is often the most startling passage in Book II for modern readers. The idea that drinking the blood of a young person could extend life seems barbaric. But Ficino is not recommending vampirism. He is working within a theoretical framework in which the qualities of a substance — including its vitality — can be transferred through ingestion. If the spirit is a vapour of blood, then the blood of a young person, whose spirit is still strong, carries more life than the blood of an old person, whose spirit is depleted.

*Sanguis iuvenis plus vitae habet quam sanguis senis. Haec non est magia sed theoria spiritalis.*

The theory is consistent. It is not sensational. The same principle that makes gold a medicine because of its incorruptibility makes young blood a medicine because of its vitality. Both are applications of the same logic: like preserves like.

*Lac humanum et sanguis vitam, iuventutem, substantiam de corpore ad corpus transferre possunt.*

Life is not an abstract property. It is a substance that can be carried by blood, by milk, by breath. The vitality of a young person can, in principle, be transferred to an older person through the medium of the body's fluids. This is the logical extension of the spiritus doctrine: if the spirit is a vapour of blood, then the quality of the blood determines the quality of the spirit, and the quality of the spirit determines the quality of life.

*Si spiritus est vapor sanguinis, qualitas sanguinis qualitatem spiritus determinat.*

*De vita* II.12 adds that dwelling and custom matter as much as ingestion. Longevity is not what you take in but where you are, what you do, who you are with. Place, climate, habit, and continuity structure the conditions under which life is preserved or lost.

*Habitatio et consuetudo non minus quam cibus valent. Longaevitas non est ingestio sed locus, climate, habitus, continentia.*

---

The later chapters of Book II begin to lean toward Book III. *De vita* II.13 states that planets support bodily members; the body ages within a cosmic ecology. *De vita* II.14 describes how Venus brings green fields and restorative delight — pleasure, greenness, and beauty become medicine when rightly ordered. *De vita* II.15 gives Mercury's contribution: odours, song, and medicines that operate on the subtle body.

*Planetae membra corporis sustinent. Venus campos virentes affert. Mercurius odores, cantus, medicinas subtiles.*

*De vita* II.16 returns to a theme from Book I: thought and sex both spend vital force. In old age, expenditure matters more than ever. *De vita* II.18 identifies odours and honey as substances that preserve and nourish the spirit directly — a bridge from ordinary to subtle medicine. *De vita* II.19 introduces the Medicine of the Magi, which anticipates Book III's magical turn.

*De vita* II.20 — "Each seventh year brings a major bodily change because rulership returns from Saturn to the Moon. These are climacteric years."

"Natural lifespan can be moved nearer or farther by stars and material things. Doctors and astrologers both matter."

---

The emphasis on dwelling and custom in II.12 connects this essay to the broader argument of the *Liber de vita*. The scholar who lives in the wrong place will age faster, not because the place is unhealthy in itself but because it does not resonate with his nature. The preservation of life requires alignment — alignment of food with body, of place with person, of habit with nature.

*Conservatio vitae congruentiam requirit. Congruentia cibi cum corpore, loci cum persona, habitus cum natura.*

The theme that unites these chapters is preservation against corruption. Gold symbolises incorruptibility. Milk and blood represent life as transferable nourishment. Putrefaction is the breaking point — the moment when the body can no longer preserve the spirit.

Ficino's material ecology is a theory of how life resists its own dissolution through right intake, right place, and right habit. The body that eats well, dwells well, and lives well is a body that holds its form against time.

*Theoria Ficini est quomodo vita dissolutionem resistit per rectum cibum, rectum locum, rectum habitum. Corpus quod bene edit, bene habitat, bene vivit, formam suam contra tempus tenet.*

The body that eats well, dwells well, and lives well is a body that holds its form against time. This is the message of Book II's strangest chapters — a message that is not about diet or gold or milk but about the fundamental nature of life as an ordered resistance to disorder.

*Vita est ordo contra chaos. Conservatio est resistentia contra dissolutionem.*

Life is order against chaos. Preservation is resistance against dissolution.
