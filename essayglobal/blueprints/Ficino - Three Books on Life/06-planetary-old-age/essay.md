# Planetary Old Age and the Seven-Year Dangers

The later chapters of Book II begin to lean toward Book III. Old age is treated not only through diet but through planets, pleasure, scent, song, and the rhythmic structure of time itself.

*De vita* II.13 states the principle: planets support bodily members. The body does not age in isolation. It ages within a cosmic ecology, and the planets that presided over its birth continue to influence its decline.

This chapter is the turning point of Book II. Before it, the discussion has been about food, gold, milk, blood, and dwelling — all material concerns. After it, the discussion shifts to planets, odours, honey, Magian medicine, and the septenary years. The shift is gradual but unmistakable:Book II is leading toward Book III.

*Caput II.13 est punctum conversionis libri II. Ante: cibus, aurum, lac, sanguis. Post: planetae, odores, mel, magia. Liber II ad III ducit.*

The principle that planets support bodily members is the foundation for everything that follows. It means that the body is not a closed system. It is open to the heavens at every moment, and the quality of its aging depends partly on the quality of the planetary influences it receives. The old person who lives under a favourable Jupiter will age differently from one who lives under a harsh Saturn — not because of fate but because of influence.

*Planetae membra corporis sustentant. Corpus non solum sed cum astris senescit.*

The planets are not distant observers. They are active participants in the life of the body, supporting or weakening the members they rule. The Sun governs the heart and vitality. The Moon governs the fluids and the digestive system. Saturn governs the bones and the spleen. When a planet is weakened by its position in the sky, the corresponding part of the body is weakened as well.

*De vita* II.14 describes how Venus brings green fields and restorative delight. Pleasure, greenness, and beauty become medicine when rightly ordered. For Ficino, delight can be therapeutic. The old person who surrounds himself with green fields and beautiful things is not indulging in luxury. He is receiving planetary medicine through the senses.

*Venus campos virentes et gaudium restitutivum affert. Delectatio medicina est. Non est luxuria sed medicina planetaria.*

*De vita* II.15 gives Mercury's contribution: odours, song, and medicines that operate on the subtle body. Mercury is mobile and communicative. Its medicine travels through air and through sound. This prepares Book III's treatment of music as celestial medicine. The same Mercury that governs communication also governs the transmission of influence from the heavens to the earth.

*Mercurius odores, cantus, medicinas subtiles affert. Eius medicina per aerem et sonum iter facit.*

*De vita* II.16 returns to a theme from Book I: thought and sex both spend vital force. In old age, expenditure matters more than ever. The scholar who continues intense study in old age must manage depletion differently than the young scholar. The same mental activity that was sustainable at thirty becomes dangerous at sixty.

*Cogitatio et coitus vim vitalem consumunt. In senectute, sumptus maximi momenti est. Idem studium quod triginta annos tolerabile est sexaginta periculosum fit.*

*De vita* II.18 identifies odours and honey as substances that preserve and nourish the spirit directly. This is the bridge from ordinary to subtle medicine. Scent operates on the spiritus without passing through the digestive system. It is the most direct form of material medicine before we enter the realm of celestial reception.

*Odores et mel spiritum directe nutriunt. Hi sunt pontes inter medicinam ordinariam et subtilem. Odor spiritum attingit sine digestione.*

*De vita* II.19 introduces the Medicine of the Magi. This chapter anticipates Book III's magical turn before the full celestial machinery appears. Ficino is already preparing the reader for what comes next.

*Medicina Magorum librum tertium praefigurat. Iam hic, in libro secundo, magia naturalis apparet.*

*De vita* II.20 contains the philosophical heart of Book II:

"Each seventh year brings a major bodily change because rulership returns from Saturn to the Moon. These are climacteric years."

"Natural lifespan can be moved nearer or farther by stars and material things. Doctors and astrologers both matter."

*Quolibet septennio corpus magnam mutationem patitur, quia dominatio a Saturno ad Lunam redit. Vita naturalis propius aut longius moveri potest per stellas et res materiales.*

Time is not empty duration. It is patterned by planetary sequence. The body changes according to temporal rulership. Each seventh year, rulership returns from Saturn to the Moon, and the body undergoes a major transition. Old age is not a gradual decline but a series of thresholds, each one a danger and an opportunity.

But Ficino is not a fatalist. The stars threaten and promise, but medicine and prudence can intervene. The same passage that describes the septenary dangers also affirms that natural lifespan can be moved. The bridge to Book III is now complete. The body ages within a cosmic ecology, and medicine without astrology is incomplete.

The septenary years are the culmination of Book II's argument about time. Time is not a neutral container for life. It is a structured sequence of planetary rulerships that shape the body's changes. The scholar who knows the rhythm of the septenary years can anticipate the dangers of aging and prepare for them. The scholar who ignores this rhythm will be surprised by each transition and weakened by each crisis.

*Septenarii sunt culmen argumenti libri II de tempore. Qui rhythmum novit, pericula anticipare potest. Qui ignorat, quaque mutatione debilitatur.*

The rhythm of the seven-year cycles connects Book II to Book III in a deeper way. The same planets that structure the years of a life are the planets that the astrologer consults for the timing of treatment. The same Moon that returns every seventh year to shift the body's rulership is the Moon that the physician watches for the preparation of medicines. Time and timing are the same thing, seen from different angles.

*Tempus et opportunitas eadem res sunt, ex diversis angulis visa.*

The bridge to Book III is now complete. The body ages within a cosmic ecology, and medicine without astrology is incomplete. The scholar who has followed Book II from its beginning in the epistemological argument of II.1 arrives at II.20 ready for the celestial medicine of Book III — not because the subject has changed but because it has been gradually expanded from diet to planets, from the body to the stars.

The transition from Book II to Book III is the transition from the question "how long will I live?" to the question "how will I live?" The septenary years answer the first question with precision: this long, with these dangers, at these thresholds. The celestial medicine of Book III answers the second question with an invitation: you can live not only longer but more fully, by opening yourself to the influences that the planets offer.

*Transitio a II ad III est transitio a "quam diu vivam?" ad "quomodo vivam?"*

The later chapters of Book II prepare the reader for this transition by showing that the body is already embedded in a cosmic ecology. The planets already support the bodily members. The seasons already affect the humours. The seven-year cycles already structure the changes of a lifetime. Book III does not introduce a new world. It reveals the world that has always been there.

*Liber III non novum mundum introducit. Mundum qui semper ibi erat revelat.*

The planetary old age that Ficino describes is not a decline. It is a transformation. Each seven-year threshold is an opportunity to shift the balance of influences, to receive new planetary support, to align the aging body with the celestial order that has been structuring it all along. Old age, in Ficino's hands, becomes a cosmic art.

*Senectus planetaria non est declinatio sed transformatio. Quodlibet septennium occasio est novum aequilibrium inveniendi.*

*Ars senescendi est ars coelestis. Corpore senescimus, anima crescimus.*

The art of aging is a celestial art. In body we age, in soul we grow.

The medicines of Book II prepare the body for the receptions of Book III. The odours and honey of II.18, the Magian medicine of II.19, and the septenary awareness of II.20 all serve a single purpose: to make the scholar capable of receiving celestial influence when Book III offers it. The body that has been preserved through these practices is a body that can be opened to the stars.

*Medicinae libri II corpus ad receptationes libri III praeparant. Corpus conservatum est corpus aperiendum.*

*Corpus senescit sed anima maturescit. Tempus non est hostis sed magister.*

The body ages but the soul matures. Time is not an enemy but a teacher.

The seven-year cycles that Ficino describes are not just a theory of aging. They are a discipline of attention. The scholar who knows that his seventh year brings a major change will pay attention to his body in that year. He will adjust his diet, his study habits, his social engagements. He will consult his astrologer and his physician. The knowledge of the cycle is itself a medicine.

*Septenarii non sunt theoria senectutis sed disciplina attentionis. Cognitio cycli ipsa medicina est.*

*Attendere corpori est sapere. Ignorare corpus est ignorare vitam.*

To attend to the body is wisdom. To ignore the body is to ignore life itself.

The planetary old age that Ficino describes is not a fate to be endured but an art to be practised. The scholar who learns the rhythms of the planets, who adjusts his life to the seven-year cycles, who receives the support of Venus and Mercury and the Medicine of the Magi — this scholar does not merely age. He ages well. And in aging well, he prepares himself for the celestial opening that Book III promises.

*Senectus planetaria non est fatum tolerandum sed ars exercenda. Qui bene senescit, ad apertionem coelestem se praeparat.*

The later chapters of Book II are the threshold between the material medicine of Book I and the celestial medicine of Book III. They stand at the boundary, looking both ways. They remind us that the body is always already embedded in a cosmic ecology, and that the art of medicine is incomplete without the art of astrology. Ficino's old age is not a solitary decline but a participation in the rhythms of the heavens — a final, deepening attunement to the order that has been there all along.

*Ultima capita libri II sunt limen inter I et III. Stant in fine, utrimque spectant. Mone nos corpus semper iam in oecologia cosmica immersum esse.*
The art of aging is the art of alignment — of body with soul, of soul with stars, of life with the order that sustains it. Ficino's planetary old age is not a decline. It is a deepening.
And in that deepening, the scholar finds not the end of his work but its fulfillment. The body prepared by Book I, preserved by Book II, now stands ready for the opening that Book III will bring.

*Senectus non est finis sed complementum. Corpus paratum est. Coelum apertum est.*
