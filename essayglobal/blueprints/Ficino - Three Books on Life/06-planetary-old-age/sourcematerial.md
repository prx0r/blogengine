## Passage 6.1 — Planetary Support for Bodily Members

**Source**: **De vita** II.13

**What it says**: Planets support bodily members. The bodily powers are linked to planetary powers.

**Analysis**: This is where Book II begins to lean toward Book III. Old age is not treated only through diet but through planetary support.

---

## Passage 6.2 — Venus and Green Fields

**Source**: **De vita** II.14

**What it says**: Venus brings green fields and restorative delight. Pleasure, greenness, beauty, and Venus become medicine.

**Analysis**: Not trivial pastoral decoration. For Ficino, delight can be therapeutic when rightly ordered. This is the bridge from ordinary medicine to subtle medicine.

---

## Passage 6.3 — Mercury: Odours, Song, Medicines

**Source**: **De vita** II.15

**What it says**: Mercury brings odours, song, and medicines. Mercury's medicine is subtle, mobile, communicative.

**Analysis**: This prepares Book III's sound and scent material. Mercury is the planetary bridge between bodily medicine and celestial reception.

---

## Passage 6.4 — Continual Thought and Sexual Depletion

**Source**: **De vita** II.16

**What it says**: Thought and sex both spend vital force. In old age, expenditure matters.

**Analysis**: Connects back to Essay 2 (the bodily cost of thought) but focuses on age. The scholar who continues intense study in old age must manage depletion differently.

---

## Passage 6.5 — Odours and Honey

**Source**: **De vita** II.18

**What it says**: Odours and honey preserve and nourish the spirit.

**Analysis**: Key bridge from ordinary medicine to subtle medicine. Scent operates on the spirit directly — it is the most subtle form of material medicine before we enter the realm of celestial reception.

---

## Passage 6.6 — Medicine of the Magi

**Source**: **De vita** II.19

**What it says**: The Medicine of the Magi anticipates Book III's magical turn. Magian medicine appears before the full celestial machinery.

**Analysis**: Book II already contains magian medicine. This prepares the reader for Book III without announcing it. Ficino is building the bridge chapter by chapter.

---

## Passage 6.7 — Septenary Years (THE CORE PASSAGE)

**Source**: **De vita** II.20 (Via Hygeia excerpt)

**What it says**: Ficino maps planetary order onto years of life, repeating seven-year cycles. Each seventh year brings a major bodily change because rulership returns from Saturn to the Moon. These are climacteric/decretorial years.

**The deeper idea**:
- Time is not empty duration
- Time is patterned by planetary sequence
- The body changes according to temporal rulership
- Old age requires knowing the rhythm of danger

**Also from this passage**: Ficino appeals to Pietro d'Abano and says natural lifespan can be moved nearer or farther by stars and material things; doctors and astrologers both matter.

**Analysis**: This is the philosophical hinge. Ficino is not a fatalist. The stars threaten and promise, but medicine and prudence can intervene. This matters for the whole book: celestial influence is real, but care is also real.

### Drafting Notes

This essay has a clear arc: from diet to planets to time itself. Begin with the mundane (planets support bodily members), build through the subtle (odours, honey, Magi medicine), and climax with the septenary years (II.20). The bridge to Book III should feel inevitable — by the end of Book II, the reader should understand that the body ages within a cosmic ecology, and that medicine without astrology is incomplete.

The key structural insight from the blueprint: "Ficino is not a fatalist. The stars threaten and promise, but medicine and prudence can intervene." This is the essay's pivot — old age is not merely endured but actively negotiated with the cosmos.

### Voss on Ficino's Saturn Cycle

From Voss (p.49): "After a period of profound inner turmoil in his twenties, Ficino was presented with the task of translating Hermes Trismegistus at the age of twenty-nine, the completion of the first Saturn cycle or 'Saturn return' in his life, a time of discrimination and rejection of outworn parental value-systems and the re-birth of an individual sense of destiny."

This personal context illuminates II.20's septenary theory. Ficino is not writing abstract astrology — he is describing his own experience of time as patterned by planetary cycles.

## Post-Essay Review

### Initial vs Required
The sourcematerial.md for Essay 06 had the II.20 septenary passage well identified via the Via Hygeia excerpt but treated II.13-19 as a list: "De vita II.13 — Planetary support for bodily members. Analysis: This is where Book II begins to lean toward Book III."

### The Gap
The finished essay needed the full planetary context — Venus bringing green fields, Mercury bringing odours and song, the Medicine of the Magi — each with its own analysis and connection to Book III. The transition from material medicine to planetary medicine needed to be traced chapter by chapter.

### Lesson
Transitional chapters (II.13-19) are the most difficult to handle because they are neither fully medical nor fully magical. The sourcematerial.md must trace the transition explicitly, showing how each chapter prepares for the next.
