# Draft — Essay 1: Why Ficino Writes Medicine for Philosophers

## Rough Structure

### Opening
The shock of the book: Ficino, Renaissance Platonist, translator of Plato and Plotinus, begins not with Forms or metaphysics but with the health problems of scholars. Why does the path to celestial life begin with headaches, digestion, sleep, and medicine? The answer: Ficino's Platonism is practical and embodied. The scholar is not a floating mind but a fragile embodied instrument.

### Section 1 — The Missing Physician of the Muses
Use I.1. The nine guides. Ficino inserts the physician into the classical education of the soul. Scholarship depends on a triple ecology: celestial, inner, earthly.

### Section 2 — The Scholar as Damaged Instrument
Use I.2. Craftsmen care for their tools; scholars neglect the instrument by which they grasp the world. The instrument is the spirit — vapour of blood. The chain: blood → spirit → sense → reason.

### Section 3 — Not Materialism
This is the crucial distinction. Ficino does not reduce soul to body. He claims soul works through body. If the instrument is damaged, higher life is obstructed. Medicine becomes the first stage of ascent.

### Section 4 — The Hierarchy of Care
Use I.26. Medicine serves the corporeal spirit. Moral discipline serves the incorporeal. Religion venerates truth. Medicine is not the goal but the preparation.

### Closing — Why This Begins the Whole Book
Book I repairs. Book II preserves. Book III opens. The sequence is not arbitrary — it is the logic of embodied ascent.

## V7 Notes
- **Personal stake**: The scholar as Everyman. Every reader who has felt their body protest against prolonged thought knows what Ficino means. The essay should make the reader feel the weight of the instrument.
- **Extended metaphor**: The instrument. A single image that carries through: the scholar's body as a finely-tuned instrument that is damaged by the very act of playing it.
- **Register shift**: Move from historical exposition (Ficino in 15th-century Florence) to immediate address (the reader's own experience of embodied thought).
- **Opening**: Must commit. "Why does a Platonist write a medical manual?" — the question itself is the hook.
- **Ending**: Point forward to the rest of the sequence. The reader should want to follow the ascent.
