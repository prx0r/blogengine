# The Apology: Natural Magic, Christianity, and the Magi

Ficino opens his Apology to the *Liber de vita* by naming the objections that will be made against him:

"The title *De vita* will act as a pleasant bait, then, and will attract as many as possible to taste of it; but in such a great number, a good many will be ignorant... and not a few malicious to boot. Someone therefore will say: Marsilio is a priest, isn't he? Indeed he is. What business then do priests have with medicine or, again, with astrology? Another will say: What does a Christian have to do with magic or images? And someone else, unworthy of life, will begrudge life to the heavens."

Three accusations: medicine, astrology, magic. The Apology answers each in turn.

---

## I. What the Three Books Establish

The first accusation answers itself, because the *Liber de vita* is, from its first pages, a medical book.

*De vita* I.1 — "Nine guides protect the scholar. Three heavenly: Mercury, Phoebus, and Venus. Three internal: will, intelligence, and memory. Three earthly: father, teacher, and physician. The father and teacher have been treated by classical authors — Plato, Aristotle, Plutarch, and Quintilian have already handled their formation. But the physician for scholars is still missing. Only a physician can help scholars with salutary counsels and medicines."

*Novem studiosorum duces.*

*De vita* I.2 — "Painters care for their brushes. Soldiers care for their weapons. Musicians care for their instruments. Yet scholars neglect the instrument by which they measure and grasp the whole world. That instrument is the spirit, produced from subtle blood, refined by the heat of the heart, rising to the brain, and used by the soul for both inner and outer sense."

*Instrumentum. Spiritus. Vapor sanguinis.*

"The soul and body, far different in nature, are joined together by the spirit as a medium, which is a certain very thin and clear vapour, produced by the heat of the heart from the thinnest part of the blood."

*Anima et corpus natura longe inter se diversa spiritu medio copulantur, qui vapor quidam est tenuissimus et perlucidus, per cordis calorem ex subtilissima parte sanguinis genitus.*

The chain: stomach and liver produce blood. The heart refines blood into spirit. Spirit rises to the brain. The brain serves the senses. The senses serve reason. If any link breaks, philosophical life breaks.

*De vita* I.3 — "Learned people are vulnerable to phlegm and black bile. Ficino compares the danger to Scylla and Charybdis. Phlegm dulls intelligence. Black bile, when excessive or badly tempered, unsettles judgment and leads toward fear, depression, instability, or madness."

*Litterati pituitae et atrae bili obnoxii sunt.*

*De vita* I.4 — Three causes of learned melancholy: celestial, natural, and human. The celestial cause involves Mercury and Saturn. Mercury stimulates investigation. Saturn stabilises investigation and preserves memory. Both incline toward dryness, so the scholar's vocation is already dangerous.

*De vita* I.4 — "In difficult contemplation, the soul withdraws from external things toward internal things, from the circumference to the centre. Ficino associates this inward centering with Earth and with black bile."

*A circumferentia ad centrum.*

Study requires inwardness. Inwardness concentrates the soul. Concentration resembles movement toward the centre. The centre corresponds to black bile. Therefore intense study tends toward melancholy.

*De vita* I.4 — "Intense mental activity dries the brain, consumes its moisture, weakens natural heat, disperses the spirits, uses up the subtler blood, leaves the remaining blood dense, thick, and dark, pulls nature toward brain and heart, abandons the stomach and liver, impairs digestion, and produces dark vapours that rise to the soul, causing fear, sadness, and disturbed judgment."

"Philosophers especially suffer because they recall the mind from the body and apply it to incorporeal things. The more difficult the work of contemplation, the more the body is disjoined and can become almost half-alive."

Ficino distinguishes between kinds of black bile in *De vita* I.5 and I.6. The chapter titles ask why melancholics are intelligent and how black bile aids genius. The answer is precise: burnt, excessive, overcold, or putrefied melancholy harms judgment; natural black bile, properly tempered, aids depth, memory, and wisdom. The same substance that wounds the scholar also enables his deepest work.

Having identified the constitutional wound — the melancholy that study produces — Ficino turns to the enemies that attack the scholar's instrument day by day. *De vita* I.7 names them: phlegm, black bile, sexual intercourse, gluttony, and morning sleep. Each clouds, thickens, or misdirects the forces needed for study. In *De vita* I.8, he addresses the best hour to begin study and how long to continue. The mind is not equally available at all hours. Time is itself a medicine.

The deepest practical insight appears in *De vita* I.11, where Ficino traces the chain from digestion to reason: "Since blood comes from digestion, and spirit from blood, and sense from spirit, and reason from sense — the stomach indirectly supports reason." The stomach is not beneath the mind. It feeds the medium through which the mind operates. *De vita* I.12 extends this logic to the whole body, describing how the natural, vital, and animal powers conceive, nourish, and sustain the spirits.

The remaining chapters of Book I — *De vita* I.13 through I.25 — form a complete pharmacy for the scholar. Medicines against phlegm, remedies for rheum and headache, care of sight, restoring taste, the special cure of black bile, syrups, pills, liquid medicine, bloodletting, electuaries. For sleeplessness, dullness, and forgetfulness. Each remedy targets a link in the chain from blood to reason.

*De vita* I.26 — "Care for the corporeal spirit. Cultivate the incorporeal. Venerate truth. Medicine serves the first, moral discipline the second, religion the third."

*Corporeum quidem spiritum cura; incorporeum vero cole; veritatem denique venerare.*

Medicine clears the instrument. Moral discipline orders the inner person. Religion turns the whole system toward truth.

## II. The Preservation of Life

*De vita* II.1 — "Judgment requires experience. Experience is difficult because occasions are brief. Hippocrates said that art is long and is attained by a long life."

Truth requires judgment. Judgment requires experience. Experience requires time. Time requires preserved life.

The physiological model that underlies Book II is simple and powerful. Life depends on vital heat sustained by radical moisture. Heat consumes moisture. Moisture sustains heat. Aging is the failure of this balance. Digestion renews what life consumes. The body is always spending its substance and must be replenished.

*De vita* II.5 names the vehicle of this renewal: "Blood and suitable moisture are necessary for life. Blood carries the substance that life consumes and must replenish."

From this foundation, Ficino develops an ecology of preservation that covers every dimension of human life. *De vita* II.6 and II.7 address the basic problem: the body is always spending its substance, and putrefaction — the breakdown of ordered life against corruption — is the enemy that must be resisted. Bad foods and bad regions accelerate this breakdown. *De vita* II.8 and II.9 give specific guidance for the elderly diet: wine, wheat, spices, and cordials that preserve warmth and moisture.

*De vita* II.10 introduces a substance that stands outside the ordinary economy of decay: "Gold is incorruptible, solar, bright, noble, stable, anti-putrefactive." Gold images the permanence that the aging body seeks. It belongs both to the preservation of life and to the solarisation that Book III will describe.

*De vita* II.11 extends the logic of preservation into the most intimate register. Human milk and blood can carry life, youth, and substance from one body to another. Vital transfer is possible through bodily materials — not as a practical method but as evidence that life is transmissible. *De vita* II.12 adds that dwelling and custom matter as much as ingestion. Longevity is place, climate, habit, and continuity.

The later chapters of Book II begin to lean toward Book III. *De vita* II.13 states that planets support bodily members; the body ages within a cosmic ecology. *De vita* II.14 describes how Venus brings green fields and restorative delight — pleasure, greenness, and beauty become medicine when rightly ordered. *De vita* II.15 gives Mercury's contribution: odours, song, and medicines that operate on the subtle body.

*De vita* II.16 returns to a theme from Book I: thought and sex both spend vital force. In old age, expenditure matters more than ever. *De vita* II.18 identifies odours and honey as substances that preserve and nourish the spirit directly — a bridge from ordinary to subtle medicine. *De vita* II.19 introduces the Medicine of the Magi, which anticipates Book III's magical turn before the full celestial machinery appears.

*De vita* II.20 — "Each seventh year brings a major bodily change because rulership returns from Saturn to the Moon. These are climacteric years."

"Natural lifespan can be moved nearer or farther by stars and material things. Doctors and astrologers both matter."

Time is patterned by planetary sequence. The body changes according to temporal rulership. But the stars do not compel — medicine and prudence can intervene.

## III. The Living Cosmos

*De vita* III.1 — "Zoroaster called the correspondences of forms to World-soul reasons 'divine lures' and Synesius called them 'magical baits.'"

*Magicas illecebras.*

Forms in matter can be arranged as lures for gifts from the World-soul.

"Lower forms depend on celestial forms. Celestial forms proceed from reasons in the Soul. Those reasons trace back to Forms in the Intellect, and the Intellect to the One and Good."

The ladder: One, Intellect, Soul, celestial forms, lower forms, material reception.

*De vita* III.2 describes the harmony of the world and human nature according to the stars. The cosmos is not dead space. It is a living order. *De vita* III.3 gives the three-level structure that makes this order coherent: "World-soul animates all things. World-body receives form. World-spirit mediates between soul and body. Human spirit can receive world-spirit."

*De vita* III.4 describes absorbing world-spirit through the Sun and Jupiter — reception, not domination. *De vita* III.5 presents the welcoming triad: "Jupiter, Sun, and Venus are the three Graces. They give life, temperance, beauty, joy, and expansion."

The passage that follows, from later in Book III, is the bridge between Book I's physiology and Book III's cosmology. Ficino writes: "Our spiritus is in conformity with the rays of the heavenly spiritus, which penetrates everything either secretly or obviously. It shows a far greater kinship when we have a strong desire for that life and are seeking a benefit that is consistent with it, and thus transfer our own spiritus into its rays by means of love, particularly if we make use of song and light and the perfume appropriate to the deity, like the hymns that Orpheus consecrated to the cosmic deities."

*Similiter spiritus noster radiis illius tam occultis, quam manifestis omnia penetrantibus. Evadit etiam longe cognatior, quando erga vitam illam vehementer afficimur consentaneum illi beneficium exoptantes, atque ita spiritum nostrum in illius radios transferentes amore praesertim si cantum et lumen adhibemus, odoremque numini consentaneum quales Orpheus hymnos mundanis numinibus consecravit.*

The same vapour that connects soul and body within the human being connects the human being to the cosmos. The spirits are conformable. Through desire, love, song, light, and perfume, the human spirit transfers itself into the celestial rays.

The remaining chapters of Book III's opening section map this cosmic medicine onto the physiological architecture of Book I. *De vita* III.6 returns to the natural, vital, and animal powers — the same three forces that conceive, nourish, and sustain the spirits in the human body — now in astrological key. *De vita* III.7 presents the Moon as the mediator: closest to Earth, linked to bodily change, timing, growth, moisture, and medical application. *De vita* III.8 adds that fixed stars matter as specific powers; the celestial influence is not limited to the seven planets. *De vita* III.9 addresses planetary dignities, which affect medical timing — planets work differently depending on their condition. *De vita* III.10 concludes this section with the practical application: medicines must be prepared under the right celestial conditions.

## IV. The Celestial Medicine

*De vita* III.11 — "World-soul spreads its force through quintessence as spirit inside the World's Body, especially into things that absorb more of that spirit. Things filled with purer spirit include wine, sugar, balsam, gold, precious stones, sweet smells, shining things, and hot, moist, clear, subtle substances."

"Ordinary food becomes our life through spirit. Bodies drink from world-life when rightly accommodated to world-body and world-spirit."

Book I: the stomach produces blood, blood produces spirit. Book III: the same digestive process operates at the cosmic scale.

"Diligent natural philosophers, when they separate this sort of spirit from gold by sublimation over fire, will employ it on any of the metals and will make it gold."

*De vita* III.12 extends the principle to human making: "Not only natural objects but artificial things can receive occult powers from stars." This one sentence prepares the entire talismanic art. The human-made object is not outside the system of correspondences.

*De vita* III.14 presents the most vivid catalogue in the entire work: "Solar things include heliotrope, gold, golden colours, chrysolite, carbuncle, myrrh, frankincense, musk, amber, balsam, yellow honey, saffron, cinnamon. Solar animals include the ram, hawk, cock, swan, lion, scarab, and crocodile. There are also solar human types and solar-looking people." The catalogue includes metals, gems, plants, scents, foods, animals, human appearances, practices, habits, light, thought, and love. "Jovial things include silver, jacinth, topaz, coral, crystal, beryl, sapphire, airy colours, wine, sugar, white honey. Also Jovial thoughts, feelings, and company with Jovial people."

To become solar, one uses not only solar objects but solar perception, solar thought, solar love, solar habits. Celestial influence is social and affective. Company matters. Feeling matters.

*De vita* III.15 makes a claim that ties Book III back to Books I and II: "Medicines are stronger than images because they enter the body more directly." Book I gave remedies. Book II gave longevity substances. Book III reveals them as part of a cosmic ecology.

The next group of chapters — *De vita* III.16 through III.20 — describes the mechanics of the talismanic image. *De vita* III.16 establishes the principle: "Heavenly rays do not merely signify. They emit, transmit, and impress power." *Radiis... vim habere mirabilem.* *De vita* III.17 traces this power to its source: "The World-soul constructs figures in the heavens and impresses properties on them. Zodiacal images, faces, degrees, images outside the zodiac. The right figure below corresponds to a figure above." *De vita* III.18 describes how the image is engraved — under specific celestial conditions, on specific materials, for specific purposes. *De vita* III.19 presents the figure of the universe, the image as a model of the whole, a configured microcosm that receives macrocosmic order.

*De vita* III.20 describes the reciprocal relationship between image and spirit: "Images act on spirit through the subtle medium linking perception, body, and celestial influence. Spirit acts on images in return. The relationship is reciprocal. The operator's emotional state matters: affect, intention, confidence, fear, joy, devotion, or disturbance alter the work."

*De vita* III.21 and III.22 extend the same principle to sound. Ficino writes: "The first music takes place in reason, the second in fantasy, and the third in words. Thence follows song, and after that the movement of the fingers in sound. Lastly the movement of the whole body in gymnastics or dancing. Thus the music of the soul is led by steps to all the limbs of the body."

The same principle applies to sound as to images. Both work through the spiritus. Both require the engagement of the imaginative faculty. In his letter to Canigiani, quoted in this section, Ficino describes the mechanism:

"For sound and song arise from consideration in the mind, the impulse of fantasy and the desire of the heart, and in disturbing the air and lending measure to it they vibrate the airy spirit of the listener, which is the link between body and soul. Thus sound and song easily arouse the fantasy, affect the heart, and reach the inmost recesses of the mind; they still, and also set in motion, the humours and the limbs of the body."

*Nam quum cantus sonusque ex cogitatione mentis, et impetu phantasiae, cordisque affectu proficiscatur, atque una cum aere facto et temperato, aereum audientis spiritum pulset, qui animae corporisque nodus est, facile phantasiam movet, afficitque cor et intima mentis penetralia penetrat. Corporis quoque humores, et membra sistit, et movet.*

*De vita* III.22 completes the musical theory: "Celestial gifts enter bodies through rightly prepared spirit. Higher gifts reach souls through rays, affection, free will, and prayer-like orientation." And in a wry observation that could serve as a motto for the entire work: "Some powers are harmful to some, favourable to others. Celestial influence is not one-size-fits-all."

The final chapters of Book III, *De vita* III.23 through III.25, turn from celestial mechanics to the art of living. *De vita* III.23 opens with a startling claim: "The unfortunate include those who do nothing and those who choose a profession unsuited to their natural bent and Genius." Misfortune is not bad luck. It is misalignment between action, nature, celestial patronage, and daimonic guide.

Ficino continues: "One should investigate what region one's star and daemon designated for habitation and cultivation. The right place refreshes spirit, strengthens sense, improves health, wins support, and favours wishes." Place is not neutral. A person's genius is geographically keyed. Vocation is not only what you do but where you are, under which star, with which daemon, in which motion. "Keep in motion," Ficino adds. "Make circular movements like heavenly bodies."

*De vita* III.24 extends this logic to self-knowledge: how learned people recognise their nature and guardian spirit. *De vita* III.25 makes it concrete, extending astrology into procreation, meals, buildings, dwelling, clothing, and the limits of permissible astrological care. Ficino's astrology becomes a complete art of living.

## V. The Apology

*De vita* III.26 — "Lower things exposed to higher things. Higher gifts received through lower materials."

If this principle sounds dangerous, Ficino has an answer. The Magi who greeted Christ at birth were astrologers. They read the stars. They followed a star. They were guided by that star to the incarnate God.

The Apology rests on four pillars: medicine, astrology, the life of the world, and the Magi who greeted Christ at birth. The Magi are the culmination of the defence. Their star-knowledge was not paganism. It was the means by which God called them to the incarnation. If astrology could serve revelation in the Gospel, it could serve revelation in Ficino's Florence.

Ficino defines the practice he defends in the Apology:

"The kind of magic ... practised by those who seasonably subject natural materials to natural causes to be formed in a wondrous way."

*Neque de magia hic prophana, quae cultu daemonum nititur, verbum quidem ullum asseverari, sed de magia naturali, quae rebus naturalibus ad prosperam corporum valetudinem coelestium beneficia captat, effici mentionem.*

This is not about profane magic, which relies on the cult of daemons. It is about natural magic, which by natural things seeks to obtain the services of the celestials for the prosperous health of the body. The key words are *naturalibus* — natural things — and *valetudinem* — health.

"Spirit, which is the airy vapour of our blood and the link between body and soul, is tempered and nourished by airy smells, by sounds, and by song. In nature a union is made from soul, body and spirit. To the Egyptian priests medicine, music and the mysteries were one and the same study. Would that we could master this natural and Egyptian art as successfully as we tenaciously and wholeheartedly apply ourselves to it!"

*Spiritus autem qui aereus sanguinis vaporem, et quasi quidam animae corporisque nodus, aereis quoque odoribus sonisque et cantibus temperatur, et aliter... Apud naturam unum quiddam est ex anima et corpore, spirituque compositum. Apud Aegyptios sacerdotes una eademque facultas erat, pharmacis fidibus, mysteriis occupata. Utinam hanc naturalem et Aegyptiam facultatem tam foeliciter assequamur, quam obnixe libenterque sectamur.*

Medicine, music, and the mysteries were one study for the Egyptian priests. Ficino's project is to reunite them. The Apology is the moment when he names this reunion and defends it.
