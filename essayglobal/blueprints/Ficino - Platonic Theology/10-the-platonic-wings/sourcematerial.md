# Essay 10 — The Platonic Wings

## Source Text
Ficino, *Platonic Theology*, Volume 1 (Books I–IV), I Tatti Renaissance Library.
English translation by Michael J.B. Allen with John Warden.

---

## Passage 1: The Flight on Platonic Wings
**Book I, Chapter 1, §1** (lines 888–909)

**Context:** The most famous passage in the entire work. Ficino invokes the Platonic image of the soul's wings from the *Phaedrus*, calling on souls to cast off earthly chains and fly to their ethereal home. This is the poetic climax of the opening argument for immortality.

> Were the soul not immortal, no creature would be more miserable than man. Since man's mind is never at rest, his body is frail and he is totally without resources, the life he leads on earth is harsher than that of the beasts. Had nature set exactly the same term to his life as she has to the other creatures, no animal would be more miserable than man. But man by his worship of God comes closer to God than all other mortal things, and God is the author of happiness. So it is utterly impossible that man should be the most unhappy of all. However, only after the death of the body can man become any happier. It seems therefore to follow of necessity that once our souls leave this prison, some other light awaits them. Our human minds, "immured in darkness and a sightless dungeon," may look in vain for that light, and we are often driven to doubt our own divine provenance. But I pray that as heavenly souls longing with desire for our heavenly home we may cast off the bonds of our terrestrial chains; cast them off as swiftly as possible, so that, uplifted on Platonic wings and with God as our guide, we may fly unhindered to our ethereal abode, where we will straightway look with joy on the excellence of our own human nature.

**Latin terms:** *alae platonicae* (Platonic wings), *sedes aetherea* (ethereal abode), *vincula terrena* (terrestrial chains), *career* (prison), *patria caelestis* (heavenly home), *lux* (light), *divinitas propria* (own divinity)

## Passage 2: The Soul Freed from the Body's Bonds
**Book I, Chapter 3, §11** (lines 1715–1736)

**Context:** Ficino describes how the mind progressively frees itself from material limitations through rational ascent, culminating in the indivisible essence. The soul's capacity for such ascent proves its kinship with the divine.

> The human mind in its day-to-day activities proceeds from particular forms to universal and absolute forms. From natural forms, which are limited to definite bits of matter, it customarily ascends by way of mathematical forms, for which indefinite matter will suffice, to metaphysical forms, which have no need of matter, definite or indefinite. Likewise it ascends from dimensions, which require both location and parts, to the point, which has no parts but in a sense has location; and again from points to numbers which need parts but not location. Finally, it wings its way from numbers to the unity which needs neither parts nor location. It travels beyond the unity which is indivisible but accidental to the unity which is substantial, in other words to the substantial form; it travels to the indivisible essence, at once the foundation and origin of all that is accidental, as to something fixed, and to the axis, in itself unchanging, of all that is in itself accidental, changeable, always clinging to another. If the human mind has such a capacity for rational ascent, though it is only a part of the universe and encumbered by the chains of the body, even more certainly the universe possesses within itself a far greater capacity for the same ascent.

**Latin terms:** *ascensus rationalis* (rational ascent), *formae mathematicae* (mathematical forms), *formae metaphysicae* (metaphysical forms), *unitas* (unity), *essentia indivisibilis* (indivisible essence), *vincula corporea* (bodily chains)

## Passage 3: The Soul's Ascent to the Vision of God
**Book IV, Chapter 2, §§4–5** (lines 11300–11320)

**Context:** Ficino describes the soul's circular motion from God through mind and reason and back to God again. The soul naturally desires, understands, loves, and ultimately unites with God. This is the final ascent of the soul to its source.

> In our case, however, because of the needs of our body, the middle part is moved too like the lowest part, compelled by the difficult and extremely laborious task of looking after the body. According to Plotinus, some movement is apparent in a way even in the rational [middle] part of the celestial souls. For since that part flows out from God, from the unity of all, down through its own unity which is its mind's head, and then down through its mind which is the reason's head, it also flows back through its mind and its unity into God, the unity of all — since indeed this part of the soul first naturally desires God Himself, secondly understands Him, thirdly loves Him intellectually, and fourthly attains Him through a kind of loving union. The Platonists call this motion "motion" because it passes through various means to a certain end; and if it passes in time, it is "temporal motion." If it passes outside time, however, it is not motion but rest.

**Latin terms:** *unio amatoria* (loving union), *motus* (motion), *intellectus* (intellect/understanding), *amor intellectualis* (intellectual love), *unitas* (unity), *deus* (God), *reditus* (return/flight back)

## Passage 4: The Soul's Communion with Angelic Mind
**Book IV, Chapter 2, §4** (lines 11284–11299)

**Context:** Ficino describes the soul's dual nature as the mean between highest and lowest things. It contains within itself the images of the above and the models of the below, and when it communicates with angelic mind it becomes like angel.

> Zoroaster says about [the soul]: "It holds within the many plenitudes of the world's bosom and folds." For if it is the mean between the highest and the lowest things, it must contain within itself the gifts and images of those above but the powers and models of those below. But in that part whereby it communicates with angelic mind it becomes like angel, in its other part unlike. So in their minds the souls of the spheres contain all the ideas simultaneously in habit and in act. In their lowest powers, those that move bodies, the souls again have all the seeds for generating, but they have them together in possession and habit, but not in act. The middle part, that is the reason, follows mind. This is because the bodies of the spheres' souls, being most perfect and in need of nothing virtually, are sufficiently ruled by the soul's lowest part alone. So the middle part of the celestial souls, though mobile by nature, because it lives in leisure from corporeal labor, has been absorbed by mind and becomes stable.

**Latin terms:** *communio cum mente angelica* (communion with angelic mind), *medium* (mean/middle), *anima* (soul), *rationes* (reasons/ideas), *semina* (seeds), *pars media* (middle part), *pars infima* (lowest part)
