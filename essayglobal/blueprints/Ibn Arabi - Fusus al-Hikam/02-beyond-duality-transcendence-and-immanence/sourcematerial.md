# Source Material: Essay 2 — Beyond Duality: Transcendence and Immanence

**Chapters:** Ch.3 (Noah, l.1915–2258) + Ch.10 (Hud, l.3489–3835) + Ch.4 (Idris/Enoch, l.2261–2489)

---

## Passage 1 — Transcendence Alone Is Limitation

**Context:** Ibn Arabi opens the Noah chapter with a radical claim: holding God's transcendence (*tanzīh*) alone amounts to restriction and misbehavior toward God.

> Know, may God inspire you through His spirit, that according to the people of truth, transcendence concerning the Divine amounts to limitation and restriction. Whoever believes in God's transcendence is either foolish or ill mannered. If he, as a believer in religion, holds (this doctrine) unreservedly and believes in it and does not take into consideration something else, he misbehaves, denies the truth and the messengers, without being aware of this (consequence). He imagines that he is right, but he is wrong. He is like one who believes in parts (of religion) and disbelieves in others, especially if he knows that divine expressions in the Scriptures that speak of the Real convey easily understood meanings to the common people, but to the elite (they convey) all meanings that are comprehensible according to the aspects of a certain phrase in the ordinary usage of a given language. The Real is manifest in every created and comprehended thing, and He is hidden from all comprehension, except for the comprehension of the one who holds that the cosmos is His form and ipseity. The cosmos is His manifest name, just as He is in another sense the spirit of that which is manifest, and this spirit means His being hidden. The Real's relationship to the manifest forms of the cosmos is like the relationship of the spirit that governs a form. [l.1919–1936]

**Arabic terms:** *tanzīh* (transcendence), *taḥdīd* (limitation/restriction), *al-ʿālam* (the cosmos), *ṣūratuhu wa-huwiyyatuhu* (His form and ipseity), *al-ism al-ẓāhir* (the manifest name), *al-rūḥ* (spirit)

## Passage 2 — The Perfect Knowledge Combines Transcendence and Immanence

**Context:** Whoever holds immanence alone also limits God. True knowledge conjoins both. The famous poem of the chapter.

> Likewise, whoever holds the Real's immanence and does not hold His transcendence limits and restricts Him and does not know Him. Whoever combines in his knowledge transcendence and immanence in a general way, for it is inconceivable to know the cosmos in detail, because one cannot embrace all the forms of the cosmos, knows the cosmos in a general way but not in detail, just as he knows himself in general but not in detail. For this reason, the Prophet connected knowledge of the Real with knowledge of the self (or soul), saying: "Whoever knows himself (or his soul) knows his Lord." And God said: "We shall show them Our signs on the horizons," meaning the outside world, "and in themselves (or in their souls)," meaning the inside world, "until it becomes evident to them," meaning to the observer, "that He is the Real," in that you are His form, and He is your spirit. [l.1955–1966]

> If you hold transcendence, you restrict Him/and if you hold immanence, you limit Him
> If you hold the two doctrines, you are right/and you will be a leader and a master in knowledge
> Whoever holds His being as two things is a polytheist/and whoever holds that He is one unifies Him
> Beware of likening Him if you hold duality/and of making Him transcendent if you unify Him
> You are not He, but you are He and you see Him/in the essences of things both boundless and restricted. [l.1982–2000]

**Arabic terms:** *tashbīh* (immanence/likening), *tanzīh* (transcendence), *man ʿarafa nafsahu* (whoever knows himself), *al-ʿālam al-ẓāhir* (external world), *al-ʿālam al-bāṭin* (internal world)

## Passage 3 — Noah's Separation vs Muhammad's Combination

**Context:** Noah erred by separating the two calls (transcendence then immanence). Muhammad combined them in "There is nothing like Him."

> God said: "There is nothing like Him," thus making Himself transcendent, and "He is the All-Hearing, the All-Seeing," thus likening Himself to creation. (However), when He says "there is nothing like His likeness," He likens Himself to creation and makes Himself two, and when He says "the All-Hearing the All-Seeing," He makes Himself transcendent and united. If Noah had combined the two calls to his people, they would have answered him. (However), he first called them to the external (doctrine of transcendence) then to the internal (doctrine of immanence) and then said to them: "Ask forgiveness of your Lord, for He is always Forgiving." ... Those who know God apprehend what Noah pointed out regarding his people; this was praise in the form of blame. They (those who know God) understand that the people did not respond to his call only because his call separated (between the transcendent and the immanent aspects of the Reality), whereas one should combine the two aspects and not separate them. Whoever holds a combination (of the two aspects) does not pay attention to separation even if he is in a state of separation, because the combination includes the separation and not vice versa. For this reason, only Muhammad and this community, which is the "best community singled out for people," were distinguished by the Qur'an. (The verse) "There is nothing like Him" combines the two aspects in one place. [l.2001–2019]

**Arabic terms:** *furqān* (separation/division), *qurʾān* (combination/joining), *al-daʿwatān* (the two calls), *al-ẓāhir* (external), *al-bāṭin* (internal)

## Passage 4 — Every Object of Worship Is God

**Context:** The knower recognizes that the Real is reflected in every worshiped form. Separation and multiplicity are facets of the One.

> Concerning the Muhammadans, God decreed that you should worship none but Him, meaning He ordered. The knower is aware of the object of worship and the form through which it was made manifest for the purpose of being worshiped. He also knows that separation and multiplicity (of the parts of this object) are like parts in the sensible form and like spiritual faculties in the spiritual form, hence in every object of worship it is God who is worshiped. The inferior person is he who imagines (the existence of) divinity in these objects. If he had not imagined this, he would not have worshiped stone or other things. [l.2106–2113]

> The superior person, the knower, says: "Your God is one, so submit yourselves only to Him" wherever He is manifest. [l.2130–2132]

**Arabic terms:** *maʿbūd* (object of worship), *ḥaqq* (Real), *ṣūra* (form), *al-ʿārif* (the knower), *al-mutakhayyal* (the imagined)

## Passage 5 — The Straight Path and Universal Mercy (Ch.10 Hud)

**Context:** Everything walks according to God's Straight Path; the essence of all creatures is the Real.

> The Straight Path belongs to God/It is universally manifest not hidden
> His Essence exists in young and old people/in the ignorant and in the knower
> For this reason, His Mercy encompasses/everything be it despicable or exalted
> "There is no creature which He does not seize its forelock. Verily, my Lord acts according to a straight path." Every moving creature walks according to God's Straight Path. Those (who walk according to the Straight Path) are not the object of God's anger in this respect, nor do they go astray. Just as going astray is accidental, so is the divine wrath, and (all things) go back to God's Mercy which encompasses everything and precedes (His Wrath). All things other than God are creatures, because they possess a spirit. There is nothing that moves by virtue of itself, but everything moves by virtue of another. Each thing moves according to God's Straight Path, which serves as a path only by walking according to it. [l.3492–3503]

> There is no existent that you see in the cosmos/which does not speak
> Nor is there a creation seen by the eyes/but its essence is the Real
> The Real is deposited in creation/for this reason, the forms of creation are His. [l.3507–3509]

**Arabic terms:** *al-ṣirāṭ al-mustaqīm* (the Straight Path), *raḥmat Allāh* (Mercy of God), *al-ḥaqq* (the Real), *kull shayʾ* (everything), *al-nāṣiya* (forelock)

## Passage 6 — God as the Essence of Our Faculties

**Context:** Building on the supererogatory tradition, Ibn Arabi declares that God is the essence of our hearing, seeing, hands, and feet.

> Know that the divine experiential sciences which the People of God experience are different by virtue of the difference of the faculties from which these experiences derive, although they go back to one Essence. That is because God says: "I have been his hearing by which he hears, his seeing by which he sees, his hand by which he seizes, and his foot by which he walks." God points out that His Essence is the essence of the human being's organs, which is the essence of the servant. However, the essence is one, while the organs are many. Each organ possesses an experiential science peculiar to that organ. Each science originates in one essence, but differs in accordance with a specific organ. This is like water, which possesses one essence, but differs in taste according to its place. Some water is sweet and fresh and some is salty and bitter; however, it is water in all its states and its essence does not change, even if its tastes vary. [l.3527–3538]

**Arabic terms:** *al-ʿulūm al-ilāhiyya al-dhawqiyya* (divine experiential sciences), *huwiyya* (Essence/Ipseity), *al-qawā* (faculties/organs), *al-māʾ* (water), *al-ʿayn al-wāḥida* (one essence)

## Passage 7 — Limitation and the Cloud: God Describes Himself Only Through Limitation

**Context:** All divine self-descriptions are limitations. Even the assertion that God is unlimited is itself a limitation.

> The first limitation is the Cloud which has no air above or beneath it. The Real came to be in it before He created the creation. Then He said that He "Sat Himself upon the Throne," which is also a limitation. He then said that He descends to the lower heaven, which is yet another limitation. He also said that He is in the heaven and on the earth, and that He is with us wherever we are, until He informed us that He is our essence. We are limited; therefore, He can describe Himself only in terms of limitation. And His saying "There is nothing like Him" is also a limitation, if we regard the kāf as redundant and not descriptive. That which is distinguished from the limited thing is itself limited, because of its being distinct from the limited thing. Being free of limitation is limitation, and one who understands (knows that) the free (the unlimited) thing is limited by its freedom. [l.3683–3693]

> He is the Essence of existence and He is "the preserver of everything" by His Essence; the preservation of everything "does not weary Him." His preservation of all things means His preservation of His form lest the thing assume a different form, which is inconceivable. He is both witness and witnessed. The cosmos is His form, and He is the spirit of the cosmos and directs it, and He is the Great Man (Macrocosm). [l.3716–3721]

**Arabic terms:** *al-ʿamāʾ* (the Cloud), *al-ḥadd* (limitation), *lā kayfa* (without modality), *al-ʿarsh* (Throne), *al-insān al-kabīr* (the Great Man/Macrocosm)

## Passage 8 — The Perplexity of the Knower and the Breadth of Belief (Ch.10 Hud)

**Context:** The knower must not confine God to a single belief. Be primal matter receptive to all forms of belief.

> Observe! The levels of people regarding the knowledge of God are the same levels concerning the seeing of God in the Resurrection. I have informed you of the reason which entails this. Beware of limiting yourself by a specific belief and disbelieving in everything else lest you miss much good; moreover, you will miss knowing of things as they really are. Be in yourself primal matter, receptive to all forms of belief, for God is too vast and great to be confined by one belief rather than another. For this reason, He said: "Wherever you turn there is the face of God"; He did not mention one place rather than another. [l.3785–3792]

**Arabic terms:** *iʿtiqād* (belief), *al-ʿaql al-awwal* (primal matter), *al-tanāwul* (receptivity), *wajh Allāh* (face of God), *al-ḥayra* (perplexity)

## Passage 9 — Elevation of Place and Status — The Celestial Spheres (Ch.4 Idris)

**Context:** Enoch/Idris rules the sphere of the sun. Two kinds of elevation: physical place and spiritual status.

> (The term) elevation has two meanings: elevation with respect to physical place and elevation with respect to status. The elevation of place (is attested in Qur'an 19:57), "We raised him to a high place." The highest place is the place around which the Spheres revolve. This is the Sphere of the Sun, the place of the spirituality of Enoch. Seven Spheres are situated below the Sphere of Enoch and seven others above it, and his Sphere is the fifteenth. The Spheres above him are those of Mars, Jupiter, Saturn, the Mansions, the Constellations, the Footstool, and the Throne. The Spheres below him are those of Venus, Mercury, the Moon, Ether, Air, Water, Earth. Because (the Sphere of the Sun) is the pivot of (all) the Spheres, it is an elevated place. As for the elevated status, it belongs to us, the Muhammadans. God said: "You are the elevated, and God is with you" in this elevation; He is far above physical place but not above status. [l.2265–2279]

**Arabic terms:** *ʿuluww* (elevation), *al-makān* (place), *al-jāh* (status), *al-aflāk* (the Spheres), *shams* (sun), *al-kursī* (Footstool), *al-ʿarsh* (Throne)

## Passage 10 — One Essence, Many Forms — The Sacrifice of Isaac as Unity (Ch.4 Idris)

**Context:** Through the analogy of numbers and the sacrifice of Isaac, Ibn Arabi demonstrates that Creator and creation are One Essence.

> Whoever knows that which we have established regarding the numbers, namely, that their negation is their very affirmation, knows that the transcendent Real is the same as the immanent created thing, even though the created differs from the Creator. The statements that the Creator is like the created and the created is like the Creator refer to One Essence. All this derives from One Essence. Furthermore, it is (simultaneously) One Essence and many essences. ("When the boy was old enough to work with his father, Ibrahim said: 'My son, I have seen myself sacrificing you in a dream.') What do you think?' He said: 'Father, do as you are commanded . . .'" And the son is identical to his father. Thus, he (Ibrahim) saw that he would sacrifice only himself. He ransomed him "with a mighty sacrifice," so that what had appeared in a human form appeared as a ram. (Furthermore), he who was the essence of a father appeared in the form of a boy, nay, as a real boy. [l.2401–2412]

**Arabic terms:** *al-ʿayn al-wāḥida* (One Essence), *al-khalq* (creation), *al-ḥaqq* (the Real), *al-fidāʾ* (ransom), *al-kabsh* (ram)
