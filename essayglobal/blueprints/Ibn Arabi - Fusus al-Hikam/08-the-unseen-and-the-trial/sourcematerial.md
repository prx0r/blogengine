# Source Passages for Essay 8: The Unseen and the Trial

**Chapters:** Ch.19 (Job — The Wisdom of the Unseen) + Ch.23 (Luqmān — The Wisdom of Virtue) + Ch.7 (Ishmael — The Wisdom of Loftiness)

---

## Passage 1 — The Mystery of Life Permeates Through Water (Ch.19, Job)

*Context: The chapter opens by establishing water as the root of life and linking the Throne resting on water to the human being's relationship with God.*

> Know that the mystery of life permeates through water, since it is the root of the elements and foundations (`'anāṣir, arkān`). For this reason God makes "of water every living thing" (Qur'ān 21:30). Everything in the cosmos is living, for everything celebrates God's praise; however, we can understand this celebration only through divine unveiling (`kashf ilāhī`). Only a living being can praise. Hence everything is living. And the root of everything is water. Do you not see the Throne, how it rests on the water, because it was composed of water; it floats on the water, and the water preserves it from beneath. Likewise, God created the human being as a servant and (then) he became haughty and wished to excel Him, but God, notwithstanding, preserved him from beneath, taking into account the seeming loftiness of the servant who is ignorant of himself. This is (illustrated in Muhammad's tradition): "If you let a rope fall down, it will fall on God."

**Arabic terms:** `غيب ghayb` (the Unseen), `ماء mā'` (water), `عرش 'arsh` (Throne), `كشف إلهي kashf ilāhī` (divine unveiling), `عناصر 'anāṣir` (elements), `أركان arkān` (foundations)

---

## Passage 2 — The Throne, Water, and the Image of the Merciful (Ch.19, Job)

*Context: Ibn Arabi develops the theme of the world's existence through water and the Throne, and the human being as created in the image of the Merciful.*

> God alluded to His being both below and above in His saying: "They fear their Lord above them" (Qur'ān 16:50, trans. AH), and in His saying: "He is the High Master over His servants" (Qur'ān 6:18, 61). Thus, God possesses the high and the low positions. For this reason, the six directions are manifested only through the human being, who (was created) in the image of the Merciful. There is no sustainer but God. He said regarding a group of people: "If they carried out (the precepts) of the Torah and the Gospel" (Qur'ān 5:66), then He said in an indefinite and general manner: "and what was sent down for them from their Lord" (ibid.). In this last sentence He included every law which was sent down to people through a messenger or inspired human beings. ("If they carried out . . ."), "they would eat from above" (ibid.), because He sustains from above, as is ascribed to Him, "and from beneath their feet" (ibid.), and sustaining from beneath is (also) ascribed to Him by the medium of His Messenger who transmitted from Him.

**Arabic terms:** `صورة الرحمن ṣūrat al-raḥmān` (image of the Merciful), `الجهات الست al-jihāt al-sitt` (the six directions), `فوق wa fawq` (above), `تحت taḥt` (beneath)

---

## Passage 3 — Job's Affliction, Water, and the Removal of Pain (Ch.19, Job)

*Context: Ibn Arabi interprets Job's story — the command to stamp his foot and the spring of water — as a symbol of the restoration of balance.*

> God said to Job: "Stamp your foot! This is a place to wash" (Qur'ān 38:42), meaning water, "cool" (ibid.), because of the extreme heat caused by his pain. Therefore, God calmed him with cool water. For this reason, (the core) of medicine is to decrease that which is increased and to increase that which is decreased, with the aim of achieving temperance, which can be attained only approximately. [...] In the divine prophetic knowledge, the Real is described as content, angry, and (other) attributes. However, contentment nullifies anger and anger nullifies contentment, while temperance means the equality of anger and contentment. One who is angry with someone cannot be content with him at the same time, and vice versa.

**Arabic terms:** `بلاء balā'` (affliction/trial), `صبر ṣabr` (patience/forbearance), `اعتدال i'tidāl` (temperance/balance), `غضب ghaḍab` (anger/wrath), `رضā riḍā` (contentment)

---

## Passage 4 — The World in the Image of the Merciful (Ch.19, Job)

*Context: Ibn Arabi affirms the famous dictum that there cannot be a more wonderful world than this one, for it is in the image of the Merciful.*

> This is because there cannot be a world more wonderful than the present world (laysa fī'l-imkān abda' min hādhā al-'ālam), for it is in the image of the Merciful. God brought the world into existence; that is, His existence is manifested by the manifestation of the world, just as the human being is manifested by the existence of his natural form. We are His external form, while His Ipseity is the spirit which governs the form. This governing is only in Him, just as it is from Him, for He is the First in essence, and the Last in form. He is the External through the changing of determinations (of things) and states, and He is the Internal through His governing (of the world), and He is the Knower of everything. He is Witness to everything (Qur'ān 58:6, 85:9), knowing by witnessing and not by reflection.

**Arabic terms:** `ليس في الإمكان أبدع من هذا العالم laysa fī'l-imkān abda' min hādhā al-'ālam` (there is nothing more wonderful in possibility than this world — al-Ghazālī's phrase), `صورة الرحمن ṣūrat al-raḥmān` (image of the Merciful), `أول awwal` (First), `آخر ākhir` (Last), `ظاهر ẓāhir` (External), `باطن bāṭin` (Internal)

---

## Passage 5 — True Patience: Complaining Only to God (Ch.19, Job)

*Context: Ibn Arabi redefines patience (ṣabr) — it is not refraining from complaint altogether, but restricting complaint to God alone.*

> God praised Job for his forbearance (`ṣabr`), notwithstanding Job's invocation to remove the injury from him. Thus, we know that when a servant invokes God to remove injury from him, this does not impair his forbearance and his excellence as a servant, for as God said, he "always turns to God" (Qur'ān 38:44), meaning, he directs himself to God, and not to causes. Concerning the removal of injury, the Real acts through a cause, because the servant relies on causes, for the causes which remove something are many, while the Causer is one entity. The turning of the servant to the single entity which removes injury by a cause is better than turning to a specific cause, which might not coincide with God's knowledge of that cause. [...] The (true) definition is only restraining the soul from complaining to an entity other than God, not to God. The Sufis confined their approach to the idea that the one who complains impairs his contentment with God's decree (`al-riḍā bi'l-qaḍā'`). However, this is not so, because contentment with God's decree is not impaired by complaining to God or to another entity; however, what is impaired is the contentment with the thing decreed (`al-maqḍī`).

**Arabic terms:** `صبر ṣabr` (patience/forbearance), `رضا بالقضاء riḍā bi'l-qaḍā'` (contentment with the decree), `رضا بالمقضي riḍā bi'l-maqḍī` (contentment with the thing decreed), `شكوى shakwā` (complaint), `أواب awwāb` (one who constantly turns to God)

---

## Passage 6 — Affliction as Theophany: The Gnostic's Response to Suffering (Ch.19, Job)

*Context: Ibn Arabi tells the story of a gnostic who was censured for weeping in hunger, and defends the complaint to God as a form of theophany.*

> This resembles (what happened to) one of the gnostics who suffered hunger and wept, and one of those who has no mystical experience censured him. The gnostic's response was: "He (God) made me hungry only to cause my weeping," meaning, He put me to test with suffering so that I would beseech Him to take away my suffering. "This in no way impairs my being forbearing." Thus, we know that forbearance is only restraining the soul from complaining to an entity other than God. By "other than God" I mean a specific aspect of God. God (allāh) established the Real as a specific aspect of Himself called the aspect of Ipseity. So one should invoke Him using this aspect to alleviate an affliction and not to use other aspects called causes, which are identical to Him only with respect to particularizing God's aspects. When the gnostic beseeches His Ipseity in order to cure him of his affliction, he does not prevent himself from knowing that all causes constitute His essence and that each (cause) is a different aspect of Him.

**Arabic terms:** `ذوق dhawq` (taste/experiential knowledge), `أدب adab` (courtesy/proper conduct), `هوية hawiyya` (Ipseity), `ابتلاء ibtilā'` (testing/affliction)

---

## Passage 7 — Luqmān's Wisdom: The Spoken and the Unspoken (Ch.23, Luqmān)

*Context: Ibn Arabi opens the Luqmān chapter by distinguishing between spoken wisdom (the words God transmits) and unspoken wisdom (the reality behind them).*

> God said: "We gave Luqmān wisdom" (Qur'ān 31:12), "and whoever is given wisdom, is given much good" (Qur'ān 2:269). Thus, in the Qur'ān, (that is), through God's witness, Luqmān possessed much good. Wisdom may be spoken or unspoken, as Luqmān said to his son: "My son, if even the weight of a mustard seed were hidden in a rock or anywhere in the heavens or earth, God would bring it (to light)" (Qur'ān 31:16). This is wisdom that is spoken, an act of God which He transmitted and affirmed in His Book; it is not ascribed to the one who utters it. However, unspoken wisdom is known by its circumstantial connections, for God neither spoke of the one to whom this seed was brought nor mentioned him, and Luqmān did not say to his son that God would bring it to him or to someone else. God spoke of this bringing in general, and placed the thing brought either in the heavens or on earth to call the thinker's attention to God's saying, "He is God in the heavens and the earth" (Qur'ān 6:3).

**Arabic terms:** `حكمة ḥikma` (wisdom), `منطوق manṭūq` (spoken/explicit), `مفهوم mafhūm` (unspoken/implied), `لقمان Luqmān`

---

## Passage 8 — The All-Subtle and the All-Knowing: The Essence of Every Known Thing (Ch.23, Luqmān)

*Context: Ibn Arabi explains how Luqmān's mention of "the All-Subtle, the All-Knowing" points to the doctrine that the Real is the essence of every known thing.*

> Through the spoken and the unspoken (content), Luqmān draws attention to (the idea) that the Real is the Essence of every known thing, for the known is more general than the thing, because (the Real) is most indefinite of all. Then he completed and exhausted the wisdom so that the cosmos (nash'a) will be perfected by it and said: "God is the All-Subtle (laṭīf)" (Qur'ān 31:16). Owing to His grace (luṭf) and subtlety (laṭāfa), regarding the thing that is so named and so defined, He is its essence, so that of this thing it is said about it only what its name indicates through agreement and usage. Thus, one speaks of the heaven, the earth, the rock, the tree, the animal, the angel, the sustenance, and the food. The essence of everything and in everything is one, just as the Ash'arites said that the whole world takes the form of one substance, which is the core of our view, that the essence is one.

**Arabic terms:** `لطيف laṭīf` (All-Subtle), `خبير khabīr` (All-Knowing/All-Aware), `لطف luṭf` (grace/subtlety), `نشأة nash'a` (cosmos/formation), `جوهر واحد jawhar wāḥid` (one substance)

---

## Passage 9 — Ishmael's Loftiness and the Pleasing Servant (Ch.7, Ishmael)

*Context: Ibn Arabi opens the Ishmael chapter by explaining that each existent has a single lord, and the happy person is the one whose lord is pleased with him.*

> Know that the (entity) called (musammā) God is one with respect to essence and all with respect to names. Each existent has from God only a single lord, and it is inconceivable for it to have all the lords. As for God's Unity (aḥadiyya), no single entity enters it, for one cannot call part of it a thing and another a thing, for it does not admit division. However, His Unity is the totality of His attributes in potentiality. The happy person is the one whose Lord is pleased with him, and there is none but that who is pleasing in the eyes of his Lord, because Lordship applies to everyone, hence the Lord finds everyone pleasing, and so everyone is happy. For this reason Sahl said: Lordship has a mystery – and it is you, ergo Sahl's saying refers to every entity – if it had disappeared, the Lordship would also have been cancelled.

**Arabic terms:** `علو 'uluww` (loftiness), `أحدية aḥadiyya` (Unity), `ربّ rabb` (Lord), `ربوبية rubūbiyya` (Lordship), `سahl بن عبد الله التستري Sahl ibn 'Abd Allāh al-Tustarī`

---

## Passage 10 — The Sacrifice and Ishmael's Faithfulness to His Promise (Ch.7, Ishmael)

*Context: Ibn Arabi discusses Ishmael's faithfulness, the relation between God's promise and threat, and concludes that only the one faithful to his promise remains.*

> Do not contemplate (only) the Real/(so that) you divest Him of creation. Do not contemplate (only) creation/(so that) you clothe it (in all things) except the Real. Make Him incomparable and comparable/and place yourself in the domain of truth. If you will, be in a state of joining or in a state of separation. Then, if all is revealed to you/you will gain the greatest success. [...] Praise (for the people is carried out by God) through faithfulness to the promise (ṣidq al-wa'd) and not through faithfulness to the threat. The Divine Presence requires commendable praise through God's Essence. The Essence is praised through faithfulness to the promise and not through faithfulness to the threat, moreover, through overlooking (one's sins). "Do not think that God will break His promise to His Messengers" (Qur'ān 14:47). He does not say "His threat," but rather "We shall overlook their sins" (Qur'ān 46:16), although He made a threat regarding sins. He praised Ishmael, because he was faithful to his promise (ṣādiq al-wa'd). Regarding the Real (in his praising of Ishmael), possibility disappears, because possibility requires a preponderant (murajjiḥ). Only the one who is faithful to his promise remains/while the Real's threat has no established essence.

**Arabic terms:** `صادق الوعد ṣādiq al-wa'd` (faithful to his promise), `وعيد wa'īd` (threat), `وعد wa'd` (promise), `إسماعيل Ismā'īl` (Ishmael), `ذبيحة dhabīḥa` (sacrifice), `تنزيه tanzīh` (incomparability/transcendence), `تشبيه tashbīh` (comparability/immanence)
