# Source Passages for Essay 6: The Two Mercies and the Hierarchy of Names

**Chapters:** Ch.16 (Solomon — The Wisdom of Mercy) + Ch.21 (Zakariah — The Wisdom of Dominion) + Ch.17 (David — The Wisdom of Existence)

---

## Passage 1 — The Two Kinds of Mercy: Favor and Obligation (Ch.16, Solomon)

*Context: Ibn Arabi introduces the two mercies — mercy of favor (raḥmat al-imtinān) and mercy of obligation (raḥmat al-wujūb) — corresponding to al-Raḥmān and al-Raḥīm.*

> Solomon introduced two kinds of mercy: the mercy of favor (`raḥmat al-imtinān`) and the mercy of obligation (`raḥmat al-wujūb`), which correspond to the All-Merciful (al-raḥmān) and the All-Compassionate (al-raḥīm), respectively. As the All-Merciful He bestows His favors freely, and as the All-Compassionate He is obliged to give favors. This obligation derives from His bestowal; the All-Compassionate is included in the All-Merciful. That is because God decreed on Himself mercy for (the benefit of) the servant, because the Real mentions the acts carried out by the servant for which he deserves mercy. Mercy is incumbent on God, and He ordained it for Himself, I mean the mercy of obligation. Servants who belong to this kind (of people who carry out acts and deserve mercy) know the identity of the one who carries out their acts. Human acts are divided among eight organs. The Real informed us that He is the essence of every organ, hence the agent of human acts is solely the Real, while the form (of the act) belongs to the servant.

**Arabic terms:** `رحمة الامتنان raḥmat al-imtinān` (mercy of favor), `رحمة الوجوب raḥmat al-wujūb` (mercy of obligation), `الرحمن al-raḥmān` (the All-Merciful), `الرحيم al-raḥīm` (the All-Compassionate)

---

## Passage 2 — Mercy Encompasses All Things, Even the Divine Names (Ch.16, Solomon)

*Context: Ibn Arabi shows that God's mercy encompasses everything, including the divine names themselves, and that the hierarchy of names is mirrored in creation.*

> Regarding this issue, our aim is only to discuss it, calling attention to the two kinds of mercy Solomon mentioned whose names in Arabic are al-raḥmān al-raḥīm (the All-Merciful, the All-Compassionate). God restricts the mercy of obligation and derestricts the mercy of favor, in His saying, "My mercy encompasses all things" (Qur'ān 7:156), even the divine names, that is, the realities of relationships. He bestows favors on the divine names through us. Thus, we are the result of the mercy of favor through the divine names and the lordly relationships. Then he obliged Himself to bestow favors through our self-manifestation and He made us know that He is our essence so we may know that He obliged Himself to bestow favors only for Himself. Consequently, mercy does not exceed Him, for on whom would He bestow favors were none to exist other than He?

**Arabic terms:** `وسعت رحمتي كل شيء wasi'at raḥmatī kulla shay'` (My mercy encompasses all things), `الأسماء الإلهية al-asmā' al-ilāhiyya` (the divine names), `حقائق الإضافات ḥaqā'iq al-iḍāfāt` (realities of relationships)

---

## Passage 3 — The Hierarchy of the Divine Names and Attributes (Ch.16, Solomon)

*Context: Ibn Arabi explains that the divine names are graded in a hierarchy, and that just as one divine name precedes another, so too creatures excel one another while the Essence remains one.*

> However, we should not ignore the difference between entities, for people excel one another in knowledge, so it is said that this person is more learned than another, although the Essence (of all things) is one. This means that the attribute of knowledge overcomes the attribute of will (`irāda`), just as the attribute of will overcomes the attribute of power, and this is the hierarchy of the divine attributes. Likewise, the divine hearing and seeing (are arranged). All the divine names are graded in a hierarchy. In the same manner creation is arranged according to a hierarchy; for example, we say that one individual is more knowledgeable than another, although the Essence is one. Just as you give precedence to a certain divine name and you call and depict it by all the names, the manifested creation has all the attributes of that with which it is compared. Thus, every part of the cosmos contains all its parts; that is, every part is the substrate of the realities of the whole cosmos.

**Arabic terms:** `تراتب الأسماء الإلهية tarātib al-asmā' al-ilāhiyya` (hierarchy of divine names), `علم 'ilm` (knowledge), `إرادة irāda` (will), `قدرة qudra` (power), `حقيقة واحدة ḥaqīqa wāḥida` (one reality)

---

## Passage 4 — Solomon's Dominion and the Manifestation of Power (Ch.16, Solomon)

*Context: Ibn Arabi compares Solomon and Muhammad regarding the manifestation of spiritual power over the jinn and demons.*

> This knowledge was not concealed from Solomon, but rather it was part of the dominion (he enjoyed over things) which was appropriate to none after him, that is, the manifestation of his dominion in the sensuous world. Muhammad was given the same trait Solomon received, but he did not manifest it. God gave Muhammad the power to overcome a demon which came to him in the night to kill him. He planned to seize and tie it to one of the mosque's columns until morning so that the children of al-Madīna could play with it. He (Muhammad) mentioned Solomon's request, but God thwarted it. Although Muhammad was granted power, he did not manifest it, while Solomon did. Solomon (asked God to grant him) a dominion (`mulk`), not absolute dominion, hence we know that he wished to possess some dominion.

**Arabic terms:** `ملك mulk` (dominion/kingdom), `تسخير taskhīr` (subjugation), `جن jinn` (spirits/demons), `همّة himma` (spiritual aspiration), `جميعية jam'iyya` (concentration of power)

---

## Passage 5 — Asaf, the Throne of Bilqīs, and Perpetual Creation (Ch.16, Solomon)

*Context: Ibn Arabi explains the miracle of the transportation of Bilqīs' throne through the doctrine of perpetual creation at every instant.*

> Asaf ibn Berechya was faster than the demons, for his speech and action occurred in the same instant. As a result, in this moment, Solomon saw with his own eyes Bilqīs' throne established before him, lest he should imagine that he perceived the throne in its place without its being moved (from its place). In our view, because of the simultaneous occurrence (of speech and the establishment of the throne), no movement (of the throne from its original place to Solomon's palace) happened. Instead, (the throne in its original place) entered a state of non-existence and was brought back into existence (in Solomon's palace) with the awareness of none, except the one who knew God's saying: "No indeed, they are perplexed regarding a new creation" (Qur'ān 50:15). They immediately saw what they were looking at. If the case is as we pointed out, then the time of its absence, I mean the absence of the throne from its place, is the time of its existence with Solomon, because of the repetition of creation by means of (God's) breaths.

**Arabic terms:** `خلق جديد khalq jadīd` (new creation), `أنفاس الله anfās Allāh` (God's breaths), `إعادة i'āda` (repetition/return), `عدم 'adam` (non-existence)

---

## Passage 6 — God's Mercy Encompasses Everything, Even Wrath (Ch.21, Zakariah)

*Context: Ibn Arabi opens the Zakariah chapter by reaffirming that Mercy encompasses all, including Wrath, and that this Mercy brings all essences into existence.*

> Know that God's Mercy encompasses everything that relates to existence and rule and that the existence of His Wrath derives from His Mercy with His Wrath. His Mercy precedes His Wrath, meaning that the relation of Mercy to Him precedes the relation of Wrath to Him. Since every essence has an existence which it seeks from God, His Mercy encompasses every essence, for with the Mercy by which He is Merciful He fulfills the essence's request to have existence and then brings it into existence. For this reason we said that God's Mercy encompasses everything with respect to existence and rule. The divine names belong to the (genus of) things and they derive from one essence. The first thing God's Mercy encompassed was the thingness (`shay'iyya`) of the essence which brought into existence Mercy through Mercy. The first thing Mercy encompassed was itself, then its thingness, to which we have alluded, then the thingness of every existent which is brought into existence unendingly with respect to this world and the next, accidents and atoms (`jawhar wa-'araḍ`), composed and simple things.

**Arabic terms:** `وسعت رحمتي كل شيء wasi'at raḥmatī kulla shay'` (My mercy encompasses all things), `شيئية shay'iyya` (thingness), `سبقت رحمتي غضبي sabaqat raḥmatī ghaḍabī` (My mercy precedes My wrath), `جوهر jawhar` (atom/substance), `عرض 'araḍ` (accident)

---

## Passage 7 — The God Created in Beliefs and Mercy's Two Effects (Ch.21, Zakariah)

*Context: Ibn Arabi develops the idea that the "god created in beliefs" is a fixed entity upon which mercy is bestowed through bringing it into existence.*

> First you should know Mercy alone is bringing into existence, in general, for through Mercy torments are brought into existence. Also, Mercy exerts influence in two ways. Its first effect is on the essence, meaning it brings every existent essence into existence, without any regard for a specific aim or absence thereof, or for suitability or lacks of it, because it only heeds the essence of every existent before its existence. Moreover, it concerns itself with the thing (purely) as a fixed entity (`'ayn thābita`). For this reason, it sees the god created in beliefs as one of the fixed entities, and hence has mercy on it through bringing it into existence. Because of this, we said that the god created in beliefs is the first thing on which mercy is bestowed after Mercy bestows mercy on Itself, (when we refer to) its connection to the bringing of existents into existence. Another effect Mercy has is initiated by request. The veiled ones ask the Real to have mercy on them in their belief, while the people of revelation ask God's mercy to exist in them, and request it by God's name, saying: "O God, have mercy on us" (Qur'ān 23:109).

**Arabic terms:** `عين ثابتة 'ayn thābita` (fixed entity), `إله المعتقد ilāh al-mu'taqad` (god of belief), `حكم ḥukm` (dominating principle), `راحم rāḥim` (the one who shows mercy)

---

## Passage 8 — Mercy, the Divine Names, and Invocation (Ch.21, Zakariah)

*Context: Ibn Arabi discusses how the comprehensive Mercy differs relative to each divine name and how one may invoke God through any name to seek Mercy, even the Avenger.*

> Even though Mercy is comprehensive, it differs relative to each divine name. For this reason, God is requested to show His Mercy to people through every name. Hence, God's Mercy and the pronoun connected with it encompass everything. Mercy has many branches, owing to the multiplicity of the divine names. However, when God is asked to have mercy on someone through a specific name, Mercy is not comprehensive regarding that name or another. Even regarding the name the Avenger, one who seeks revenge is allowed to say: "O Avenger, have Mercy on me." That is because these names refer to the Essence indicated, but through their real expressions they point to different meanings. By these names that exist in Mercy, one invokes (God) in their reference to the Essence which is indicated by a specific name and no other; one does not invoke the name which is different from other names (but God's Essence).

**Arabic terms:** `المنتقم al-muntaqim` (the Avenger), `الرحمن al-raḥmān` (the All-Merciful), `ترادف tarāduf` (synonymity of names), `اسم ism` (name), `مدلول madlūl` (the indicated meaning)

---

## Passage 9 — David's Vicegerency and the Bestowal of Existence (Ch.17, David)

*Context: Ibn Arabi discusses the uniqueness of David's vicegerency (khilāfa), which was specifically named as such, unlike Adam's.*

> The first favor God bestowed on David was His giving him a name composed of disconnected letters, dāl, alif, and wāw. Thus, He severed his connections from the world, informing us about him purely by his name. [...] God granted him power and described him by it, and granted him wisdom and decisive resolution (`faṣl al-khiṭāb`). Then He (granted) him the greatest favor and nearness to Us and stated specifically that the vicegerency belonged to him alone. God did not do this for any other human being, even if there were vicegerents among them. He said: "O David, We appointed you a vicegerent in the earth, so judge justly between people and do not follow your own will" (Qur'ān 38:26). [...] Regarding David, there is an additional element of particularization, because He appointed him a vicegerent of judgment that originates only from God, for He said to him: "Judge justly between people" (Qur'ān 38:26). However, Adam's vicegerency was likely not of this degree.

**Arabic terms:** `خلافة khilāfa` (vicegerency), `فضل الخطاب faṣl al-khiṭāb` (decisive resolution), `داود Dāwūd` (David), `جعل خليفة ja'ala khalīfa` (to appoint a vicegerent)

---

## Passage 10 — The Softening of Iron and Divine Mercy (Ch.17, David)

*Context: Ibn Arabi concludes the David chapter with a reflection on the softening of iron for David, interpreting it as a symbol of the integration of divine attributes.*

> As for the "softening of iron," (it is said) that hardened hearts are softened by rebuke and threat as fire softens iron. However, the difficult thing is hearts that are harder than stone, for fire breaks and calcifies the stone but does not soften it. God softened iron for him so that he could make shields, and (in this act) He called his attention to (the notion) that what attacks you, that is, spears, swords, knives, and arrowheads, are made of the iron from which you make shields. So you protect yourself from iron with iron. The Muhammadan religion sets forth (the rule): "I seek refuge from You in You." So understand! Thus, this is the spirit of softening iron, for God is both the Avenger and the All-Compassionate, and He gives us success.

**Arabic terms:** `إلانة الحديد ilānat al-ḥadīd` (softening of iron), `المنتقم al-muntaqim` (the Avenger), `الرحيم al-raḥīm` (the All-Compassionate), `أعوذ بك منك a'ūdhu bika minka` (I seek refuge in You from You)
