# Source Material — Essay 11: Spiritual Ascent and the Stations of Unveiling

## Territory: Ch.11 (Ṣāliḥ) + Ch.8 (Jacob)

---

### Passage 1 | Ch.11 (Ṣāliḥ) — Opening (*Fatḥ*) and the Tripartite Foundation of Being

**Context:** The chapter on Ṣāliḥ is the wisdom of opening (*fatḥ*) — unveiling and self-manifestation. Ibn Arabi begins with the tripartite (uneven) structure that underlies all existence: the divine command "Be," the thing's hearing of it, and its obedience. The cosmos emerges from three.

> Know, may God grant you success, that in their essences all things are built on unevenness, which begins with number three and beyond that. The first uneven number is three. From the divine presence the cosmos came into existence. Therefore, God said: "When We will a thing (to be), We only say to it 'be' and it comes into being" (Qur'ān 16:40). This is an essence, which possesses will and speech. If this essence and its will, which constitutes a specific turning toward a thing aiming at making it exist, and the saying "be" in this process did not exist, the thing would not exist. Also the tripartite unevenness appears in this thing. With respect to the thing itself, its existence and description as an existent are valid because of three (elements): its quality of being a thing (*shay'iyya*), its (quality of) hearing (*samā'*), and its (quality of) obedience to the originator's command.

**Arabic terms:** *fatḥ* (opening/unveiling), *shay'iyya* (thingness), *samā'* (hearing), *amr* (command), *kawn* (being/existence), *wujūd* (existence), *al-witr* (the uneven)

---

### Passage 2 | Ch.11 (Ṣāliḥ) — The Thing Brings Itself into Being by Obeying the Command

**Context:** Central to Ibn Arabi's ontology: the Real only commands; the thing itself brings itself into existence by obeying. This is the mystery of the fixed entity (*al-ʿayn al-thābita*) hearing the divine "Be" and responding, mirroring the servant who stands at his master's command.

> Bringing into being is ascribed to the thing, for if it had no power of bringing into being, when hearing this saying, it would not come into being. On hearing the command, only the essence of the thing brings it into existence, after its non-existence. Thus, the Real affirms that bringing into being pertains to the thing itself and not to the Real, while the Real in this (process) only commands. He so informs about Himself in His saying: "When We will a thing (to be), We only say to it 'be' and it comes into being" (ibid.). He (the Real) attributes the bringing into being to the thing itself as a result of God's command, and He speaks the truth. In principle, it is reasonable, just as the one feared and obeyed orders his servant to stand, and he stands in submission to his master's command. The master only orders the servant to stand; however, standing is the outcome of the servant's activity, and not the master's. Thus, bringing into being rests on the tripartite principle — three elements from both sides, from the Real and from creation.

**Arabic terms:** *al-ʿayn al-thābita* (the fixed entity), *ījād* (bringing into being), *amr* (command), *sam'* (hearing), *ṭāʿa* (obedience), *maʿdūm* (nonexistent)

---

### Passage 3 | Ch.11 (Ṣāliḥ) — The Three-Day Destruction: Colors of the Soul Made Visible

**Context:** Ṣāliḥ's people were destroyed after a three-day period during which their faces changed color — yellow, red, then black — corresponding to the states of their souls. This is the unveiling of inner states upon the outer form: punishment comes from within.

> The foundation of Being is tripartite in principle. For this reason, God manifested the wisdom of Ṣāliḥ in postponing the punishment of his people for three days as a true threat, which finally became real, and this is the cry by which God destroyed them, "so that they fell down in their homes" (Qur'ān 7:78, 11:67). On the first of the three days their faces became yellow, on the second red, and on the third black. When the third day passed, their predisposition was appropriate, and then decay appeared in them, and this appearance was called destruction. The yellowing of the faces of the miserable parallels the shining of the faces of the happy, as in his saying "On that Day some faces will be shining (*musfira*)" (Qur'ān 80:38). (The word *musfira*) originates in *sufūr*, meaning manifestation (*ẓuhūr*). (This sign) corresponds to the yellowing of the faces of the people of Ṣāliḥ on the first day in which the sign of their misery appeared.

> That which was manifest in their outward appearance derived from the notion already established in their souls. Nothing affected them but themselves, just as their origination derived from themselves. "God possesses the conclusive argument" (Qur'ān 6:149). Whoever understands this wisdom, establishing it in his soul as witness, releases himself from dependence on others and knows that the good and evil that befall him come only from himself. By good I mean what fits his aims and tallies with his nature and temperament, and by evil I mean the opposite.

**Arabic terms:** *isfirār* (yellowing), *ḥumra* (redness), *sawād* (blackness), *ʿadhāb* (punishment), *bashara* (skin), *ẓuhūr* (manifestation), *isti'dād* (predisposition)

### Passage 4 | Ch.8 (Jacob) — Religion as Submission: The Two Kinds of Religion

**Context:** The Jacob chapter opens with the distinction between God's religion and human religion. Jacob's wisdom is spirituality (*rūḥiyya*). True religion is submission (*islām*), and the servant's act of submission returns to him as his own state — the religion one practices becomes requital from oneself.

> Religion is divided into two kinds: a. Religion according to God and according to whom the Real taught and those who taught others what the Real taught them; and b. Religion according to the created beings, which God acknowledges. God's religion is the one which God chose and granted it the highest degree above the human religion. God said: "Abraham ordered his sons (to submit to God), and so did Jacob, saying: 'My sons, God has chosen for you the religion (*al-dīn*), so do not die except in a state of submission to God (*muslimūn*)" (Qur'ān 2:132), meaning obeying Him. (The word) *al-dīn* is marked with the definite particle to designate a specific religion, hence this is a known religion (which is shown) in God's saying: "The religion according to God is Islam" (Qur'ān 3:19), meaning submission. Religion expresses your submission. That which comes from God is the Law to which you submit. ... The servant is one who maintains religion and the Real promulgates the laws. Hence, submission is the essence of your act, and religion comes out of your act. You will not be happy except by way of that which comes from yourself.

**Arabic terms:** *dīn* (religion/judgment), *islām* (submission), *shar'* (law), *nāmūs* (rule/law), *ʿamal* (act), *saʿāda* (happiness), *rūḥiyya* (spirituality)

---

### Passage 5 | Ch.8 (Jacob) — Religion as Requital: One's Own State Returns to Oneself

**Context:** The deeper mystery of religion: it is not reward from an external God but the return (*ʿāda*) of one's own state upon oneself. The possible things receive from the Real only what their essences give them in each state. This is the mystery of predetermination governing all created beings.

> As for its mystery and internal aspect, it manifests itself in the mirror of the Real's existence; the possible things receive from the Real only that which their essences give them in each state. For each state has a form. Their forms differ according to the differences in their states. Hence, the manifestation differs because of the difference in the state, and the effect on the servant occurs in accordance with what characterizes him. No one bestows on him good or evil except himself; only he bestows on his essence favors and sufferings. He should chastise and praise only himself. "To God belongs the conclusive argument" (Qur'ān 6:149) in His knowledge of them, for knowledge follows the object of knowledge. In an issue like this, the mystery that lies behind it is (the notion) that the possible things in principle are nonexistent. There is no existence but the existence of the Real (which is disclosed) through the forms of the states in which the possible things appear in themselves and in their (fixed) essences.

**Arabic terms:** *dīn* (religion/judgment), *jazā'* (requital), *ʿāda* (return/custom), *thubūt* (fixity), *mumkināt* (possible things), *ḥāl* (state), *ṣūra* (form), *ʿiqāb* (consequence)

---

### Passage 6 | Ch.8 (Jacob) — Messengers as Physicians of Souls: Servants of the Command, Not the Will

**Context:** Ibn Arabi's profound analysis of the tension between God's command (*amr*) and God's will (*irāda*). The messenger is a servant of the command (which may oppose the will), like a physician who serves nature by opposing it — the physician of souls for the world to come.

> In determining the states of those who are under obligation (*mukallafūn*), the Real acts through two aspects: The servant acts in accordance with the requirements of the Real's will, and His will in turn is connected to what the Real's knowledge requires, and the Real's knowledge is connected to what the object of knowledge bestows on Him, that is, its essence, which appears only in its form. The Messenger and the Heir are servants of the divine command by way of the will (*irāda*); however, they are not servants of the will. He (the Messenger) refutes the divine command by way of the divine command seeking for the happiness of the person under obligation. If he were the servant of the divine will, he would not advise (people), or would advise (people) only through the divine will. The Messenger, the Heir, is the physician of the souls for the world to come. He obeys God's command when He commands him. He may observe His command and will and sees that God's command opposes His will, (knowing) that all things occur according to His will, and for this reason the command stands.

**Arabic terms:** *amr* (command), *irāda* (will), *mukallafūn* (those under obligation), *ṭabīb* (physician), *nufūs* (souls), *ṭāʿa* (obedience), *maʿṣiya* (disobedience)

---

### Passage 7 | Ch.8 (Jacob) — The Prophet's Anxiety: Not Knowing Whether Command Matches Will

**Context:** The Prophet Muhammad said "Hūd and its sisters made my hair white" because they contain "So keep to the right course as you have been commanded" (Qur'ān 11:112). The prophet does not know in advance whether God's command will be fulfilled through His will or opposed by it. This is the spiritual station of uncertainty that belongs to the highest unveiling.

> The messenger is a transmitter (of God's commands and sayings). For this reason he (Muḥammad) said: "Hūd and its sisters made me anxious" (literally: made my hair white), because they (these *sūras*) contain God's saying: "So keep to the right course, as you have been commanded" (Qur'ān 11:112). The words "as you have been commanded" made him anxious, because he did not know whether the command fitted His will and hence would be implemented, or opposed His Will, and hence would not be implemented. No one knows the way of the will (*ḥukm al-irāda*), until the object of the will occurs, except one to whom God has revealed his insight, and as a result he perceives the possible things as they really are in their states as fixed entities and thereupon he judges according to his insight. This may happen to a very few individuals in times of seclusion. (Consequently), the Prophet said: "I do not know what will be done with me or you" (Qur'ān 46:9). He (the Prophet) explicitly said of the veil (existing between God and the humans). The aim (of this verse) is to inform that the Prophet had knowledge only of some things, not all things.

**Arabic terms:** *ḥukm al-irāda* (the way/rule of the will), *ḥadīth* (tradition), *ḥijāb* (veil), *kashf* (unveiling), *khalwa* (seclusion), *thubūt* (fixity), *aʿyān thābita* (fixed entities)

---

### Passage 8 | Ch.8 (Jacob) — The Physician is Servant/Not Servant of Nature

**Context:** The physician analogy is extended to show the paradoxical relationship of the messenger to the divine command — serving nature by opposing it, just as the physician introduces a contrary temperament to restore health. The messengers and heirs are both servant and not-servant of the Real.

> Know that just as it is said of a physician that he is the servant of nature, so the Messengers and the Heirs are the servants of the Divine Command in general, and actually they are the servants of the states of the possible things. Their service is part of their states which have been established by their fixed entities. ... The physician is a servant of nature, only if he helps nature. That is because nature put in the body of the ill person a unique temperament (*mizāj*) because of which he is called an ill person. If the physician were to help nature through his service, he would also increase the measure of illness. He prevents nature (from worsening the illness) only in order to seek health — and health also belongs to nature — through introducing another temperament that opposes the present temperament. Hence, the physician is not a servant of nature (regarding all aspects); he only serves it through curing the ill person and changing his temperament by using nature itself. Regarding nature, he exerts efforts by using a particular, not a general aspect, because generality is not correct in such an issue. Hence, the physician is servant/not servant of nature, just as messengers and heirs are regarding the servitude of the Real.

**Arabic terms:** *ṭabīb* (physician), *ṭabī'a* (nature), *mizāj* (temperament/constitution), *khidma* (service), *amr ilāhī* (divine command), *mumkināt* (possible things)

---

### Passage 9 | Ch.8 (Jacob) — Jacob's Loss of Joseph: The Weeping That Is Vision

**Context:** Though not in the text I read directly (the Jacob chapter ended before reaching this narrative), the theme of Jacob's weeping and his whitened eyes from grief for Joseph — yet his weeping was itself a form of vision, for his inner eye remained open. This passage weaves together the stations of loss, patience, and the unveiling that comes through suffering. [Note: The Abrahamov translation of the Jacob chapter focuses on the philosophical dimensions of religion, requital, and the command/will distinction rather than the narrative of Joseph's loss. The themes of Jacob's spirituality are developed through these doctrinal passages rather than the story itself.]

**Arabic terms:** *yaʿqūb* (Jacob), *yūsuf* (Joseph), *ḥuzn* (grief), *ṣabr* (patience), *baṣīra* (inner vision), *farq* (separation), *jamʿ* (union)
