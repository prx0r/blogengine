## Charged Hook

In September 2025, one girl left Kathmandu's goddess-palace—and another entered.

The outgoing Kumari had been bowed to by kings, presidents and thousands of devotees. The incoming child was carried from her family home because her feet were not supposed to touch public ground.

One explanation says Taleju literally leaves one body and enters another. Another says the girl only symbolizes the goddess.

But neither explains why Buddhist priests help select a child who once legitimized Hindu kings—or why puberty can end her office without ending the goddess.

If the Kumari is truly divine, what exactly becomes mortal when she is replaced?

## Narrative Engine

### Central Mystery
How can a child be worshipped as a goddess for years and then cease to be the goddess through a predictable bodily transition?

### Hypotheses
- **HYP-01**: Literal divine inhabitation (strong emic account, not externally testable)
- **HYP-02**: Symbolic representation (insufficient—devotees don't treat her as acting)
- **HYP-03**: Sacred kingship mechanism (explains royal/state dimension)
- **HYP-04**: Hindu-Buddhist civic bridge (explains lineage and selection)
- **HYP-05**: Transferable relational body (strongest synthesis—office vs person)

## Key Evidence

- Kathmandu's Royal Kumari transfers roughly at puberty
- Selected from Newar Buddhist Śākya family
- Venerated by both Hindus and Buddhists
- Historically blessed Nepalese kings during Indra Jatra
- Institution continued after monarchy abolished (2008)
- Selection involves lineage, bodily criteria, ritual authorities and public recognition
- Current office-holder reportedly installed September 2025
