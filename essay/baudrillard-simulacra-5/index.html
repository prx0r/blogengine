The Hysteria of Production and the Strategy of the Real

other · From Simulacra and Simulations · 1981
Baudrillardreality principleproductionhysteriafascism
Baudrillard considers the practical consequences of simulation. The simulation of a hold-up is not just transgression, but a fundamental threat to law and order.
> Simulation is infinitely more dangerous since it always suggests, over and above its object, that law and order themselves might really be nothing more than a simulation.
— Jean Baudrillard
Transgression plays within the rules; simulation questions the board. This is why power works to reduce simulation to reality.
> The only weapon of power, its only strategy against this defection, is to reinject realness and referentiality everywhere, in order to convince us of the reality of the social, of the gravity of the economy and the finalities of production.
— Jean Baudrillard
This reinjection of realness manifests as a collective hysteria of production.
> Whence the characteristic hysteria of our time: the hysteria of production and reproduction of the real.
— Jean Baudrillard
Material production becomes a desperate attempt to restore a lost referent. Baudrillard ends by diagnosing a profound cultural melancholy.
> Melancholy for societies without power: this has already given rise to fascism, that overdose of a powerful referential in a society which cannot terminate its mourning.
— Jean Baudrillard
The collection reveals the underlying mood of the simulation age: a hysterical production of the real driven by an unacknowledged melancholy for the referentiality that has been lost. Power's remaining strategy is to manufacture crisis in a desperate attempt to feel real.
