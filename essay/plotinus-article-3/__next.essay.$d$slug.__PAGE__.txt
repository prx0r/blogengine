3:T11c1,Modern biology tends to see form as an emergent property of matter, constructed from the bottom up by genes and environment. The conversations with Max Wade, Jack Bagby, and Matt Segall reveal a deeply Plotinian counter-vision: the whole is prior to the parts, and the embryo is not assembled from below but expressed from above, as a descent of form into the willing mirror of matter.</p><p>&gt; "Matt Segall: I've had the thought that matter, to the extent that it is indefiniteness and cannot be intellectually grasped, is similar to the One, which also cannot be intellectually grasped. Matter is indefinite because it is too poor to possess form, and the One is indefinite because it is too rich for form. Max Wade: That is exactly right. Matter is indefinite, undefined. The One is infinite, in the sense that there is no form, because form is an external imposition. For Plotinus, limits are good. Limits are what make something be anything at all."</p><p>This structural parallel between the lowest and the highest suggests that form is not a product of matter but a visitor to it. Matter provides the necessary resistance for form to appear.</p><p>&gt; "For Plotinus, matter in a purely unqualified sense is like a limit... Matter is the stopping point. When you get down to matter, there is a rebounding upward toward the One in the sense that matter becomes the condition for the entire universe."<br/>&gt; – Max Wade</p><p>Jack Bagby notes the peculiar status of this pure matter: it is a hypothetical limit, not a substance among others.</p><p>&gt; "It feels like Plotinus just posits matter. We have the One, then all of a sudden matter, and then everything we see that seems to have matter is really a body. Matter is trailing off. If we are already talking about something, it is intelligible and determinate. The matter of a statue is relative matter, but not real matter. Real matter is this hypothetical thing we need at the limit."<br/>&gt; – Jack Bagby</p><p>This matter functions as a mirror for the intelligibles.</p><p>&gt; "It is connected to the mirror image: matter is a mirror to the intelligibles. It is conscious of the image in the mirror, which is both itself and not itself."<br/>&gt; – Jack Bagby</p><p>Because the whole is prior to the parts, the body is an expression of soul, not a mechanism serving external ends.</p><p>&gt; "Plotinus says no: the whole is prior to the parts. We have all these parts because a unified principle needs to express itself in all its fullness. For the form of the human being to fully express itself, it needs eyebrows, eyelids, eyes, teeth, and so on. So we are not explaining teleology in terms of something external. We are not saying we have eyes in order to see something else, or hands in order to grab food."<br/>&gt; – Max Wade</p><p>The immanent principles that guide this descent are the Logoi Spermatikoi, the seminal reasons that Nature gives to the embryo.</p><p>&gt; "Plotinus does have a notion of seminal principles, logoi spermatikoi. These are immanent formative principles operating within the embryo, so the developmental process does not have to rely on a transcendent cause at every moment. The formative principles necessary for the body to constitute itself are given to it by Nature."<br/>&gt; – Max Wade</p><p>Matt Segall connects this vertical descent directly to the mystery of embryogenesis: each life is a fresh arrival of an eternal form into time.</p><p>&gt; "It seems to me that what Plotinus meant by emanation into matter and bodies comes into what appears to us as a sensory world extended back in time through embryogenesis. Each life is born out of nothing, seemingly. It is not preformation; we know embryogenesis is made there and then by something that is not just a genetic trace or a tiny preformed organism."<br/>&gt; – Matt Segall</p><p>Without this top-down infusion of form, matter is inert. Bodies cannot self-assemble into meaningful wholes without a principle.</p><p>&gt; "Plotinus has a line where he talks about bodies coming together on their own and says that this only produces things like puddles of mud. You need some kind of principle to generate something new."<br/>&gt; – Max Wade</p><p>The universe, for Plotinus, is read from top to bottom. Matter is not the source of form but its horizon, the necessary mirror in which the eternal forms can find a place to appear, to wander, and ultimately to return to themselves.
